Paper Title: On Functional Plurality: A Taxonomy of Benign and Problematic Functions

Paper Abstract: Philosophers interested in conceptual engineering take it for granted that the same concept can unproblematically play diverse functions, but this view overlooks the fact that conceptual and functional change often impair concepts and even functions themselves. I demonstrate that while conceptual and functional engineering may improve concepts and functions, they can also produce detrimental effects. Therefore, it is crucial to carefully assess the potential benefits or problems before making any modifications. Frequently, we overlook the fact that, for instance, adding extra functions to our concepts modifies them; this may increase, but also impair, their theoretical and practical efficacy. I analyze and clarify these possibilities through a general classificatory framework encompassing concepts, functions, and conceptual and functional change. The larger aim of this paper is to bring attention to these complex and under-researched relationships and pave the way for further research in this area.

Introduction

We often assume that we use concepts appropriately: that we know their meanings, referents, and uses.However, we usually and problematically employ more than one concept; we use two, three, or more conflicting concepts at the same time.The problems that generate this defective employment range from harmless ambiguity to impaired communication, impaired concepts and their functions.These difficulties may be of little consequence for daily communication but are dangerously confusing in empirical and theoretical investigations.When a fisherman is attempting to catch whales, for example, it is beside the point whether whales fall under the concept MAMMAL or FISH, but it becomes much more relevant when biologists are doing their taxonomic job.

The importance of understanding the way we use concepts is more evident when concepts and functions experience change.Conceptual change, such as transitioning from the fishers' concept WHALE to the taxonomic WHALE*, can produce functional change in concepts and vice versa: concepts' modification causes a modification in their function and vice versa.But are all kinds and combinations of changes appropriate?We do not have a systematic treatment of the relationships between concepts, functions, and change.We often assume that it is unproblematic if we devise new functions for concepts to attain new goals, yet conceptual and functional modification can have beneficial or problematic implications.In this paper, I outline a necessary and general framework for discussing, assessing, and modifying these relationships.Although I do not intend to provide a complete and completely correct framework, it can provide us with a better understanding of concepts and their functions.

The literature on concepts and functions is immense.Psychologists (see, e.g., Carey, 1985, 1991; diSessa, 2006) and philosophers (Kitcher, 1978; Nersessian, 1980, 2008; Rusanen & Pöyhönen, 2013; Thagard, 1990, 2008) have heavily inquired into the nature of concepts and conceptual change.Recently, the study of functions and functional change has increased among philosophers (Brigandt & Rosario, 2020; Marques & Wikforss, 2020; Nado, 2019; Prinzing, 2017; Riggs, 2021).Despite this vast body of literature, the multiple and complex relationships between concepts, functions, their modifications, and their consequences remain largely obscure.In this paper, I demonstrate that there are two general kinds of conceptual and functional modifications, "benign" and "problematic," that produce benign and problematic concepts and functions.I also highlight the importance of studying the distinctions and parallels between concepts and functions.

This paper has the following organization.First, I analyze the general characteristics of concepts, functions, and the process of conceptual and functional change in Sect. 2.Then, resorting to popular concepts like GENE, SPECIES, and CLIMATE CHANGE, we will see that the functions that remain the same or improve (often due to conceptual improvement) and functional relationships that result from concepts fulfilling compatible functions are benign.Next, in Sect.3, I show that problematic functions and functional relationships result from preserving or worsening functions and concepts fulfilling incompatible functions.In the last section, I synthesize the findings and bring attention to the complex relationships between concepts, functions, and conceptual and functional change.There is not only a plurality in the conceptual realm but also in the functional one.Both interact in different ways and lead to multiple and complex patterns.Thus, I expect that the present analysis encourages further research on conceptual and functional issues like individuation, continuity, verbal disputes, and conceptual and functional engineering.

Benign Functions and Functional Relationships

We have explored some relevant properties of concepts, functions, and conceptual and functional change.However, given that this paper deals mainly with functions, in this and the following section, I offer a non-exhaustive general framework for classifying them.The fundamental classification is between benign and problematic functions and benign and problematic functional relationships.In this section, we will see the benign cases of which there are two broad kinds: when we directly preserve or improve a function or if the functional relationship is one of compatibility-which is an innocuous relationship that could even improve functions.

Function Preservation and Improvement

My main interest is in functions, so I will not discuss the details of conceptual change, but please note that functional change, as mentioned above, is the product of conceptual change (and remember that the second is compatible with the stability of functions).There are two broad kinds of functional change that preserve or produce benign functions: first, functional preservation (of already benign functions) or improvement, and second, the conceptual fulfillment of novel compatible functions.Let us begin with the first kind.

Functions can either improve or remain the same. 12In both cases, except when the original function is problematic (we will tackle these cases in the next section), the resulting functions are benign.Logically, the preservation of benign functions gives us benign functions because no change has taken place to the original benign functions.The Mendelian concept GENE, for example, and its benign function of explaining the dynamics of inheritance remained the same for decades.The function of the Mendelian concept GENE fulfilled at that time was benign and remained benign even after the arrival of molecular genetics.Another kind of benign change occurs when conceptual change improves the original functions. 13Categories like the Mendelian concept GENE and the pre-Darwinian concept SPECIES have improved thanks to advances in molecular genetics and the introduction of the theory of evolution.The pre-Darwinian GENE and SPECIES have evolved alongside scientific knowledge and have led to better functions like explaining, predicting, and providing a comprehensive understanding of genes and species. 14

Compatible Functional Relationships

Another kind of benign functional change occurs when concepts play novel functions that are compatible with the existing ones.The new functional relationship is benign regardless of whether the novel functions are benign or problematic by themselves.

species from an evolutionary perspective in a phylogenetic context.There is no incompatibility either at the coarse-grained epistemic function of describing species because SPECIES fulfills this general function in both contexts.On the other hand, the two epistemic functions are compatible with the fine-grained epistemic functions of describing species in a biological context (describing species' patterns of reproduction) and in a phylogenetic one (describing their patterns of evolution).Now, both fine-grained epistemic functions of SPECIES support fine-grained non-epistemic ones.The biological concept supports biodiversity preservation (remember the case of the Alabama sturgeon), and the phylogenetic one can be helpful in following the spread of malaria disease (remember the case of the Anopheles mosquito).There is no incompatibility because both epistemic and non-epistemic functions are operating in different contexts.A conflict could, of course, arise between epistemic and non-epistemic functions if we use the biological concept for tracking malaria and the phylogenetic for biodiversity preservation.However, incompatibility does not arise because each concept supports its respective practical role.

We must also pay attention to the relationships between fine-grained and coarsegrained functions.The fine-grained epistemic functions of both the biological and the phylogenetic concepts fulfill the coarse-grained epistemic function of describing species and the fine-grained non-epistemic ones of preserving biodiversity and understanding the distribution of malaria, respectively.Why is it important?Because there are also compatibility and incompatibility relationships between finegrained and coarse-grained functions.The fine-grained non-epistemic function of preserving biodiversity is incompatible with the phylogenetic concept SPECIES** (its fine-grained epistemic counterpart) but compatible with the coarse-grained epistemic function of describing species.So, we must be careful to contextually assess these multifarious relationships.Compatibility is contextual.

The above considerations suggest that preserving already impaired functions or directly impairing them and consenting to functional incompatibility are problematic.Functional relationships may produce problematic functions when functional change preserves incompatibility and when the latter worsens one or more of the interacting functions.Therefore, these principles (parallel to those at the end of Sect.3) follow:

(a) Functional preservation (of already problematic functions) and impairment preserve problematic and bring about worse functions, respectively.(b) Incompatible functional relations preserve or worsen problematic functions.(c) The incompatibility of functions is often context-dependent and it may take place in just some contexts or across all of them.

Functional Plurality

Let us take stock.I have argued that there are benign and problematic functions and functional relationships.We must note that concepts and functions influence each other.Conceptual change can preserve, improve, or deteriorate functions and vice versa.Without losing sight of this mutual influence and noting that I focus on the dynamics of functions, we can see that the principles (i) and (a) of Sects. 3 and 4 imply that:

  1. Functional preservation (of already benign functions) and improvement preserve benign functions and bring about better ones, respectively, and functional preservation (of already problematic functions) and impairment preserve problematic functions and worsen problematic ones, respectively. 24

Compatibility or incompatibility also play a crucial role because:

  1. Compatible functional relations preserve or improve benign functions (principle (ii) of Sect.3). 3. Incompatible functional relations preserve or worsen problematic functions (principle (b) of Sect.4).

From principles 1, 2, and 3, we can conclude that if we want to avoid problematic cases, we must preserve or improve functions and ensure that they are compatible.Conceptual engineers and philosophers interested in the diverse functions fulfilled by concepts have not carefully addressed this issue.However, a deep examination of this plurality is a crucial element for using concepts effectively.We cannot take for granted that a given concept can fulfill diverse roles without anticipating that functional change leads to either benign functions and functional relationships or to their problematic counterparts.We need to assess compatibility first.Furthermore, philosophers and other engineers and users of concepts must seriously consider the ideas that: 4. The compatibility and incompatibility of functions are often context-dependent (by principles (iii) and (c) of Sects. 3 and 4). 5.There may be compatible and incompatible functions in just some contexts or across all of them (by principles (iii) and (c) of Sects. 3 and 4).

Principles 4 and 5 warn us about the necessity of identifying the contextual range of functions to determine compatibility or incompatibility.Some functions are context-relative, but others may span across many or all contexts.25These principles have not received sufficient attention in the literature despite their importance in identifying genuine incompatibilities and, therefore, avoiding problematic uses of concepts.

The existence of epistemic, non-epistemic, fine-grained, and coarse-grained functions, along with principles 4 and 5 remind us that functional plurality is quite complex.In the previous section, we saw that the fine-grained functions (biological and phylogenetic) of SPECIES are fulfilled by two concepts (SPECIES and SPECIES) or these functions are fulfilled by the sole concept of SPECIES but in different contexts.The epistemic function of describing species from a biological perspective is compatible with the non-epistemic function of preserving biodiversity, but the epistemic function of describing species from a phylogenetic perspective is not.However, as principle 5 implies, compatibility and incompatibility may occur across all contexts, so we must also assess them across all contexts.Since the coarse-grained epistemic function of describing species has a greater contextual reach, it is compatible-unlike the fine-grained epistemic function of the phylogenetic concept SPECIES-with the non-epistemic function of preserving biodiversity.In other words, the non-epistemic function of biological preservation is incompatible at the fine-grained level but compatible at the coarse-grained level.Specific functions may affect their more general counterparts and vice versa.This discussion suggests that we need to analyze case by case against a general classificatory framework like the one I present and that it would pay off to investigate the not yet fully recognized complex relationships that functions maintain across different contexts.

The present analysis has additional relevant implications.To begin with, we must acknowledge the close similarities between concepts and functions, as well as between conceptual change and functional change.Most of the ongoing research has focused on the individuation of concepts and the modeling of conceptual change, but functions and functional change exhibit similar properties.We can regard both conceptual and functional change as a matter of degree (and, as with concepts, we do not need clear-cut conditions of individuation for functions to analyze their transformation).In the same way that we are careful in assessing the conditions of individuation of concepts, we must pay attention to functional individuation, especially when conceptual and functional change occur.

In the same way that we strive to preserve, improve, and maintain consistency among our concepts, we must care about functions.Moreover, the plurality of functions intertwines with that of concepts, and one can influence each other.We must be careful to preserve good concepts and functions, improve them when needed, and assess their compatibility.

Another fundamental implication of the present analysis is that the plurality of functions demands further research on each aspect of this topic. 26We need to pursue a line of research parallel to conceptual engineering regarding functions: a kind of "functional engineering" research (Nado, 2019).From conceptual engineering to conceptual amelioration and metalinguistic negotiations, we must consider the applicability of these procedures to functions.

Finally, if the thesis that there are close similarities between concepts and functions is correct, then the current discussions concerning individuations, continuity, and verbal disputes apply to functions too.We need more clarity on the identity conditions of functions and their relationship to topic continuity.Finally, in the face of the probable difficulty of functional discontinuity, could we appeal to functions of a higher kind or any other similar notion that does the job?

Conclusion

I have argued that functions possess some properties analogous to those of concepts.To effectively understand their nature, I have offered a non-exhaustive general framework that emphasizes the importance of classifying various kinds of functions and their interrelationships.Concepts can play different functions that can be benign or problematic, and we must carefully assess them.We must be especially alert to address problematic cases, particularly when we want a concept to fulfill a non-epistemic function that clashes with the already epistemic function that the concept fulfills (or vice versa).We must also be cautious and make the necessary tradeoffs, revisions, and eliminations.

We need to do much more research on the topic of functions.We must be aware of the existence of the plurality not only of concepts but also of functions and that both concepts and functions can be subject to parallel treatment, allowing us to ameliorate or impair them through functional engineering.
