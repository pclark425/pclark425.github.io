Although Blocq and Marinesco' first described plaques i n 1892 a n d Alzheimer2 described NFTs in 1907, the relationship of NFTs and SPs to the degree of cognitive impairment in AD still remains controversial. To overcome these limitations, we counted NFTs and SPs in 13 different brain regions of 10 AD patients who were examined prospectively in the Memory Disorders Unit a t t h e Massachusetts General Hospital. We sought to test the following hypotheses: (1) t h e number of NFTs and SPs increase with increasing severity or duration of dementia, (2) NFTs and SPs accumulate independently in various brain regions, according to a consistent pattern of hierarchic vulnerability, and (3) limbic areas tend to be involved by NFTs and association cortices by SPs relatively early in the disease process.

Relation of t h e severity of disease and NFT and SP accumulation. The severity of dementia in AD may be viewed as either the degree of cognitive impairment (BDS score) or the duration of disease; clinical experience suggests that the two variables should, in general, correlate well. In our sample, scores on the BDS were significantly correlated with duration of disease (R = 0.71, p < 0.05). Nonetheless, we analyzed both measures of disease severity independently. For initial analyses, the number of NFTs and SPs was averaged over all areas examined to provide an estimate of overall "burden" of NFTs and SPs. In addition, factor analysis revealed two groups of related regions for NFTs: one group contained the hippocampal fields (DG, CA3/4, CAl/S) and AMG, and the second group, neocortical areas (areas 819, 20/21, 7, and 17). The EC did not factor into either group, perhaps because of a ceiling effect.

The first was the Newcastle upon Tyne study by Blessed et al.4 In that study, however, the authors pointed out that a positive correlation between SP number and cognitive assessment score depended upon including in the analysis individuals who were not demented. If the nondemented or mildly demented subjects were removed from the analysis, the correlation coefficient would be reduced to c0.4 and would lose statistical significance. Similar issues are present in the study of Duyckaerts et a1,6 who examined brains of intellectually normal as well as AD subjects, and reported that the SP density in four neocortical areas (first temporal, supramarginal, calcarine, and precentral gyri) was significantly correlated with the degree of mental impairment. In an extension of that study, Delaere In our data, correlations between the number of SPs and duration of illness or BDS score were not significant in any individual region nor when grouped. In other words, we could not establish a link between SP number and degree of dementia. There are several possible explanations for this finding. SPs may, in fact, not contribute to progression of dementia. Alternatively, SPs may be both formed and destroyed by metabolic processes over the course of years, so that SP numbers at a given time do not reflect total accumulation of p-A4 amyloid. Such a possibility is consistent with the observation that the number of SPs and NFTs in the frontal cortex did not differ significantly between time of biopsy and death in a small series of AD patients.23

Whereas SPs were not related to dementia severity, our results reinforce the idea that the formation of NFTs contributes to cognitive impairment. This conclusion is consistent with the results of Morris e t all1: in a study of mildly demented individuals, they found that severity of dementia was better correlated with the density of neocortical tangles than with the plaque density, and that there was no significant difference in total plaque count in individuals with mild dementia compared with individuals with more advanced dementia. In another report,13 the same group studied 12 AD patients (six of whom were "mild"), and reported "substantial concentrations of SPs throughout the cortex" in the mildly demented individuals. The limbic areas were the most severely affected by NFTs in cases with mild dementia, and "the neocortex did not have substantial densities of NFTs unless moderate to severe dementia was clearly established.'' In a study of 35 individuals with AD, which included samples from frontal, temporal, parietal and occipital cortices, hippocampus, and parahippocampal gyrus, Wilcock et a15 also demonstrated that the presence and severity of dementia correlated better with NFTs than with SPs in the cerebral cortex.

We found a significant correlation between BDS and NFTs in neocortical areas but not in limbic areas. The lack of correlation between limbic NFTs and BDS score is likely due to the nature of the clinical test. Only five points of the total BDS score depend upon retaining newly acquired verbal information for 5 minutes. AD patients always fail this task early in the course of the disease. There is, therefore, a ceiling effect on the portion of the BDS that is most likely affected by pathologic changes in the EC, AMG, and hippocampus; thus, an increasing severity of limbic area pathologic changes would not necessarily be reflected by worsening in the overall BDS score.

Topographic distribution of AD changes form a stereotyped p a t t e r n that is different f o r NFTs a n d SPs. By examination of 13 cytoarchitectural regions in AD, we found a consistent pattern of hierarchic vulnerability for NFT formation. The EC, area CAlIS of the hippocampus, and the AMG, followed by association cortex (high-order > primary), were the most vulnerable areas; primary sensory cortex was least affected. The distribution of SPs was markedly different from that of NFTs, but also followed a consistent pattern, with neocortical rather than medial temporal lobe areas (except for AMG) being most severely affected. Association cortex was still more affected than primary sensory cortex, but the relative difference for SPs was far less than for NFTs. These patterns confirm and extend the results of other investigations in AD and Down's syndrome.5J3,22.24-35 A notable exception to this pattern of hierarchic change is the prominent parieto-occipital pathologic changes found in a subset of AD patients with the clinical presentation of B a h t ' s syndrome.36 None of our patients had this clinical syndrome, but had the much more common presenting symptom of memory impairment. It is likely that anatomic variations in the distribution of pathologic lesions such as these account for clinical subtypes of AD-type dementia. The topographic distribution of NFTs and SPs observed in the four control cases of non-AD dementia resembles the pattern observed in nondemented elderly individual^.'^^^' This pattern was similar to AD cases in terms of rank order, but the degree of NFT deposition was considerably less. The presence of SPs was less constant than the presence of NFTs, and when present, SPs usually involved neocortical areas rather than EC, hippocampal formation, or AMG.

The hierarchic pattern of vulnerability of different cytoarchitectural areas to pathologic changes in AD and the relation between degree of abnormality and duration of clinical disease suggest a pattern of progression, although this pattern is different for NFTs and SPs. One individual with the shortest duration (<1 year) had NFTs almost exclusively in the vulnerable medial temporal lobe areas, namely EC (affecting layers I1 and IV) and C A M field of the hippocampus. Cases with longer disease duration (1 to 4 years) had NFTs in neocortical association areas as well a s in the limbic areas. Individuals with the longest duration b 5 years) continued to accumulate more NFTs i n t h e s e areas, and also developed NFTs in primary sensory areas (figure 5). SPs had a different pattern of distribution and degree in relation to duration of disease than NFTs (figure 6). SPs early in the disease involved neocortical areas and the AMG rather than the hippocampal formation and EC. Even early in the disease, there can be considerable numbers of SPs. Overall, the pattern and degree of SP distribution do not change with increasing length of disease. Thus the number of SPs is not a good histologic marker of illness progression.

We proposed that the early memory impairment in AD is related to the disruption of projections among the EC, hippocampal formation, and AMG a t multiple sites, and also of projections between these structures and cortical and subcortical target^. Dystrophic neurites have also been shown to correlate positively with the presence of dementia i n AD,41 and again may reflect progressive disruption of the neuropil. Because our current observations show that the anatomic distribution and degree of SPs did not correlate with the severity or duration of cognitive impairment, it is unlikely that the cognitive decline is related to progressive accumulation of SPs. Instead, our data emphasize the relationship between NFTs and dementia symptoms. We suggest that NFTs preferentially accumulate in ventromedial temporal lobe structures in the early stages of AD and contribute to early memory impairment. It is likely that the number of NFTs in neocortical areas and NBM increases with time. The RN and LC have been reported to have neuronal loss early in AD and in middle-aged individuals with Down's syndrome.42 Such changes could provide a n anatomic substrate of the progression of dementia a s the result of progressive disruption of neural systems in and between neocortical association areas and widely projecting subcortical nuclei.
