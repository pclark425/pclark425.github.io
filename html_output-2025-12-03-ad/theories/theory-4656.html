Theory Statements

Statement 0: Molecular Pathology Propagates via Brain Networks (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has_local_amyloid_tau_pathology → true, and
• brain_region_A → is_connected_to → brain_region_B
THEN
• brain_region_B → develops_pathology → amyloid_tau

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Tau and amyloid pathology spreads along synaptically connected brain regions in both animal models and human imaging studies.
  2. Connectomic analyses show that regions with high connectivity are more vulnerable to early AD pathology.
  3. Network-based models predict the spatial-temporal progression of AD pathology.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The propagation concept is established, but the explicit network-based law is a more formal, predictive extension.

What Already Exists: Trans-synaptic propagation of tau and amyloid is established in models.

What is Novel: This law formalizes the necessity of network connectivity for propagation and predicts specific vulnerable nodes.

References:

  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Network vulnerability]
  • Braak & Braak (1991) Neuropathological stageing of Alzheimer-related changes [Pathology progression]
  • Raj et al. (2012) A network diffusion model of disease progression in dementia [Network propagation]

Statement 1: Network Disintegration Precedes Clinical Symptoms (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has_network_disintegration → default_mode_or_memory_network
THEN
• individual → is_at_high_risk_for_AD → true

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Functional MRI and EEG studies show network-level disruptions (e.g., in the default mode network) in preclinical AD.
  2. Network disintegration predicts cognitive decline better than regional atrophy or amyloid load alone.
  3. Longitudinal studies show network changes precede overt cognitive symptoms.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The predictive sufficiency of network disintegration is a novel, testable claim.

What Already Exists: Network changes are observed in preclinical AD.

What is Novel: This law asserts that network disintegration is a necessary and sufficient early marker for AD risk.

References:

  • Buckner et al. (2005) Molecular, structural, and functional characterization of Alzheimer's disease: Evidence for a relationship between default activity, amyloid, and memory [Network focus]
  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Network vulnerability]
  • Jones et al. (2016) Cascading network failure across the Alzheimer's disease spectrum [Network disintegration]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early network disintegration (e.g., reduced default mode network connectivity) will develop AD at higher rates than those without such changes.
  2. Interventions that preserve or restore network integrity will delay or prevent AD onset, even if molecular pathology is present.
  3. Network-based biomarkers (e.g., EEG/fMRI connectivity) will outperform regional atrophy or amyloid PET in predicting conversion to AD.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Non-invasive brain stimulation targeting network hubs will halt or reverse network disintegration and slow AD progression.
  2. Network disintegration patterns will allow subtyping of AD with distinct clinical trajectories and treatment responses.
  3. Network-level interventions (e.g., cognitive training) will be effective even in the presence of significant molecular pathology.

Negative Experiments

  1. If network disintegration does not precede clinical symptoms, the theory is undermined.
  2. If AD pathology spreads to unconnected regions, the network propagation law is challenged.
  3. If network-based biomarkers do not outperform molecular or regional markers, the theory's predictive claims are weakened.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with minimal network disruption but significant molecular pathology are not fully explained.
  2. Some individuals with network disintegration do not develop AD, suggesting additional protective factors.
