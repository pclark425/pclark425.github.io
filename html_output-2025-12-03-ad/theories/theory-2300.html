Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2300

Name: Network Disintegration and Propagation Theory of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory proposes that Alzheimer's disease is fundamentally a disorder of large-scale brain network disintegration, initiated by local molecular pathology (e.g., amyloid, tau) that propagates through synaptically connected networks. The spread of pathology follows the brain's functional and structural connectivity, leading to progressive cognitive and behavioral deficits. Early detection is possible by identifying network-level changes (e.g., via functional MRI, EEG, or digital cognitive mapping) that precede overt atrophy or clinical symptoms.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Pathology Propagates Along Brain Networks (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → local amyloid or tau pathology in a brain region, and
• brain region → is highly connected to → other brain regions
THEN
• pathology → spreads to → connected regions following network topology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Imaging studies show tau and amyloid spread along functional and structural brain networks.
  2. Network-based models predict the spatial and temporal progression of AD pathology.
  3. Synaptic connectivity correlates with vulnerability to pathology spread.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law extends propagation models to detection and prediction.

What Already Exists: Network-based propagation models are emerging but not widely formalized.

What is Novel: The explicit linkage of molecular pathology to network disintegration and its use for early detection is novel.

References:

  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network vulnerability]
  • Raj (2012) A network diffusion model of disease progression in dementia [network propagation]

Statement 1: Network Dysfunction Precedes Structural Atrophy (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → shows → altered functional connectivity (e.g., fMRI, EEG) in key brain networks
THEN
• individual → is at increased risk for → future regional atrophy and cognitive decline

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Functional connectivity changes are detectable before structural atrophy in AD progression.
  2. Longitudinal studies show that network dysfunction predicts subsequent atrophy and clinical decline.
  3. Digital cognitive mapping can reveal early network-level deficits.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law advances the use of network analysis for early detection.

What Already Exists: Functional network changes are observed in AD, but their predictive value is underexplored.

What is Novel: The formalization of network dysfunction as a primary, predictive biomarker is novel.

References:

  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network vulnerability]
  • Brier (2014) Functional connectivity and graph theory in preclinical Alzheimer's disease [network biomarkers]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early network dysfunction will develop regional atrophy and cognitive decline in predictable patterns.
  2. Network-based imaging and digital cognitive mapping will detect preclinical AD earlier than traditional structural imaging.
  3. Interventions that preserve or restore network connectivity will slow disease progression.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Non-invasive brain stimulation targeting network hubs will prevent or reverse network disintegration in preclinical AD.
  2. Distinct network propagation patterns will define AD subtypes with different clinical trajectories.
  3. Digital behavioral signatures will map onto specific network dysfunctions, enabling remote detection.

Negative Experiments

  1. Failure of network dysfunction to predict atrophy or clinical decline would challenge the theory.
  2. Demonstration that pathology spreads independently of network connectivity would call the propagation model into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some cases of AD may show diffuse pathology not clearly following network topology.
