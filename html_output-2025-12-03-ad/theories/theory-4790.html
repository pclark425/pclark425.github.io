Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4790

Name: Network Topology-Metabolic Coupling Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory posits that the selective vulnerability of visual cortical neurons to amyloid-β and tau pathology is determined by the interplay between their position in the cortical network (network topology, e.g., hubness, centrality) and their metabolic phenotype (e.g., mitochondrial efficiency, glycolytic capacity). Neurons that are network hubs with high centrality and low metabolic reserve are most susceptible to proteopathic stress, while peripheral or metabolically robust neurons are more resistant.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Network hub neurons in the cortex are more metabolically stressed and show earlier signs of degeneration in AD models.
  2. Neurons with high centrality and low mitochondrial reserve are more likely to accumulate protein aggregates.
  3. Functional imaging shows that highly connected visual cortical regions are early sites of amyloid and tau deposition.

THEN

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  2. Regions with low network centrality in the visual cortex are relatively spared in early AD.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law proposes a new, specific mechanism for resistance based on network and metabolic features.

What Already Exists: Metabolic reserve and network topology are known to influence neuronal resilience.

What is Novel: The law's focus on the interaction between low centrality and high metabolic reserve as a determinant of resistance is novel.

References:

  • de Haan et al. (2012) Disrupted modular brain dynamics reflect cognitive dysfunction in Alzheimer's disease [Network hub vulnerability]
  • Mattson et al. (2008) Mitochondria in neuroplasticity and neurological disorders [Metabolic stress]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Reducing network centrality (e.g., by synaptic pruning) in metabolically vulnerable neurons will decrease their susceptibility to amyloid and tau pathology.
  2. Enhancing mitochondrial function in network hub neurons will confer resistance to aggregate formation.
  3. Network hubs with low metabolic reserve will show the earliest pathology in the visual cortex.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially increasing network centrality in metabolically robust neurons will not increase their vulnerability to pathology.
  2. Disrupting network topology without altering metabolic reserve will not induce pathology.
  3. Boosting metabolic reserve in network hubs will delay, but not fully prevent, pathology if amyloid burden is high.

Negative Experiments

  1. If network hub neurons with high metabolic reserve are still highly vulnerable, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If peripheral neurons with low metabolic reserve are equally vulnerable, the theory would be called into question.
  3. If altering network topology does not affect vulnerability, the theory would be falsified.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The role of local microenvironmental factors (e.g., glial support, vascularization) is not addressed.
  2. Genetic and epigenetic modifiers of metabolic reserve are not incorporated.
