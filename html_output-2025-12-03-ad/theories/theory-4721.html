Name: Integrated Network Failure Theory of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the cause of Alzheimer's disease and how it can be effectively detected.

Description: Alzheimer's disease arises from the failure of interconnected neural, vascular, and immune networks, where disruptions in one system propagate dysfunction across others, leading to progressive neurodegeneration. Detection is most effective when multi-modal network-level biomarkers are assessed, capturing early breakdowns in connectivity, perfusion, and immune regulation.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Network Disruption Cascade (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → chronic dysfunction in neural, vascular, or immune network
THEN
• dysfunction → propagates → to other networks (neural, vascular, immune), and
• individual → develops → progressive neurodegeneration

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neural, vascular, and immune dysfunctions are each associated with AD, and evidence shows cross-talk between these systems (e.g., neurovascular unit breakdown, neuroinflammation, blood-brain barrier leakage).
  2. Longitudinal imaging and biomarker studies show that early vascular or immune changes can precede and predict neural degeneration.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law synthesizes multi-system evidence into a network failure model, rather than isolated pathologies.

What Already Exists: Individual network dysfunctions (neural, vascular, immune) are recognized in AD.

What is Novel: The explicit framing of AD as a failure of interconnected networks, with dysfunction propagating between systems, is novel.

References:

  • Zlokovic (2011) Neurovascular pathways to neurodegeneration in Alzheimer's disease [Neurovascular unit dysfunction]
  • Heneka (2015) Neuroinflammation in Alzheimer's disease [Immune-neural interactions]
  • Iturria-Medina (2016) Early role of vascular dysregulation in late-onset Alzheimer's disease [Network-level modeling]

Statement 1: Multi-Modal Network Biomarker Detection (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → abnormalities in neural connectivity (e.g., fMRI, EEG), and
• individual → has → vascular dysfunction (e.g., perfusion MRI, blood-brain barrier leakage markers), and
• individual → has → immune dysregulation (e.g., CSF cytokines, microglial activation)
THEN
• individual → is at high risk for → Alzheimer's disease onset or progression

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Multi-modal biomarker studies show that combining neural, vascular, and immune markers improves early AD prediction.
  2. Network-level imaging abnormalities can precede clinical symptoms and are associated with future decline.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law proposes a new detection paradigm based on network-level, multi-system integration.

What Already Exists: Individual biomarker modalities are used for AD risk assessment.

What is Novel: The requirement for multi-modal, network-level biomarker integration for early detection is novel.

References:

  • Jack (2018) NIA-AA Research Framework: Toward a biological definition of Alzheimer's disease [Biomarker-based diagnosis]
  • Iturria-Medina (2016) Early role of vascular dysregulation in late-onset Alzheimer's disease [Network modeling and multi-modal biomarkers]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early, subtle abnormalities in two or more network systems (e.g., neural and vascular) will progress to AD at higher rates than those with single-system abnormalities.
  2. Interventions that restore function in one network (e.g., vascular health) will slow or prevent dysfunction in other networks and delay AD onset.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificial intelligence models integrating multi-modal network biomarkers will outperform current single-modality approaches in predicting AD conversion.
  2. Restoring immune regulation alone (e.g., via microglial modulation) may be sufficient to halt network failure and prevent AD, even if neural or vascular dysfunction is present.

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals with multi-system network dysfunction do not develop AD, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If AD can develop in the absence of any detectable network-level dysfunction, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with no detectable network-level dysfunction in neural, vascular, or immune systems.
