Name: Network Disintegration Cascade Theory

Theory Query: Build a theory of the cause of Alzheimer's disease and how it can be effectively detected.

Description: Alzheimer's disease is fundamentally a disorder of progressive large-scale brain network disintegration, triggered by the convergence of multiple molecular pathologies (amyloid, tau, neuroinflammation, vascular dysfunction, etc.). Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Convergent Pathology Triggers Network Breakdown (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• brain → exhibits → multiple molecular pathologies (amyloid, tau, neuroinflammation, vascular dysfunction)
THEN
• large-scale brain networks → undergo → progressive disintegration

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Functional MRI and connectomics studies show early network disruption in AD, often preceding overt atrophy or amyloid/tau PET positivity.
  2. Multiple pathologies (amyloid, tau, vascular, inflammation) are often present in AD brains and interact synergistically.
  3. Network-based analyses reveal that the default mode network and other large-scale networks are disrupted early in AD.
  4. Vascular dysfunction and neuroinflammation can independently cause network disruption, even in the absence of amyloid or tau.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While network changes are studied, this theory elevates network disintegration as the central, earliest event, integrating multiple pathologies as triggers.

What Already Exists: Network dysfunction is recognized as a feature of AD, and multiple pathologies are known to co-occur.

What is Novel: The primacy of network disintegration as the earliest, unifying event, and as a superior detection target, is novel.

References:

  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Describes network targeting in neurodegeneration]
  • Brier (2014) Functional connectivity and graph theory in preclinical Alzheimer's disease [Shows early network changes]
  • Iturria-Medina (2016) Early role of vascular dysregulation in late-onset Alzheimer's disease [Multiple pathologies interact]

Statement 1: Network Disintegration Precedes Clinical Symptoms (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → early network disintegration (measured by fMRI/EEG/MEG/connectomics)
THEN
• individual → is at increased risk for → future cognitive decline and Alzheimer's diagnosis

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Longitudinal studies show that network connectivity loss predicts conversion from mild cognitive impairment to AD.
  2. Network changes can be detected in asymptomatic individuals with genetic risk (e.g., APOE4 carriers).
  3. EEG and MEG studies reveal altered network synchrony in preclinical AD.
  4. Network disintegration is observed in individuals with subjective cognitive decline before measurable memory loss.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: Existing work recognizes early network changes, but this law asserts their primacy for detection and prediction.

What Already Exists: Network changes are known to occur early in AD.

What is Novel: The assertion that network disintegration is the most sensitive and earliest marker, and should be the primary detection target, is novel.

References:

  • Brier (2014) Functional connectivity and graph theory in preclinical Alzheimer's disease [Early network changes]
  • Jones (2016) Cascading network failure across the Alzheimer's disease spectrum [Network failure as a progression marker]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with measurable large-scale network disintegration (via fMRI/EEG/connectomics) but no cognitive symptoms will have a higher rate of progression to AD than those with only amyloid or tau positivity.
  2. Interventions that restore or stabilize network connectivity (e.g., neuromodulation, cognitive training) will delay onset or progression of AD symptoms, even if molecular pathology is present.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Network disintegration patterns may allow subtyping of AD into distinct progression trajectories, independent of amyloid/tau burden.
  2. Non-invasive network-based biomarkers (e.g., EEG-derived metrics) may outperform current molecular biomarkers in predicting AD onset in diverse populations.

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals with clear network disintegration do not progress to AD or cognitive decline, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If AD can develop in the absence of any measurable network disintegration, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with minimal network disruption but severe molecular pathology.
