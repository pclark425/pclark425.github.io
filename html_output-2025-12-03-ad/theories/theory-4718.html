Name: Network Disconnection and Propagation Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the cause of Alzheimer's disease and how it can be effectively detected.

Description: Alzheimer's disease arises from the propagation of misfolded protein seeds (amyloid, tau) along vulnerable neural networks, leading to progressive disconnection of functional brain circuits. Early detection is possible by identifying network-level dysfunction (e.g., via functional MRI or EEG) before overt pathology accumulates.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Misfolded Protein Propagation Along Neural Networks (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• misfolded protein seeds → are present in → vulnerable brain regions, and
• brain networks → are highly interconnected → True
THEN
• misfolded proteins → propagate along → anatomical and functional connections, and
• propagation → leads to → network-level dysfunction and disconnection

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Tau and amyloid pathology spreads in stereotyped patterns along neural circuits.
  2. Functional connectivity loss precedes overt atrophy in AD.
  3. Animal models show prion-like spread of tau/amyloid along synaptic connections.
  4. Early AD symptoms correlate with disruption of specific brain networks (e.g., default mode network).

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: Propagation and network disconnection are known, but their integration as a predictive detection paradigm is new.

What Already Exists: Prion-like propagation of tau/amyloid and network disconnection are both described in AD literature.

What is Novel: The explicit linking of propagation dynamics to network-level detection and prediction is novel.

References:

  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Network vulnerability]
  • Braak (1991) Neuropathological staging of Alzheimer-related changes [Stereotyped spread]
  • de Calignon (2012) Propagation of tau pathology in a model of early Alzheimer's disease [Prion-like spread]

Statement 1: Network Dysfunction as Early Detection Marker (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → shows → disrupted functional connectivity in AD-vulnerable networks (e.g., default mode network)
THEN
• individual → is at increased risk for → Alzheimer's onset or progression

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Functional MRI and EEG detect network dysfunction before clinical symptoms.
  2. Network-level changes predict cognitive decline better than amyloid/tau burden alone.
  3. Longitudinal studies show that network disconnection precedes atrophy and overt pathology.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: Network changes are studied, but their primacy as detection tools is a new synthesis.

What Already Exists: Network dysfunction is recognized as an early feature of AD.

What is Novel: The assertion that network-level detection outperforms molecular markers for early diagnosis is novel.

References:

  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Network vulnerability]
  • Greicius (2004) Default-mode network activity distinguishes Alzheimer's disease from healthy aging [Functional MRI in AD]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early network dysfunction (e.g., reduced default mode network connectivity) will develop AD symptoms before those with only amyloid/tau positivity.
  2. Interventions that preserve network connectivity (e.g., cognitive training, neuromodulation) will delay AD onset.
  3. Network-level imaging will detect preclinical AD earlier than amyloid/tau PET or CSF markers.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Restoring network connectivity (e.g., via brain stimulation) may reverse or halt AD progression.
  2. Distinct network disconnection patterns may predict different AD subtypes or symptom profiles.
  3. Peripheral interventions (e.g., sensory enrichment) may modulate network vulnerability to propagation.

Negative Experiments

  1. If network dysfunction does not precede clinical symptoms or pathology, the theory is undermined.
  2. If individuals with network disconnection do not develop AD, the predictive value is challenged.
  3. If propagation of misfolded proteins does not follow network architecture, the central mechanism is questioned.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with minimal network dysfunction but significant pathology.
  2. Individuals with network disconnection due to other causes (e.g., vascular disease) who do not develop AD.
