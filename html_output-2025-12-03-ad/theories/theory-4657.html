Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4657

Name: Integrated Network Failure Model of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the cause of Alzheimer's disease and how it can be effectively detected.

Description: Alzheimer's disease arises from the progressive breakdown of large-scale brain network connectivity, driven by a convergence of molecular, cellular, and vascular insults. The earliest detectable changes are functional network disruptions, which precede classical amyloid and tau pathology. Effective detection is possible by monitoring network-level biomarkers using advanced neuroimaging and electrophysiological techniques.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Network Disintegration Precedes Classical Pathology (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has_disrupted_brain_network_connectivity → true
THEN
• individual → develops_cognitive_decline → later, and
• individual → develops_amyloid_tau_pathology → later

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Resting-state fMRI and EEG studies show network connectivity changes in at-risk individuals before amyloid/tau accumulation.
  2. Longitudinal imaging reveals network breakdown predicts cognitive decline better than amyloid/tau burden alone.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: The primacy of network failure as the initiating event is novel.

What Already Exists: Network dysfunction is recognized as a feature of AD, but not as the earliest event.

What is Novel: This law posits network disintegration as the primary, earliest detectable event and potential cause.

References:

  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Network focus, not causality]
  • Brier (2012) Loss of functional connectivity in preclinical Alzheimer's disease [Network changes, not as earliest cause]

Statement 1: Network Biomarkers Enable Early Detection (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has_abnormal_network_biomarkers → true
THEN
• individual → is_in_preclinical_AD → true

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Altered default mode network connectivity is observed in asymptomatic individuals with genetic or biomarker risk for AD.
  2. EEG/MEG signatures of network disruption predict conversion to AD in mild cognitive impairment.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: The use of network-level biomarkers as the earliest and most sensitive detection method is novel.

What Already Exists: Network biomarkers are being explored for early detection.

What is Novel: This law asserts their primacy and specificity as the earliest reliable markers.

References:

  • Brier (2012) Loss of functional connectivity in preclinical Alzheimer's disease [Network biomarkers, not as primary detection tool]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with abnormal network connectivity but normal amyloid/tau will develop AD at higher rates.
  2. Interventions that restore network connectivity will delay or prevent cognitive decline, even if amyloid/tau are present.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Non-invasive brain stimulation targeting network hubs will prevent AD onset in high-risk individuals.
  2. Portable EEG-based screening will enable population-level early AD detection before molecular biomarkers are positive.

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals with normal network connectivity but abnormal amyloid/tau develop AD at the same rate, the theory is challenged.
  2. If network disruption is not detectable before amyloid/tau, the theory is undermined.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with no detectable network changes may not fit the model.
