Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2319

Name: Dynamic Peripheral-Central Biomarker Flux Theory (Integrated Network Model)

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory proposes that Alzheimer's disease (AD) arises from the breakdown of a homeostatic network regulating the flux of multiple interacting biomarkers (proteins, metabolites, immune mediators) between the central nervous system (CNS) and peripheral systems. The network is governed by transporters, barrier integrity (e.g., blood-brain barrier), and feedback loops. Dysregulation leads to both pathological accumulation in the brain and altered peripheral biomarker signatures. Effective detection and intervention require modeling and targeting this networked flux, rather than single-molecule measurements.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Networked Flux Homeostasis Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• biomarker_network → has_balanced_flux → between_CNS_and_periphery
THEN
• CNS → maintains → homeostasis, and
• peripheral_biomarker_signatures → are_stable → True

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Healthy individuals show stable levels of multiple CNS and peripheral biomarkers over time.
  2. Disruption of blood-brain barrier (BBB) or transporter function leads to altered biomarker profiles and neurodegeneration.
  3. Longitudinal studies show that in the absence of neurodegenerative disease, peripheral and CNS biomarker levels remain within narrow physiological ranges.
  4. BBB integrity is essential for maintaining CNS environment and preventing neurotoxic influx.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While homeostasis and BBB function are known, the networked, multi-biomarker flux model is a new systems-level synthesis.

What Already Exists: Homeostatic regulation of CNS environment and the role of BBB are established.

What is Novel: The explicit networked, multi-biomarker flux model and its application to both pathogenesis and detection is novel.

References:

  • Sweeney et al. (2018) Blood–brain barrier: from physiology to disease and back [BBB and homeostasis]
  • Hampel et al. (2018) Blood-based biomarkers for Alzheimer disease: mapping the road to the clinic [Peripheral biomarker networks]

Statement 1: Network Breakdown and Pathology Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• biomarker_network → has_dysregulated_flux → between_CNS_and_periphery
THEN
• AD_pathology → is_initiated_or_exacerbated_by → network_dysregulation, and
• peripheral_biomarker_signatures → become_predictive_of → CNS_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Multi-marker blood panels can predict AD risk and progression, especially when incorporating markers of inflammation, metabolism, and vascular function.
  2. Network analysis of biomarker interactions reveals early changes before clinical symptoms.
  3. BBB breakdown and altered transporter expression are observed in early AD and correlate with both CNS and peripheral biomarker changes.
  4. Peripheral inflammation and metabolic dysfunction are associated with increased risk and progression of AD.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law extends existing multi-marker approaches into a dynamic, flux-based network model.

What Already Exists: Multi-marker panels and network analysis are emerging in AD biomarker research.

What is Novel: The theory's focus on flux dysregulation across the network as the root cause and detection principle is novel.

References:

  • Hampel et al. (2018) Blood-based biomarkers for Alzheimer disease: mapping the road to the clinic [Multi-marker panels]
  • Sweeney et al. (2018) Blood–brain barrier: from physiology to disease and back [Networked regulation]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Network-based analysis of peripheral biomarkers will outperform single-molecule tests for early AD detection.
  2. Restoring network flux homeostasis (e.g., by targeting multiple transporters or barrier functions) will slow or prevent AD progression.
  3. Longitudinal monitoring of biomarker network flux will identify preclinical AD before cognitive symptoms emerge.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificial intelligence models trained on dynamic biomarker flux data will identify novel, actionable subtypes of AD.
  2. Interventions targeting peripheral network nodes (e.g., liver, immune system) will have CNS-protective effects in AD.
  3. Manipulation of peripheral biomarker flux (e.g., via plasmapheresis or metabolic modulation) will alter CNS pathology in unpredictable ways.

Negative Experiments

  1. If network-based biomarker models do not improve prediction over single markers, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If restoring network flux homeostasis does not impact AD pathology, the theory would be called into question.
  3. If peripheral biomarker network changes are not observed in early or preclinical AD, the theory would be undermined.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with minimal peripheral biomarker changes, suggesting CNS-autonomous mechanisms.
