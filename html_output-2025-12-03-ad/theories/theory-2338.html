Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2338

Name: Network Disintegration and Synergistic Cascade Theory of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory proposes that Alzheimer's disease results from the synergistic breakdown of neural network integrity due to the convergence of multiple pathological processes (amyloid, tau, vascular, metabolic, inflammatory, and synaptic dysfunction). The loss of network resilience, rather than any single pathology, is the proximate cause of cognitive decline. Detection should focus on network-level biomarkers (e.g., functional connectivity, network entropy) in combination with multi-domain molecular markers.

Statement 0: Network Collapse Synergy Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has_evidence_of → amyloid_pathology, and
• individual → has_evidence_of → tauopathy, and
• individual → has_evidence_of → vascular_dysfunction, and
• individual → has_evidence_of → metabolic_impairment, and
• individual → has_evidence_of → synaptic_dysfunction, and
• network_resilience → falls_below → collapse_threshold
THEN
• individual → develops → Alzheimer's disease

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Functional imaging shows network disintegration precedes clinical symptoms and correlates with multi-domain pathology.
  2. Synaptic loss is the strongest correlate of cognitive decline in AD.
  3. Network-based biomarkers (e.g., default mode network connectivity) predict conversion to AD.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: While network dysfunction is established, its formalization as the final common pathway for multi-domain synergy is new.

What Already Exists: Network dysfunction and synaptic loss are recognized as correlates of AD.

What is Novel: The explicit requirement for synergistic, multi-domain-driven network collapse as the proximate cause is novel.

References:

  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Network-based vulnerability]
  • Selkoe (2002) Alzheimer's disease is a synaptic failure [Synaptic loss as correlate]

Statement 1: Network Biomarker Integration Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has_elevated → network-level biomarker panel (functional connectivity, entropy, molecular markers)
THEN
• individual → has_high_risk_of → Alzheimer's disease

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Combined network and molecular biomarker panels outperform single-domain markers in predicting AD conversion.
  2. Network entropy and connectivity changes are early indicators of disease risk.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: While multi-modal detection is established, the specific integration of network and molecular domains is novel.

What Already Exists: Network and molecular biomarkers are used in AD research.

What is Novel: This law specifies the necessity of integrating network-level and multi-domain molecular markers for optimal detection.

References:

  • Brier et al. (2014) Functional connectivity and graph theory in preclinical Alzheimer's disease [Network biomarkers]
  • Jack et al. (2018) NIA-AA Research Framework [Molecular biomarkers, not network integration]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with moderate network disruption and moderate molecular pathology will have higher AD risk than those with severe pathology in only one domain.
  2. Interventions that restore network integrity (e.g., neuromodulation) will reduce risk if the collapse threshold is not yet crossed.
  3. Integrated network-molecular biomarker panels will predict AD conversion more accurately than either alone.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Novel network metrics (e.g., network controllability) may further refine the collapse threshold.
  2. Some individuals may have compensatory network reorganization that delays clinical onset despite pathology.
  3. Genetic or environmental factors may modulate network resilience, explaining individual variability.

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals with multi-domain pathology but preserved network integrity do not develop AD, the network collapse law would be challenged.
  2. If network biomarkers do not improve prediction over molecular markers alone, the detection law would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with minimal network disruption may not fit the network collapse model.
