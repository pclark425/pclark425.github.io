Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4684

Name: Proteostatic Collapse and Early Detection via Peripheral Biomarkers

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the cause of Alzheimer's disease and how it can be effectively detected.

Description: Alzheimer's disease is initiated by a collapse in cellular proteostasis, leading to the accumulation of misfolded proteins (amyloid-beta, tau) that propagate via prion-like mechanisms. This proteostatic failure is systemically reflected, allowing for early detection through peripheral biomarkers (e.g., blood, CSF, exosomes) that mirror central nervous system pathology.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Proteostatic Collapse Initiates Pathology (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal proteostasis → is → impaired
THEN
• misfolded proteins → accumulate → intracellularly and extracellularly, and
• misfolded proteins → propagate → via prion-like mechanisms

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Proteostasis impairment leads to accumulation of amyloid-beta and tau in cellular and animal models.
  2. Prion-like propagation of tau and amyloid has been demonstrated in vitro and in vivo.
  3. Genetic and environmental factors that impair proteostasis increase Alzheimer's risk.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While proteostasis and prion-like spread are known, their unification as the primary cause is a novel synthesis.

What Already Exists: Proteostasis impairment and prion-like propagation are recognized in neurodegeneration.

What is Novel: This law frames proteostatic collapse as the initiating event and unifying mechanism for both amyloid and tau pathology.

References:

  • Soto (2012) Prion hypothesis in Alzheimer's disease [prion-like propagation]
  • Hetz (2017) The unfolded protein response: controlling cell fate decisions under ER stress and beyond [proteostasis in neurodegeneration]

Statement 1: Peripheral Biomarkers Mirror Central Pathology (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• central nervous system → has → proteostatic collapse
THEN
• peripheral fluids (blood, CSF, exosomes) → contain → misfolded protein signatures, and
• peripheral biomarkers → predict → Alzheimer's onset and progression

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Blood and CSF levels of amyloid-beta and phosphorylated tau correlate with brain pathology and clinical progression.
  2. Exosomal cargo from blood reflects central nervous system protein misfolding.
  3. Peripheral proteostatic markers can predict conversion from mild cognitive impairment to Alzheimer's.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: Peripheral biomarkers are known, but their direct mirroring of proteostatic collapse as a unifying mechanism is novel.

What Already Exists: Peripheral biomarkers are increasingly used for Alzheimer's detection.

What is Novel: This law asserts that peripheral proteostatic signatures are a direct and early mirror of central pathology, enabling preclinical detection.

References:

  • Palmqvist (2019) Discriminative accuracy of plasma phospho-tau217 for Alzheimer disease vs other neurodegenerative disorders [blood biomarkers]
  • Fiandaca (2015) Identification of preclinical Alzheimer's disease by a profile of pathogenic proteins in neurally derived blood exosomes [exosomal biomarkers]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early peripheral proteostatic biomarker changes will develop Alzheimer's at higher rates than those without such changes.
  2. Therapies that restore proteostasis will reduce both central and peripheral misfolded protein signatures and slow disease progression.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Peripheral proteostatic biomarkers will enable detection of Alzheimer's decades before clinical symptoms.
  2. Novel proteostasis-targeting drugs will prevent the appearance of both central and peripheral misfolded protein signatures.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding individuals with clear central pathology but no peripheral biomarker changes would challenge the theory.
  2. Demonstrating that peripheral proteostatic markers do not predict disease progression would refute the theory.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases where peripheral biomarkers are abnormal but no central pathology develops.
