Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4746

Name: Hierarchical Network-Activity Gradient Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory posits that selective vulnerability in the visual cortex arises from a hierarchical organization of network connectivity and activity gradients, where neurons at higher hierarchical levels (e.g., integrative hub neurons, layer 3/5 pyramidal cells) are more vulnerable due to their central role in information processing and higher metabolic burden. Lower-level, more peripheral neurons (e.g., local circuit interneurons, layer 4 spiny stellate cells) are more resistant. The theory integrates anatomical, functional, and metabolic hierarchies to explain the spatial and cell-type-specific patterns of pathology.

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Neurons at higher hierarchical levels (e.g., layer 3/5 pyramidal cells) will show earlier and more severe pathology than those at lower levels (e.g., layer 4 cells).
  2. Reducing network centrality or activity of hub neurons will decrease their vulnerability to amyloid-β and tau.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially increasing the network centrality or activity of peripheral neurons will increase their vulnerability.
  2. Intermediate hierarchical levels will show intermediate vulnerability, potentially revealing non-linear effects.

Negative Experiments

  1. If neurons at high hierarchical levels and activity are not more vulnerable than those at low levels, the theory is challenged.
  2. If peripheral, low-activity neurons show early pathology, the theory is called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Molecular or genetic factors conferring vulnerability independent of network hierarchy or activity.
  2. Influence of local microenvironment (e.g., glial cells, vasculature) not accounted for.
