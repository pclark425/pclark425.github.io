Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4706

Name: Network Disconnection and Propagation Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the cause of Alzheimer's disease and how it can be effectively detected.

Description: Alzheimer's disease arises from the progressive disconnection of large-scale brain networks, initiated by focal pathology (amyloid, tau, or inflammation) that propagates trans-synaptically. This network breakdown precedes and predicts clinical symptoms. Early detection is possible by identifying network-level changes via advanced neuroimaging and electrophysiology, even before molecular biomarkers become abnormal.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Network Propagation Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• focal brain region → develops → pathological protein aggregation (amyloid or tau)
THEN
• pathology → spreads → anatomically connected regions via synaptic/axonal pathways, and
• large-scale brain networks → undergo → progressive functional disconnection

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Tau pathology spreads along connected brain regions in both animal models and human imaging studies.
  2. Functional MRI and EEG show early network disconnection in preclinical AD.
  3. Network-based models predict the spatial pattern of atrophy and dysfunction in AD.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: While propagation is studied, its primacy and predictive value for detection is a novel, testable law.

What Already Exists: Network disconnection and trans-synaptic spread are recognized in AD research.

What is Novel: The law formalizes network propagation as the primary driver and predictor of clinical onset, independent of molecular sequence.

References:

  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network disconnection]
  • Braak & Braak (1991) Neuropathological stageing of Alzheimer-related changes [pathology spread]
  • Raj (2012) A network diffusion model of disease progression in dementia [network propagation]

Statement 1: Network Biomarker Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → early network disconnection (e.g., reduced functional connectivity in default mode network)
THEN
• individual → is at high risk for → future cognitive decline and Alzheimer's diagnosis

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Resting-state fMRI and EEG detect network changes in at-risk individuals before clinical symptoms.
  2. Network disconnection predicts conversion from mild cognitive impairment to AD.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: This is a novel, testable prediction about the temporal sequence and diagnostic value of network biomarkers.

What Already Exists: Network biomarkers are under investigation for early AD detection.

What is Novel: The law predicts that network-level changes precede molecular biomarker abnormalities and are superior for earliest detection.

References:

  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network disconnection]
  • Buckner (2005) Molecular, structural, and functional characterization of Alzheimer's disease: Evidence for a relationship between default activity, amyloid, and memory [network biomarkers]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early network disconnection but normal molecular biomarkers will develop AD pathology and symptoms over time.
  2. Network-based imaging will identify at-risk individuals before conventional biomarkers become abnormal.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Interventions that restore network connectivity will delay or prevent clinical onset, even if molecular pathology persists.
  2. Distinct network disconnection patterns will predict different clinical subtypes of AD.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding individuals with network disconnection who do not develop AD would challenge the theory.
  2. Failure of network biomarkers to predict conversion to AD would refute the network biomarker law.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some individuals with molecular pathology but preserved networks may not develop symptoms.

Existing Theory Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: This theory extends existing models by prioritizing network-level changes as the earliest and most predictive events.

  • Raj (2012) A network diffusion model of disease progression in dementia [network propagation]
  • Buckner (2005) Molecular, structural, and functional characterization of Alzheimer's disease: Evidence for a relationship between default activity, amyloid, and memory [network biomarkers]
