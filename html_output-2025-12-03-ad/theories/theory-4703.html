Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4703

Name: Integrated Network Failure Theory of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the cause of Alzheimer's disease and how it can be effectively detected.

Description: Alzheimer's disease arises from the breakdown of large-scale brain network connectivity, driven by the convergence of multiple pathophysiological processes (amyloid, tau, neuroinflammation, vascular dysfunction, and metabolic stress). This network failure precedes and predicts clinical symptoms, and can be detected by advanced neuroimaging and network analysis before overt neurodegeneration.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Convergent Pathology Network Disruption Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → amyloid accumulation, and
• individual → has → tau pathology, and
• individual → has → neuroinflammation, and
• individual → has → vascular/metabolic dysfunction
THEN
• individual → develops → large-scale brain network connectivity disruption

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Multimodal imaging studies show that amyloid, tau, and neuroinflammation co-localize in brain network hubs and precede atrophy.
  2. Functional MRI and PET studies reveal early network dysfunction in preclinical AD, even before significant neuronal loss.
  3. Vascular and metabolic risk factors increase risk of network breakdown and AD.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law synthesizes multiple lines of evidence into a unified, testable causal chain, moving beyond single-pathology models.

What Already Exists: Individual pathologies (amyloid, tau, inflammation, vascular) are known contributors to AD; network dysfunction is recognized as a feature.

What is Novel: The explicit law that convergence of these pathologies causes network failure as the proximate cause of AD is novel.

References:

  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network vulnerability]
  • Iturria-Medina (2016) Early role of vascular dysregulation in late-onset Alzheimer's disease [vascular/metabolic]
  • Jack (2018) NIA-AA Research Framework: Toward a biological definition of Alzheimer's disease [biomarker convergence]

Statement 1: Network Failure Precedes Clinical Symptoms Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → large-scale brain network connectivity disruption
THEN
• individual → is at increased risk for → future cognitive decline and Alzheimer's diagnosis

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Longitudinal studies show that network connectivity loss predicts cognitive decline better than amyloid or tau alone.
  2. Resting-state fMRI and EEG network metrics are altered in at-risk individuals before symptom onset.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This is a novel, testable prediction based on emerging network neuroscience evidence.

What Already Exists: Network dysfunction is observed in AD and mild cognitive impairment.

What is Novel: The law formalizes network failure as a predictive, not just correlative, biomarker and causal step.

References:

  • Brier (2014) Functional connectivity and graph theory in preclinical Alzheimer's disease [network metrics]
  • Buckner (2005) Molecular, structural, and functional characterization of Alzheimer's disease: Evidence for a relationship between default activity, amyloid, and memory [network focus]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early network connectivity loss (measured by fMRI/EEG) will progress to clinical AD faster than those with only amyloid or tau pathology.
  2. Interventions that restore or preserve network connectivity will delay onset of cognitive symptoms, even if amyloid/tau burden remains.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Non-invasive brain stimulation targeting network hubs will prevent or reverse early network failure and halt AD progression.
  2. Distinct network disruption patterns will differentiate Alzheimer's from other dementias before clinical symptoms emerge.

Negative Experiments

  1. Demonstrating that network connectivity remains intact in individuals with convergent pathology who develop AD would challenge the theory.
  2. Finding that network disruption does not predict cognitive decline better than single-pathology biomarkers would refute the network failure model.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of Alzheimer's with minimal network disruption or with atypical network patterns.
