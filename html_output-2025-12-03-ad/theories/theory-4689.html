Statement 1: Pathology Propagation Follows Network Topology (quantitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• pathological protein (amyloid/tau) → is_present_in → node A, and
• node A → is_connected_to → node B
THEN
• pathological protein → spreads_to → node B, and
• rate of spread → is → proportional to connection strength

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Tau and amyloid spread along functional and structural connections in both animal models and human imaging studies.
  2. Regions with stronger connectivity show faster pathology accumulation.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: Propagation along networks is established, but the quantitative law and its predictive use are novel.

What Already Exists: Trans-neuronal spread of tau and amyloid along networks is supported by experimental and imaging data.

What is Novel: The explicit proportionality to connection strength and predictive mapping for detection is a novel quantitative extension.

References:

  • de Calignon (2012) Propagation of tau pathology in a model of early Alzheimer's disease [tau spread]
  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network-based spread]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Regions with high connectivity and metabolic demand will show earliest molecular pathology in preclinical AD.
  2. Mapping individual network topology will predict future sites of pathology and cognitive decline.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially altering network connectivity (e.g., via stimulation or training) will change the pattern of pathology spread.
  2. Individuals with atypical network topology (e.g., due to neurodevelopmental differences) will have altered AD progression.

Negative Experiments

  2. Demonstrating that pathology spreads independently of network connections would refute the propagation law.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of Alzheimer's with focal pathology not explained by network topology.
