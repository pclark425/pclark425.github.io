Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2418

Name: Intrinsic Molecular Resilience and Pathway Cohesiveness Theory (Specific 2: Synaptic-Activity-Linked Resilience)

Type: specific

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This specific theory proposes that the selective resistance of certain visual cortex neurons to amyloid-β (Aβ) and tau pathology is due to their ability to maintain synaptic activity-linked resilience programs. Neurons that sustain activity-dependent expression of neuroprotective genes (e.g., BDNF, Arc, synaptic scaffolding proteins) and maintain cohesive synaptic signaling networks are less susceptible to pathology, while those with impaired activity-dependent gene regulation are more vulnerable.

  2. Electrophysiological studies show that these neurons maintain synaptic activity and plasticity.

  4. Network analysis demonstrates that resistant neurons preserve synaptic connectivity and signaling integrity under pathological conditions.

  2. Electrophysiological studies show reduced synaptic activity in these neurons.
  3. Single-cell RNA-seq data indicate downregulation of neuroprotective genes in vulnerable neuronal populations.
  4. Network analysis reveals that vulnerable neurons exhibit disrupted or fragmented synaptic connectivity in the presence of Aβ and tau.

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Enhancing activity-dependent neuroprotective gene expression in vulnerable neurons will increase their resistance to Aβ and tau pathology.
  2. Neurons with naturally high synaptic activity and cohesive signaling networks will show less pathology in early dementia.
  3. Pharmacological or optogenetic stimulation that boosts synaptic activity will reduce Aβ/tau-induced degeneration in susceptible visual cortex neurons.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Pharmacological agents that boost synaptic activity and network cohesiveness will reduce selective vulnerability in human neurons.
  2. Disrupting activity-dependent gene regulation in resilient neurons will render them vulnerable to pathology.
  3. Artificially enhancing network cohesiveness in otherwise vulnerable neurons will confer resistance to Aβ and tau pathology.

Negative Experiments

  1. If enhancing activity-dependent gene expression does not confer resistance, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If loss of synaptic network cohesiveness does not increase vulnerability, the theory would be called into question.
  3. If neurons with high synaptic activity and cohesive networks still accumulate pathology at the same rate as vulnerable neurons, the theory would be falsified.

Unaccounted for Evidence
