Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2358

Name: Proteostatic-Inflammatory Threshold Theory of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory proposes that Alzheimer's disease (AD) is triggered when the combined burden of proteostatic stress (misfolded/aggregated proteins such as amyloid-beta and tau) and chronic neuroinflammation surpasses a critical, individual-specific threshold, overwhelming cellular clearance and repair mechanisms. The disease remains latent until this threshold is crossed, after which neurodegeneration accelerates. Detection methods should focus on quantifying the combined proteostatic and inflammatory load, and identifying individuals approaching the threshold.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Proteostatic-Inflammatory Threshold Law (quantitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• brain → has → proteostatic stress level = P, and
• brain → has → chronic neuroinflammation level = I, and
• P + I → exceeds → individual-specific threshold T
THEN
• brain → initiates → irreversible neurodegenerative cascade

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Proteostatic stress (amyloid-beta, tau) and neuroinflammation are both elevated in AD brains; their interaction accelerates pathology.
  2. Some individuals with high amyloid or tau do not develop AD unless neuroinflammation is also present.
  3. Animal models show that reducing either amyloid or inflammation alone is less effective than reducing both.
  4. Threshold concepts are used in other neurodegenerative diseases (e.g., prion diseases) to explain sudden onset after a latent period.
  5. Longitudinal biomarker studies show that both amyloid/tau and inflammatory markers rise before clinical symptoms.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While both mechanisms are recognized, their explicit quantitative integration as a threshold for disease onset is novel.

What Already Exists: Proteostatic and inflammatory mechanisms are both implicated in AD, and threshold concepts are used in other neurodegenerative diseases.

What is Novel: This law formalizes a quantitative, additive threshold model for AD onset, integrating proteostatic and inflammatory loads.

References:

  • Heneka et al. (2015) Neuroinflammation in Alzheimer's disease [Role of inflammation]
  • Selkoe & Hardy (2016) The amyloid hypothesis of Alzheimer's disease at 25 years [Proteostatic stress]
  • Prusiner (2012) Cell biology. A unifying role for prions in neurodegenerative diseases [Threshold concepts in proteostasis]

Statement 1: Threshold-Based Detection Law (quantitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• detection_method → quantifies → combined proteostatic and inflammatory load, and
• individual → has → P + I approaching T
THEN
• detection_method → predicts → imminent conversion to symptomatic AD

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Longitudinal studies show that individuals with rising amyloid, tau, and inflammatory markers are at highest risk for conversion.
  2. Current detection methods rarely integrate both proteostatic and inflammatory markers quantitatively.
  3. Blood and CSF biomarker panels for AD are being developed, but most focus on single pathways.
  4. Combining multiple biomarker modalities improves predictive accuracy for AD conversion.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The explicit, quantitative threshold model is a novel extension of current biomarker approaches.

What Already Exists: Biomarker-based risk prediction is used, but not as a quantitative threshold of combined loads.

What is Novel: This law proposes a quantitative, threshold-based risk model integrating both proteostatic and inflammatory markers.

References:

  • Jack et al. (2018) NIA-AA Research Framework [Biomarker-based risk]
  • Heneka et al. (2015) Neuroinflammation in Alzheimer's disease [Inflammatory markers]
  • Selkoe & Hardy (2016) The amyloid hypothesis of Alzheimer's disease at 25 years [Proteostatic markers]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with high combined proteostatic and inflammatory loads, even if sub-threshold, will show accelerated biomarker progression and cognitive decline.
  2. Therapies that reduce both proteostatic and inflammatory loads will delay or prevent threshold crossing and AD onset.
  3. Individuals with high cognitive reserve will tolerate higher combined loads before symptom onset.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Personalized threshold values (T) can be estimated using genetic, epigenetic, and environmental data, enabling individualized risk prediction.
  2. Novel blood-based assays quantifying both proteostatic and inflammatory markers will enable population-level screening for preclinical AD.
  3. Interventions targeting both pathways in pre-symptomatic individuals will prevent or significantly delay AD onset.

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals with high combined loads do not progress to AD, or if AD occurs in the absence of elevated proteostatic or inflammatory markers, the theory is challenged.
  2. If therapies that reduce both loads do not delay onset, the threshold model is called into question.
  3. If detection methods quantifying both loads do not improve prediction over single-pathway methods, the theory is weakened.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Non-proteostatic, non-inflammatory mechanisms (e.g., primary vascular or synaptic dysfunction) may trigger AD in some cases.
