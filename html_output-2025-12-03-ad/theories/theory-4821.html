Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4821

Name: Intrinsic Molecular Signature Theory of Neuronal Vulnerability (General Formulation 2: Network-Integrated Signature)

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory extends the molecular signature concept by integrating the influence of a neuron's network context—its synaptic partners, activity patterns, and local circuit environment—into the definition of its 'intrinsic' signature. The theory posits that the combination of cell-intrinsic molecular features and network-derived molecular adaptations determines selective vulnerability or resistance to Aβ and tau pathology.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Network-Integrated Molecular Signature Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal_population → has_intrinsic_molecular_signature → signature_X, and
• neuronal_population → has_network_context → context_Y
THEN
• neuronal_population → has_effective_signature → signature_XY (combination of X and Y)

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neuronal activity and synaptic input modulate gene expression and proteostasis pathways relevant to Aβ/tau vulnerability.
  2. Neurons in highly interconnected hubs are more vulnerable to pathology, even with similar baseline molecular profiles.
  3. Activity-dependent transcriptional programs can alter stress response and protein homeostasis genes.
  4. Network disconnection or silencing can reduce vulnerability in otherwise susceptible neurons.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: This law synthesizes two previously separate lines of evidence into a unified, predictive framework.

What Already Exists: Network connectivity and activity are known to influence vulnerability, and molecular signatures are known to differ between populations.

What is Novel: The explicit integration of network context into the definition of the effective molecular signature is novel.

References:

  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Network vulnerability in neurodegeneration]
  • De Jager et al. (2018) A multi-omic atlas of the human frontal cortex for aging and Alzheimer's disease [Molecular signatures in AD]

Statement 1: Effective Signature Predicts Pathological Outcome (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal_population → has_effective_signature → signature_XY, and
• Aβ_or_tau → is_present_in → local_microenvironment
THEN
• neuronal_population → exhibits_pathological_response → response_Z (determined by signature_XY)

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Activity-dependent changes in gene expression can increase or decrease vulnerability to Aβ/tau pathology.
  2. Neurons with similar intrinsic signatures but different network contexts show different pathological outcomes.
  3. Optogenetic manipulation of network activity alters vulnerability to Aβ/tau in animal models.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: This law is a novel synthesis, not previously formalized in the literature.

What Already Exists: Activity and network context are known to modulate vulnerability, but not as a formalized component of the molecular signature.

What is Novel: The law formalizes the predictive power of the combined signature, not just the intrinsic or network factors alone.

References:

  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Network vulnerability]
  • De Jager et al. (2018) A multi-omic atlas of the human frontal cortex for aging and Alzheimer's disease [Molecular signatures]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Neuronal populations with similar intrinsic molecular signatures but different network contexts will show different vulnerability to Aβ/tau pathology.
  2. Modulating network activity (e.g., via optogenetics) will alter the effective molecular signature and change vulnerability to pathology.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially engineering a network context to mimic a resistant population will confer resistance to otherwise vulnerable neurons.
  2. Disrupting network connectivity in resistant populations may unmask latent vulnerability to Aβ/tau pathology.

Negative Experiments

  1. If network context does not alter the vulnerability of neurons with identical intrinsic signatures, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If changes in network activity do not lead to changes in molecular signatures relevant to pathology, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The influence of non-neuronal cells (e.g., astrocytes, microglia) on the effective signature is not explicitly modeled.
