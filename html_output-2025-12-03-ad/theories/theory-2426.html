Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2426

Name: Network Connectivity and Propagation Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory proposes that the selective vulnerability or resistance of visual cortex neuronal populations to amyloid-β and tau pathology is governed by their position and connectivity within large-scale neural networks. Neurons that are highly interconnected with other vulnerable regions or serve as network hubs are more likely to accumulate pathology due to trans-synaptic spread and increased exposure to pathogenic factors, while isolated or peripherally connected neurons are more resistant.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Network Hub Neurons Are More Vulnerable (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuron → is_network_hub → true
THEN
• neuron → is_vulnerable_to → Aβ/tau pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Highly connected neurons (network hubs) in the cortex are more likely to accumulate tau and amyloid pathology.
  2. Pathology spreads preferentially along highly connected neural pathways.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The general network propagation concept is known, but the formalization for visual cortex selectivity is new.

What Already Exists: Network-based propagation of tau and amyloid pathology is established.

What is Novel: The explicit law linking hub status to selective vulnerability in visual cortex is novel.

References:

  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network vulnerability]
  • de Calignon et al. (2012) Propagation of tau pathology in a model of early Alzheimer's disease [network spread]

Statement 1: Peripheral or Weakly Connected Neurons Are Resistant (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuron → is_peripherally_connected → true
THEN
• neuron → is_resistant_to → Aβ/tau pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neurons with fewer long-range connections or isolated network positions are less likely to accumulate pathology.
  2. Visual cortex subregions with sparse connectivity to affected areas show delayed or absent pathology.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The general principle is established, but the formalization for visual cortex selectivity is new.

What Already Exists: Network isolation is associated with reduced pathology spread.

What is Novel: The explicit law for visual cortex neurons is novel.

References:

  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network vulnerability]
  • de Calignon et al. (2012) Propagation of tau pathology in a model of early Alzheimer's disease [network spread]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Mapping network connectivity in the visual cortex will predict regions of early vs. late pathology.
  2. Disrupting hub connectivity will reduce pathology spread to otherwise vulnerable neurons.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially increasing connectivity of resistant neurons will render them vulnerable.
  2. Altering network topology in animal models will change the spatial pattern of pathology.

Negative Experiments

  1. If network hubs do not show increased vulnerability, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If isolated neurons develop pathology at the same rate as hubs, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some highly connected neurons remain resistant, suggesting additional protective mechanisms.
