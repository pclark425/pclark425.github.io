Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4698

Name: Systemic Pathophysiological Synchronization Theory for Early Alzheimer's Detection

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the cause of Alzheimer's disease and how it can be effectively detected.

Description: This theory proposes that Alzheimer's disease is characterized by a temporally synchronized cascade of pathophysiological events—amyloid deposition, tauopathy, neuroinflammation, and microvascular dysfunction—that occur in both the CNS and select peripheral tissues. The temporal synchronization of these events enables the use of peripheral biomarkers as real-time proxies for CNS pathology, providing a window for early detection and intervention.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Temporal Synchronization Law of AD Pathology (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → is in → preclinical or prodromal AD stage
THEN
• CNS and peripheral tissues → undergo → synchronized pathological changes (amyloid, tau, inflammation, vascular)

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Longitudinal studies show parallel changes in plasma amyloid/tau and CNS imaging in early AD.
  2. Retinal and blood biomarkers change in synchrony with early brain pathology.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: Existing work describes timing in CNS and some peripheral changes, but not as a law of systemic synchronization.

What Already Exists: Temporal progression of AD pathology is described for CNS, and some peripheral markers show early changes.

What is Novel: The law of temporal synchronization between CNS and peripheral tissues as a generalizable detection principle is novel.

References:

  • Janelidze et al. (2020) Plasma P-tau181 in Alzheimer's disease: relationship to other biomarkers, differential diagnosis, neuropathology and longitudinal progression to Alzheimer's dementia [Temporal biomarker changes]
  • Palmqvist et al. (2019) Performance of fully automated plasma assays as screening tests for Alzheimer disease–related β-amyloid status [Blood biomarkers track CNS pathology]

Statement 1: Peripheral Real-Time Proxy Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → synchronized peripheral and CNS pathological changes
THEN
• peripheral biomarker measurements → serve as → real-time proxies for CNS AD pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Peripheral biomarker levels (e.g., plasma P-tau, retinal amyloid) track with CNS imaging and clinical progression.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: Existing work is descriptive; the law-like formulation of real-time proxy function is new.

What Already Exists: Peripheral biomarkers are used as proxies, but not formalized as a law of real-time synchronization.

What is Novel: The explicit law that peripheral biomarkers provide real-time, temporally synchronized proxies for CNS pathology is novel.

References:

  • Janelidze et al. (2020) Plasma P-tau181 in Alzheimer's disease: relationship to other biomarkers, differential diagnosis, neuropathology and longitudinal progression to Alzheimer's dementia [Peripheral biomarkers as proxies]
  • Koronyo et al. (2017) Retinal amyloid pathology and proof-of-concept imaging trial in Alzheimer's disease [Retinal imaging as CNS proxy]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Longitudinal monitoring of peripheral biomarkers will reveal synchronized changes with CNS imaging in individuals progressing from preclinical to symptomatic AD.
  2. Interventions that alter peripheral biomarker trajectories will correspondingly alter CNS pathology progression.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Temporal desynchronization between peripheral and CNS biomarkers will identify atypical or resistant forms of AD.
  2. Peripheral biomarker synchronization patterns will predict response to disease-modifying therapies.

Negative Experiments

  1. If peripheral biomarkers do not change in synchrony with CNS pathology, the temporal synchronization law is challenged.
  2. If peripheral biomarker changes do not serve as real-time proxies for CNS pathology, the proxy law is called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases where peripheral biomarker changes are delayed or absent despite CNS pathology (e.g., due to blood-brain barrier differences or tissue-specific factors).

Existing Theory Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: No existing theory frames peripheral-CNS biomarker synchronization as a law for early AD detection.

References:

  • Janelidze et al. (2020) Plasma P-tau181 in Alzheimer's disease: relationship to other biomarkers, differential diagnosis, neuropathology and longitudinal progression to Alzheimer's dementia [Temporal biomarker changes]
  • Palmqvist et al. (2019) Performance of fully automated plasma assays as screening tests for Alzheimer disease–related β-amyloid status [Blood biomarkers track CNS pathology]
