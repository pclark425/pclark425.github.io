Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2373

Name: Activity-Dependent Proteostatic Threshold Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory proposes that each neuronal population in the visual cortex has a dynamic proteostatic threshold, determined by the balance between activity-driven protein production (including APP and tau) and the capacity for protein clearance. Selective vulnerability arises when activity or stress exceeds this threshold, leading to local amyloid-β and tau accumulation, while populations with lower activity or higher clearance capacity remain resistant.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Proteostatic Threshold Exceeded by Activity (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuron → has_property → high activity-dependent protein synthesis, and
• neuron → has_property → low proteostasis capacity
THEN
• neuron → exceeds_proteostatic_threshold → True, and
• neuron → has_increased_risk → Aβ and tau pathology accumulation

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neuronal activity increases APP and tau production; populations with high activity and low clearance accumulate pathology.
  2. Experimental increases in neuronal firing accelerate amyloid and tau deposition.
  3. Proteostasis impairment (e.g., autophagy inhibition) increases vulnerability to pathology.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The threshold concept and its dynamic, activity-dependent nature is a new integration.

What Already Exists: Activity-dependent protein production and proteostasis have been separately linked to pathology.

What is Novel: The explicit threshold model, where vulnerability is determined by the balance of these factors, is novel.

References:

  • Yamamoto et al. (2015) Neuronal activity regulates APP processing and Aβ production [activity-dependent production]
  • Koren et al. (2019) Proteostasis in the aging brain and Alzheimer's disease [proteostasis]

Statement 1: Resistance via Subthreshold Activity or Enhanced Clearance (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuron → has_property → low activity-dependent protein synthesis
THEN
• neuron → remains_below_proteostatic_threshold → True, and
• neuron → has_decreased_risk → Aβ and tau pathology accumulation

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neuronal populations with low activity or high clearance capacity are relatively spared in AD.
  2. Primary visual cortex neurons, with lower spontaneous activity, show delayed pathology.
  3. Enhancing proteostasis (e.g., via autophagy) protects against pathology even in active neurons.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The threshold framing and its application to resistance is new.

What Already Exists: Low activity and high clearance have been associated with resistance.

What is Novel: The explicit subthreshold model and its predictive power for resistance is novel.

References:

  • Braak & Braak (1991) Neuropathological stageing of Alzheimer-related changes [regional resistance]
  • Koren et al. (2019) Proteostasis in the aging brain and Alzheimer's disease [proteostasis]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Artificially increasing neuronal activity in resistant visual cortex populations will induce pathology if proteostasis is not simultaneously enhanced.
  2. Boosting proteostasis in vulnerable populations will raise the threshold, reducing pathology even under high activity.
  3. Neuronal populations with naturally low activity will remain resistant unless proteostasis is impaired.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Chronic low-level increases in activity (e.g., via sensory enrichment) in resistant populations may eventually lead to pathology if sustained over years.
  2. Genetic or pharmacological enhancement of proteostasis may fully prevent pathology in high-activity populations.

Negative Experiments

  1. If high-activity neurons with low proteostasis do not develop pathology, the theory is challenged.
  2. If boosting proteostasis does not protect high-activity neurons, the threshold model is called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some populations may be resistant despite high activity, possibly due to unknown compensatory mechanisms.
