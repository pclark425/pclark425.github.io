Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2296

Name: Network Disconnection and Propagation Theory of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory posits that Alzheimer's disease arises from the progressive disconnection of large-scale brain networks, initiated by focal molecular pathology (amyloid, tau) that propagates along vulnerable neural circuits. The spread of pathology is governed by network topology, synaptic activity, and regional vulnerability. Early detection is possible by identifying subtle network dysfunction using advanced neuroimaging and electrophysiological techniques, even before overt atrophy or cognitive symptoms.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Pathology Propagates Along Vulnerable Brain Networks (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → focal amyloid or tau pathology in a hub region, and
• brain network → has → high synaptic activity and connectivity
THEN
• pathology → propagates along → connected network nodes, and
• network → shows → progressive functional disconnection

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Tau PET imaging shows spread of pathology along functional connectivity networks.
  2. Regions with high baseline activity (e.g., default mode network) are early sites of amyloid deposition.
  3. Network-based models predict the spatial pattern of atrophy and hypometabolism in AD.
  4. Animal models show trans-synaptic spread of tau and amyloid pathology.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: While network propagation is recognized, the explicit necessity and sufficiency for disease progression and detection is a novel, formalized claim.

What Already Exists: Network-based propagation of pathology is an emerging concept in AD research.

What is Novel: This law formalizes the network propagation as a necessary mechanism for disease progression and links it to early detection via network dysfunction.

References:

  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network vulnerability]
  • Raj et al. (2012) A network diffusion model of disease progression in dementia [network-based propagation]
  • Franzmeier et al. (2019) Functional connectivity drives tau spreading and cognitive decline in Alzheimer's disease [network spread]

Statement 1: Network Dysfunction Precedes Structural Atrophy (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → subtle network-level functional connectivity changes
THEN
• individual → is at increased risk for → future Alzheimer's disease, and
• network → shows → reduced synchrony and efficiency before atrophy

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Resting-state fMRI detects reduced connectivity in default mode and other networks in preclinical AD.
  2. EEG/MEG studies show altered network synchrony in at-risk individuals before cognitive symptoms.
  3. Longitudinal imaging shows network dysfunction precedes regional atrophy and hypometabolism.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: While early network changes are known, the sufficiency and necessity for early detection is a novel, formalized claim.

What Already Exists: Functional network changes are observed in preclinical and prodromal AD.

What is Novel: This law asserts that network dysfunction is a necessary and sufficient early marker, preceding structural changes, and can be used for early detection.

References:

  • Brier et al. (2014) Functional connectivity and graph theory in preclinical Alzheimer's disease [network dysfunction precedes atrophy]
  • Sperling et al. (2011) Toward defining the preclinical stages of Alzheimer's disease: recommendations from the National Institute on Aging-Alzheimer's Association workgroups [preclinical network changes]
  • Stam (2014) Modern network science of neurological disorders [network analysis in AD]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early network dysfunction (as measured by fMRI/EEG) will develop AD pathology and symptoms before those without such changes.
  2. Interventions that restore network synchrony will delay or prevent progression to AD.
  3. Network-based imaging will outperform structural imaging for early AD detection.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Targeted stimulation of network hubs will halt or reverse propagation of pathology.
  2. Individuals with preserved network function despite amyloid/tau burden will not develop clinical AD.
  3. Network disconnection patterns will predict specific cognitive deficits and disease subtypes.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding individuals with advanced network dysfunction but no AD pathology or symptoms would challenge the theory.
  2. Demonstrating that AD can develop without any detectable network changes would call the theory into question.
  3. Showing that network-based interventions do not alter disease progression.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with minimal network dysfunction but significant atrophy and pathology.
