Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2721

Name: Cell-Intrinsic Proteostasis and Immune Signature Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia, based on the following results.

Description: This theory proposes that the selective vulnerability or resistance of neuronal populations in the visual cortex to amyloid-β and tau pathology is determined primarily by cell-intrinsic molecular programs governing proteostasis (protein homeostasis), lysosomal degradation, and innate immune signaling (notably NF-κB/Rel pathways). Neurons with lower expression of protein degradation machinery, higher basal innate immune activation, or specific synaptic/ion-transport gene signatures are more vulnerable, while those with robust proteostasis, efficient lysosomal degradation, and low innate immune activation are more resistant. This theory accounts for cell-type, laminar, and species differences, and explains exceptions to connectivity/metabolic models.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Proteostasis Deficiency Vulnerability Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuron → has_low_expression_of → protein degradation/proteostasis genes
THEN
• neuron → is_vulnerable_to → tau aggregation and neurofibrillary tangle formation

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Excitatory neurons in cortex accumulate tau aggregates and are vulnerable, linked to lower expression of protein homeostasis factors compared to inhibitory neurons. [e10065.0] [e10065.1]
  2. RORB+ excitatory neurons are selectively vulnerable to tau pathology, likely due to intrinsic molecular identity. [e10079.1]
  3. hiPSC-derived TBR1+ excitatory neurons show variable tau aggregation, not explained by uptake/degradation, suggesting intrinsic molecular heterogeneity. [e10079.2]

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: Proteostasis is a known factor, but its explicit application to visual cortex neuronal subtypes and integration with tau/amyloid vulnerability is novel.

What Already Exists: Proteostasis and protein degradation capacity have been implicated in neurodegenerative disease vulnerability.

What is Novel: This law formalizes the direct link between cell-intrinsic proteostasis gene expression and selective vulnerability at the neuronal subtype level in visual cortex.

References:

  • Xu et al. (2021) A tau homeostasis signature is linked with the cellular and regional vulnerability of excitatory neurons to tau pathology [Proteostasis and vulnerability]
  • Roussarie et al. (2020) Selective vulnerability in Alzheimer's disease: a network-based analysis [Cell-intrinsic factors]

Statement 1: Lysosomal Degradation Capacity Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuron → has_high_lysosomal_degradation_capacity → true
THEN
• neuron → is_resistant_to → tau aggregation

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Spinal cord-like hiPSC-derived neurons show low tau aggregation and high lysosomal-dependent degradation (bafilomycin-sensitive), indicating resilience. [e10079.4]
  2. Midbrain dopaminergic neurons are resistant to tau aggregation despite high aggregate uptake and degradation, suggesting efficient clearance mechanisms. [e10079.3]
  3. Hypothalamic-like neurons are resistant to tau aggregation, not explained by uptake/degradation rates, but by baseline transcriptional differences. [e10079.5]

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The general principle is known, but its application to visual cortex and specific neuronal subtypes is novel.

What Already Exists: Lysosomal degradation and autophagy are recognized as protective in neurodegeneration.

What is Novel: This law applies the principle to specific neuronal subtypes in the visual cortex and related models, and links it to resistance to tau aggregation.

References:

  • Nixon (2013) The role of autophagy in neurodegenerative disease [Lysosomal/autophagic clearance]
  • Xu et al. (2021) A tau homeostasis signature is linked with the cellular and regional vulnerability of excitatory neurons to tau pathology [Proteostasis and clearance]

Statement 2: Innate Immune/NF-κB Activation Vulnerability Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuron → has_high_basal_NF-κB/Rel_activity → true
THEN
• neuron → is_vulnerable_to → tau-induced degeneration

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Drosophila visual excitatory neurons with high basal Rel/NF-κB activity show greater tau-triggered cell loss; regression models predict vulnerability from Rel activity. [e10077.0]
  2. Human DLPFC neuronal subclusters (Ex8, In4) show AD-associated increases in NF-κB signaling gene expression, indicating cell-type-specific innate immune pathway modulation. [e10077.4]

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: NF-κB's role is known, but its predictive value for neuronal subtype vulnerability is novel.

What Already Exists: Innate immune activation and NF-κB signaling are implicated in neurodegeneration.

What is Novel: This law links basal NF-κB/Rel activity at the neuronal subtype level to selective vulnerability in the visual system and cortex.

References:

  • Glass et al. (2010) Mechanisms underlying inflammation in neurodegeneration [NF-κB and neurodegeneration]
  • DeVos et al. (2018) Synaptic Tau Seeding Precedes Tau Pathology in Human Alzheimer's Disease Brain [Synaptic/immune mechanisms]

Statement 3: Synaptic/Ion-Transport Gene Signature Vulnerability Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuron → has_high_expression_of → synaptic and ion-transport genes
THEN
• neuron → is_vulnerable_to → tau aggregation

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. SST+ CTIP2+ cortical inhibitory neurons (deep-layer interneurons) with enriched synaptic and ion-transport gene expression are highly vulnerable to tau aggregation. [e10079.0]
  2. Hypothalamic-like neurons, with lower expression of these genes, are resistant to tau aggregation. [e10079.5]

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The general importance of synaptic genes is known, but their direct link to tau aggregation vulnerability in specific interneuron subtypes is novel.

What Already Exists: Synaptic gene expression has been associated with neuronal function and vulnerability.

What is Novel: This law identifies a specific gene signature (synaptic/ion-transport) as a determinant of tau vulnerability in defined neuronal subtypes.

References:

  • Mathys et al. (2019) Single-cell transcriptomic analysis of Alzheimer's disease [Cell-type gene signatures]
  • Xu et al. (2021) A tau homeostasis signature is linked with the cellular and regional vulnerability of excitatory neurons to tau pathology [Gene signatures and vulnerability]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Enhancing proteostasis or lysosomal degradation in vulnerable neurons will reduce tau aggregation and neurodegeneration.
  2. Neuronal subtypes with high basal NF-κB/Rel activity in other brain regions will be more vulnerable to tau-induced degeneration.
  3. Knockdown of synaptic/ion-transport gene expression in SST+ CTIP2+ interneurons will reduce their vulnerability to tau aggregation.
  4. hiPSC-derived neurons from donors with higher expression of proteostasis genes will show lower tau aggregation after seeding.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Manipulating innate immune signaling (e.g., NF-κB inhibition) in human cortical neurons will alter their vulnerability to tau pathology in vivo.
  2. Engineering neurons to express a 'resistant' proteostasis signature will confer resistance to both amyloid-β and tau pathology in animal models.
  3. If a neuron subtype with high synaptic/ion-transport gene expression but robust proteostasis is found, its vulnerability will clarify the interplay of these factors.

Negative Experiments

  1. Demonstrating that neurons with low proteostasis gene expression are resistant to tau aggregation would challenge the theory.
  2. Finding that increasing lysosomal degradation does not reduce tau aggregation in vulnerable neurons would contradict the lysosomal law.
  3. Observing that NF-κB/Rel activity does not correlate with vulnerability in neuronal subtypes would challenge the immune signature law.
  4. Knockdown of synaptic/ion-transport genes in vulnerable interneurons does not alter tau aggregation.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Regional and laminar gradients of vulnerability associated with connectivity and metabolic phenotype, not fully explained by cell-intrinsic molecular programs. [e10080.0] [e10080.1] [e10080.2] [e10080.3] [e10090.0] [e10090.1] [e10090.2] [e10087.0] [e10074.0]
  2. Delayed vulnerability of V1 neurons (resistant until late Braak stages), which may reflect network propagation rather than cell-intrinsic factors. [e10089.0]
