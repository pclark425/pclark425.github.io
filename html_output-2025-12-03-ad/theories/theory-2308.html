Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2308

Name: Network Disintegration and Systemic Vulnerability Model of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory proposes that Alzheimer's disease arises from the progressive disintegration of large-scale brain networks due to the convergence of molecular pathology (amyloid, tau, neuroinflammation), systemic metabolic/vascular stress, and impaired neural resilience. The model asserts that network-level dysfunction precedes and predicts clinical symptoms, and that systemic factors (e.g., peripheral inflammation, metabolic syndrome) modulate both vulnerability and progression. Detection is optimized by integrating network-level neuroimaging, systemic biomarkers, and digital phenotyping.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Network Vulnerability Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → molecular AD pathology (amyloid, tau, neuroinflammation), and
• individual → has → systemic metabolic/vascular stress (e.g., insulin resistance, hypertension, chronic inflammation), and
• individual → has → reduced neural resilience (e.g., low cognitive reserve, impaired synaptic plasticity)
THEN
• individual → exhibits → early disruption of large-scale brain networks (e.g., default mode, salience, executive networks)

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Functional MRI and PET studies show early network disruption in AD, often before clinical symptoms.
  2. Systemic metabolic and vascular stressors are associated with accelerated network degeneration.
  3. Low cognitive reserve and impaired synaptic plasticity increase vulnerability to network dysfunction.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While network and systemic factors are studied, their formal integration as a law is new.

What Already Exists: Network dysfunction and systemic risk factors are recognized in AD.

What is Novel: The explicit law linking molecular pathology, systemic stress, and neural resilience to network disintegration is novel.

References:

  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network vulnerability]
  • Iturria-Medina et al. (2016) Early role of vascular dysregulation on late-onset Alzheimer's disease based on multifactorial data-driven analysis [systemic factors]
  • Stern (2012) Cognitive reserve in ageing and Alzheimer's disease [neural resilience]

Statement 1: Systemic Modulation Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → peripheral inflammation or metabolic syndrome
THEN
• individual → shows → accelerated network degeneration and earlier clinical symptoms

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Peripheral inflammation and metabolic syndrome are associated with faster cognitive decline and network atrophy in AD.
  2. Animal models show that systemic inflammation accelerates neurodegeneration.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The role of systemic factors is recognized, but their formalization as a law modulating network degeneration is new.

What Already Exists: Systemic inflammation and metabolic syndrome are known risk factors for AD.

What is Novel: The explicit law of systemic modulation of network degeneration is novel.

References:

  • Heneka et al. (2015) Neuroinflammation in Alzheimer's disease [systemic inflammation]
  • Iturria-Medina et al. (2016) Early role of vascular dysregulation on late-onset Alzheimer's disease [systemic modulation]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with high systemic inflammation or metabolic syndrome will show earlier and more severe network disruption on neuroimaging.
  2. Combining network-level imaging, systemic biomarkers, and digital phenotyping will improve early detection and risk stratification.
  3. Interventions reducing systemic inflammation/metabolic stress will slow network degeneration and cognitive decline.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Novel systemic biomarkers (e.g., gut microbiome metabolites) will predict network vulnerability and AD progression.
  2. Restoring network integrity via neuromodulation will delay or reverse clinical symptoms in some individuals.
  3. Digital phenotyping of network-level dysfunction (e.g., speech, gait, sleep) will enable remote, preclinical detection.

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals with high systemic risk do not show accelerated network degeneration, the systemic modulation law is challenged.
  2. If network disruption does not precede clinical symptoms, the network vulnerability law is falsified.
  3. If interventions targeting systemic factors do not slow network degeneration, the model is challenged.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some individuals with high systemic risk do not develop AD, suggesting unknown protective mechanisms.
  2. Rare cases of AD without significant network disruption are not explained.
