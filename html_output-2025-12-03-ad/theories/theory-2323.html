Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2323

Name: Dynamic Peripheral-Central Biomarker Flux Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory posits that Alzheimer's disease (AD) pathogenesis and detection are fundamentally governed by the dynamic, bidirectional flux of molecular biomarkers—including proteins, metabolites, and immune mediators—between the central nervous system (CNS) and peripheral compartments (e.g., blood, lymph). The magnitude, directionality, and temporal patterns of this flux reflect underlying disease processes (such as amyloid/tau pathology, neuroinflammation, and blood-brain barrier (BBB) integrity) and can be leveraged for early, sensitive, and specific detection of AD through peripheral sampling, especially when measured dynamically or in response to physiological perturbations.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Bidirectional Biomarker Flux Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• CNS_pathology → alters → BBB_permeability, and
• BBB_permeability → is_increased → relative_to_baseline
THEN
• biomarker_flux → is_increased → from_CNS_to_periphery, and
• peripheral_biomarker_levels → reflect → CNS_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. BBB breakdown is observed in early and late AD and is associated with increased CNS-to-blood transfer of proteins and inflammatory mediators.
  2. Peripheral blood levels of CNS-derived proteins (e.g., neurofilament light, tau) correlate with AD progression and CNS pathology.
  3. Dynamic changes in BBB permeability (e.g., after systemic inflammation) alter peripheral biomarker profiles.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While BBB dysfunction and peripheral biomarkers are established, the dynamic, flux-based, and bidirectional conceptualization is a novel synthesis.

What Already Exists: BBB dysfunction and peripheral detection of CNS proteins are recognized in AD research.

What is Novel: The explicit framing of AD as a disease of dynamic, bidirectional biomarker flux and the use of flux magnitude/patterns as a diagnostic principle is novel.

References:

  • Montagne et al. (2020) Blood-brain barrier breakdown in Alzheimer disease and other neurodegenerative disorders [BBB dysfunction, peripheral biomarker leakage]
  • Zetterberg & Blennow (2021) Blood biomarkers: democratizing Alzheimer’s diagnostics [Peripheral detection of CNS proteins]

Statement 1: Perturbation-Enhanced Biomarker Sensitivity Law (quantitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• systemic_or_CNS_perturbation → is_applied → to_subject, and
• subject → has_altered_biomarker_flux → between_CNS_and_periphery
THEN
• dynamic_peripheral_biomarker_response → is_predictive_of → CNS_pathology_and_AD_risk

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Acute systemic inflammation (e.g., infection, surgery) can transiently increase BBB permeability and alter peripheral biomarker levels, revealing latent CNS pathology.
  2. Dynamic changes in blood biomarkers after physiological stressors are more sensitive to early AD than static measurements.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law extends static biomarker approaches to a dynamic, systems-level diagnostic paradigm.

What Already Exists: Peripheral biomarkers are used in research, and perturbation-based diagnostics are emerging in other fields.

What is Novel: The use of dynamic, perturbation-induced biomarker flux as a sensitive and specific diagnostic for AD is novel.

References:

  • Heneka et al. (2015) Neuroinflammation in Alzheimer's disease [Inflammatory perturbations in AD]
  • Zetterberg & Blennow (2021) Blood biomarkers: democratizing Alzheimer’s diagnostics [Peripheral biomarker measurement]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early AD will show exaggerated peripheral biomarker responses (e.g., tau, neurofilament light, cytokines) to systemic inflammatory challenges compared to controls.
  2. Restoration of BBB integrity will reduce the correlation between peripheral and CNS biomarker levels in AD patients.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Dynamic, perturbation-based peripheral biomarker tests will enable preclinical AD detection before cognitive symptoms arise.
  2. Non-invasive monitoring of biomarker flux patterns (e.g., via wearable sensors) will allow real-time tracking of AD progression.

Negative Experiments

  1. If dynamic changes in peripheral biomarkers do not correlate with CNS pathology or AD risk, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If BBB permeability does not modulate peripheral biomarker levels in AD, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with normal BBB function and minimal peripheral biomarker changes, suggesting alternative mechanisms.
