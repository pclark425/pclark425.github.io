Name: Network Disintegration and Propagation Theory of Alzheimer's Pathogenesis

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the cause of Alzheimer's disease and how it can be effectively detected.

Description: Alzheimer's disease is initiated by molecular misfolding events (Aβ, tau) in vulnerable brain regions, which propagate via neural networks, leading to progressive network disintegration. This process is mirrored by functional and structural connectivity changes detectable by advanced imaging and electrophysiology, providing a mechanistic and predictive framework for both causation and detection.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Molecular Seeding and Network Propagation Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• brain_region → accumulates → misfolded_proteins (Aβ, tau)
THEN
• misfolded_proteins → propagate_along → anatomical_and_functional_networks, and
• connected_regions → develop → secondary_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Tau pathology spreads in a stereotyped pattern along connected brain regions (Braak staging).
  2. Aβ and tau can propagate trans-synaptically in animal models.
  3. Functional connectivity loss in default mode network correlates with tau and Aβ spread.
  4. PET imaging shows sequential involvement of brain networks in AD progression.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law synthesizes molecular and network-level evidence into a formal propagation law.

What Already Exists: The prion-like propagation of tau and Aβ is increasingly recognized.

What is Novel: The explicit law that network connectivity determines the spatial and temporal pattern of pathology spread and thus detection.

References:

  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network-based propagation]
  • Braak (1991) Neuropathological stageing of Alzheimer-related changes [stereotyped spread]
  • de Calignon (2012) Propagation of tau pathology in a model of early Alzheimer's disease [prion-like spread]

Statement 1: Network Disintegration Detection Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has_network_disintegration → in_vulnerable_brain_networks
THEN
• individual → will_show → detectable_changes_in_functional_connectivity (e.g., fMRI, EEG), and
• individual → will_show → early_cognitive_impairment

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Resting-state fMRI shows early loss of connectivity in the default mode network in preclinical AD.
  2. EEG/MEG detect network slowing and reduced synchrony in early AD.
  3. Network-level imaging changes precede overt atrophy and clinical symptoms.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law elevates network changes from a research observation to a formal detection principle.

What Already Exists: Network imaging is used for research, but not formalized as a law of detection.

What is Novel: The law that network disintegration is a necessary and sufficient condition for early detection.

References:

  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network-based detection]
  • Greicius (2004) Default-mode network activity distinguishes Alzheimer's disease from healthy aging [fMRI detection]
  • Stam (2007) Graph theoretical analysis of complex networks in the brain [network analysis in AD]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early tau or Aβ pathology will show network-level functional connectivity loss before significant atrophy.
  2. Longitudinal network imaging will predict the spatial pattern of future atrophy and cognitive decline.
  3. Network-based biomarkers will outperform regional atrophy measures for early detection.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Interventions that preserve network connectivity will slow or halt the spread of pathology and clinical progression.
  2. Non-invasive brain stimulation targeting network hubs will delay or reverse early network disintegration.
  3. Network disintegration patterns may allow for subtyping of AD with different clinical trajectories.

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals with early molecular pathology do not show network changes, the theory is challenged.
  2. If network disintegration does not precede or predict cognitive decline, the theory is weakened.
  3. If network-based detection does not outperform regional or molecular markers, the theory is called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases where molecular pathology accumulates without detectable network changes (e.g., due to compensatory mechanisms or technical limitations).
