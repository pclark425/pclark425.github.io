Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4761

Name: Cell-Intrinsic Proteostasis and Immune Signature Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory posits that the selective vulnerability or resistance of neuronal populations in the visual cortex to amyloid-β (Aβ) plaques and tau tangles in dementia is determined by cell-intrinsic differences in proteostasis capacity and immune gene expression signatures. Neurons with lower proteostasis capacity and a pro-inflammatory immune signature are more susceptible to pathological aggregation and degeneration, while those with robust proteostasis and anti-inflammatory or immune-suppressive signatures are more resistant.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Proteostasis Capacity Determines Neuronal Vulnerability (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuron → has_low → proteostasis gene expression
THEN
• neuron → is_vulnerable_to → Aβ and tau pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Single-cell transcriptomic studies show that neurons with lower expression of chaperones, autophagy, and ubiquitin-proteasome system genes are more likely to accumulate misfolded proteins and develop pathology.
  2. Proteostasis impairment is a hallmark of neurodegenerative diseases, and enhancing proteostasis can reduce pathology in animal models.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While proteostasis is a known factor in neurodegeneration, its cell-intrinsic, population-specific role in the visual cortex is a new conceptual advance.

What Already Exists: Proteostasis impairment is known to contribute to neurodegeneration, but its cell-type-specific role in selective vulnerability is less established.

What is Novel: The explicit link between intrinsic proteostasis gene expression and selective neuronal vulnerability in the visual cortex is novel.

References:

  • Mathys et al. (2019) Single-cell transcriptomic analysis of Alzheimer's disease [Cell-type transcriptomics, proteostasis genes]
  • Roussarie et al. (2020) Selective neuronal vulnerability in Alzheimer's disease: a network-based analysis [Selective vulnerability, proteostasis]

Statement 1: Immune Signature Modulates Neuronal Susceptibility (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuron → has_high → pro-inflammatory gene expression
THEN
• neuron → is_vulnerable_to → Aβ and tau pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neurons expressing higher levels of pro-inflammatory cytokines and immune-activating molecules are found in regions with early and severe pathology.
  2. Experimental induction of pro-inflammatory signaling in neurons increases their susceptibility to protein aggregation and degeneration.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law extends the concept of neuroinflammation to include cell-intrinsic immune signatures as a determinant of selective vulnerability.

What Already Exists: Neuroinflammation is implicated in neurodegeneration, but neuron-intrinsic immune signatures as determinants of vulnerability are less established.

What is Novel: The focus on neuron-intrinsic immune gene expression, rather than glial or systemic inflammation, is novel.

References:

  • Mathys et al. (2019) Single-cell transcriptomic analysis of Alzheimer's disease [Cell-type immune signatures]
  • Henstridge et al. (2019) The role of immune cells in neurodegeneration [Neuroinflammation, but not neuron-intrinsic focus]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Neuronal populations with higher proteostasis gene expression will show delayed or reduced Aβ and tau pathology in both human and animal models.
  2. Pharmacological or genetic enhancement of proteostasis in vulnerable neurons will confer resistance to pathology.
  3. Neurons with anti-inflammatory or immune-suppressive gene signatures will be less likely to develop pathology, even in the presence of extracellular Aβ.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Transplanting neurons with high proteostasis and anti-inflammatory signatures into vulnerable regions will confer local resistance to pathology.
  2. Longitudinal single-cell profiling will reveal that changes in proteostasis and immune signatures precede the onset of pathology in susceptible neurons.
  3. Manipulating neuron-intrinsic immune gene expression alone (without affecting glia) will be sufficient to alter vulnerability in vivo.

Negative Experiments

  1. If neurons with low proteostasis and high pro-inflammatory signatures do not develop pathology in models of dementia, the theory is challenged.
  2. If enhancing proteostasis or suppressing immune gene expression in vulnerable neurons does not reduce pathology, the theory is called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The role of synaptic connectivity, network activity, or extrinsic factors (e.g., glial interactions) in vulnerability is not addressed.

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This theory synthesizes and extends existing knowledge to propose a new, cell-intrinsic framework for selective vulnerability.
