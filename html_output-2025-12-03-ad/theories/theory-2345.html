Name: Integrated Network Disruption Theory of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory posits that Alzheimer's disease arises from the convergence of multiple pathological processes—amyloid accumulation, tau pathology, neuroinflammation, and vascular dysfunction—that interact to disrupt large-scale brain network connectivity. The breakdown of these networks underlies the clinical symptoms, and early detection is possible by identifying network-level changes through advanced neuroimaging and fluid biomarkers.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Convergent Pathology Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → amyloid accumulation, and
• individual → has → tau pathology, and
• individual → has → chronic neuroinflammation, and
• individual → has → vascular dysfunction
THEN
• individual → develops → large-scale brain network disruption

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Amyloid, tau, neuroinflammation, and vascular dysfunction are all observed in AD brains and interact to worsen pathology.
  2. Functional MRI and PET studies show early network connectivity loss in AD, correlating with pathology.
  3. Vascular risk factors increase AD risk and accelerate network breakdown.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While each factor is known, their required convergence and network-level focus is a new integrative framework.

What Already Exists: Individual roles of amyloid, tau, inflammation, and vascular dysfunction are established in AD.

What is Novel: The explicit law of their convergence as a necessary and sufficient cause of network-level disruption is novel.

References:

  • Iturria-Medina (2016) Early role of vascular dysregulation in late-onset Alzheimer's disease [vascular and network disruption]
  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network focus]
  • Jack (2018) NIA-AA Research Framework: Toward a biological definition of Alzheimer's disease [biomarker convergence]

Statement 1: Network Biomarker Detection Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → early network connectivity loss
THEN
• advanced neuroimaging (e.g., resting-state fMRI, connectomics) → detects → network disruption before clinical symptoms, and
• fluid biomarkers (e.g., plasma tau, neurofilament light) → correlate_with → network breakdown

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Resting-state fMRI detects network changes in preclinical AD.
  2. Fluid biomarkers rise before overt symptoms and correlate with imaging findings.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While biomarkers are used, the focus on network-level disruption as the primary detectable event is new.

What Already Exists: Imaging and fluid biomarkers are used for early AD detection.

What is Novel: The law that network-level changes are the earliest and most sensitive markers, and that fluid biomarkers track these changes, is novel.

References:

  • Buckner (2005) Molecular, structural, and functional characterization of Alzheimer's disease: Evidence for a relationship between default activity, amyloid, and memory [network disruption]
  • Palmqvist (2019) Discriminative accuracy of plasma phospho-tau217 for Alzheimer disease vs other neurodegenerative disorders [fluid biomarkers]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with combined amyloid, tau, inflammation, and vascular risk will show earlier and more severe network connectivity loss than those with only one or two factors.
  2. Network-level imaging changes will precede cognitive symptoms and correlate with fluid biomarker elevations.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Interventions targeting all four convergent pathologies will synergistically preserve network integrity and delay AD onset.
  2. Novel network-based biomarkers (e.g., graph theory metrics) will outperform traditional regional atrophy measures in predicting AD progression.

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals with all four pathologies do not show network disruption, the theory is challenged.
  2. If network-level imaging changes do not precede symptoms or do not correlate with fluid biomarkers, the theory is called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with minimal vascular dysfunction or inflammation.
  2. Familial AD with rapid progression despite limited network disruption on imaging.
