Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4687

Name: Network Disintegration Cascade Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the cause of Alzheimer's disease and how it can be effectively detected.

Description: Alzheimer's disease arises from a cascade of disruptions in large-scale brain network connectivity, initiated by multifactorial insults (genetic, metabolic, vascular, inflammatory), which precede and drive classical proteinopathies (amyloid, tau). Early detection is possible by identifying network-level dysfunction via advanced neuroimaging and electrophysiological biomarkers, which manifest before overt molecular pathology.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Network Disruption Precedes Proteinopathy (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → network-level brain connectivity disruption
THEN
• risk of Alzheimer's → is → increased, and
• protein aggregation (amyloid/tau) → is → subsequent

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Functional MRI and MEG studies show network dysfunction in at-risk individuals before amyloid/tau PET positivity.
  2. Longitudinal studies reveal that network breakdown predicts cognitive decline better than amyloid load alone.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The causal primacy of network disruption is a novel extension of existing observations.

What Already Exists: Network dysfunction is observed in Alzheimer's and mild cognitive impairment.

What is Novel: This law posits network disruption as a primary, upstream event, not a consequence of proteinopathy.

References:

  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network vulnerability]
  • Brier (2012) Loss of intranetwork and internetwork resting state functional connections with Alzheimer's disease progression [network changes in AD]

Statement 1: Network Biomarkers Enable Preclinical Detection (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → abnormal large-scale network connectivity (fMRI/MEG/EEG)
THEN
• risk of Alzheimer's → is → elevated, and
• proteinopathy → may be → absent or minimal

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Resting-state fMRI and MEG detect network changes in asymptomatic APOE4 carriers.
  2. EEG slowing and connectivity loss predict conversion to Alzheimer's in longitudinal cohorts.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: The focus on network-level detection as primary is a novel application.

What Already Exists: Network biomarkers are used in research but not as primary detection tools.

What is Novel: This law proposes network biomarkers as the earliest, most sensitive detection method.

References:

  • Stam (2014) Modern network science of neurological disorders [network analysis in AD]
  • Dubois (2016) Preclinical Alzheimer's disease: Definition, natural history, and diagnostic criteria [biomarker staging]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early network connectivity loss will develop Alzheimer's pathology at higher rates, even if amyloid/tau negative.
  2. Interventions that restore network connectivity (e.g., neuromodulation, cognitive training) will delay or prevent Alzheimer's onset.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Network-based interventions in preclinical individuals will prevent or reverse downstream protein aggregation.
  2. Network biomarkers will outperform amyloid/tau PET in predicting conversion from at-risk to symptomatic Alzheimer's.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding individuals with severe network disruption but no increased Alzheimer's risk would challenge the theory.
  2. Demonstrating that Alzheimer's can develop in the presence of normal network connectivity would refute the theory.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of Alzheimer's with normal network imaging and no connectivity loss.

Existing Theory Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This theory extends network science concepts to causality and detection, which is not standard in current models.

References:

  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network vulnerability]
  • Stam (2014) Modern network science of neurological disorders [network analysis in AD]
