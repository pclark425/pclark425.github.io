Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2320

Name: Dynamic Peripheral-Central Biomarker Flux Theory (Adaptive Feedback Model)

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory posits that Alzheimer's disease is driven by maladaptive feedback loops between the CNS and peripheral systems, where changes in one compartment (e.g., CNS amyloid accumulation) trigger compensatory or pathological responses in the periphery (e.g., immune activation, metabolic shifts), which in turn feed back to exacerbate CNS pathology. The flux of biomarkers is dynamically regulated by these feedbacks, and breakdown of adaptive feedback leads to runaway pathology. Detection and intervention should focus on identifying and modulating these feedback loops.

ConditionDetails

• CNS_biomarker_levels → deviate_from → homeostatic_range

• biomarker_flux → is_adjusted_by → feedback_mechanisms

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Peripheral immune activation is observed in response to CNS amyloid and tau pathology.
  2. Metabolic and vascular changes in the periphery are triggered by CNS injury and can modulate disease progression.
  3. Feedback loops between CNS and peripheral compartments are observed in animal models of neurodegeneration.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

What Already Exists: Feedback between CNS and periphery is recognized in neuroimmunology and neurovascular research.

What is Novel: The explicit focus on maladaptive feedback as a driver of AD and as a detection/intervention target is novel.

Statement 1: Maladaptive Feedback Escalation Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• feedback_loops → become_maladaptive → due_to_chronic_or_excessive_stress
THEN
• biomarker_flux → becomes_dysregulated → between_CNS_and_periphery, and
• AD_pathology → is_accelerated_by → maladaptive_feedback, and
• peripheral_biomarker_signatures → reflect → maladaptive_feedback_state

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Chronic inflammation and metabolic syndrome are associated with increased AD risk and progression.
  2. Peripheral biomarker changes (e.g., cytokines, acute phase proteins) correlate with disease severity and progression.
  3. Failure of compensatory feedback (e.g., persistent immune activation) leads to worsening CNS pathology.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law synthesizes feedback concepts into a new explanatory and predictive model for AD.

What Already Exists: Maladaptive feedback is a concept in chronic disease but not central in AD models.

What is Novel: The law's application of maladaptive feedback escalation as a core driver and detection principle in AD is novel.

References:

  • Heneka et al. (2015) Neuroinflammation in Alzheimer's disease [Chronic inflammation and feedback]
  • De Strooper & Karran (2016) The Cellular Phase of Alzheimer's Disease [Cellular feedback and escalation]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Interventions that restore adaptive feedback (e.g., anti-inflammatory or metabolic modulators) will slow AD progression.
  2. Peripheral biomarker patterns will shift from adaptive to maladaptive profiles as AD progresses.
  3. Early detection of maladaptive feedback signatures in blood will predict future cognitive decline.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Targeting peripheral feedback loops (e.g., gut-brain axis, liver-brain axis) will have unexpected effects on CNS pathology.
  2. Novel feedback-modulating drugs will produce distinct biomarker flux signatures in responders vs. non-responders.
  3. AI models can classify AD subtypes based on feedback loop dynamics inferred from longitudinal biomarker data.

Negative Experiments

  1. If peripheral feedback signatures do not correlate with AD progression, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If restoring adaptive feedback does not alter biomarker flux or disease course, the theory would be undermined.
  3. If maladaptive feedback is not observed in preclinical or early AD, the theory's central premise would be questioned.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with minimal evidence of peripheral feedback involvement.
