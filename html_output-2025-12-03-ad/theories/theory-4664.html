Name: Multifactorial Cascade Model of Alzheimer's Disease Detection (General Formulation)

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the cause of Alzheimer's disease and how it can be effectively detected.

Description: This theory proposes that effective detection of Alzheimer's disease requires a systems-level approach that integrates multi-domain biomarkers—genetic, proteomic, metabolic, vascular, neuroimaging, and digital phenotyping—reflecting the multifactorial cascade. The model asserts that early, preclinical detection is possible by identifying convergent changes across these domains, and that the temporal sequence and pattern of biomarker changes can be used to stratify risk, predict progression, and personalize interventions.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Multidomain Biomarker Convergence Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → shows_convergent_changes_in → multiple_biomarker_domains (genetic, proteomic, metabolic, vascular, neuroimaging, digital)
THEN
• individual → has_high_probability_of → preclinical_or_early_AD

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Studies show that combining amyloid PET, tau PET, CSF biomarkers, and neuroimaging improves early AD detection.
  2. Digital phenotyping (e.g., speech, gait) adds predictive value to traditional biomarkers.
  3. Blood-based biomarkers (e.g., plasma p-tau, neurofilament light) are increasingly sensitive for early AD detection when combined with other domains.
  4. Machine learning models integrating multidomain data outperform single-domain models in predicting AD conversion.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law synthesizes current trends into a formal, testable principle.

What Already Exists: Multimodal biomarker approaches are increasingly used, but not formalized as a convergence law.

What is Novel: This law formalizes the necessity of multidomain convergence for robust early detection.

References:

  • Jack et al. (2018) NIA-AA Research Framework: Toward a biological definition of Alzheimer's disease [multimodal biomarkers]
  • Hampel et al. (2018) Blood-based biomarkers for Alzheimer disease: mapping the road to the clinic [biomarker integration]

Statement 1: Temporal Pattern Stratification Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has_longitudinal_biomarker_data → across_multiple_domains
THEN
• temporal_sequence_of_changes → predicts → risk_and_rate_of_AD_progression

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Longitudinal studies show that the order and timing of biomarker changes (e.g., amyloid before tau, before atrophy) predict clinical progression.
  2. Temporal clustering of biomarker changes (e.g., rapid tau accumulation after amyloid plateau) is associated with faster cognitive decline.
  3. Machine learning models using temporal biomarker trajectories improve prediction of conversion from mild cognitive impairment to AD.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law extends existing temporal models to a personalized, predictive framework.

What Already Exists: Temporal biomarker models exist, but are not widely used for personalized risk stratification.

What is Novel: This law asserts that individualized temporal patterns can be used for precision prediction and intervention.

References:

  • Jack et al. (2013) Tracking pathophysiological processes in Alzheimer's disease: an updated hypothetical model of dynamic biomarkers [temporal biomarker sequence]
  • Villemagne et al. (2013) Amyloid β deposition, neurodegeneration, and cognitive decline in sporadic Alzheimer's disease: a prospective cohort study [temporal biomarker changes]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with convergent changes in at least three biomarker domains will develop clinical AD within five years at higher rates than those with single-domain changes.
  2. Temporal patterns where amyloid changes precede tau and neurodegeneration will predict faster progression than other sequences.
  3. Digital phenotyping will identify at-risk individuals before traditional biomarkers become abnormal.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Novel combinations of biomarker changes (e.g., metabolic + digital, without amyloid) may define new AD subtypes.
  2. Temporal patterns of biomarker change may differ in non-amyloid, non-tau driven dementias, allowing differential diagnosis.
  3. Machine learning models integrating multidomain, longitudinal data will outperform current clinical risk scores.

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals with convergent multidomain biomarker changes do not develop AD, the convergence law is challenged.
  2. If temporal patterns do not predict progression rates, the stratification law is invalid.
  3. If digital phenotyping does not add predictive value, the multidomain approach is weakened.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some individuals with abnormal biomarkers never develop clinical AD, possibly due to cognitive reserve or resilience factors.
