Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4668

Name: Network Vulnerability and Propagation Model

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the cause of Alzheimer's disease and how it can be effectively detected.

Description: This theory proposes that Alzheimer's disease is initiated and propagated through selective vulnerability of large-scale brain networks, particularly those with high metabolic demand and connectivity (e.g., the default mode network). The model asserts that pathological processes (amyloid, tau, inflammation) preferentially target these networks, and that disease spreads via network-based mechanisms (e.g., trans-synaptic propagation, activity-dependent vulnerability). Early detection is possible by identifying network dysfunction before overt pathology or clinical symptoms.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Network Selective Vulnerability Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• brain_network → has_high_connectivity_and_metabolic_demand → True
THEN
• brain_network → is_preferentially_affected_by → AD_pathology, and
• network_dysfunction → precedes → clinical_symptoms_and_overt_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. The default mode network (DMN) shows early hypometabolism and connectivity loss in preclinical AD.
  2. Amyloid and tau deposition patterns overlap with highly connected brain networks.
  3. Functional MRI detects network dysfunction before cognitive decline.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law elevates network vulnerability from a correlative observation to a mechanistic principle.

What Already Exists: Network-based vulnerability is recognized, but not formalized as a primary driver.

What is Novel: This law asserts network properties as the primary determinant of early AD targeting and detection.

References:

  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network vulnerability]
  • Buckner et al. (2005) Molecular, structural, and functional characterization of Alzheimer's disease: evidence for a relationship between default activity, amyloid, and memory [DMN and amyloid]

Statement 1: Trans-synaptic Propagation Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• pathological_protein → is_present_in → vulnerable_network, and
• network → has_high_synaptic_connectivity → True
THEN
• pathology → spreads_along → network_connections, and
• spread → is_activity-dependent → True

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Tau pathology spreads in patterns consistent with network connectivity rather than anatomical proximity.
  2. Animal models show trans-synaptic propagation of tau and amyloid.
  3. Regions with high activity and connectivity are more susceptible to early pathology.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law extends propagation principles to a network-level, activity-dependent framework.

What Already Exists: Trans-synaptic spread of tau is recognized in models.

What is Novel: This law generalizes propagation to all AD pathologies and formalizes activity-dependence.

References:

  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network propagation]
  • de Calignon et al. (2012) Propagation of tau pathology in a model of early Alzheimer's disease [tau spread]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early network dysfunction (e.g., DMN hypoconnectivity) will develop AD pathology and symptoms earlier than those without.
  2. Interventions that reduce network hyperactivity or metabolic demand will slow propagation of pathology.
  3. Network-based biomarkers (e.g., functional connectivity) will outperform regional biomarkers for early detection.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Altering network connectivity (e.g., via neuromodulation) will change the pattern and rate of AD pathology spread.
  2. Some individuals may resist AD progression due to resilient network architecture despite high pathology burden.
  3. Non-amyloid, non-tau pathologies may also propagate via network-based mechanisms.

Negative Experiments

  1. If network dysfunction does not precede pathology or symptoms, the network vulnerability law is invalid.
  2. If pathology spreads independently of network connectivity, the propagation law is challenged.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases where pathology accumulates in regions with low connectivity/metabolic demand.
