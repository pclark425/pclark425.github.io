Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2479

Name: Network-Connectivity-Driven Selective Vulnerability Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory proposes that the selective vulnerability of neuronal populations in the visual cortex to amyloid-β and tau pathology is fundamentally determined by their network connectivity profiles. Neurons with extensive long-range, high-degree, or hub-like connectivity are more susceptible to pathological protein spread and accumulation, while neurons with predominantly local, low-degree, or isolated connectivity are more resistant. The theory posits that the architecture of the cortical network, including both anatomical and functional connectivity, governs the likelihood of pathological seeding, propagation, and cellular stress responses.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: High-Connectivity Neurons Are More Vulnerable (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuron → has_connectivity_degree → high, and
• neuron → has_long_range_connections → true
THEN
• neuron → is_vulnerable_to → amyloid-β_and_tau_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Hub neurons and highly connected projection neurons are early sites of tau and amyloid-β pathology in multiple cortical regions.
  2. Pathological protein spread in neurodegeneration follows network connectivity patterns, with hubs acting as propagation centers.
  3. Functional MRI and connectomics studies show that regions with high network centrality are more affected in Alzheimer's disease.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law generalizes network-based propagation to the single-cell level and applies it to selective vulnerability in the visual cortex.

What Already Exists: Network-based propagation of pathology is observed in Alzheimer's and other dementias.

What is Novel: The explicit prediction that individual neuron vulnerability is determined by their network degree and long-range connectivity in the visual cortex is new.

References:

  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Network-based vulnerability]
  • de Haan et al. (2012) Disrupted modular brain dynamics reflect cognitive dysfunction in Alzheimer's disease [Network centrality and vulnerability]

Statement 1: Low-Connectivity Neurons Are More Resistant (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuron → has_connectivity_degree → low, and
• neuron → has_long_range_connections → false
THEN
• neuron → is_resistant_to → amyloid-β_and_tau_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neurons with predominantly local connections, such as certain interneurons and spiny stellate cells, are less affected in Alzheimer's disease.
  2. Regions with low network centrality show delayed or reduced pathology in both human and animal models.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law extends network-based vulnerability to explain resistance at the cellular level.

What Already Exists: Some cell types and regions are relatively spared in Alzheimer's disease.

What is Novel: The mechanistic link to low network connectivity as a protective factor is new.

References:

  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Network-based vulnerability]
  • Braak & Braak (1991) Neuropathological stageing of Alzheimer-related changes [Laminar and regional resistance]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. In connectomics datasets, neurons with higher degree centrality in the visual cortex will show greater accumulation of amyloid-β and tau pathology.
  2. Disruption of long-range connections in animal models will reduce vulnerability of previously susceptible neurons.
  3. Regions with higher functional connectivity to other cortical areas will show earlier pathological changes.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially increasing the network degree of resistant neurons (e.g., by viral overexpression of synaptic adhesion molecules) will increase their vulnerability to pathology.
  2. Selective ablation of hub neurons will alter the spatial pattern of pathology spread in the visual cortex.

Negative Experiments

  1. If neurons with low connectivity degree show equal or greater vulnerability than high-degree neurons, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If pathology spreads independently of network connectivity patterns, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some molecularly defined neuron subtypes may show vulnerability or resistance independent of their connectivity profile.
  2. Non-neuronal factors (e.g., glial responses, vascular changes) may modulate vulnerability in ways not captured by connectivity alone.
