Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4702

Name: Network Disconnection and Propagation Theory of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the cause of Alzheimer's disease and how it can be effectively detected.

Description: Alzheimer's disease arises from the propagation of pathological protein aggregates (amyloid-beta and tau) along vulnerable neural networks, leading to progressive disconnection of brain regions. Early detection is possible by identifying network-level functional and structural changes, as well as molecular signatures of aggregate propagation in cerebrospinal fluid and blood.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Pathological Propagation Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• brain region → is connected to → region with amyloid or tau pathology
THEN
• connected brain region → is at increased risk for → developing similar pathology, and
• network → undergoes → progressive disconnection

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Tau and amyloid pathology spreads along anatomically connected brain regions.
  2. Functional MRI shows early network disconnection in preclinical Alzheimer's.
  3. Animal models demonstrate trans-synaptic propagation of tau aggregates.
  4. Loss of connectivity in the default mode network is an early feature of Alzheimer's.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: Aggregate propagation is established, but the explicit focus on network disconnection as both cause and detection target is a novel synthesis.

What Already Exists: Propagation of tau and amyloid along neural networks is a recognized phenomenon.

What is Novel: This law formalizes the network disconnection as a central driver and links it to early detection.

References:

  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network vulnerability]
  • Braak (2011) Staging of Alzheimer-related tau pathology [propagation]
  • de Haan (2012) Disconnection syndrome in Alzheimer's disease: mechanistic insights from functional connectivity studies [network disconnection]

Statement 1: Network Biomarker Detection Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → network-level functional or structural disconnection (e.g., via fMRI, EEG, or connectomics)
THEN
• individual → is at increased risk for → Alzheimer's progression, and
• network biomarker panel → can detect → preclinical Alzheimer's

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Functional and structural connectivity changes precede clinical symptoms in Alzheimer's.
  2. EEG and fMRI network biomarkers can distinguish preclinical Alzheimer's from controls.
  3. Network disconnection correlates with cognitive decline.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: Network biomarkers are being studied, but the explicit centrality of network disconnection as both cause and detection is novel.

What Already Exists: Network biomarkers are under investigation for early detection.

What is Novel: This law proposes that network-level disconnection is a primary, not secondary, biomarker and detection tool.

References:

  • de Haan (2012) Disconnection syndrome in Alzheimer's disease: mechanistic insights from functional connectivity studies [network disconnection]
  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network vulnerability]
  • Brier (2014) Functional connectivity and graph theory in preclinical Alzheimer's disease [network biomarkers]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early network disconnection on fMRI or EEG will progress to Alzheimer's symptoms faster than those without such changes.
  2. Network-level biomarkers will outperform single-molecule biomarkers in predicting conversion from mild cognitive impairment to Alzheimer's.
  3. Therapies that restore network connectivity will slow cognitive decline.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Non-invasive brain stimulation to enhance network connectivity will delay or prevent Alzheimer's onset.
  2. Distinct patterns of network disconnection will differentiate Alzheimer's from other dementias before clinical symptoms.
  3. Network disconnection signatures in blood-derived exosomes will correlate with imaging findings.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding individuals with pronounced network disconnection who do not develop Alzheimer's would challenge the theory.
  2. Demonstrating that network biomarkers do not predict progression would refute the detection law.
  3. Therapies that restore network connectivity but do not affect disease progression would challenge the causal link.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of Alzheimer's with minimal network disconnection on imaging.
