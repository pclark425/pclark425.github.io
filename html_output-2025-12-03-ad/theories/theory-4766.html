Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4766

Name: Activity-Dependent Proteostatic Vulnerability Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory posits that the selective vulnerability or resistance of visual cortical neuronal populations to Aβ and tau pathology is governed by their intrinsic patterns of electrical activity and associated proteostatic stress. Neurons with high baseline or stimulus-driven activity experience increased protein synthesis, trafficking, and turnover, which can overload proteostasis mechanisms and promote aggregation of misfolded proteins. Conversely, neurons with lower or more regulated activity maintain proteostasis and are more resistant.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: High Activity Increases Proteostatic Stress (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal_population → has_high_baseline_or_evoked_activity → True
THEN
• neuronal_population → has_increased_risk_of → Aβ_and_tau_aggregation

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Highly active neurons in the default mode network and visual cortex are early sites of Aβ/tau pathology.
  2. Increased synaptic activity promotes Aβ secretion and tau phosphorylation in vitro.
  3. Optogenetic stimulation of cortical neurons increases local Aβ deposition in animal models.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The application to fine-scale, population-specific vulnerability in the visual cortex is a novel extension.

What Already Exists: Activity-dependent Aβ secretion and tau phosphorylation are established in vitro and in animal models.

What is Novel: This law extends the principle to explain selective vulnerability within the visual cortex based on endogenous activity patterns.

References:

  • Bero et al. (2011) Neuronal activity regulates the regional vulnerability to amyloid-β deposition [Activity-dependent Aβ]
  • Busche & Konnerth (2016) Impairments of neural circuit function in Alzheimer's disease [Activity and vulnerability]

Statement 1: Proteostatic Capacity Buffers Activity-Driven Stress (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal_population → has_high_activity → True, and
• neuronal_population → has_high_proteostatic_capacity → True
THEN
• neuronal_population → is_resistant_to → Aβ_and_tau_aggregation

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neurons with robust expression of chaperones and proteasome components are less prone to Aβ/tau aggregation despite high activity.
  2. Genetic upregulation of proteostasis pathways reduces pathology in highly active neurons.
  3. Single-cell RNA-seq reveals that some visual cortical neurons express higher levels of proteostatic genes and are less affected by AD pathology.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The explicit interaction between activity and proteostatic buffering as a determinant of local vulnerability is a novel synthesis.

What Already Exists: Proteostasis mechanisms are known to protect against protein aggregation in neurodegenerative disease.

What is Novel: This law predicts that the interplay between activity and proteostatic capacity determines selective vulnerability at the population level within the visual cortex.

References:

  • Koren et al. (2019) Proteostasis in the brain: mechanisms and roles in neurodegeneration [Proteostasis and neurodegeneration]
  • Bero et al. (2011) Neuronal activity regulates the regional vulnerability to amyloid-β deposition [Activity-dependent Aβ]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Visual cortical regions with higher baseline activity will show earlier and more severe Aβ/tau pathology.
  2. Enhancing proteostatic capacity in highly active neurons will reduce their vulnerability to aggregation.
  3. Reducing neuronal activity pharmacologically or optogenetically will decrease local Aβ/tau deposition.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially increasing activity in resistant neuronal populations will induce local vulnerability only if proteostatic capacity is insufficient.
  2. Simultaneous upregulation of activity and proteostasis in the same neurons will maintain resistance to aggregation.
  3. Chronic visual stimulation in animal models will accelerate pathology in specific visual cortical layers.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding highly active neuronal populations that remain resistant to Aβ/tau aggregation despite low proteostatic capacity would challenge the theory.
  2. Demonstrating that reducing activity does not affect local pathology would call the theory into question.
  3. Observing no correlation between activity patterns and vulnerability in the visual cortex would contradict the theory.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some low-activity neuronal populations are still vulnerable, suggesting additional factors may be involved.
