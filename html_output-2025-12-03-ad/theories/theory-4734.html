Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4734

Name: Molecular Signature and Connectivity Gradient Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory posits that selective vulnerability or resistance of visual cortical neuronal populations to Aβ and tau pathology is determined by a combination of their molecular expression profiles (e.g., receptor types, proteostasis machinery, synaptic proteins) and their position within large-scale connectivity gradients (e.g., feedforward vs. feedback, hubness). Neurons with high expression of vulnerable molecular signatures and centrality in connectivity gradients are more likely to accumulate pathology.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Molecular Vulnerability Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal_population → expresses_high_levels_of → Aβ/tau_binding_or_processing_molecules
THEN
• neuronal_population → is_selectively_vulnerable_to → Aβ_and_tau_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neurons expressing high levels of APP, tau, or specific kinases (e.g., GSK3β) are more prone to pathology.
  2. Proteomic studies show vulnerable neurons have distinct expression of synaptic and proteostasis-related proteins.
  3. Single-cell RNA-seq reveals molecularly defined subpopulations with differential vulnerability.

What is Novel: The explicit conditional linking of molecular signature to selective vulnerability in the visual cortex.

Statement 1: Connectivity Gradient Vulnerability Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal_population → has_high_connectivity_centrality → visual_cortex_network
THEN
• neuronal_population → is_selectively_vulnerable_to → Aβ_and_tau_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Highly connected hub neurons are more likely to accumulate pathology in AD.
  2. Regions with high connectivity gradients (e.g., association cortices) show early pathology.
  3. Disruption of hub neurons leads to widespread network dysfunction.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law is a formalization and extension of existing observations.

What Already Exists: Hub vulnerability is described in connectomics literature.

What is Novel: Application to visual cortex and explicit law linking connectivity gradient to selective vulnerability.

References:

  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Hub vulnerability]
  • Buckner et al. (2009) Cortical hubs revealed by intrinsic functional connectivity [Hubness and vulnerability]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Neuronal populations with high expression of Aβ/tau processing proteins and high network centrality will show early pathology.
  2. Manipulating molecular signatures (e.g., reducing APP or tau expression) in hub neurons will reduce their vulnerability.
  3. Visual cortex subregions with lower connectivity centrality will be more resistant to pathology.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially increasing the connectivity of resistant neurons will increase their vulnerability to Aβ/tau pathology.
  2. Altering molecular signatures in resistant populations will render them vulnerable, even if their connectivity is low.
  3. Simultaneous manipulation of both molecular and connectivity factors will have synergistic effects on vulnerability.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding highly connected neurons with resistant molecular signatures that remain resistant would challenge the theory.
  2. Observing selective vulnerability in neurons with neither high-risk molecular signatures nor high connectivity would contradict the theory.
  3. Demonstrating that manipulation of molecular or connectivity factors does not alter vulnerability would refute the theory.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some neurons with high-risk molecular signatures but low connectivity remain resistant, suggesting additional factors.
