Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2357

Name: Dynamic Homeostatic Failure Theory of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory proposes that Alzheimer's disease results from a progressive failure of the brain's homeostatic mechanisms to maintain synaptic, proteostatic, and metabolic balance in the face of age-related and environmental stressors. The breakdown of these adaptive systems leads to the accumulation of pathological proteins, synaptic dysfunction, and ultimately neurodegeneration. Detection should focus on early markers of homeostatic stress and compensatory failure, such as synaptic activity changes, proteostasis network collapse, and metabolic inflexibility.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Homeostatic Collapse Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• brain → experiences → chronic stressors (aging, inflammation, metabolic stress), and
• homeostatic systems → fail to compensate → increased proteostatic, synaptic, or metabolic load
THEN
• brain → accumulates → misfolded proteins (amyloid, tau), synaptic loss, and metabolic dysfunction, and
• individual → develops → Alzheimer's disease

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Proteostasis network impairment (e.g., reduced autophagy, chaperone function) precedes amyloid and tau accumulation.
  2. Synaptic dysfunction and loss are early events in AD and correlate with cognitive decline.
  3. Aging and chronic inflammation reduce the brain's ability to clear misfolded proteins.
  4. Metabolic inflexibility (e.g., impaired glucose utilization) is observed before overt neurodegeneration.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While elements exist in the literature, the explicit framing of AD as a dynamic homeostatic failure is a novel synthesis.

What Already Exists: Proteostasis and synaptic failure are recognized as contributors to AD, and homeostatic mechanisms are studied in neurodegeneration.

What is Novel: This law unifies these failures as a primary, dynamic process, emphasizing the progressive collapse of adaptive systems as the root cause.

References:

  • Selkoe (2002) Alzheimer's disease is a synaptic failure [Synaptic dysfunction]
  • Morimoto & Cuervo (2014) Proteostasis and aging: new insights into the etiology of neurodegeneration [Proteostasis failure]
  • Mattson (2004) Pathways towards and away from Alzheimer's disease [Homeostatic adaptation]

Statement 1: Early Homeostatic Stress Detection Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• detection_method → measures → early markers of synaptic, proteostatic, or metabolic stress
THEN
• detection_method → enables → preclinical identification of individuals at risk for AD

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Synaptic activity markers (e.g., SV2A PET, EEG slowing) change before clinical symptoms.
  2. Proteostasis stress markers (e.g., unfolded protein response, chaperone levels) are altered in at-risk individuals.
  3. Metabolic imaging (e.g., FDG-PET) detects hypometabolism years before diagnosis.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The focus on homeostatic stress as the earliest and most reliable detection target is a novel extension.

What Already Exists: Early detection using synaptic and metabolic markers is under investigation.

What is Novel: This law asserts that homeostatic stress markers are the most sensitive and specific for preclinical AD risk.

References:

  • Chen et al. (2018) Imaging synaptic density in Alzheimer disease with SV2A PET [Synaptic marker]
  • Mosconi et al. (2008) FDG-PET in the early diagnosis of Alzheimer's disease [Metabolic marker]
  • Hetz & Saxena (2017) ER stress and the unfolded protein response in neurodegeneration [Proteostasis marker]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early evidence of synaptic or proteostatic stress will have a higher risk of developing AD, even in the absence of amyloid or tau pathology.
  2. Interventions that enhance homeostatic capacity (e.g., boosting autophagy, synaptic plasticity) will delay or prevent AD onset.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Novel blood-based markers of proteostasis stress will predict AD risk before any imaging or cognitive changes.
  2. Restoring homeostatic function in aged brains will reverse early AD pathology.

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals with robust homeostatic markers still develop AD at the same rate as those with impaired markers, the theory is challenged.
  2. If amyloid/tau pathology develops in the absence of any detectable homeostatic stress, the theory is called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Familial AD due to deterministic mutations may bypass homeostatic failure and directly induce pathology.
