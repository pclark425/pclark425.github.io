Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2476

Name: Activity-Dependent Network Vulnerability Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory proposes that the selective vulnerability of visual cortex neurons to amyloid-β and tau pathology is governed by their participation in highly active, recurrent network motifs. Neurons that are part of frequently reactivated ensembles or that experience high synaptic activity are more prone to metabolic stress, increased protein synthesis, and local accumulation of misfolded proteins, thereby increasing their susceptibility to neurodegenerative pathology.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. High synaptic activity increases local production and release of amyloid-β.
  2. Metabolic stress from sustained activity impairs proteostasis and increases vulnerability to misfolded protein accumulation.
  3. In vivo imaging shows that visual stimulation increases amyloid-β levels in the visual cortex.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law extends known activity-dependent mechanisms to explain selective vulnerability at the subpopulation level in the visual cortex.

What Already Exists: Activity-dependent amyloid-β production is established in the literature.

What is Novel: The explicit link between metabolic stress from recurrent activity and selective vulnerability in visual cortex neurons is new.

References:

  • Cirrito et al. (2005) Synaptic activity regulates interstitial fluid amyloid-β levels in vivo [Activity-dependent amyloid-β production]
  • Yamamoto et al. (2015) Chronic optogenetic activation augments amyloid-β pathology in a mouse model of Alzheimer’s disease [Activity and pathology link]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Visual cortex neurons that are part of highly active, recurrent ensembles will show greater amyloid-β and tau pathology in both animal models and human tissue.
  2. Reducing the activity of these ensembles (e.g., via optogenetic silencing) will decrease their vulnerability to pathology.
  3. Regions of the visual cortex with lower baseline activity will show delayed or reduced pathology.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially inducing recurrent activity in previously resistant visual cortex neurons will increase their susceptibility to amyloid-β and tau accumulation.
  2. Disrupting recurrent network motifs without altering overall activity will reduce selective vulnerability.
  3. Enhancing proteostasis mechanisms in highly active neurons will mitigate their vulnerability despite high activity.

Negative Experiments

  1. If highly active, recurrently engaged neurons in the visual cortex do not show increased vulnerability to amyloid-β and tau pathology, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If reducing activity in these neurons does not decrease pathology, the theory would be weakened.
  3. If metabolic stress does not correlate with protein aggregation in visual cortex neurons, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Neuronal vulnerability may also depend on molecular identity, such as expression of specific receptors or chaperones, independent of activity.
  2. Non-neuronal factors, such as local inflammation or vascular changes, may influence vulnerability.
