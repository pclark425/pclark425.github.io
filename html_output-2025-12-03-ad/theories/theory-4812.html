Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4812

Name: Activity-Dependent Homeostatic Protection Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory proposes that neuronal populations in the visual cortex that maintain robust homeostatic plasticity mechanisms in response to activity fluctuations are selectively resistant to amyloid-β and tau pathology. Conversely, populations with impaired or rigid homeostatic responses are more vulnerable, as they cannot adapt to early synaptic dysfunction or excitotoxic stress induced by Aβ/tau accumulation.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neuronal populations with strong synaptic scaling and intrinsic excitability regulation show less degeneration in models of Alzheimer's disease.
  2. Impaired homeostatic plasticity is associated with increased vulnerability in both animal models and human tissue.
  3. Visual cortical neurons with preserved homeostatic mechanisms maintain synaptic integrity and resist Aβ/tau-induced dysfunction.
  4. Experimental enhancement of homeostatic plasticity in neurons reduces Aβ/tau-induced degeneration.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: While homeostatic plasticity is known, its role as a determinant of selective resistance in visual cortex is novel.

What Already Exists: Homeostatic plasticity is known to stabilize neural circuits, and its impairment is linked to neurodegeneration.

What is Novel: This law directly links the degree of homeostatic plasticity to selective resistance in visual cortex populations, making explicit predictions about which populations are spared.

References:

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Populations with reduced capacity for synaptic scaling or intrinsic excitability adjustment degenerate more rapidly in Alzheimer's models.
  2. Impaired homeostatic plasticity is observed in vulnerable visual cortical neurons prior to degeneration.
  3. Genetic or pharmacological disruption of homeostatic mechanisms increases Aβ/tau-induced pathology in visual cortex.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The general link is known, but the explicit, predictive application to visual cortex populations is novel.

What Already Exists: Impaired homeostatic plasticity is associated with neurodegeneration.

What is Novel: This law predicts that the degree of impairment directly determines vulnerability at the population level in visual cortex.

References:

  • Styr & Slutsky (2018) Imbalance between firing homeostasis and synaptic plasticity drives early-phase Alzheimer's disease [Describes homeostatic impairment, not population-level predictions]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Visual cortical neurons with experimentally enhanced homeostatic plasticity will show reduced pathology after Aβ/tau exposure.
  2. Populations with naturally high homeostatic capacity (e.g., certain inhibitory interneurons) will be relatively spared in dementia models.
  3. Disruption of homeostatic plasticity in otherwise resistant populations will increase their vulnerability to Aβ/tau pathology.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Pharmacological or genetic enhancement of homeostatic plasticity in vulnerable visual cortical populations will prevent or reverse Aβ/tau-induced degeneration.
  2. Chronic visual deprivation (reducing activity fluctuations) will increase vulnerability of visual cortical neurons to pathology.
  3. Artificially inducing homeostatic plasticity in non-neuronal cells (e.g., glia) will confer resistance to Aβ/tau pathology.

Negative Experiments

  1. If populations with robust homeostatic plasticity are found to be highly vulnerable to Aβ/tau pathology, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If enhancing homeostatic plasticity does not confer resistance in experimental models, the theory would be called into question.
  3. If populations with impaired homeostatic plasticity remain resistant to Aβ/tau pathology, the theory would be undermined.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some populations with low homeostatic plasticity remain resistant, suggesting additional protective mechanisms.
