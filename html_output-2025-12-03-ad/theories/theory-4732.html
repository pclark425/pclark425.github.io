Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4732

Name: Network-Driven Propagation and Local Defense Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory proposes that the selective vulnerability of visual cortex neurons to amyloid-β and tau pathology is determined by the interplay between large-scale network-driven propagation of misfolded proteins and the local defense mechanisms (e.g., autophagy, antioxidant response, glial support) of each neuronal population. Neurons that are highly embedded in long-range, high-traffic network hubs are more likely to receive and propagate pathology, but only succumb if their local defense mechanisms are insufficient.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Network Propagation Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal_population → is_network_hub → True, and
• neuronal_population → receives_high_pathology_traffic → True
THEN
• neuronal_population → has_high_risk_of_pathology_accumulation → Aβ_and_tau

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Connectomic studies show that hub regions accumulate more tau and amyloid pathology.
  2. Trans-synaptic spread of tau is observed along highly connected pathways.
  3. Visual cortex regions with high interareal connectivity show earlier and more severe pathology.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: While network propagation is established, the explicit conditional on traffic and hub status is a novel formalization.

What Already Exists: Network-based propagation of tau and amyloid has been described, especially in the context of prion-like spread.

What is Novel: This law formalizes the conditional risk based on both hub status and pathology traffic, not just connectivity.

References:

  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Network vulnerability]
  • de Calignon et al. (2012) Propagation of tau pathology in a model of early Alzheimer's disease [Trans-synaptic spread]

Statement 1: Local Defense Threshold Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal_population → has_high_pathology_traffic → True, and
• neuronal_population → has_weak_local_defense → True
THEN
• neuronal_population → is_selectively_vulnerable_to → Aβ_and_tau_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neurons with low autophagy and antioxidant gene expression are more likely to degenerate when exposed to misfolded proteins.
  2. Experimental upregulation of local defense pathways (e.g., autophagy) reduces pathology in high-traffic regions.
  3. Visual cortex neurons with robust local defense mechanisms remain healthy despite high network connectivity.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The threshold model integrating network and local defense is not present in prior literature.

What Already Exists: Local defense mechanisms have been studied in isolation.

What is Novel: This law introduces a threshold model where vulnerability is conditional on both high pathology traffic and insufficient local defense.

References:

  • Yoshiyama et al. (2007) Synapse loss and microglial activation precede tangles in a P301S tauopathy mouse model [Local defense and pathology]
  • Holtzman et al. (2011) Tau pathology propagation in neurodegenerative disease [Network and local factors]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Visual cortex neurons that are network hubs but have high autophagy and antioxidant gene expression will resist pathology despite high exposure.
  2. Disruption of network connectivity will slow the spread of pathology to downstream regions.
  3. Enhancing local defense mechanisms in network hubs will reduce their vulnerability to Aβ/tau pathology.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially increasing network traffic to a resistant population will only induce vulnerability if local defense mechanisms are simultaneously impaired.
  2. Selective enhancement of local defense in non-hub neurons will not significantly alter their already low vulnerability.
  3. Blocking trans-synaptic spread in the visual cortex will prevent pathology even in populations with weak local defense.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding network hub neurons with high pathology traffic and weak local defense that remain resistant would challenge the theory.
  2. Observing rapid pathology accumulation in non-hub, low-traffic neurons with strong local defense would contradict the theory.
  3. Demonstrating that local defense enhancement does not protect high-traffic neurons would challenge the threshold law.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some non-hub neurons accumulate pathology in late-stage disease, possibly due to secondary mechanisms.

Existing Theory Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: No prior theory has formalized the interplay between network-driven propagation and local defense as a threshold for selective vulnerability.
