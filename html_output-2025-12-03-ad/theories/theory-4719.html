Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4719

Name: Integrated Network Failure Model of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the cause of Alzheimer's disease and how it can be effectively detected.

Description: Alzheimer's disease arises from the progressive breakdown of large-scale brain network connectivity, driven by the interplay of molecular pathologies (amyloid-beta, tau, neuroinflammation, vascular dysfunction) that disrupt synaptic and neuronal communication. This network disintegration precedes and predicts clinical symptoms, and can be detected by advanced neuroimaging and network-based biomarkers before overt neurodegeneration.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Molecular Pathology Drives Network Disintegration (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• brain → accumulates → amyloid-beta, tau, or inflammatory markers
THEN
• large-scale brain networks → show → reduced functional and structural connectivity

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Amyloid and tau deposition correlates with loss of functional connectivity in default mode and other networks in preclinical and prodromal AD.
  2. Neuroinflammation and vascular dysfunction are associated with network disruption in AD models and patients.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While network changes are observed, this law posits network disintegration as the central, causative process linking molecular pathology to clinical symptoms.

What Already Exists: Network dysfunction and molecular pathology are both recognized in AD, and their correlation is established.

What is Novel: The explicit causal link from molecular pathology to network-level breakdown as the primary driver of early AD is novel.

References:

  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Network vulnerability in neurodegeneration]
  • Buckner (2005) Molecular, structural, and functional characterization of Alzheimer's disease: Evidence for a relationship between default activity, amyloid, and memory [Network and amyloid link]
  • Brier (2012) Loss of intranetwork and internetwork resting state functional connections with Alzheimer's disease progression [Network connectivity loss in AD]

Statement 1: Network Biomarkers Enable Early Detection (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → shows → reduced connectivity in key brain networks (e.g., default mode, salience, executive)
THEN
• individual → is at increased risk for → future cognitive decline and Alzheimer's disease

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Resting-state fMRI and EEG/MEG studies show network connectivity loss precedes clinical symptoms and predicts conversion to AD.
  2. Network-based biomarkers outperform some traditional markers in predicting progression from mild cognitive impairment to AD.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law elevates network biomarkers to a primary role in early detection, beyond traditional molecular or structural markers.

What Already Exists: Network biomarkers are being explored for early AD detection.

What is Novel: The emphasis on network-level changes as the most sensitive and specific early detection method is novel.

References:

  • Zhou (2010) Predicting regional neurodegeneration from the healthy brain functional connectome [Network-based prediction of neurodegeneration]
  • Damoiseaux (2012) Functional connectivity tracks clinical deterioration in Alzheimer's disease [Network connectivity as biomarker]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early network connectivity loss (by fMRI/EEG) will develop cognitive symptoms before those with only molecular pathology.
  2. Interventions that restore or preserve network connectivity will delay onset of clinical AD symptoms.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Distinct patterns of network breakdown may predict different AD subtypes or progression rates.
  2. Non-invasive, portable network biomarker tools (e.g., EEG-based) could enable population-level screening for preclinical AD.

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals with network connectivity loss do not develop AD, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If AD can progress without any detectable network disintegration, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with normal network connectivity on imaging.

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This theory synthesizes molecular and network perspectives, positioning network failure as the primary, actionable event.
