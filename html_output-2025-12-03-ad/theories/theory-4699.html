Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4699

Name: Network Failure Cascade Theory of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the cause of Alzheimer's disease and how it can be effectively detected.

Description: Alzheimer's disease arises from a convergence of proteinopathy (amyloid-beta and tau), vascular dysfunction, and immune dysregulation, which together disrupt neural network homeostasis. This disruption initiates a self-amplifying cascade of synaptic failure, neuroinflammation, and neurodegeneration, leading to progressive cognitive decline. Detection is most effective by identifying early network dysfunction and multi-modal biomarker signatures.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Convergent Pathology Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• brain → exhibits → amyloid-beta accumulation, and
• brain → exhibits → tau pathology, and
• brain → exhibits → vascular dysfunction, and
• brain → exhibits → immune dysregulation
THEN
• neural network → undergoes → homeostatic failure, and
• brain → develops → progressive neurodegeneration

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Amyloid and tau pathologies are necessary but not sufficient for dementia; vascular and immune factors modulate risk and progression.
  2. Neuroimaging and biomarker studies show that combinations of amyloid, tau, vascular lesions, and neuroinflammation best predict cognitive decline.
  3. Some individuals with high amyloid and tau burden do not develop dementia unless vascular or immune dysfunction is also present.
  4. Post-mortem studies reveal that mixed pathologies are more common in Alzheimer's than pure amyloid/tau pathology.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While multi-factorial models exist, the explicit framing of Alzheimer's as a network homeostasis failure due to convergent pathologies is a novel synthesis.

What Already Exists: The amyloid cascade and tau hypotheses are well-established, and the role of vascular and immune factors is increasingly recognized.

What is Novel: This law posits that it is the convergence and interaction of these pathologies, rather than any single one, that triggers network failure and disease progression.

References:

  • Jack (2018) NIA-AA Research Framework: Toward a biological definition of Alzheimer's disease [multi-modal biomarker approach]
  • Iturria-Medina (2016) Early role of vascular dysregulation on late-onset Alzheimer's disease pathogenesis [vascular contributions]
  • Heneka (2015) Neuroinflammation in Alzheimer's disease [immune dysregulation]

Statement 1: Self-Amplifying Cascade Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neural network → experiences → homeostatic failure
THEN
• synaptic dysfunction → increases → neuroinflammation, and
• neuroinflammation → exacerbates → proteinopathy, and
• proteinopathy → worsens → network failure

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Animal models show that synaptic loss, neuroinflammation, and protein aggregation reinforce each other.
  2. Longitudinal studies reveal that once network dysfunction begins, decline accelerates even if amyloid levels plateau.
  3. Microglial activation is both a response to and a driver of synaptic and neuronal loss.
  4. Interventions that reduce neuroinflammation can slow progression even without reducing amyloid/tau.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The concept of positive feedback in AD is known, but its explicit role as the engine of progression is a novel emphasis.

What Already Exists: Feed-forward loops between neuroinflammation and proteinopathy are described in the literature.

What is Novel: This law formalizes the self-amplifying nature of the cascade and its centrality to disease progression.

References:

  • Heneka (2015) Neuroinflammation in Alzheimer's disease [feedback between inflammation and pathology]
  • Busche & Hyman (2020) Synergy between amyloid-β and tau in Alzheimer's disease [interactions between pathologies]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with high levels of amyloid, tau, vascular dysfunction, and immune activation will show earlier and more rapid cognitive decline than those with only one or two pathologies.
  2. Multi-modal biomarker panels (combining amyloid, tau, vascular, and immune markers) will outperform single-modality tests in predicting conversion from mild cognitive impairment to Alzheimer's dementia.
  3. Early detection of network dysfunction via functional MRI or EEG will precede overt neurodegeneration and cognitive symptoms.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Interventions that restore network homeostasis (e.g., neuromodulation, multi-target drugs) will halt or reverse early Alzheimer's progression, even if amyloid/tau levels remain elevated.
  2. Individuals with robust network resilience (e.g., high cognitive reserve, efficient connectivity) will not develop dementia despite high pathological burden.
  3. Targeting vascular and immune dysfunction in preclinical stages will prevent the onset of network failure and dementia.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding individuals with all four pathologies (amyloid, tau, vascular, immune) but no network dysfunction or cognitive decline would challenge the theory.
  2. Demonstrating that single-pathology cases (e.g., amyloid only) progress to dementia at the same rate as multi-pathology cases would refute the convergence hypothesis.
  3. Showing that network homeostasis can be maintained despite the presence of all pathologies would contradict the central premise.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some individuals with minimal pathology still develop dementia, suggesting other unknown factors.
