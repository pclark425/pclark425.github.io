Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2725

Name: Intrinsic Molecular Signature Theory of Neuronal Vulnerability

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia, based on the following results.

Description: This theory proposes that the selective vulnerability or resistance of neuronal populations in the visual cortex to amyloid-β and tau pathology is determined primarily by their intrinsic molecular signatures, including expression of proteostasis-related genes, synaptic and cytoskeletal remodeling programs, and innate immune signaling pathways. These cell-intrinsic factors, rather than solely regional or network context, dictate the propensity for pathological aggregation and degeneration.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Proteostasis Signature Vulnerability Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuron → has_low_expression_of → protein_degradation_and_proteostasis_genes
THEN
• neuron → is_vulnerable_to → tau_aggregation_and_neurodegeneration

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Excitatory neurons in cortex accumulate tau aggregates due to lower expression of protein degradation machinery compared to inhibitory neurons. [e10065.0] [e10065.1]
  2. RORB+ excitatory neurons are selectively vulnerable, likely due to intrinsic molecular identity. [e10079.1]
  3. V1 excitatory neurons are resistant, possibly due to more robust proteostasis programs. [e10069.0] [e10066.0]

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: Proteostasis is a known factor, but its cell-type-specific application to visual cortex vulnerability is novel.

What Already Exists: Proteostasis and protein homeostasis have been implicated in neurodegenerative disease vulnerability.

What is Novel: Explicitly linking cell-type-specific proteostasis gene expression to selective vulnerability in visual cortex neurons.

References:

  • Koren et al. (2019) A tau homeostasis signature is linked with the cellular and regional vulnerability of excitatory neurons to tau pathology [Proteostasis and vulnerability]
  • Mathys et al. (2019) Single-cell transcriptomic analysis of Alzheimer's disease [Cell-type-specific vulnerability]

Statement 1: Innate Immune Signature Vulnerability Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuron → has_high_basal_NFkB_or_innate_immune_regulon_activity → true
THEN
• neuron → is_vulnerable_to → tau_induced_transcriptional_perturbation_and_cell_loss

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Drosophila visual excitatory neurons with high basal Rel/NF-κB activity show greater tau-induced cell loss. [e10077.0]
  2. Human DLPFC neuronal subclusters (Ex8, In4) show AD-associated increases in NF-κB signaling gene expression. [e10077.4]
  3. Inhibitory neurons with low basal Rel activity are relatively resistant to tau-induced loss. [e10077.1]

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The role of innate immunity is established, but its cell-intrinsic, predictive role for vulnerability is novel.

What Already Exists: Innate immune signaling and NF-κB have been implicated in neurodegeneration, but not as a cell-intrinsic determinant of neuronal vulnerability.

What is Novel: This law proposes that intrinsic, neuron-specific innate immune activity predicts vulnerability to tau pathology.

References:

  • De Strooper & Karran (2016) The Cellular Phase of Alzheimer's Disease [Innate immunity in AD]
  • Mhatre et al. (2015) NF-κB and the regulation of neuroinflammation in Alzheimer's disease [NF-κB in AD]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Enhancing proteostasis gene expression in vulnerable excitatory neurons will reduce tau aggregation and neurodegeneration.
  2. Neurons with high basal NF-κB activity in other cortical regions will also be more vulnerable to tau-induced loss.
  3. Inhibitory neurons with upregulated innate immune pathways will become more susceptible to tau pathology.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially increasing proteostasis capacity in already resistant neurons will confer super-resistance or have no effect.
  2. Suppressing innate immune signaling in vulnerable excitatory neurons will prevent tau-induced degeneration.
  3. Single-cell transcriptomics in human visual cortex will reveal new subtypes with unique vulnerability signatures.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding that neurons with low proteostasis gene expression are resistant to tau aggregation would challenge the proteostasis law.
  2. Observing that neurons with high basal NF-κB activity are not vulnerable to tau-induced loss would challenge the innate immune law.
  3. Demonstrating that enhancing proteostasis in vulnerable neurons does not reduce aggregation would call the theory into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Regional and network-level factors (e.g., connectivity, metabolic phenotype) that modulate vulnerability beyond intrinsic molecular signatures. [e10080.0] [e10087.0] [e10074.0]
  2. Developmental and disease-stage changes in gene expression that may alter vulnerability over time. [e10077.2] [e10089.0]
