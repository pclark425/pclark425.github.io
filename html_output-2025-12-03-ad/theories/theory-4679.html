Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4679

Name: Integrated Digital-Pathobiological Detection Theory of Alzheimer's Disease (IDPD-AD): Network Disintegration Cascade Model

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the cause of Alzheimer's disease and how it can be effectively detected.

Description: This theory posits that Alzheimer's disease (AD) is fundamentally a disorder of progressive brain network disintegration, initiated by molecular pathobiological insults (amyloid, tau, neuroinflammation) and propagated through large-scale neural circuits. These network disruptions manifest as quantifiable digital behavioral changes, which can be detected through advanced digital phenotyping and computational modeling. The theory asserts that digital biomarkers are direct proxies for underlying network dysfunction, and that their integration with pathobiological data enables precise staging and subtyping of AD.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Network Disintegration-Digital Proxy Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → disrupted functional or structural brain networks (e.g., default mode, salience, executive networks) as measured by neuroimaging or electrophysiology
THEN
• individual → exhibits → specific digital behavioral anomalies (e.g., speech pauses, navigation errors, reaction time variability) corresponding to affected networks

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Functional MRI and EEG studies show network disruptions in AD correlate with cognitive and behavioral deficits.
  2. Digital behavioral changes (e.g., speech, navigation) map onto specific brain networks.
  3. Graph theory analyses reveal that network-level changes precede overt clinical symptoms in AD.
  4. Wearable and mobile device data can detect subtle behavioral changes in preclinical AD.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: While network dysfunction and digital changes are studied, their direct, law-like mapping is not established.

What Already Exists: Network dysfunction is recognized in AD, and digital behavioral changes are observed, but their direct mapping is not formalized.

What is Novel: The law that digital anomalies are direct, network-specific proxies for underlying brain network disintegration is novel.

References:

  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Network-based degeneration]
  • Brier et al. (2014) Functional connectivity and graph theory in preclinical Alzheimer's disease [Network disruption]
  • Kourtis et al. (2019) Digital biomarkers for Alzheimer's disease: the mobile/wearable devices opportunity [Digital proxies]

Statement 1: Cascade Amplification Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → early molecular pathobiological changes (e.g., amyloid, tau, neuroinflammation)
THEN
• individual → develops → progressive network disintegration, and
• individual → exhibits → increasingly severe digital behavioral anomalies over time

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Longitudinal studies show molecular pathology precedes network dysfunction, which in turn precedes clinical symptoms.
  2. Digital behavioral changes worsen with disease progression.
  3. PET imaging demonstrates amyloid and tau accumulation before network breakdown.
  4. Digital phenotyping can track progression of cognitive and functional decline.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: The explicit cascade linking molecular pathology, network disintegration, and digital anomalies is new.

What Already Exists: The temporal sequence of molecular pathology to network dysfunction to symptoms is hypothesized, but not formalized with digital proxies.

What is Novel: The law that digital anomalies amplify in parallel with network disintegration, providing a quantifiable readout of disease stage, is novel.

References:

  • Jack et al. (2013) Tracking pathophysiological processes in Alzheimer's disease: an updated hypothetical model of dynamic biomarkers [Temporal cascade]
  • Brier et al. (2014) Functional connectivity and graph theory in preclinical Alzheimer's disease [Network dysfunction]
  • Kourtis et al. (2019) Digital biomarkers for Alzheimer's disease: the mobile/wearable devices opportunity [Digital proxies]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Digital behavioral anomalies will map onto specific disrupted brain networks as measured by neuroimaging.
  2. Severity of digital anomalies will correlate with degree of network disintegration and disease stage.
  3. Interventions that restore network integrity (e.g., neuromodulation) will improve digital behavioral metrics.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Novel digital tasks targeting specific brain networks will enable subtyping of AD based on network vulnerability.
  2. Some individuals may show network disintegration and digital anomalies without classical amyloid/tau pathology, suggesting alternative disease mechanisms.
  3. AI models trained on digital and network data will predict individual response to network-targeted therapies.

Negative Experiments

  1. If digital anomalies do not correspond to specific network disruptions, the network proxy law is falsified.
  2. If network disintegration does not precede or parallel digital changes, the cascade law is challenged.
  3. If interventions that restore network function do not improve digital metrics, the theory is undermined.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Non-AD neurodegenerative diseases may cause similar network and digital changes, complicating specificity.
  2. Some digital anomalies may arise from peripheral or non-neural factors (e.g., arthritis affecting gait).
