Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4659

Name: Integrated Network Failure Hypothesis

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the cause of Alzheimer's disease and how it can be effectively detected.

Description: Alzheimer's disease arises from the breakdown of large-scale neural network homeostasis, driven by the convergence of multiple stressors (genetic, metabolic, inflammatory, and proteostatic) that disrupt synaptic connectivity and information flow. Early detection is possible by identifying network-level dysfunction through advanced neuroimaging and electrophysiological biomarkers.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Convergent Stressor-Induced Network Breakdown (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has_combination_of → genetic_risk_factors, and
• individual → has_combination_of → metabolic_dysregulation, and
• individual → has_combination_of → chronic_inflammation, and
• individual → has_combination_of → proteostatic_stress
THEN
• individual → develops_large_scale_neural_network_dysfunction → true

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Genetic risk (e.g., APOE4), metabolic syndrome, chronic inflammation, and protein misfolding are all independently associated with increased AD risk.
  2. Functional MRI and EEG studies show early network-level changes in at-risk individuals before overt pathology.
  3. Network breakdown correlates with cognitive decline better than regional atrophy alone.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While network dysfunction and multifactorial risk are known, their integration as a primary, convergent cause is new.

What Already Exists: Individual risk factors and their roles in AD are well-studied; network dysfunction is recognized as a feature.

What is Novel: The explicit convergence of multiple stressors as a necessary and sufficient cause for network breakdown is novel.

References:

  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Network focus, not convergence]
  • De Strooper & Karran (2016) The Cellular Phase of Alzheimer's Disease [Multifactorial, but not network-centric]

Statement 1: Network Dysfunction as Early Biomarker (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has_abnormal_neural_network_connectivity → detected_by_fMRI_or_EEG
THEN
• individual → is_in_preclinical_AD → true

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Resting-state fMRI and EEG detect network changes in asymptomatic individuals with AD risk.
  2. Network-level biomarkers predict cognitive decline before amyloid or tau pathology is detectable.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The primacy of network-level changes as a universal early marker is novel.

What Already Exists: Network biomarkers are being explored for early AD detection.

What is Novel: This law asserts network dysfunction as the earliest and most sensitive biomarker.

References:

  • Brier (2014) Functional connectivity and preclinical Alzheimer's disease [Network changes precede symptoms]
  • Sperling (2011) Toward defining the preclinical stages of Alzheimer's disease [Biomarker focus, not network primacy]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early network dysfunction but no amyloid/tau pathology will develop AD at higher rates.
  2. Interventions that restore network homeostasis (e.g., neuromodulation) will delay or prevent AD onset.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Network-level interventions (e.g., targeted brain stimulation) will reverse preclinical AD changes.
  2. Population-level screening with network biomarkers will outperform amyloid/tau PET in predicting AD risk.

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals with normal network connectivity but abnormal amyloid/tau develop AD at the same rate, the theory is challenged.
  2. If network dysfunction is not detectable before amyloid/tau pathology, the theory is undermined.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with no detectable network dysfunction may not fit the model.

Existing Theory Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This theory synthesizes existing concepts into a novel, integrated framework.

References:
