Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2411

Name: Activity-Dependent Vulnerability Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: Selective vulnerability of neuronal populations in the visual cortex to amyloid-β and tau pathology is governed by their patterns of synaptic activity and metabolic demand. Neurons with high baseline activity, frequent synaptic firing, and elevated metabolic rates are more susceptible to proteostatic stress and aggregation of amyloid-β and tau, while less active or more energy-efficient neurons are relatively resistant.

Statement 0: High Activity Increases Vulnerability (qualitative)

ConditionDetails

• neuronal_population → has_high_synaptic_activity → True, and
• neuronal_population → has_high_metabolic_demand → True
THEN
• neuronal_population → is_vulnerable_to → amyloid-β_and_tau_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Highly active neurons accumulate more amyloid-β due to increased APP processing.
  2. Elevated metabolic demand leads to oxidative stress, promoting tau phosphorylation and aggregation.
  3. Regions with high baseline activity (e.g., default mode network) are early sites of AD pathology.

Statement 1: Low Activity Confers Resistance (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal_population → has_low_synaptic_activity → True, and
• neuronal_population → has_low_metabolic_demand → True
THEN
• neuronal_population → is_resistant_to → amyloid-β_and_tau_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neurons with low activity show reduced amyloid-β production and less tau phosphorylation.
  2. Inhibiting neuronal activity in animal models reduces amyloid-β and tau pathology.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The mechanism is known, but its application to visual cortex selectivity is new.

What Already Exists: Reduced activity is associated with lower amyloid-β and tau pathology in experimental models.

What is Novel: The law's predictive use for mapping selective resistance in visual cortex is novel.

References:

  • Yamamoto (2015) Chronic optogenetic activation augments Aβ pathology in a mouse model of Alzheimer disease [activity and amyloid-β]
  • Busche (2012) Critical role of soluble amyloid-β for early hippocampal hyperactivity in a mouse model of Alzheimer's disease [activity and AD]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Visual cortex neurons with higher spontaneous firing rates will show greater amyloid-β and tau pathology in dementia.
  2. Pharmacological or genetic reduction of activity in vulnerable populations will reduce pathology.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially increasing activity in resistant populations may render them vulnerable to pathology.
  2. Discovery of a neuronal subtype with high activity but unique resistance mechanisms.

Negative Experiments

  1. If highly active neurons are found to be resistant to pathology, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If reducing activity does not alter vulnerability, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some low-activity neurons may still develop pathology, suggesting additional factors are involved.
