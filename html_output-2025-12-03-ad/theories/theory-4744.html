Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4744

Name: Network-Resource Allocation Theory of Selective Vulnerability

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory proposes that selective vulnerability in the visual cortex arises from the interplay between network-level resource allocation (i.e., the distribution of synaptic, metabolic, and proteostatic resources) and the demands imposed by connectivity. Neurons that are central to information flow (network hubs) and have high resource demands are more likely to experience resource exhaustion, leading to increased susceptibility to amyloid-β and tau pathology.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  2. Network analysis reveals that central nodes are more likely to accumulate pathology.

Statement 1: Peripheral Node Resistance Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal_population → is_network_peripheral → True, and
• neuronal_population → has_low_resource_demand → True
THEN
• neuronal_population → is_selectively_resistant_to → amyloid-β_and_tau_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Peripheral or less connected neurons in the visual cortex are relatively spared in early AD.
  2. Lower resource demand is associated with reduced vulnerability to neurodegeneration.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law extends network theory by incorporating resource allocation as a determinant of resistance.

What Already Exists: Relative sparing of peripheral network nodes is observed in AD.

What is Novel: The explicit connection to resource allocation and exhaustion is novel.

References:

  • de Haan et al. (2012) Disrupted modular brain dynamics reflect cognitive dysfunction in Alzheimer's disease [Network vulnerability]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Network analysis of the visual cortex will show that hub neurons accumulate pathology earlier than peripheral neurons.
  2. Interventions that enhance resource allocation (e.g., mitochondrial support, proteostasis) in hub neurons will reduce their vulnerability.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Redistributing network load (e.g., via targeted synaptic pruning or stimulation) will shift the pattern of vulnerability within the visual cortex.
  2. Artificially increasing the resource demand of peripheral neurons will increase their vulnerability to pathology.

Negative Experiments

  1. If network hubs do not show greater vulnerability than peripheral nodes, the theory is challenged.
  2. If resource enhancement in hub neurons does not reduce pathology, the theory is called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cell-intrinsic factors (e.g., genetic mutations, local microenvironment) that may modulate vulnerability independent of network position or resource demand.
