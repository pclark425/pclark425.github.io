Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2315

Name: Integrated Network Disruption Theory of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory posits that Alzheimer's disease arises from the convergence of multiple, interacting network disruptions—genetic, metabolic, vascular, and immune—leading to a self-reinforcing cycle of synaptic dysfunction, neuroinflammation, and proteinopathy. The disease is not caused by a single factor (e.g., amyloid or tau), but by the breakdown of homeostatic resilience in neural networks, with different initiating events in different individuals. Early detection is possible by identifying network-level dysfunctions through multimodal biomarkers.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Network Vulnerability Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → multiple risk factors (genetic, metabolic, vascular, immune), and
• individual → experiences → age-related decline in network resilience
THEN
• individual → is_at_increased_risk_for → network-level synaptic dysfunction and Alzheimer's disease

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Epidemiological studies show that combinations of risk factors (APOE4, diabetes, hypertension, chronic inflammation) greatly increase AD risk compared to any single factor.
  2. Network analysis of brain imaging reveals early disruptions in functional connectivity in at-risk individuals before overt pathology.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law synthesizes and extends existing multifactorial and network-based models into a unified, testable framework.

What Already Exists: Multifactorial risk models and network-based analyses are increasingly used in AD research.

What is Novel: The explicit law that the convergence of multiple risk factors and loss of network resilience is the primary driver of AD, rather than any single pathology.

References:

  • Iturria-Medina et al. (2016) Early role of vascular dysregulation on late-onset Alzheimer's disease pathogenesis [network models]
  • Sperling et al. (2011) Toward defining the preclinical stages of Alzheimer's disease [multimodal risk factors]

Statement 1: Multimodal Early Detection Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → subtle disruptions in brain network connectivity (fMRI/EEG), and
• individual → has → peripheral or central biomarker abnormalities (e.g., plasma tau, neurofilament light, inflammatory markers)
THEN
• individual → can_be_identified_as → at high risk for future Alzheimer's disease before clinical symptoms

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Longitudinal studies show that subtle changes in brain connectivity and blood-based biomarkers precede cognitive decline.
  2. Combining imaging and fluid biomarkers improves prediction of AD conversion in at-risk populations.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law extends current biomarker research by emphasizing the predictive power of network-peripheral integration.

What Already Exists: Multimodal biomarker approaches are being developed for early AD detection.

What is Novel: The law formalizes the necessity of integrating network-level and peripheral markers for optimal early detection.

References:

  • Jack et al. (2018) NIA-AA Research Framework: Toward a biological definition of Alzheimer's disease [biomarker integration]
  • Dubois et al. (2016) Preclinical Alzheimer's disease: Definition, natural history, and diagnostic criteria [multimodal detection]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with multiple risk factors and early network connectivity changes will show accelerated progression to AD compared to those with only one risk factor.
  2. Combining network imaging and blood-based biomarkers will outperform either modality alone in predicting AD onset.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Interventions that restore network resilience (e.g., neuromodulation, anti-inflammatory agents) in at-risk individuals will prevent or delay AD onset.
  2. Some individuals with high-risk profiles may remain cognitively normal if compensatory network mechanisms are robust.

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals with multiple risk factors and network disruptions do not develop AD at higher rates, the theory is challenged.
  2. If multimodal biomarker integration does not improve early detection accuracy, the theory is weakened.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with no identifiable risk factors or network disruptions prior to symptom onset.
