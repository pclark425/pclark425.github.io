Name: Network Disintegration Hypothesis of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the cause of Alzheimer's disease and how it can be effectively detected.

Description: Alzheimer's disease is fundamentally a disorder of progressive large-scale brain network disintegration, where molecular pathologies (amyloid, tau, inflammation) are secondary to, or amplified by, the breakdown of functional and structural connectivity. Early detection is possible by identifying network-level disruptions before overt molecular or cognitive symptoms.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Network Vulnerability Precedes Molecular Pathology (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has_brain_network → highly_integrated, and
• individual → has_no_overt_amyloid_tau → true
THEN
• individual → has_low_risk → Alzheimer's disease

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Longitudinal fMRI and DTI studies show network disruption (e.g., default mode network) precedes amyloid/tau accumulation and cognitive decline.
  2. Individuals with preserved network integrity show resilience to AD pathology.
  3. Network hubs are selectively vulnerable in early AD, and their disruption predicts later molecular pathology.
  4. Some individuals with high amyloid/tau burden but intact networks remain cognitively normal.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While network changes are known, the primacy of network disintegration as the root cause is a novel framing.

What Already Exists: Network dysfunction is observed in AD, and some studies suggest early network changes.

What is Novel: This law posits network breakdown as a primary, causative event, not just a consequence, and as a superior early biomarker.

References:

  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Describes network targeting, not causality]
  • Brier (2014) Functional connectivity and AD progression [Shows network changes, not as primary cause]
  • Jones et al. (2016) Cascading network failure across the Alzheimer's disease spectrum [Network failure, not primary cause]

Statement 1: Network Biomarkers Enable Preclinical Detection (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has_network_disruption → measurable_by_fMRI_or_EEG
THEN
• individual → has_high_risk → future_Alzheimer's_disease, and
• network_biomarker → is_superior_to → amyloid_PET_in_preclinical_detection

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Resting-state fMRI and EEG studies show network changes in at-risk individuals before amyloid PET positivity.
  2. Network connectivity measures predict conversion from mild cognitive impairment to AD better than amyloid PET in some cohorts.
  3. EEG-based network metrics can distinguish preclinical AD from controls with high sensitivity.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The superiority claim is novel; most work treats network and molecular biomarkers as complementary.

What Already Exists: Network biomarkers are being explored for early detection.

What is Novel: This law asserts network biomarkers are superior to molecular imaging for preclinical detection.

References:

  • Sperling (2011) Toward defining the preclinical stages of Alzheimer's disease [Discusses biomarkers, not superiority]
  • Zhou (2010) Predicting AD with network measures [Shows predictive value, not superiority]
  • Dauwels et al. (2010) EEG network analysis for early AD detection [EEG network metrics, not superiority]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early network disruption (measured by fMRI/EEG) but no amyloid/tau pathology will develop AD at higher rates than those with normal networks.
  2. Interventions that restore or preserve network connectivity will delay or prevent onset of AD symptoms, even if amyloid/tau are present.
  3. Network-based digital biomarkers will outperform fluid biomarkers in predicting AD conversion in longitudinal studies.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Non-invasive brain stimulation targeting network hubs will reverse or halt early AD progression.
  2. Network-based digital biomarkers (e.g., from wearable EEG) will outperform all current fluid or imaging biomarkers in predicting AD conversion.
  3. Network disintegration patterns will predict specific cognitive symptom profiles before clinical onset.

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals with normal network connectivity but high amyloid/tau burden develop AD at the same rate as those with network disruption, the theory is challenged.
  2. If network disruption is not detectable before molecular pathology, the theory is undermined.
  3. If interventions that restore network connectivity do not alter AD risk or progression, the theory is weakened.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of familial AD with early, aggressive molecular pathology may not fit the network-first model.
  2. Some individuals with network disruption do not progress to AD, suggesting additional factors are involved.
