Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4736

Name: Network Connectivity and Propagation Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory proposes that the selective vulnerability or resistance of visual cortex neuronal populations to Aβ and tau pathology is governed by their position and connectivity within large-scale cortical networks. Highly interconnected hub neurons or populations with extensive long-range connections are more susceptible to the spread and accumulation of toxic protein aggregates, while sparsely connected or peripherally located populations are more resistant.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Highly connected hub regions in the cortex are early sites of Aβ and tau accumulation in Alzheimer's disease.
  2. Network analysis shows that hub neurons are more likely to propagate misfolded proteins.

Statement 1: Peripheral Connectivity Resistance Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal_population → has_sparse_long_range_connections → True
THEN
• neuronal_population → is_resistant_to → Aβ_and_tau_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neuronal populations with fewer long-range connections are less likely to accumulate Aβ/tau pathology.
  2. Peripheral or isolated regions show delayed or reduced pathology in AD models.

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

What Already Exists: Peripheral resistance is observed but not formalized as a law.

What is Novel: The law formalizes sparse connectivity as a determinant of resistance in visual cortex populations.

References:

  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Network hub vulnerability]
  • de Haan et al. (2012) Disrupted modular brain dynamics reflect cognitive dysfunction in Alzheimer's disease [Network connectivity and AD]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Artificially increasing the connectivity of resistant populations will increase their vulnerability to Aβ/tau pathology.
  2. Disrupting hub connectivity in vulnerable populations will reduce their pathology burden.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Altering network topology to reduce hubness will confer resistance even in genetically susceptible neurons.
  2. Introducing artificial long-range connections to resistant populations will render them vulnerable.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding hub neurons that remain resistant would challenge the theory.
  2. Observing peripheral neurons with high pathology would contradict the theory.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some sparsely connected neurons may still become vulnerable due to local microenvironmental factors.
