Name: Proteostatic-Immunometabolic Cascade Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the cause of Alzheimer's disease and how it can be effectively detected.

Description: Alzheimer's disease is caused by a cascade beginning with proteostasis failure (impaired clearance of misfolded proteins), which triggers chronic immune activation and metabolic dysfunction, creating a self-reinforcing loop. Effective detection requires multi-modal biomarkers capturing proteostatic, immune, and metabolic changes systemically.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Proteostasis Failure Initiates Cascade (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has_impaired_proteostasis → true
THEN
• individual → develops_misfolded_proteins → amyloid_and_tau, and
• individual → activates_chronic_inflammation → true

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Genetic and experimental models show impaired proteostasis leads to amyloid/tau accumulation and microglial activation.
  2. Proteostasis impairment (e.g., via mutations in APP, PSEN1/2, or tau) precedes amyloid and tau pathology in familial and sporadic AD.
  3. Microglial activation and neuroinflammation are observed following amyloid/tau accumulation in both human and animal models.
  4. Proteostasis regulators (e.g., autophagy, ubiquitin-proteasome system) are dysfunctional in AD brains.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The cascade model is more integrated and sequential than most current models, which often treat these as parallel or loosely connected processes.

What Already Exists: Proteostasis failure and inflammation are both implicated in AD.

What is Novel: This law asserts a strict causal sequence: proteostasis failure → misfolded proteins → immune activation.

References:

  • Selkoe (2002) Alzheimer's disease is a synaptic failure [Proteostasis focus]
  • Heneka (2015) Neuroinflammation in Alzheimer's disease [Immune activation]
  • De Strooper & Karran (2016) The Cellular Phase of Alzheimer's Disease [Integration of cellular processes]

Statement 1: Multi-modal Systemic Biomarkers Enable Early Detection (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has_biomarker_changes → proteostatic_immune_metabolic
THEN
• individual → is_in_preclinical_AD → true

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Blood and CSF studies show early changes in proteostasis (e.g., neurofilament light), immune (e.g., cytokines), and metabolic (e.g., glucose) markers in preclinical AD.
  2. Single-modality biomarkers (e.g., amyloid PET, CSF tau) have limited sensitivity/specificity for preclinical AD.
  3. Multi-modal biomarker panels (combining proteostatic, immune, and metabolic markers) improve prediction of AD conversion in longitudinal studies.
  4. Systemic metabolic dysfunction (e.g., insulin resistance) is detectable before cognitive symptoms in AD-prone individuals.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The multi-modal, systemic approach is more comprehensive than most current single-modality biomarker strategies.

What Already Exists: Individual biomarker classes are studied for AD detection.

What is Novel: This law posits that only a multi-modal, systemic biomarker panel can reliably detect preclinical AD.

References:

  • Jack (2018) NIA-AA Research Framework: Toward a biological definition of Alzheimer's disease [Biomarker framework]
  • O'Bryant (2017) Blood-based biomarkers in Alzheimer's disease: Current state of the science [Blood biomarkers]
  • Hampel (2018) Blood-based biomarkers for Alzheimer disease: mapping the road to the clinic [Multi-modal biomarker discussion]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early, subtle changes in proteostatic, immune, and metabolic blood markers will progress to AD at higher rates than those with only one class of marker abnormality.
  2. Therapies that restore proteostasis or dampen chronic immune activation will slow or halt AD progression, even if amyloid/tau are present.
  3. Longitudinal monitoring of multi-modal biomarkers will predict cognitive decline more accurately than single-modality approaches.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. A composite blood test combining proteostatic, immune, and metabolic markers will outperform amyloid PET and CSF tau in predicting AD conversion.
  2. Systemic metabolic interventions (e.g., insulin sensitizers) will prevent AD in high-risk individuals with early biomarker changes.
  3. Restoring proteostasis alone (without targeting amyloid/tau directly) will be sufficient to prevent or reverse early AD pathology.

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals with multi-modal biomarker changes do not develop AD at higher rates, the theory is challenged.
  2. If proteostasis failure does not precede immune/metabolic changes, the cascade model is undermined.
  3. If therapies targeting only one component (e.g., immune or metabolic) are as effective as multi-target approaches, the integrated cascade is questioned.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some cases of AD with minimal systemic biomarker changes may not fit the model.
  2. Late-onset AD cases with atypical biomarker profiles (e.g., normal amyloid/tau but cognitive decline) are not fully explained.
