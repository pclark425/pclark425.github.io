Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4836

Name: Activity-Dependent Delayed Propagation and Synaptic Seeding Theory (General Formulation)

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory proposes that the resistance of V1 neuronal populations to amyloid-β (Aβ) and tau pathology is governed by the interplay between (1) activity-dependent delayed propagation—where the unique sensory-driven, low baseline activity of V1 reduces opportunities for trans-synaptic spread of aggregates—and (2) a lack of synaptic seeding events due to the absence of activity-induced molecular changes (e.g., phosphorylation, local protein synthesis) that facilitate aggregation. Thus, V1's functional specialization for stimulus-driven, low spontaneous activity creates both a physical and molecular barrier to the initiation and spread of neurodegenerative proteinopathies.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Activity-Dependent Propagation Delay (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal population → is_in → V1, and
• V1 → has_activity_pattern → low spontaneous, high stimulus-driven
THEN
• trans-synaptic spread of Aβ/tau → is_reduced → in V1

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Trans-synaptic spread of tau and Aβ is enhanced by high neuronal activity; V1 has low spontaneous activity compared to association cortex.
  2. Sensory deprivation further reduces pathology in primary sensory cortices in animal models.
  3. V1 is among the last cortical regions to show Aβ and tau pathology in Alzheimer's disease, correlating with its low baseline activity.
  4. Increased neuronal activity in other cortical regions accelerates Aβ and tau pathology.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law extends known activity-dependence to a new anatomical and functional context, specifically V1's resistance.

What Already Exists: Activity-dependent propagation of protein aggregates is established in animal models.

What is Novel: Application to V1's unique activity profile as a resistance mechanism is novel.

References:

  • Wu (2016) Neuronal activity enhances tau propagation and pathology in vivo [activity-dependence]

Statement 1: Activity-Dependent Synaptic Seeding Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal population → is_in → V1, and
• V1 synapses → lack → activity-induced molecular changes (e.g., phosphorylation, local protein synthesis)
THEN
• Aβ and tau aggregation → is_inhibited → in V1

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Activity-induced phosphorylation and local protein synthesis are required for efficient tau and Aβ aggregation in vitro and in vivo.
  2. V1 shows lower levels of activity-induced molecular changes compared to association cortex.
  3. Synaptic activity triggers tau release and aggregation in other cortical regions.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law applies known molecular mechanisms to a new anatomical context (V1).

What Already Exists: Activity-induced molecular changes are known to facilitate aggregation.

What is Novel: The explicit link to V1's activity profile and resistance is novel.

References:

  • Wu (2016) Neuronal activity enhances tau propagation and pathology in vivo [activity-dependence]
  • Pooler (2013) Physiological release of endogenous tau is stimulated by neuronal activity [activity and tau release]
  • Braak & Braak (1991) Neuropathological stageing of Alzheimer-related changes [regional progression of pathology]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Increasing spontaneous activity in V1 (e.g., via chemogenetic activation) will increase susceptibility to Aβ/tau pathology.
  2. Sensory deprivation (e.g., dark rearing) will further reduce V1 vulnerability to pathology.
  3. Inducing activity-dependent molecular changes in V1 synapses will increase local aggregation of Aβ/tau.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If V1 is chronically activated in the absence of sensory input, will this override resistance and permit pathology propagation?
  2. If a new class of activity-independent seeding factors is introduced to V1, will resistance persist?
  3. If V1 is transplanted into a high-activity association cortex environment, will it acquire vulnerability?

Negative Experiments

  1. If V1 neurons develop Aβ/tau pathology at the same rate as association cortex under matched activity conditions, the theory is challenged.
  2. If activity-induced molecular changes are found to be equally present in V1 and association cortex, the theory is called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases where V1 is affected despite low activity (e.g., in certain genetic models) are not fully explained.
