Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4666

Name: Network Disintegration Threshold Model

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the cause of Alzheimer's disease and how it can be effectively detected.

Description: This general theory proposes that Alzheimer's disease is initiated and progresses when the cumulative burden of multiple pathological cascades (amyloid, tau, vascular, metabolic, immune) causes the brain's functional and structural networks to cross a critical threshold of disintegration. This threshold is modulated by genetic, environmental, and resilience factors. Detection is most effective by identifying early network-level changes, which precede overt clinical symptoms.

Statement 0: Network Collapse Threshold Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails

• individual → has_cumulative_pathological_burden → above_threshold
THEN

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Functional MRI and connectomics studies show that network breakdown correlates with cognitive decline and precedes clinical AD.
  2. Network resilience (e.g., cognitive reserve) delays symptom onset despite pathology.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law synthesizes network neuroscience and multifactorial pathology into a threshold-based, testable model.

What Already Exists: Network dysfunction is recognized in AD, but not as a threshold phenomenon integrating multiple cascades.

What is Novel: This law formalizes a threshold model where network collapse is the proximate cause of clinical AD, integrating multifactorial pathology.

References:

  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network vulnerability]

ConditionDetails

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Resting-state fMRI and EEG studies detect network changes in preclinical AD.
  2. Network biomarkers predict conversion from mild cognitive impairment to AD.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law elevates network biomarkers to a central role in early detection, beyond traditional molecular markers.

What Already Exists: Network biomarkers are under investigation, but not as primary early detection tools.

What is Novel: This law asserts that network-level changes are the earliest and most sensitive indicators of AD risk.

References:

  • Brier et al. (2014) Functional connectivity and graph theory in preclinical Alzheimer's disease [network biomarkers]
  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network vulnerability]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with high pathological burden but strong network resilience will show delayed symptom onset.
  2. Network-level imaging will detect AD risk before molecular biomarkers become abnormal.
  3. Interventions that enhance network resilience will delay or prevent AD symptoms even in the presence of pathology.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially enhancing network connectivity may reverse early cognitive symptoms.
  2. Network disintegration patterns will define distinct AD subtypes with different progression rates.
  3. Non-invasive brain stimulation targeting network hubs may prevent network collapse.

Negative Experiments

  1. If network disintegration does not precede or correlate with symptom onset, the model is challenged.
  2. If individuals with network collapse do not develop AD symptoms, the theory is weakened.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with minimal network disruption on imaging.
