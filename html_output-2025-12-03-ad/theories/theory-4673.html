Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Network Vulnerability Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has_accumulation → amyloid-beta, and
• individual → has_accumulation → tau, and
• individual → has_elevated → neuroinflammation
THEN
• individual → has_disruption → large-scale brain network connectivity, and
• individual → has_increased_risk → Alzheimer's clinical symptoms

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Amyloid and tau pathology co-localize in vulnerable brain networks (e.g., default mode network).
  2. Neuroinflammation is observed in regions of early network disruption.
  3. Functional and structural connectivity loss precedes cognitive symptoms.
  4. PET and MRI studies show network-level changes before atrophy.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law synthesizes known pathologies into a network-centric, predictive model, extending beyond traditional amyloid/tau-centric views.

What Already Exists: Amyloid and tau pathology, and their association with network disruption, are established.

What is Novel: The explicit integration of neuroinflammation and the predictive, network-based framework for early detection.

References:

  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network vulnerability]
  • Buckner (2005) Molecular, structural, and functional characterization of Alzheimer's disease: Evidence for a relationship between default activity, amyloid, and memory [default mode network involvement]
  • Heneka (2015) Neuroinflammation in Alzheimer's disease [role of inflammation]

Statement 1: Network Biomarker Detection Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has_disruption → large-scale brain network connectivity
THEN
• individual → can_be_detected_by → multimodal imaging (fMRI, PET, EEG), and
• individual → can_be_detected_by → network-based biomarkers (e.g., graph metrics)

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Resting-state fMRI and PET detect early network changes in preclinical AD.
  2. Graph theoretical metrics (e.g., hubness, modularity) are altered in AD.
  3. EEG network analysis reveals early functional disconnection.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law elevates network-level biomarkers to a central, predictive role, integrating multiple modalities.

What Already Exists: Imaging and network analysis are used for AD detection.

What is Novel: The law formalizes network disruption as a primary, integrative biomarker for early detection.

References:

  • Brier (2012) Loss of intranetwork and internetwork resting state functional connections with Alzheimer's disease progression [network loss precedes symptoms]
  • Stam (2007) Graph theoretical analysis of complex networks in the brain [network metrics in AD]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early network disruption but no cognitive symptoms will develop AD symptoms within a predictable timeframe.
  2. Network-based biomarkers will outperform single-modality biomarkers in early AD detection.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Interventions targeting network integrity (e.g., neuromodulation) will delay or prevent symptom onset.
  2. Individuals with high network resilience will remain asymptomatic despite amyloid/tau accumulation.

Negative Experiments

  1. If network disruption does not precede symptoms, the theory is challenged.
  2. If network-based biomarkers do not improve early detection, the theory is called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with minimal network disruption but significant cognitive decline.
  2. Non-AD dementias with similar network changes.
