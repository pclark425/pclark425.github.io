Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2393

Name: Molecular Signature-Driven Vulnerability Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory asserts that the selective vulnerability or resistance of visual cortical neuronal populations to amyloid-β and tau pathology is determined by their unique molecular expression profiles, including cell-surface receptors, proteostasis machinery, and synaptic protein composition. These molecular signatures dictate the propensity for aggregate uptake, seeding, and propagation.

THEN

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neurons expressing high levels of LRP1, heparan sulfate proteoglycans, or PrPC show increased amyloid-β and tau uptake.
  2. Blocking these receptors reduces aggregate internalization and pathology.

Statement 1: Proteostasis Capacity and Aggregate Resistance (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal_population → has_high_expression_of → proteostasis_machinery
THEN
• neuronal_population → is_resistant_to → amyloid-β_and_tau_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neurons with robust autophagy and proteasome activity clear aggregates more efficiently.
  2. Enhancing proteostasis reduces pathology in animal models.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: Known mechanisms are applied to a new, regionally specific context.

What Already Exists: Proteostasis mechanisms are known to modulate aggregate accumulation.

What is Novel: Directly linking proteostasis capacity to selective resistance in visual cortex populations.

References:

  • Ciechanover & Kwon (2015) Proteostasis and the ubiquitin-proteasome system in neurodegeneration [Proteostasis and aggregates]
  • Nixon (2013) The role of autophagy in neurodegenerative disease [Autophagy and resistance]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Overexpressing aggregate-binding receptors in resistant populations will increase their vulnerability to pathology.
  2. Enhancing proteostasis machinery in vulnerable populations will confer resistance to amyloid-β and tau aggregation.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Altering synaptic protein composition in resistant populations may render them vulnerable to aggregate seeding.
  2. Targeted knockdown of specific proteostasis genes in resistant populations will increase pathology, but may have off-target effects.

Negative Experiments

  1. If blocking aggregate-binding receptors does not reduce pathology in vulnerable populations, the theory is challenged.
  2. If enhancing proteostasis does not confer resistance, the theory is called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some populations with low receptor expression or high proteostasis still develop pathology, suggesting additional factors are involved.
