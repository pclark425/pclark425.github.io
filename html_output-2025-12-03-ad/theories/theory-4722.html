Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4722

Name: Proteostatic Collapse and Cellular Senescence Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the cause of Alzheimer's disease and how it can be effectively detected.

Description: Alzheimer's disease is driven by a collapse in proteostasis (the cellular machinery for protein folding, degradation, and clearance), which triggers cellular senescence and a pro-inflammatory environment. Early detection is possible by identifying proteostatic stress markers and senescence-associated secretory phenotype (SASP) factors in peripheral fluids and CNS.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Proteostatic Collapse Triggers Senescence (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → impaired proteostasis (e.g., reduced autophagy, proteasome dysfunction, chaperone deficiency)
THEN
• neurons and glia → undergo → cellular senescence, and
• senescent cells → secrete → SASP factors (e.g., IL-1β, IL-6, MMPs)

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Proteostasis impairment is observed in AD brains, with accumulation of misfolded proteins and reduced autophagy.
  2. Senescent cells and SASP factors are increased in AD and promote neuroinflammation.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law integrates proteostasis and senescence into a unified causal pathway.

What Already Exists: Proteostasis impairment and cellular senescence are individually implicated in AD.

What is Novel: The direct causal link from proteostatic collapse to senescence and SASP-driven neuroinflammation is novel.

References:

  • Hipp (2019) The proteostasis network and its decline in ageing [Proteostasis in neurodegeneration]
  • Bussian (2018) Clearance of senescent glial cells prevents tau-dependent pathology and cognitive decline [Senescence in AD]
  • Musiek (2015) Mechanisms linking circadian clocks, sleep, and neurodegeneration [Proteostasis and neuroinflammation]

Statement 1: Proteostatic and Senescence Biomarkers Enable Early Detection (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → elevated proteostatic stress markers (e.g., ubiquitinated proteins, p62, LC3-II), and
• individual → has → increased SASP factors in CSF or plasma
THEN
• individual → is at increased risk for → Alzheimer's disease onset or progression

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Proteostatic stress and SASP factors are detectable in peripheral fluids and correlate with AD risk and progression.
  2. Early increases in these markers precede clinical symptoms in at-risk populations.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law proposes a new detection strategy based on combined proteostatic and senescence biomarkers.

What Already Exists: Individual proteostatic and senescence markers are studied in AD.

What is Novel: The combined use of these markers for early detection is novel.

References:

  • Bussian (2018) Clearance of senescent glial cells prevents tau-dependent pathology and cognitive decline [Senescence markers in AD]
  • Hipp (2019) The proteostasis network and its decline in ageing [Proteostatic markers in neurodegeneration]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with elevated proteostatic stress and SASP markers will show accelerated cognitive decline and AD pathology.
  2. Senolytic or proteostasis-enhancing therapies will reduce SASP factors and slow AD progression.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Peripheral detection of proteostatic and senescence markers will allow preclinical AD diagnosis years before symptom onset.
  2. Targeted removal of senescent cells in the brain will reverse established AD pathology.

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals with high proteostatic and senescence markers do not develop AD, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If AD can develop in the absence of proteostatic collapse or cellular senescence, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with no evidence of proteostatic impairment or cellular senescence.
