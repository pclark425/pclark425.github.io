Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4793

Name: Mitochondrial-Network Coupling Threshold Theory

Type: specific

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory posits that there exists a critical threshold of mitochondrial function relative to network connectivity, beyond which neurons in the visual cortex become selectively vulnerable to amyloid-β and tau pathology. Neurons with high connectivity but sub-threshold mitochondrial function cannot meet energy demands, leading to proteostatic failure and aggregation, while those above the threshold remain resistant. The theory integrates quantitative relationships between connectivity and metabolic capacity, and predicts that the ratio of connectivity to mitochondrial function (C/M) determines vulnerability.

Statement 0: Mitochondrial-Network Threshold Law (quantitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal_population → has_network_connectivity → C, and
• neuronal_population → has_mitochondrial_function → M, and
• C/M → greater_than → critical_threshold_T
THEN
• neuronal_population → is_vulnerable_to → amyloid_and_tau_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neurons with high connectivity and insufficient mitochondrial function show early signs of energy failure and protein aggregation in AD models.
  2. Experimental reduction of mitochondrial function in highly connected neurons increases vulnerability to pathology.
  3. Cortical hub regions with high connectivity are disproportionately affected in Alzheimer's disease, and these regions show early mitochondrial dysfunction.
  4. Proteostatic failure and aggregation of amyloid-β and tau are linked to energetic deficits in neurons.

What is Novel: The quantitative threshold model integrating both factors and predicting a critical C/M ratio is novel.

Statement 1: Threshold-Exceeding Resistance Law (quantitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal_population → has_network_connectivity → C, and
• neuronal_population → has_mitochondrial_function → M, and
• C/M → less_than_or_equal_to → critical_threshold_T
THEN
• neuronal_population → is_resistant_to → amyloid_and_tau_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neurons with high mitochondrial function relative to connectivity are resistant to pathology, even in high-activity regions.
  2. Experimental enhancement of mitochondrial function in vulnerable neurons confers resistance to amyloid-β and tau aggregation.
  3. Some visual cortex neurons with high connectivity but robust mitochondrial capacity remain unaffected in early AD.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: The law provides a new, quantitative mechanism for resistance, not previously formalized.

What Already Exists: Mitochondrial resilience is known to confer resistance.

What is Novel: The explicit threshold model relating connectivity and mitochondrial function is new.

References:

  • Mattson (2004) Pathways towards and away from Alzheimer's disease [Mitochondrial dysfunction]
  • Buckner et al. (2009) Cortical hubs revealed by intrinsic functional connectivity: mapping, assessment of stability, and relation to Alzheimer's disease [Network hub vulnerability]
  • Demetrius & Simon (2012) An energetics theory of aging and Alzheimer's disease [Energetic stress and neurodegeneration]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Pharmacological enhancement of mitochondrial function in highly connected neurons will shift the C/M ratio below the threshold and confer resistance to pathology.
  2. Artificially increasing connectivity in neurons with marginal mitochondrial function will push them above the threshold and increase vulnerability.
  3. Regions of the visual cortex with similar connectivity but different mitochondrial function will show differential vulnerability to amyloid-β and tau pathology.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. There may be subpopulations of neurons with adaptive mechanisms (e.g., upregulated glycolysis or alternative energy pathways) that allow them to tolerate C/M ratios above the threshold without pathology.
  2. Chronic mild mitochondrial stress may precondition neurons to resist pathology even at high C/M ratios.
  3. Interventions that modulate synaptic activity without altering mitochondrial function may have unexpected effects on vulnerability.

Negative Experiments

  1. If neurons with C/M ratios above the threshold are not more vulnerable, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If neurons with C/M ratios below the threshold are still highly vulnerable, the theory would be called into question.
  3. If manipulation of mitochondrial function or connectivity does not alter vulnerability as predicted, the theory would be falsified.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The role of non-mitochondrial energy sources (e.g., glycolysis, astrocyte-neuron lactate shuttle) is not considered.
  2. Genetic factors or local microenvironmental influences (e.g., inflammation, vascular supply) may modulate vulnerability independently of C/M ratio.
