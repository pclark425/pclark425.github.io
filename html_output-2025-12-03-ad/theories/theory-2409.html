Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2409

Name: Connectivity-Driven Propagation Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory proposes that the selective vulnerability or resistance of visual cortex neuronal populations to amyloid-β and tau pathology is determined by their connectivity patterns—specifically, their synaptic input/output relationships with upstream and downstream regions already affected by pathology. Neurons with strong connections to early-affected regions are more likely to accumulate pathology via trans-synaptic spread, while those with isolated or local circuits are more resistant.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Trans-synaptic Spread of Pathology (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuron_population → receives_strong_input_from → region_with_amyloid-β_or_tau_pathology
THEN
• neuron_population → is_likely_to_accumulate → amyloid-β_and_tau_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Tau pathology spreads along synaptically connected networks in animal models and human brains.
  2. Visual cortex regions with strong input from affected association cortices show earlier pathology.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The general mechanism is known, but its application to visual cortex selective vulnerability is new.

What Already Exists: Trans-synaptic spread of tau and amyloid-β is established in other brain regions.

What is Novel: Application to explain selective vulnerability in visual cortex based on specific connectivity patterns is novel.

References:

  • de Calignon (2012) Propagation of tau pathology in a model of early Alzheimer's disease [Trans-synaptic spread]
  • Braak & Braak (1991) Neuropathological stageing of Alzheimer-related changes [Connectivity and spread]

Statement 1: Local Circuitry Confers Resistance (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuron_population → is_part_of → local_circuit_with_minimal_long-range_input
THEN
• neuron_population → is_resistant_to → amyloid-β_and_tau_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Layer 4C neurons in V1, which receive thalamic but not association cortex input, are relatively spared in Alzheimer's disease.
  2. Neurons in isolated microcircuits show delayed or absent pathology in transgenic models.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The general idea is present, but its systematic application to visual cortex is new.

What Already Exists: Local circuit resistance is observed in some brain regions, but not systematically explained.

What is Novel: The explicit link between local connectivity and resistance in visual cortex is novel.

References:

  • Hof (1990) Selective vulnerability of certain neurons in Alzheimer's disease [Layer-specific vulnerability]
  • de Calignon (2012) Propagation of tau pathology in a model of early Alzheimer's disease [Trans-synaptic spread]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Visual cortex neurons with strong association cortex input will show earlier and more severe pathology than those with only thalamic input.
  2. Disrupting long-range synaptic connections to visual cortex in animal models will reduce pathology spread.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Discovery of a visual cortex neuron population with extensive long-range input but resistance to pathology would challenge the theory.
  2. Artificially increasing connectivity from affected regions to resistant neurons will increase their vulnerability.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding that neurons with only local input nonetheless accumulate pathology would challenge the theory.
  2. Demonstrating that blocking synaptic input does not alter pathology spread would call the theory into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some neurons with minimal long-range input may still develop pathology, suggesting other factors are involved.

Existing Theory Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This theory extends known mechanisms to a new context, providing a unifying explanation for visual cortex selective vulnerability.

References:

  • de Calignon (2012) Propagation of tau pathology in a model of early Alzheimer's disease [Trans-synaptic spread]
  • Braak & Braak (1991) Neuropathological stageing of Alzheimer-related changes [Connectivity and spread]
