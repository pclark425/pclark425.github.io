Name: Network Disintegration and Systemic Inflammation Theory of Alzheimer's Causation

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory posits that Alzheimer's disease arises from the convergence of brain network disintegration (due to synaptic loss and connectivity breakdown) and chronic systemic inflammation, which together drive neurodegeneration. The interplay between peripheral immune activation and central neural network vulnerability explains both the heterogeneity of clinical presentation and the variable progression of AD.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Network Vulnerability Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → genetic or acquired risk factors for synaptic dysfunction, and
• individual → experiences → chronic systemic inflammation
THEN
• brain networks → undergo → accelerated disintegration (loss of connectivity, synaptic loss), and
• individual → develops → Alzheimer's disease pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Synaptic loss and network disintegration precede overt neurodegeneration in AD.
  2. Chronic systemic inflammation (e.g., elevated CRP, cytokines) is associated with increased AD risk and faster progression.
  3. APOE4 carriers show greater vulnerability to network breakdown under inflammatory stress.
  4. Neuroimaging shows early loss of functional connectivity in AD-prone networks.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While both mechanisms are studied, their convergence as a primary cause is not standard.

What Already Exists: Synaptic loss and inflammation are each implicated in AD.

What is Novel: The explicit interaction between peripheral inflammation and network vulnerability as a causal driver is novel.

References:

  • Henstridge (2019) Synaptic pathology: A shared mechanism in neurological disease [synaptic loss in AD]
  • Holmes (2009) Systemic inflammation and disease progression in Alzheimer disease [inflammation and AD progression]
  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network disintegration]

Statement 1: Peripheral-Central Feedback Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → chronic peripheral immune activation
THEN
• blood-brain barrier → becomes → more permeable, and
• central nervous system → experiences → increased neuroinflammation and amyloid/tau pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Peripheral inflammation increases blood-brain barrier permeability.
  2. Systemic immune activation leads to microglial priming and increased amyloid/tau deposition.
  3. Epidemiological studies link chronic inflammatory diseases to higher AD risk.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law synthesizes known mechanisms into a feedback model not yet standard.

What Already Exists: Peripheral inflammation and blood-brain barrier dysfunction are each studied in AD.

What is Novel: The feedback loop between peripheral immune activation and central pathology is not widely formalized.

References:

  • Perry (2007) The impact of systemic infection on the progression of neurodegenerative disease [peripheral-central immune interaction]
  • Heneka (2015) Neuroinflammation in Alzheimer's disease [neuroinflammation and AD]
  • Holmes (2009) Systemic inflammation and disease progression in Alzheimer disease [systemic inflammation and AD]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with chronic inflammatory conditions will show earlier and more rapid network disintegration on neuroimaging.
  2. Anti-inflammatory interventions will slow network breakdown and delay AD onset in at-risk individuals.
  3. Blood-brain barrier permeability markers will correlate with both peripheral inflammation and central AD pathology.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Targeting peripheral immune activation alone may halt or reverse early network disintegration in preclinical AD.
  2. Distinct patterns of network breakdown will emerge depending on the type of systemic inflammation (e.g., metabolic vs. autoimmune).

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals with high systemic inflammation do not show increased AD risk or network breakdown, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If anti-inflammatory treatments do not affect network integrity or AD progression, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD without evidence of systemic inflammation or network disintegration.
