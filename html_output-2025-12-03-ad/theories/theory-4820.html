Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-4820

Name: Intrinsic Molecular Signature Theory of Neuronal Vulnerability (General Formulation 1: Core Molecular Signature)

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory posits that each neuronal population in the visual cortex possesses a unique, stable 'intrinsic molecular signature'—a composite of gene expression, proteomic, and epigenetic features—that determines its baseline susceptibility or resistance to amyloid-β (Aβ) plaques and tau tangles. These signatures encode the cell-autonomous properties that modulate vulnerability, independent of network context.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Intrinsic Signature Determines Baseline Vulnerability (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal_population → has_intrinsic_molecular_signature → signature_X, and
• Aβ_or_tau → is_present_in → local_microenvironment
THEN
• neuronal_population → exhibits_pathological_response → response_Y (determined by signature_X)

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Single-cell transcriptomics reveal distinct gene expression profiles in vulnerable vs. resistant neurons in the visual cortex.
  2. Proteomic studies show that neurons with high expression of synaptic vesicle proteins or calcium-binding proteins are more resistant to Aβ/tau pathology.
  3. Epigenetic marks (e.g., DNA methylation, histone modifications) correlate with neuronal subtype-specific vulnerability.
  4. Cell-autonomous manipulation of stress response genes alters vulnerability in vitro.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While prior work has identified correlates, this law formalizes the concept of a composite, predictive signature as a causal determinant.

What Already Exists: Cell-type-specific gene expression and proteomic signatures are known to correlate with vulnerability in neurodegeneration.

What is Novel: The formalization of a stable, composite 'intrinsic molecular signature' as the primary determinant of baseline vulnerability is novel.

References:

  • Mathys et al. (2019) Single-cell transcriptomic analysis of Alzheimer's disease [Cell-type-specific vulnerability]
  • De Jager et al. (2018) A multi-omic atlas of the human frontal cortex for aging and Alzheimer's disease [Molecular signatures in AD]

Statement 1: Signature Stability Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal_population → has_intrinsic_molecular_signature → signature_X
THEN
• signature_X → is_stable_over_time → True (barring major developmental or disease-stage transitions)

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Longitudinal studies show that neuronal subtype gene expression profiles are stable in adult cortex.
  2. Epigenetic landscapes of mature neurons are largely maintained except during major disease progression.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law extends known stability of neuronal identity to the context of vulnerability signatures.

What Already Exists: Stability of neuronal subtype identity is well-established.

What is Novel: The explicit link between signature stability and persistent vulnerability/resistance is novel.

References:

  • Tasic et al. (2018) Shared and distinct transcriptomic cell types across neocortical areas [Stability of cell-type identity]
  • Luo et al. (2022) Epigenetic maintenance of neuronal identity [Epigenetic stability]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Neuronal populations with similar intrinsic molecular signatures across individuals will show similar patterns of vulnerability to Aβ/tau pathology.
  2. Manipulating key genes within the intrinsic signature (e.g., stress response, proteostasis) will alter vulnerability in a cell-autonomous manner.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Engineering a synthetic molecular signature in a resistant neuron to match that of a vulnerable population will confer vulnerability.
  2. Long-term environmental enrichment or deprivation may induce subtle, stable changes in intrinsic signatures, altering vulnerability.

Negative Experiments

  1. If neurons with identical intrinsic signatures show divergent vulnerability in the same environment, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If altering the intrinsic signature does not change vulnerability, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The influence of network context and non-neuronal cells is not addressed.
