brief_description Multiregion whole-exome/whole-genome sequencing study of seven surgically resected non--small cell lung cancers (NSCLC) that maps spatial and temporal heterogeneity of mutational processes (including tobacco-related and APOBEC-associated mutagenesis), copy-number change, structural variation, and genome-doubling to infer mechanisms that drive why some smokers develop lung cancer and how tumors progress.

study_type Multiregion tumor sequencing (M-seq) study with validation analyses in The Cancer Genome Atlas (TCGA) cohorts
population_description Seven operable NSCLC patients (25 spatially distinct tumor regions sequenced; mean sequencing coverage: M-seq WES 107x, M-seq WGS 96x). Tumor histologies included lung adenocarcinoma (LUAD) and lung squamous cell carcinoma (LUSC). Smoking status was recorded: examples include at least one current smoker (patient L002) and former smokers who quit >20 years prior (patients L001 and L008). TCGA LUAD and LUSC cohorts were used for validation (sample sizes not specified in the main text). Demographic details (age, sex, ethnicity) are not reported in the main text.
risk_factors_identified Mechanistic and molecular risk factors identified or implicated among smokers: (1) Early mutational burden from tobacco carcinogens (characterized by C>A transversions) that seeds truncal driver mutations; (2) activation/up-regulation of APOBEC cytidine deaminases producing C>T/C>G at TpC motifs that preferentially contributes to later/subclonal driver mutations and subclonal expansion; (3) genome-doubling events and chromosomal instability (CIN), including chromoanagenesis and clustered translocations, which accelerate genomic evolution and provide substrate for subclonal competition and progression; (4) the presence/timing of truncal driver mutations (most known drivers are truncal) that set the stage for later diversification; and (5) histology-specific differences (LUAD shows stronger late APOBEC activity than LUSC).
protective_factors_identified No clear molecular 'protective' factors against development of lung cancer among smokers were identified. The data suggest that cessation of smoking leaves an 'ancestral' smoking mutational footprint (truncal C>A mutations) and that later mutations show reduced smoking-signature contribution; however, the study does not demonstrate a protective mechanism (e.g., genetic resistance or efficient repair) that prevents tumor development in some smokers.
genetic_findings Key genetic observations relevant to susceptibility/progression: (1) Most high-confidence driver mutations (e.g., TP53, EGFR^L858R, BRAF^G469A, PIK3CA^E542K, others) were truncal/clonal, indicating occurrence early in evolution. (2) Many driver mutations also occur subclonally and regionally (heterogeneous drivers), some in an APOBEC mutation context (examples: PIK3CA, EP300, TGFBR1, PTPRD, AKAP9). (3) Elevated APOBEC3B mRNA expression was observed regionally where APOBEC signatures were strongest. (4) Structural events disrupted tumor suppressors (e.g., FANCM, NF1) via clustered translocations. (5) Genome-doubling preceded or followed driver acquisition depending on tumor, implying timing of ploidy changes in susceptibility/progression.
behavioral_modifiers Smoking behavior and history modified mutational patterns: (1) Current versus former smoker status and time since cessation influenced the temporal distribution of smoking-related mutations (former smokers showed an ancestral smoking footprint with reduced later C>A fraction and decreased strand bias in late mutations consistent with past exposure). (2) Continued exposure (current smokers) did not always show the same loss of strand bias in late mutations, indicating behavioral status (ongoing smoking vs cessation) affects the evolving mutational signature.
environmental_modifiers The primary environmental exposure discussed is tobacco smoking (carcinogen exposure). The paper emphasizes maintained exposure to tobacco mutagens in current smokers but shows a relative decline in smoking-signature mutations over time in tumors; no additional environmental or occupational modifiers (e.g., radon, asbestos, air pollution) are analyzed in this dataset.
biological_mechanisms Mechanistic processes described that influence which smokers develop lung cancer: (1) Direct mutagenesis from tobacco carcinogens producing characteristic C>A transversions that seed truncal driver mutations early in tumorigenesis; (2) activation of endogenous APOBEC cytidine deaminases generating C>T and C>G mutations in a TpC context, contributing disproportionately to late/subclonal mutations and to subclonal driver formation and expansion; (3) genome-doubling and chromosomal instability (including chromoanagenesis and alternative/nonhomologous end-joining double-strand break repair) creating large-scale copy-number changes and structural rearrangements that accelerate evolution, increase heterogeneity, and enable subclonal competition; (4) spatially heterogeneous expression of mutagenic processes (e.g., regional APOBEC3B up-regulation) producing regional differences in mutation spectra; (5) selective processes where certain mutations provide subclonal fitness advantages and expand regionally; and (6) a prolonged latency period after early events (including genome-doubling) before clinical detection, allowing accrual of divergent subclonal events.
quantitative_results Key quantitative findings comparing mutational signatures and subclonal contributions: (1) Across all seven M-seq tumors there was a statistically significant shift in mutation spectra over time (P < 0.05 for each tumor). (2) Every tumor had a significant decrease in the proportion of C>A transversions in late versus early mutations (P < 0.05); the mean odds ratio for LUAD was 3.13 (range 2.07–5.55) and for LUSC 1.34 (range 1.21–1.46). (3) TCGA validation: LUAD current smokers P < 0.0001 and former smokers P < 0.0001 (never-smokers P = 0.147) for decreased late C>A proportion; LUSC current smokers P = 0.003 and former smokers P < 0.0001 (never-smokers P = 0.673). (4) APOBEC contribution: on average 31% (range 8–41%) of nonsilent branch (late) mutations occurred in an APOBEC context versus 11% (7–16%) of truncal nonsilent mutations in the M-seq set. (5) In TCGA LUAD tumors with APOBEC signatures, late enrichment of APOBEC mutations was significant (P < 0.001); in TCGA data 20% of subclonal driver mutations occurred in an APOBEC context versus 11% of clonal driver mutations. (6) Probability of missing a category-1 driver by single-region sampling averaged 42% (range 0–67%); for all potential driver genes (categories 1–3) average probability was 83% (range 67–100%). (7) In LUSC, 25% of tumors displayed no decrease in C>A transversions over time, compared with <10% in LUAD.
counterexamples_or_exceptions Notable exceptions: (1) Some LUSC tumors (≈25%) did not show a decrease in smoking-signature C>A transversions in late mutations, indicating heterogeneity in temporal dynamics across histologies. (2) Current smokers did not show a statistically significant decrease in strand bias of C>A transversions between early and late mutations (LUAD P = 0.23; LUSC P = 0.22), whereas former smokers did. (3) Tumor L003 demonstrated two anatomically separate tumors that shared only a highly recurrent EGFR L858R mutation (and no shared silent mutations), consistent with independent clonal origins and parallel evolution—illustrating that identical oncogenic events can arise independently rather than by dissemination.
study_limitations Limitations noted by authors: (1) Small patient number (seven tumors) limits generalizability. (2) Spatial sampling covered on average <5% of each tumor, so intratumor heterogeneity is likely underestimated. (3) The study is based on surgically resected operable tumors, which may not represent the full clinical spectrum of NSCLC. (4) Clinical demographic details (age/sex/ethnicity) and full clinical follow-up outcomes are not provided in the main text. (5) The study is not population-based and therefore cannot directly identify epidemiologic risk factors that distinguish smokers who do versus do not develop lung cancer; instead it provides mechanistic genomic correlates.
notes This paper emphasizes mechanistic genomic factors (mutational processes, timing of driver events, genome-doubling, chromosomal instability, APOBEC activation, and spatial heterogeneity) that likely contribute to why some smokers develop lung cancer and others (with similar exposures) may not, but it does not provide classical epidemiologic risk-factor comparisons (e.g., dose–response, family history) between smokers who do and do not develop lung cancer.
