Paper Abstract: Background Inherited susceptibility and environmental carcinogens are crucial players in lung cancer etiology. The lung microbiome is getting rising attention in carcinogenesis. The present work sought to investigate the microbiome in lung cancer patients affected by familial lung cancer (FLC) and indoor air pollution (IAP); and further, to compare host gene expression patterns with their microbiome for potential links. Methods Tissue sample pairs (cancer and adjacent nonmalignant tissue) were used for 16S rRNA (microbiome) and RNA-seq (host gene expression). Subgroup microbiome diversities and their matched gene expression patterns were analyzed. Significantly enriched taxa were screened out, based on different clinicopathologic characteristics. Results Our FLC microbiome seemed to be smaller, low-diversity, and inactive to change; we noted microbiome differences in gender, age, blood type, anatomy site, histology type, TNM stage as well as IAP and smoking conditions. We also found smoking and IAP dramatically decreased specific-OTU biodiversity, especially in normal lung tissue. Intriguingly, enriched microbes were in three categories: opportunistic pathogens, probiotics, and pollutant-detoxication microbes; this third category involved Sphingomonas, Sphingopyxis, etc. which help degrade pollutants, but may also cause epithelial damage and chronic inflammation. RNA-seq highlighted IL17, Ras, MAPK, and Notch pathways, which are associated with carcinogenesis and compromised immune system. Conclusions The lung microbiome can play vital roles in carcinogenesis. FLC and IAP subjects were affected by fragile lung epithelium, vulnerable host-microbes equilibrium, and dysregulated immune surveillance and response. Our findings provided useful information to study the triple interplay among environmental carcinogens, population genetic background, and diversified lung microbiome.

risk_factors_identified Inherited familial susceptibility (FLC) and environmental carcinogens: indoor air pollution from household coal combustion (IAP) and cigarette smoking; older age and later TNM stage correlated with increased abundance of opportunistic pathogens. Mechanistically, FLC was associated with a smaller, lower-diversity lung microbiome and less microbiome resilience; smoking and IAP were associated with decreased specific-OTU biodiversity (especially in normal lung) and enrichment of pollutant-degrading and opportunistic taxa, which the authors link to increased carcinogenic potential via chronic inflammation and altered host response.
protective_factors_identified Presence of probiotic-associated genera (e.g., Lactobacillus, Blautia, Oscillospira) and pollutant-detoxification microbes (e.g., certain PAH-degrading taxa) were more abundant in younger patients and earlier-stage disease and are discussed as potentially mitigating pollutant burden; however, authors emphasize these pollutant-degrading taxa may be 'double-edged' (they can reduce pollutant load but also perturb host tissue or provoke inflammation). No definitive protective genetic variants were identified in this study.
genetic_findings No specific germline susceptibility variants were reported; FLC was defined phenotypically (familial aggregation) and associated with altered microbiome features (lower diversity, altered species composition) and with host transcriptomic changes. RNA-seq (normal adjacent tissue) showed differentially expressed genes between subgroups and pathway enrichment in IL-17, Ras, MAPK, Notch signaling, and xenobiotic metabolism (cytochrome P450), implicating altered host pathways linked to carcinogenesis and immune regulation rather than reporting specific inherited sequence variants.
behavioral_modifiers Cigarette smoking (current or ex-smoker) is associated with decreased specific-OTU biodiversity in normal lung and greater microbiome change from normal to cancer; smoking was linked to increased abundance of certain opportunistic pathogens in cancer tissue. The study did not provide detailed behavioral metrics (pack-years, age at initiation, quitting) beyond smoking history yes/no (current/ex vs never).
environmental_modifiers Indoor air pollution from domestic coal use (IAP) was a major modifier: high-IAP associated with altered microbiome (reduced normal specific OTU) and enrichment of PAH/pollutant-degrading genera (e.g., Sphingomonas, Sphingopyxis, Thermomonas, Novosphingobium). The authors discuss polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) from coal/smoke as substrates that select for pollutant-degrading microbes and as direct carcinogens that drive host gene-expression responses (chemical carcinogenesis, xenobiotic metabolism).
biological_mechanisms Proposed mechanisms linking smoking and only some smokers developing lung cancer include: (1) dysbiosis — decreased microbial biodiversity and emergence of opportunistic pathogens and pollutant-degrading taxa that can damage epithelium or provoke chronic inflammation; (2) chronic inflammation mediated by microbial-host interactions (IL-17 pathway activation, neutrophil recruitment) promoting tumorigenesis; (3) epithelial barrier damage and extracellular matrix catabolism enabling microbial adherence/biofilm formation and increased exposure of cells to carcinogens; (4) altered host xenobiotic metabolism (cytochrome P450) and activation of canonical oncogenic pathways (Ras, MAPK, Notch) detected by RNA-seq in adjacent normal tissue; (5) pollutant-degrading microbes provide metabolic by-products and may act as opportunistic pathogens — a 'double-edged' effect; (6) compromised immune surveillance in FLC subjects leading to an inability to clear harmful microbes or mutated cells.
quantitative_results Key numeric comparisons reported in the paper (no odds ratios/relative risks provided): - Total OTUs across 60 samples: 2724; per-sample average OTU ~319 (range 251-398). - Specific-OTU (unique to subgroup) counts: FLC normal 314 vs sporadic normal 353; FLC cancer 289 vs sporadic cancer 266. - High-IAP normal specific OTU 295 vs low-IAP normal 374; high-IAP cancer 273 vs low-IAP cancer 279. - Smokers (ever) normal specific OTU 274 vs never-smokers normal 369; smokers cancer specific OTU 172 vs never-smokers cancer 379. - Fold-changes for taxa: in FLC cancer Capnocytophaga and Fusobacterium increased ≈5× and ≈42× respectively (from their normal to cancer comparisons); Sphingomonas and Sphingopyxis increased ≈1.3× and ≈1.4× in FLC cancer; Fusobacterium increased ≈24× in high-IAP cancer vs ≈4× in low-IAP. - RNA-seq differential gene counts (normal tissues): FLC vs sporadic: 79 upregulated and 152 downregulated DEGs; High-IAP vs Low-IAP: 119 up /136 down; Female vs Male: 111 up /211 down. - Statistical significance reported for clinicopathologic differences: e.g., IAP distribution across FLC status (p=0.0004) and age differences between FLC and sporadic (p=0.001). The study did not report conventional epidemiologic effect measures (OR/RR) comparing smokers who do vs do not develop lung cancer.
counterexamples_or_exceptions The cohort contained many never-smokers who nevertheless developed lung cancer (19/34 were never-smokers), illustrating that smoking is not the sole determinant. The benign-control lungs (n=5) had much lower subgroup-specific OTU counts (148) than cancer or adjacent-normal tissues, indicating lungs without cancer harbor distinct, lower abundant microbiome communities. The study did not include a comparison group of healthy smokers who did not develop lung cancer, so direct examples of heavy smokers without cancer are not evaluated longitudinally here.
study_limitations Authors note limitations including small sample size (n=34 cancer patients, 5 benign controls), single-region cohort (Yunnan, China) limiting generalizability, some samples unpaired or failing QC reducing paired analyses (not all subjects had both cancer and adjacent-normal sequenced), reliance on 16S rRNA (taxonomic resolution and functional inference limited), cross-sectional design (cannot determine causality or temporality between microbiome changes and tumor development), lack of germline sequencing to identify specific inherited variants, and absence of a prospective comparison of smokers who do versus do not develop lung cancer (no population-level ORs/RRs presented).
