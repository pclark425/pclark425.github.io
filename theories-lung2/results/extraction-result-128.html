citation_title here
mention_or_use use
study_type hospital-based case-control
population_description 150 Lebanese adults recruited at a tertiary hospital (March–June 2012): 50 lung cancer patients (32% adenocarcinoma, 28% small cell, 16% squamous-cell, 10% non-small cell, 14% unspecified) and 100 controls (patients or visitors); mean ages 59.6±6.0 (cases) and 59.8±6.3 (controls); 62% male, 38% female. Smoking status: among cases 92% former cigarette smokers vs 41% of controls; waterpipe smoking was less frequent but present.
risk_factors_identified Cigarette smoking (strongest risk factor, dose-dependent); waterpipe smoking (significant in bivariate analysis but not retained in multivariate models); environmental exposures including living near electricity generators (proxy for diesel exhaust), living near road traffic, urban residence, use of fuel for heating (stronger effect in females); low fruit and vegetable consumption; past occupational exposure to gases/toxins; exposure to passive smoking at home and work; personal history of chronic respiratory disease and childhood respiratory infections; positive family history of cancer and specifically lung cancer.
protective_factors_identified Higher fruits and vegetables consumption was associated with lower lung cancer risk (high consumption used as reference); past multivitamin intake was not significantly protective in this sample.
genetic_findings No direct genetic testing performed. The study found an association between positive family history of cancer (and family history of lung cancer) and increased lung cancer risk (crude ORs). The paper cites prior literature implicating genetic susceptibility loci (e.g., nicotinic acetylcholine receptor genes on 15q25) but reports no primary genetic data.
behavioral_modifiers Cigarette smoking intensity showed a dose–response: 25–35 cigarettes/day (OR = 7.33, 95% CI 1.99–26.95), >35 cigarettes/day (OR = 20.42, 95% CI 5.28–79.03) compared with <25/day. Former smoking (vs never) strongly associated (OR = 16.55, 95% CI 5.53–49.55). Waterpipe smoking associated in bivariate analysis (former waterpipe OR = 6.0, 95% CI 1.78–20.26) but not retained in multivariate models.
environmental_modifiers Living near an electricity generator (OR = 2.57 crude; adjusted in males ORa = 13.26), living near road traffic (OR = 2.59), urban residence (OR = 3.92), use of fuel for heating (crude OR = 2.83; adjusted in females ORa = 9.76), exposure to passive smoking at home (OR = 2.25) and at work (OR = 2.28), occupational exposures to gases/toxins (OR = 2.63). The paper frames generators as a potential local source of diesel exhaust.
biological_mechanisms The paper notes established biological mechanisms in the literature: cigarette smoke contains carcinogenic chemicals that promote tumor initiation and growth; chronic respiratory disease and childhood respiratory infections (inflammatory/injury history) were associated with higher risk — suggesting prior lung injury/inflammation may influence susceptibility. The study itself did not measure biomarkers or mechanisms (e.g., DNA adducts, metabolism, repair, or immune markers).
quantitative_results Key crude ORs: former cigarette smoker OR = 16.55 (95% CI 5.53–49.55); former waterpipe smoker OR = 6.00 (95% CI 1.78–20.26); daily cigarettes 25–35 OR = 7.33 (1.99–26.95); >35 OR = 20.42 (5.28–79.03); urban residence OR = 3.92 (1.10–13.89); lived near road OR = 2.59 (1.21–5.54); lived near generator OR = 2.57 (1.23–5.35); use of fuel heating OR = 2.83 (1.41–5.71); passive smoking home OR = 2.25 (1.12–4.52); passive smoking work OR = 2.28 (1.14–4.56); low fruit/veg OR = 7.70 (3.56–16.63); occupational exposure OR = 2.63 (1.27–5.44); chronic respiratory disease OR = 3.19 (1.24–8.20); childhood respiratory infection OR = 5.57 (1.96–15.81); family history of lung cancer OR = 4.42 (1.53–12.79). Adjusted (multivariate) models: females — use of fuel for heating ORa = 9.76 (95% CI 2.15–44.30), cigarette smoking ORa = 9.12 (95% CI 1.81–46.00); males — cigarette smoking ORa = 156.98 (95% CI 5.90–417.4), living near generator ORa = 13.26 (1.85–95.04), low fruit/veg ORa = 10.54 (1.76–63.24), family history of cancer ORa = 8.75 (1.22–63.03).
counterexamples_or_exceptions Waterpipe smoking was significant in bivariate comparisons but dropped from multivariate models (possibly due to small numbers or confounding). Some exposures were significant in bivariate but not retained after adjustment, indicating confounding or limited power; the study design cannot address why many smokers do not develop lung cancer (no biomarker/genetic testing), though family history and prior respiratory disease are highlighted correlates of increased susceptibility.
study_limitations Small sample size (pilot; 50 cases), hospital-based convenient selection (potential selection/admission bias), limited geographic representation (Beirut and Mount Lebanon), possible recall bias and interviewer bias, lack of biomarker/genetic measures, cross-sectional case-control design (cannot infer causality), sparse data producing wide confidence intervals and potentially inflated adjusted ORs, and potential residual confounding.
