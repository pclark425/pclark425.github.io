brief_description A prospective cohort study following 34,440 male British doctors for 20 years (1951–1971) recording smoking histories and certified causes of 10,072 deaths to relate smoking habits to cause-specific mortality, including lung cancer, and to examine behavioural modifiers (amount, type, inhaling, quitting).
citation_title Mortality in relation to smoking: 20 years’ observations on male British doctors
mention_or_use use
study_type prospective cohort
population_description 34,440 male British doctors who replied to a 1951 questionnaire about smoking; followed 1 Nov 1951–31 Oct 1971. Ages at entry spanned 20+ years (tables reported by 5-year age groups to 85+), predominantly male, UK residents; 10,072 deaths recorded. Smoking status categories: lifelong non-smokers (~17%), ex-smokers, current cigarette smokers (amounts recorded), pipe/cigar smokers; repeat questionnaires in 1957, 1966, 1972 recorded changes.
risk_factors_identified Primary risk factor: amount (dose) of cigarette smoking — clear, strong positive relation with lung cancer mortality; cigarette smoking (vs pipe/cigar or non-smoking) was strongly associated with lung cancer. Other modifiers linked to higher lung cancer risk in smokers: inhalation patterns for light/moderate smokers (see inhaling), continued cigarette use (current smokers much higher than lifelong non-smokers), and combination with environmental co-exposures mentioned (asbestos, atmospheric pollution) in interpretation.
protective_factors_identified Stopping smoking reduced relative lung cancer mortality over time: ex-smokers' relative mortality fell with years since stopping (table X), e.g., ratios vs lifelong non-smokers: current smokers (0 years stopped) = 15.8, stopped 5-9 years ≈ 5.9, stopped 10-14 ≈ 5.3, stopped ≥15 ≈ 2.0; earlier cessation (before age 30) associated with much lower mortality (ex-smokers who stopped before age 30 had mortality 53% of continuing smokers and 93% of non-smokers). Pipe/cigar-only smoking carried substantially lower lung cancer rates than cigarette smoking.
genetic_findings No specific genetic variants identified in this paper. The authors note and discuss Fisher's genetic hypothesis (that genetic susceptibility might explain the association) and cite Swedish twin results that do not support Fisher's genetic explanation, arguing against a simple genetic-only explanation; overall the cohort provides no direct genetic data.
behavioral_modifiers Amount smoked (dose-response): lung cancer mortality rose with increasing cigarettes/day; type of tobacco: cigarette smokers had much higher lung cancer mortality than pipe/cigar-only smokers; inhalation: analysed—pattern varied by amount smoked (for light/moderate smokers inhalers had higher lung cancer rates; for heavy smokers the pattern reversed); age at start and duration: earlier start associated with longer exposure; quitting: longer time since stopping associated with progressively lower lung cancer mortality (see protective_factors_identified).
environmental_modifiers Authors discuss interaction with environmental/occupational exposures as modifiers: alcohol consumption (related to oesophageal and some other respiratory cancers but not strongly to lung cancer), asbestos exposure (mentioned as likely to increase lung cancer risk), atmospheric/air pollution (noted as possibly interacting with smoking and especially relevant for chronic obstructive lung disease), and other environmental carcinogens — these were discussed as plausible effect modifiers but not measured in this cohort.
biological_mechanisms Proposed/ discussed mechanisms: deposition of smoke droplets and polycyclic hydrocarbons in respiratory tract depending on inhalation and particle dynamics (small droplets <1 μm can penetrate to alveoli and later swell and deposit; deposition site affects carcinogen contact with bronchial epithelium); impairment of ciliary activity by smoke (facilitating infections like tuberculosis); chronic obstructive lung disease and progressive loss of lung function with inflammation/destruction; lymphatic removal of particulates from alveoli; interactions between smoking and other environmental carcinogens (e.g., asbestos) increasing carcinogenic effect. No molecular genetic or biochemical mechanisms (e.g., specific metabolic enzymes or DNA repair genes) were provided.
quantitative_results Key numerical findings relevant to lung cancer: 441 deaths accepted as lung cancer (underlying cause) among cohort; lung cancer mortality rate (age-standardised) in lifelong non-smokers ≈ 10 per 100,000 vs current/ex-smokers ≈ 83 per 100,000 (table III). Ratio of deaths from lung cancer: current smokers vs lifelong non-smokers = 15.8 (table X; 'current smokers' defined as stopped 0 years ago, i.e., continuing smokers), and ex-smokers' ratios decline with years since stopping: <5 years ≈ 16.0 (biased by some stopping after disease onset), 5–9 years ≈ 5.9, 10–14 years ≈ 5.3, ≥15 years ≈ 2.0 (all ratios = observed/expected vs lifelong non-smokers). Inhalation-by-amount (table VIII): for 1–14 cigarettes/day inhalers 98/100k vs non-inhalers 54/100k (numbers in parentheses: deaths: 16 vs 8); for 15–24/day inhalers 113 vs non-inhalers 75 (29 vs 9 deaths); for ≥25/day inhalers 185 vs non-inhalers 417 (32 vs 29 deaths) — patterns limited by small numbers and potential confounding. Overall all-cause mortality ratios of smokers vs non-smokers for middle ages≈2:1 (stated in summary).
counterexamples_or_exceptions Notable exceptions discussed: many smokers (even heavy) do not develop lung cancer; pipe/cigar-only smokers have far lower lung cancer mortality than cigarette smokers; inhalation findings are inconsistent (for heavy smokers non-inhalers had higher lung-cancer rates in these data, possibly due to confounding), and a small number of men stopped smoking because they developed lung cancer (reverse causation), biasing ex-smoker risk estimates in early years after stopping.
study_limitations Limitations noted by authors: cohort of physicians (social class I) so not fully generalisable; self-selection bias of responders early in study; death-certificate misclassification (they attempted adjudication for lung cancer but other causes may be misclassified); small numbers of lung cancer deaths among non-smokers (imprecise estimates); inhalation data only collected after second questionnaire (usable for last 13 years), so limited sample and potential changes in habits over time; smoking amount reported at intervals and many changed amount (especially older/heavy smokers reducing), causing exposure misclassification and biasing dose-response; potential confounding by alcohol or other personality/behavioural factors; environmental co-exposures not measured in cohort (only discussed); no direct genetic data collected.
