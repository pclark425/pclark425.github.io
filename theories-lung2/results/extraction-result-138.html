brief_description Large hospital-based case-control study in Western Europe analyzing how changes in cigarette smoking habits (quitting, reducing number, switching filter status, inhalation) modify lung cancer risk among ever-smokers; provides stratified relative risks by duration and intensity of smoking and time since quitting or change.
citation_title Modifying risk of developing lung cancer by changing habits of cigarette smoking
mention_or_use use
study_type hospital-based case-control study
population_description Hospital-based sample from seven study areas in five Western European countries (patients admitted 1976-80). Analysis restricted to subjects who had smoked cigarettes regularly at some time: approximately 7,191 patients (6631 men, 551 women in the analytic sample reported) and 11,006 controls (10,439 men, 567 women). Cases were histologically confirmed lung cancer patients; two hospital controls per case matched on age (within five years), sex, centre, and, where possible, hospital accommodation category. Majority were middle-aged/older adults; ethnicity not specified beyond Western Europe. Smoking status: all subjects in analytic sample were ever-smokers; a large proportion were current smokers at interview (e.g., 70.6% of male patients were current smokers).
risk_factors_identified Factors associated with increased lung cancer risk among smokers identified in this study: (1) longer total duration of cigarette use (dose by years) — e.g., compared with current smokers who had smoked 1-19 years (baseline), current smokers who had smoked 20-39 years had RR ~2.2, 40-49 years RR ~2.8, ≥50 years RR ~3.0 in men (Table II); (2) higher cumulative intensity patterns (greater number smoked/day associated with higher absolute risk although quitting produced varying proportional declines); (3) smoking non-filter cigarettes (current smokers of non-filter only had RR = 1.78 vs filter-only current smokers; former smokers of non-filter had RR = 1.53 vs filter-only former smokers); (4) increasing daily consumption (those who increased daily consumption had RR = 1.26 relative to no change); (5) longer time since starting and persistent long-term smoking — long-term smokers who quit still retained substantially elevated risk versus never-smokers.
protective_factors_identified Factors associated with reduced risk among smokers in this study: (1) complete cessation — risk declines sharply with years since stopping (trend p < 0.001); for men who had smoked 1–19 years, ≥10 years since stopping reduced risk to <1/3 of current smokers and approached lifetime non-smoker levels; (2) reducing number of cigarettes/day — modest reductions in risk were seen, but reductions were generally much smaller than those achieved by quitting (stopping entirely had RR ≈ 0.76 overall in Table IV, with longer cessation producing much lower RRs); (3) smoking filter-only brands was associated with lower risk than lifelong non-filter smoking (filter-only baseline had lower risk than non-filter).
genetic_findings null
behavioral_modifiers Detailed behavioral modifiers reported: (1) Time since stopping smoking — large, graded reductions in relative risk with increasing years since quitting (Table I; trend p < 0.001). (2) Total duration of smoking habit — interacts with benefit from quitting: shorter total duration before quitting leads to greater proportional reduction (e.g., men with 1–19 years duration had much greater risk decline after quitting than men with ≥50 years). (3) Changes in daily consumption — stopped, >50% reduction, <50% reduction, increase; stopping produced the largest benefit, >50% reduction did not reliably lower risk in current smokers (RR ≈ 1.01 for >50% reduction), and increasing consumption raised risk (RR = 1.26). (4) Type/brand switching — switching from non-filter to filter reduced risk modestly but left an elevated risk relative to lifelong filter smokers; switching filter→non-filter increased risk. (5) Frequency and depth of inhalation — some indication of greater reduction in risk for those who inhaled less often or less deeply, although these findings were not uniformly strong; Table III shows stratified RRs by inhalation frequency and depth with some heterogeneity (tests for differences in linear trends p < 0.05 for some comparisons).
environmental_modifiers null
biological_mechanisms The paper discusses biological implications in conceptual terms rather than molecular mechanisms: (1) cumulative carcinogenic effect of smoking increases with duration and may become partly irreversible after long-term exposure, explaining why long-term smokers retain elevated risk despite cessation; (2) lower exposure to tar/ carcinogens (via fewer cigarettes/day or filter brands) plausibly reduces risk, consistent with observed lower RRs for filter-only smokers and for those who reduce consumption; (3) depth and frequency of inhalation modulate actual internal exposure and thus influence risk; the study did not measure metabolic, DNA repair, or genetic susceptibility mechanisms and so offers no direct molecular biological data.
quantitative_results Key quantitative findings from the study (selected): (1) Time since stopping (Table I): reference current smokers RR = 1.00; for men, 5–9 years RR = 0.71, 10–14 years RR = 0.56, 15–19 years RR = 0.43, 20–24 years RR = 0.43, ≥25 years RR = 0.29 (test for linear trend p < 0.001). (2) Duration interaction (Table II): among men current smokers: 1–19 years = 1.0 (baseline), 20–39 years = 2.2, 40–49 years = 2.8, ≥50 years = 3.0; for men who had quit ≥10 years, corresponding RRs were 0.3, 1.0, 1.6, 2.7 respectively, showing much less decline for long-duration smokers. (3) Change in daily consumption (Table IV): stopped (100%) RR = 0.76 (95% CI 0.7–0.8) relative to no change; increased consumption RR = 1.26 (95% CI 1.1–1.4); reduced >50% RR = 1.01 (95% CI 0.9–1.2); reduced <50% RR = 0.84 (95% CI 0.7–1.0). (4) Type of cigarette (Table VI): current smokers of non-filter only had RR = 1.78 (95% CI 1.5–2.1) versus filter-only current smokers; switching non-filter→filter or filter→non-filter also showed elevated RRs vs filter-only (e.g., current smokers non-filter→filter RR = 1.60, 95% CI 1.4–1.9). (5) For many stratified comparisons the authors report statistically significant trends or differences (e.g., tests for linear trend p < 0.001 for years since stopping; some tests for differences in linear trends p < 0.05 in Table III).
counterexamples_or_exceptions Notable exceptions highlighted by the authors: (1) Long-term smokers (e.g., ≥40–50 years duration) who quit still had substantially elevated risk even 10+ years after stopping and were unlikely to approach lifetime non-smoker risk — illustrating heavy smokers who remain at high risk despite cessation. (2) Reductions in daily consumption (short of complete cessation) often produced only small or no reductions in relative risk (e.g., >50% reduction in current smokers had RR ≈ 1.01), showing that some smokers who reduce amounts still develop lung cancer at similar rates. (3) Small numbers limited precision in some strata (especially women in some analyses), so some strata lack clear patterns.
study_limitations Limitations reported by the authors: (1) Hospital-based case-control design — potential biases from case acquisition method, choice of control diseases, and referral patterns, although authors judged these minimal. (2) Possible recall gaps in smoking histories (no complete accounting of short quit periods between brand changes); the questionnaire recorded only up to four cigarette brands, which could underestimate exposure duration for subjects reporting ≥5 brands. (3) Small numbers in some subgroups (notably women and some stratified cells) limiting precision. (4) Lack of molecular/biologic measures — no genetic, metabolic, or DNA repair data to explain inter-individual susceptibility. (5) Generalizability limited to similar Western European hospital populations and to ever-smokers captured by this hospital sampling process.
