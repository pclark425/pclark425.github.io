Paper TL;DR: A 35-year-old healthy male, initially thought to have drug-induced liver injury, who was subsequently diagnosed with thymoma-induced autoimmune hepatitis is presented, a rare syndrome of which only two previous cases have been reported.

Paper Abstract: Thymomas are rare neoplasms of the thymus and are often associated with immune-mediated paraneoplastic syndromes, most commonly, myasthenia gravis. The same underlying mechanism can produce antibodies to other self-antigens in various organ systems. Autoimmune hepatitis is a rare complication of thymoma. We present a 35-year-old healthy male, initially thought to have drug-induced liver injury, who was subsequently diagnosed with thymoma-induced autoimmune hepatitis, a rare syndrome of which only two previous cases have been reported.

proposed_mechanism Failure of central tolerance in the neoplastic thymus leading to defective negative selection of maturing T cells (mechanisms proposed include: 'escape' of immature T cells prior to negative selection due to thymic disorganization; genetic dysplasia impairing negative selection; AIRE gene mutation/deficiency affecting presentation of self-antigens), resulting in peripheral export and persistence of autoreactive T-lymphocytes that drive autoimmunity and autoantibody production.
supporting_evidence Clinical and pathological observations: frequent co-occurrence of thymoma with multiple autoimmune syndromes (case series and literature reviews); demonstration that thymomas can generate mature, long-lived CD4+ and CD8+ T cells (Buckley et al., cited) implying export of potentially autoreactive T cells; studies and reviews describing T-cell role in initiation of autoantibody responses in thymoma patients (Vincent & Willcox, cited); temporal relationships where paraneoplastic syndromes appear concurrently, prior to, or after thymoma diagnosis including after thymectomy, consistent with exported long-lived autoreactive T cells; case-series data (Bernard et al.) documenting many thymoma patients with associated autoimmunity.
contradictory_evidence null
patient_demographics Varies by syndrome; in the review of autoimmune hepatitis cases most patients were young (mean age ~32 years) and several were of Asian descent; myasthenia gravis affects both sexes but is commonly reported in thymoma patients (~50% of thymoma cases).
interventions_modulating_autoimmunity Thymectomy (tumor resection) and tumor-directed therapy can alter autoimmune manifestations in some cases (notably myasthenia gravis), but thymectomy has not consistently led to resolution of non-MG paraneoplastic autoimmune disorders—consistent with persistence of exported autoreactive T cells; systemic immunosuppression (e.g., corticosteroids, other immunosuppressants) often used and has induced responses in reported cases (e.g., autoimmune hepatitis cases responded to immunosuppression in multiple reports).
key_findings Thymomas are associated with a spectrum of autoimmune/paraneoplastic syndromes due to disrupted thymic negative selection and export of autoreactive T cells; while myasthenia gravis is the most common association, other autoimmune diseases including autoimmune hepatitis are rare but documented; mechanisms remain incompletely defined and likely involve multiple complementary processes (escape of immature T cells, genetic dysregulation, AIRE-related defects).
limitations Predominantly case reports and small series; mechanistic hypotheses are supported mainly by immunologic and clinical correlations rather than definitive molecular causation in humans; heterogeneity of reported syndromes and occasional confounders (e.g., medications, comorbidities) limit causal inference.

key_findings This report documents a rare thymoma-associated autoimmune hepatitis with histologic and serologic evidence of AIH and a temporal relationship to thymoma treatment and corticosteroid use, reinforcing the model that thymoma can induce peripheral autoimmunity via defective thymic selection and export of autoreactive T cells.
