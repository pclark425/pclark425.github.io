Paper TL;DR: Results demonstrate the powerful effect of GSTT1 status on SPMA levels in urine and show that uptake of benzene in African American, White, and Japanese American cigarette smokers is consistent with their lung cancer risk in the MEC.

Paper Abstract: Research from the Multiethnic Cohort (MEC) demonstrated that, for the same quantity of cigarette smoking, African Americans and Native Hawaiians have a higher lung cancer risk than Whites, while Latinos and Japanese Americans are less susceptible. We collected urine samples from 2,239 cigarette smokers from five different ethnic groups in the MEC and analyzed each sample for S-phenylmercapturic acid (SPMA), a specific biomarker of benzene uptake. African Americans had significantly higher (geometric mean [SE] 3.69 [0.2], p<0.005) SPMA/ml urine than Whites (2.67 [0.13]) while Japanese Americans had significantly lower levels than Whites (1.65 [0.07], p<0.005). SPMA levels in Native Hawaiians and Latinos were not significantly different from those of Whites. We also conducted a genome-wide association study in search of genetic risk factors related to benzene exposure. The glutathione S-transferase T1 (GSTT1) deletion explained between 14.2–31.6% (p = 5.4x10-157) and the GSTM1 deletion explained between 0.2%-2.4% of the variance (p = 1.1x10-9) of SPMA levels in these populations. Ethnic differences in levels of SPMA remained strong even after controlling for the effects of these two deletions. These results demonstrate the powerful effect of GSTT1 status on SPMA levels in urine and show that uptake of benzene in African American, White, and Japanese American cigarette smokers is consistent with their lung cancer risk in the MEC. While benzene is not generally considered a cause of lung cancer, its metabolite SPMA could be a biomarker for other volatile lung carcinogens in cigarette smoke.

population_description 2,239 cancer-free current smokers drawn from the Multiethnic Cohort (MEC): 364 African Americans, 311 Native Hawaiians, 437 Whites, 453 Latinos, 674 Japanese Americans; ages roughly 45-75 at baseline (group means ~61-65 years); both sexes included; urine samples (overnight or first morning) collected ~10 years after cohort entry; subjects with very low smoking biomarkers (TNE <1.4 nmol/ml) or SPMA below LOD were excluded.
risk_factors_identified Factors associated with higher internal benzene uptake (a proxy for exposure to volatile smoke carcinogens): (1) Ethnicity — African American smokers had higher SPMA than Whites, Japanese Americans had lower SPMA than Whites; (2) Higher tobacco exposure measured biochemically (TNE) and cigarettes per day were positively associated with SPMA; (3) GSTT1 and GSTM1 functional copy number (presence of alleles) associated with higher SPMA (i.e. GSTT1 wild-type correlated with higher urinary SPMA). The paper links these biomarker differences to previously observed ethnic differences in lung cancer risk in the MEC (African Americans and Native Hawaiians higher risk than Whites; Japanese Americans and Latinos lower), but does not itself present lung-cancer case vs control risk estimates.
protective_factors_identified Genotypes and mechanisms that reduce urinary SPMA include GSTT1 and GSTM1 deletions (null genotypes) which lower SPMA excretion; however, the authors note this is complex because null genotype reduces SPMA (less glutathione conjugation) but could increase tissue exposure to reactive benzene oxide and thus potentially increase toxicity — so 'lower SPMA' is not necessarily protective. Behavioral protective factor noted in cited literature: smoking cessation markedly reduces SPMA (~80% reduction cited).
genetic_findings GWAS identified a strong association between GSTT1 deletion and SPMA levels (beta per allele ≈ 2.06 pmol/ml, p = 6.0x10^-107), explaining 14.2%–31.6% of SPMA variance across ethnic groups (20.9% overall). GSTM1 deletion was also associated but with a much smaller effect (beta per allele ≈ 1.20 pmol/ml, p = 3.3x10^-9), explaining 0.2%–2.4% of variance. A single imputed variant near GSTM1 (1p13) correlated with the GSTM1 deletion. No strong common variant associations were found at CYP2E1; previously reported candidate SNPs near CYP2E1 were not associated with SPMA in this dataset.
behavioral_modifiers Quantitative tobacco exposure strongly modifies SPMA: total nicotine equivalents (TNE) explained ~29.4% of SPMA variability and cigarettes/day explained ~2.5% in multivariable models. Smoking cessation (cited prior study) reduces SPMA by ~80%. Differences in smoking intensity/type across ethnic groups were observed (Whites on average smoked more cigarettes/day).
environmental_modifiers Non-smoking benzene sources noted: occupational benzene exposure (petrochemical, gasoline service stations, rubber and paint industries), ambient exposures near heavy traffic or gasoline stations, and contaminated water/food. The paper acknowledges these non-occupational/occupational exposures but focuses on cigarette smoke as the dominant benzene source for smokers.
biological_mechanisms Benzene is metabolically activated to benzene oxide, which can be detoxified by conjugation with glutathione mediated principally by glutathione S-transferases (GSTs), notably GSTT1 (more efficient) and GSTM1; the glutathione conjugate is processed to S-phenylmercapturic acid (SPMA) excreted in urine. GSTT1 and GSTM1 copy-number deletions (null genotypes) reduce formation of glutathione conjugates and thus lower urinary SPMA despite potentially increasing cellular exposure to reactive benzene oxide; SPMA correlates with other urinary biomarkers (TNE, NNAL, 3-HPMA, HMPMA), suggesting SPMA may serve as a biomarker for uptake of volatile tobacco carcinogens beyond benzene (e.g., 1,3-butadiene).
quantitative_results Key quantitative findings comparing groups and genetic strata: - Geometric mean SPMA (pmol/ml): African Americans 3.69 (SE 0.2) vs Whites 2.67 (0.13) (African Americans 38% higher; p ≈ 1.4x10^-5 after adjustment); Japanese Americans 1.65 (0.07) (38% lower than Whites; p ≈ 2x10^-13). - GWAS: GSTT1 deletion beta per allele = 2.06 pmol/ml, p = 6.0x10^-107; explained variance overall ≈20.9% (range 14.2%–31.6% across ethnic groups). - GSTM1 deletion beta per allele ≈1.20 pmol/ml, p = 3.3x10^-9; explained variance overall ≈1.3% (range 0.2%–2.4%). - Covariates (age, sex, BMI, TNE, CPD, ethnicity, PCs) explained ~37% of SPMA variability; TNE alone explained ~29.4%. - SPMA correlations (partial r, adjusted): with TNE r = 0.57 overall; with total NNAL r = 0.47; with 3-HPMA r = 0.26; with HMPMA r = 0.28 (all p < 0.0001). - SPMA by GSTT1 copy number (multiethnic means): 0 copies (null) mean ≈1.1 pmol/ml; 1 copy ≈2.87 pmol/ml; 2 copies ≈4.56 pmol/ml (trend p ≈ 6.0x10^-107).
counterexamples_or_exceptions Important exceptions and complexities: (1) GSTT1 null genotype lowers urinary SPMA but is mechanistically expected to increase tissue exposure to benzene oxide (potentially increasing risk), so lower SPMA does not equate to lower carcinogenic risk; (2) benzene is an established leukemogen but not generally considered a primary cause of lung cancer in humans, though it causes lung tumors in mice—so SPMA may be a marker for other volatile lung carcinogens rather than a direct causal mediator; (3) ethnic differences in SPMA persisted after adjustment for GST deletions, indicating other unmeasured factors contribute to differences; (4) urine collection method (overnight vs first morning) and expression per ml vs per mg creatinine may introduce variability.
study_limitations Limitations noted by authors: cross-sectional biomarker measurement (single urine sample) rather than repeated measures or 24h collection; differing urine collection methods between sites (overnight vs first morning) though sensitivity checks suggested minimal effect; use of SPMA per ml rather than per mg creatinine due to wide creatinine variability across groups (hydration-related variability possible); limited GWAS power for small-effect or low-frequency variants (sample sizes limited within ethnic strata); study did not directly compare lung cancer cases vs non-cases (it analyzed biomarkers in cancer-free smokers and referenced prior MEC lung cancer risk differences), and potential residual confounding by unmeasured environmental or metabolic factors.
notes_on_relation_to_lung_cancer The paper does not present direct comparisons of smokers who developed lung cancer versus those who did not within this sample; instead it relates biomarker (SPMA) differences across ethnic groups to previously observed ethnic differences in smoking-related lung cancer risk in the MEC (cited). The authors caution that benzene itself is not generally considered a major cause of lung cancer in humans, but SPMA may track uptake of other volatile lung carcinogens in cigarette smoke (e.g., 1,3-butadiene).
