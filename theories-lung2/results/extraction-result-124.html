Paper Abstract: Background Epidemiological and pedigree studies suggest that lung cancer results from the combined effects of age, smoking, impaired lung function and genetic factors. In a case control association study of healthy smokers and lung cancer cases, we identified genetic markers associated with either susceptibility or protection to lung cancer. Methodology/Principal Findings We screened 157 candidate single nucleotide polymorphisms (SNP) in a discovery cohort of 439 subjects (200 controls and 239 lung cancer cases) and identified 30 SNPs associated with either the healthy smokers (protective) or lung cancer (susceptibility) phenotype. After genotyping this 30 SNP panel in a validation cohort of 491 subjects (248 controls and 207 lung cancers) and, using the same protective and susceptibility genotypes from our discovery cohort, a 20 SNP panel was selected based on replication of SNP associations in the validation cohort. Following multivariate logistic regression analyses, including the selected SNPs from runs 1 and 2, we found age and family history of lung cancer to be significantly and independently associated with lung cancer. Numeric scores were assigned to both the SNP and demographic data, and combined to form a simple algorithm of risk. Conclusions/Significance Significant differences in the distribution of the lung cancer susceptibility score was found between normal controls and lung cancer cases, which remained after accounting for differences in lung function. Validation in other case-control and prospective cohorts are underway to further define the potential clinical utility of this model.

genetic_findings A 20-SNP panel (12 susceptibility and 8 protective SNPs) was derived by screening 157 candidate SNPs in discovery cohort and validating in an independent cohort; individual SNP effect sizes modest (susceptibility SNP ORs ~1.1–3.2; protective SNP ORs ~0.20–0.80). The combined SNP score is independently associated with lung cancer (p<0.001). SNPs map to pathways: carcinogen metabolism (NAT2, CYP2E1), inflammation (IL1B, IL8, IL18, TNFR1, TLR9), oxidant response (α1-antichymotrypsin, SOD3), DNA repair / cell-cycle / apoptosis (XPD/ERCC2, TP73, BCL2, FASL, REV1), nicotine receptor/related loci (α5-nAChR rs16969968), and addiction pathways (DRD2, DAT1). The α5-nAChR locus has independent replication in GWAS and candidate studies (15q25).
behavioral_modifiers Smoking exposure variables measured: pack-years similar between cases and controls (mean ~40–41 pack-years, p=0.28), years smoked greater in cases (41 vs 35 years, p<0.001), cigarettes/day lower in cases (20 vs 24, p<0.001), age at smoking initiation slightly older in cases (18 vs 17, p<0.001), current smoking less frequent in cases (35% vs 48%, p<0.001) — likely reflecting quit rates related to disease/COPD. Authors report smoking exposure (pack-years) was matched by recruitment criteria and therefore made little contribution to the derived risk score; they also discuss likely gene-by-smoking interactions though these were not quantified beyond candidate SNP associations.
environmental_modifiers Several non-tobacco exposures were more common in cases vs controls: home environmental tobacco smoke (ETS) exposure as adult (79% vs 58%, p<0.001), work ETS exposure (86% vs 63%, p<0.001), work dust exposure (63% vs 47%, p<0.001), asbestos exposure (23% vs 16%, p=0.02). Authors collected occupational aero-pollutant exposure data and considered these modifiers in descriptive comparisons.
biological_mechanisms Authors propose that genetic factors modulate susceptibility by altering inflammatory responses to cigarette smoke (chronic airway inflammation → tissue remodeling → impaired lung function/COPD), carcinogen metabolism (NAT2, CYP2E1), oxidative stress handling (SOD3, α1-antichymotrypsin), DNA damage recognition and repair and cell-cycle/apoptosis control (XPD/ERCC2, p73, Bcl-2, FasL, REV1), and nicotine-mediated effects on airway inflammation via nicotinic acetylcholine receptors (α5-nAChR). These mechanisms explain how the same exposure (smoking) yields different outcomes among individuals.
quantitative_results Key quantitative comparisons and performance metrics reported: combined dataset N=930; age >60 OR 3.5 (95% CI 2.5-4.9, p<0.001); family history OR 2.5 (1.6-4.0, p<0.001). Individual SNP ORs from univariate analyses (selected examples): α5-nAChR rs16969968 AA OR 1.8 (1.2–2.7, p=0.004); CYP2E1 rs2031920 TT/TC OR 2.1 (1.0–4.3, p=0.03); IL18 rs360721 CC OR 1.4 (1.1–1.9, p=0.009); IL8 rs4073 TT OR 1.5 (1.1–2.1, p=0.005); TP73 rs2273953 CC protective OR 0.65 (0.49–0.85, p=0.001); SOD3 rs1799895 GG/GC protective OR 0.28 (0.10–0.90, p=0.02). The combined SNP+age+family history model AUC = 0.77 (SNPs alone AUC 0.68; age+SNPs AUC 0.76; SNPs+FHx AUC 0.70). Odds ratio across susceptibility-score quintiles spanned up to OR=19.1 (highest vs lowest score group); the score showed linear relationship between score and lung cancer frequency (≈18% to 81% across score range). At a score cutoff ≥3 sensitivity estimated 89% and specificity 45%.
counterexamples_or_exceptions Authors emphasize that ~90% of lung cancer cases have smoking history but only ~10–15% of chronic smokers develop lung cancer, illustrating many heavy smokers who do not develop disease; study purposely selected smoking controls with normal lung function to represent 'resistant' smokers. In descriptive data, cases smoked for more years but reported fewer cigarettes/day and were less likely to be current smokers — illustrating heterogeneity in behavioral patterns and potential reverse causation (quitting after illness). COPD is common among cases (51% spirometric COPD) but controls were selected to exclude COPD, so COPD both co-associates with cancer and was controlled for in study design.
study_limitations Modest sample size, recruitment restricted to Caucasian smokers with ≥15 pack-years (limits generalizability), cross-sectional case-control design (not prospective), selection of controls with normal lung function (intentional but limits representativeness), some SNPs in the final panel did not reach conventional significance in combined analysis (14/20 significant), potential residual confounding (age, gender, smoking patterns not perfectly matched initially), and possibility that some implicated SNPs are markers in linkage disequilibrium rather than causal variants; authors note need for independent external validation and prospective studies.
