brief_description A pooled case-control analysis (SYNERGY) of occupational diesel motor exhaust (DME) exposure and lung cancer risk using individual lifetime occupational histories and detailed smoking data from 11 studies in Europe and Canada; reports exposure-response relationships and subgroup analyses adjusted for smoking and other occupational risks.

study_type pooled case-control (multi-center) analysis
population_description Pooled dataset of 13,304 lung cancer cases and 16,282 controls (after exclusions), from 41 centers in 13 countries (data collected 1985–2005). 81.3% men (cases), 18.7% women; median age ≈64 (men) and 62 (women). Smoking distribution among cases: 65.2% current, 28.8% former, 6.0% never; among controls: 31.6% current, 39.1% former, 29.3% never. Occupational histories coded to ISCO-68 and linked to a job-exposure matrix (DOM-JEM) for DME (no/low/high scores).
risk_factors_identified Occupational diesel motor exhaust (DME) cumulative exposure (highest quartile associated with increased lung cancer risk), duration and intensity of DME exposure (high-level exposure associated with increased risk after short durations; low-level exposure associated with elevated risk after >30 years), cigarette smoking (pack-years, current smoking), employment in 'List A' occupations with known lung carcinogens (asbestos, PAHs, nickel, chromium, silica), coexposures in certain industries (miners, drivers). Geographic and temporal differences (region and older studies showing higher ORs) that modify background lung cancer risk.
protective_factors_identified Time-since-quitting smoking included as a strong modifier (longer quitting associated with lower adjusted risk in models); the paper notes that modern engine emission controls (low-sulfur fuels, particle traps) may reduce exposures and thus future risk, but provides no direct protective-effect estimates. Never-smoking status is associated with much lower baseline lung cancer prevalence in controls.
genetic_findings No primary genetic susceptibility or specific gene-variant findings are reported in this pooled analysis; genetic factors are not analyzed or identified in the paper (authors adjusted for demographic and occupational confounders but did not include genetic data).
behavioral_modifiers Cigarette pack-years (cumulative smoking dose) and time-since-quitting (categories including current and years since cessation) were major behavioral covariates and effect modifiers in analyses. The paper also highlights potential residual confounding from smoking behaviors specific to occupations (e.g., drivers who smoke in vehicles and increased second-hand smoke exposure).
environmental_modifiers Occupational DME (low vs high intensity, cumulative dose), employment in 'List A' jobs (known occupational lung carcinogens), geographic region (Western, Northern, Central, Eastern, Southern Europe, North America), era of data collection (studies completed before 1995 versus more recent), other DME sources (ships, generators, diesel tools), and changes in engine technology/fuel (which alter particle number and composition).
biological_mechanisms Diesel motor exhaust particulates: elemental carbon cores with adsorbed organic materials including aromatic hydrocarbons and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs), aldehydes, and nitrogen oxides. Ultrafine particle size (<100 nm) results in high respirability and deep lung penetration; particle-bound organics have demonstrated carcinogenicity in animal studies (sufficient evidence for whole DME and particulate extracts; gas-phase DME evidence inadequate). Mechanistic implications: deep lung deposition of ultrafine, PAH-laden particles may lead to DNA damage and carcinogenesis; particle number (not only mass) may be relevant to biologic effect.
quantitative_results Key adjusted results (OR2: adjusted for age, sex, study, ever-employment in 'List A' job, cigarette pack-years, time-since-quitting): highest quartile cumulative DME vs never: OR = 1.31 (95% CI 1.19–1.43), test for trend P < 0.01. Workers never employed in 'List A' jobs (highest quartile): OR = 1.35 (95% CI 1.23–1.49), P < 0.01. Men (highest quartile): OR = 1.28 (95% CI 1.17–1.41), P < 0.01. Women (highest quartile): OR = 1.58 (95% CI 0.96–2.59) (not statistically significant). Never-smokers (highest quartile): OR = 1.26 (95% CI 0.90–1.78) (not significant). Low-level exposure >30 years: OR = 1.17 (95% CI 1.07–1.29), P for trend < 0.01. High-level exposure 1–10 years: OR = 1.28 (95% CI 1.14–1.45); 21–30 years high-level: OR = 1.52 (95% CI 1.15–2.02). Random-effects meta-analysis for highest quartile: OR = 1.26 (95% CI 1.14–1.40). Heterogeneity: crude I^2 = 32.5% decreased to 13.8% after smoking adjustment for highest-quartile effect; regional heterogeneity and study-period heterogeneity reported (e.g., studies before 1995 OR 1.49 vs recent studies OR 1.19).
counterexamples_or_exceptions Several individual contributing studies showed OR < 1 for highest DME quartile (INCO-Romania, INCO-UK, LUCA, EAGLE, MORGEN). Elevated ORs in some subgroups (women, never-smokers) did not reach statistical significance due to limited power. Low-intensity DME exposure showed no elevation for shorter durations and only modest increase after very long durations (>30 years). Excluding farmers reduced some associations, indicating potential selection bias; excluding AUT-Munich (low control response) lowered pooled OR slightly but did not negate association.
study_limitations Exposure assessment via a general population job-exposure matrix (DOM-JEM) assigned exposure by job title (no individual-level quantitative measurements), potentially causing nondifferential misclassification and attenuated ORs; inability to estimate absolute DME concentration levels or account for temporal changes in engine use within the JEM; some centers used hospital controls (potential selection bias); possible residual confounding by smoking, especially occupation-specific smoking behavior (e.g., drivers); potential selection bias related to farmers; limited power in subgroup analyses (women, never-smokers); heterogeneity across regions and study periods.
