Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-42

Name: Host Reprogramming Dominance: Metastatic Organotropism as a Host-State Transition

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of preferential metastatic organotropism (non-random metastatic spread to specific organs) across cancers (e.g., prostate/breast to bone, lung to brain/adrenals), based on the following results.

Description: In many organotropic metastasis patterns, the dominant determinant is not the intrinsic ability of circulating tumor cells to colonize an organ, but whether the organ has undergone a discrete host-state transition into a permissive pre-metastatic niche (PMN). This transition is enacted by systemic signals (tumor EVs, host bone-marrow EVs, cytokines, and immune-cell mobilization) that rewire organ-resident stromal/parenchymal and immune compartments into a fibrotic, chemotactic, immunosuppressive, and sometimes metabolically resource-rich state. Organotropism reflects which organs are most readily driven into this permissive attractor state by a given tumor's systemic signaling repertoire and the organ's resident 'sensor' cells.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Organ PMN formation is a discrete host-state transition with causal sufficiency in 'education' paradigms

Statement: If a naïve host is 'educated' with tumor-derived EVs (or specific host-derived EVs) in the absence of tumor cells, then the host organ can transition into a PMN state that is sufficient to increase retention/extravasation and outgrowth of subsequently introduced tumor cells; conversely, interrupting key host nodes in the transition prevents the pro-metastatic effect even when tumor cells are later introduced.

Domain/Scope: Experimental EV education paradigms (systemic EV administration followed by tumor cell challenge), and host-node perturbations (genetic knockouts, conditional ECM deletion, immune depletion) in mice and microphysiologic systems.

Special Cases

  1. Education effects can be organ- and route-specific; intraparenchymal EV delivery may bypass key host sensors and produce different outcomes.
  2. Some education effects may reflect general inflammation rather than organ-specific programming if signals are non-selective.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. PDAC exosome education increases liver fibronectin deposition and macrophage recruitment; fibronectin conditional knockout or CD11b+ cell depletion abrogates exosome-enhanced liver metastasis; MIF knockdown in exosomes abolishes the education effect. [e217.0]
  2. Breast 4T1 exosome education increases lung MDSCs and lung metastasis in WT but not CCL2-/- mice, indicating a host chemokine node is required for education to translate into metastasis. [e219.0] [e219.1] [e219.3]
  3. Systemic lung-cancer exosome injection induces BMEC Dkk-1 and downstream microglial immunosuppression; GW4869 blocks exosome secretion and prevents microglial M2 shift in co-culture. [e157.0] [e157.1]
  4. Host-derived EVs (bone-marrow cell EV miR-92a) can prime hepatic stellate cells and ECM deposition, supporting liver PMN for lung cancer metastasis, demonstrating host-derived EV sufficiency in niche programming. [e162.4]

Statement 1: Host sensor-cell specialization determines which systemic signals trigger PMN

Statement: If an organ contains resident 'sensor' cell types that efficiently uptake systemic EVs and convert them into organ-local chemokine/ECM/immune programs (AEC II in lung; Kupffer cells/LSECs/HSCs in liver; BMECs/astrocytes/microglia in brain; osteo-immune units in bone), then that organ will be preferentially primed into a PMN by tumors whose EV cargos are tuned to those sensors.

Domain/Scope: Organs where recipient cell types have been directly identified as primary EV uptake/response nodes in the evidence set (lung AEC II; liver Kupffer/LSEC; brain BMEC; bone immune/osteoclast axis).

Special Cases

  1. Different EV cargos from different tumor subclones/lines may shift the dominant sensor-cell type (e.g., lung fibroblast mechanobiology vs epithelial chemokines).
  2. Some organs (liver sinusoids) may be broadly permissive; sensor specificity may be less sharp than in BBB-protected brain.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Lung: AEC II are primary recipients of breast tumor exosomes and upregulate CCL2 and niche cytokines. [e219.2] [e219.0]
  2. Liver: Kupffer cells are the predominant uptake cell type for PDAC exosomes; downstream KC→TGFβ→HSC→FN cascade is required. [e217.0]
  3. Brain: BMECs uptake lung cancer exosomes and secrete Dkk-1; downstream microglia are reprogrammed. [e157.0] [e157.1] [e157.2]
  4. Bone: tumor-primed CD4+ T cells deliver RANKL to bone marrow driving osteoclastogenesis and bone loss prior to tumor arrival; systemic OPG/RANKL ratio shifts 10–20× in metastatic model. [e218.0] [e218.2] [e158.1]

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. In cross-education experiments, swapping EVs between tumor lines will shift which organ exhibits PMN signatures primarily in the organ’s sensor-cell transcriptome (AEC II vs Kupffer vs BMEC), measurable before CTC arrival.
  2. Host-derived EV interventions (e.g., depletion or miRNA inhibition in BM-derived EVs) will reduce liver PMN features even if tumor EV output is unchanged, in contexts like lung cancer→liver PMN mediated by BM EV miR-92a.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Single-cell profiling of organs pre-metastasis will reveal low-dimensional attractor states (PMN states) shared across cancers within each organ, but reached by different upstream EV cargos; this would enable organ-specific PMN scoring independent of cancer type.
  2. Transient induction of the host-state transition (e.g., acute inflammation) could temporarily retarget organotropism even for the same tumor clone, if it activates the same sensor cells and downstream chemokine/ECM programs.

Negative Experiments

  1. If organ PMN signatures (ECM deposition, myeloid recruitment, chemokine induction) are absent yet organotropic metastasis still occurs at high frequency, the host-state dominance claim is weakened for that context.
  2. If removing a purported sensor cell type (e.g., depleting Kupffer cells, blocking AEC II uptake) fails to reduce PMN formation and metastasis, sensor-cell determinism is not supported.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Purely anatomical/hemodynamic explanations (first-pass filtration; portal drainage) and fusion-hybrid trafficking are not explicitly modeled as host-state transitions here, though they can modulate organ exposure and cell behavior. [e161.0] [e161.1] [e161.2]
