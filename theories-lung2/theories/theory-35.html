Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-35

Name: Energetic–Topology Gradient of Neuronal Susceptibility

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of which brain cell types are most susceptible and most resilient in Alzheimer's disease and why, based on the following results.

Description: Selective neuronal loss in Alzheimer’s disease follows a gradient governed by intrinsic energetic load and projection topology. Neurons with high baseline oxidative phosphorylation/translation demand and long-range, dendrite-rich integrative architecture (e.g., EC layer II, CA1, posterior cingulate, L2/3 IT) are most susceptible; neurons in primary sensory/motor cortex and BA17 layer 4 are relatively resilient. This accounts for early limbic/allocortical loss and later association-neocortical decline, with primary sensory areas largely spared.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Regional metabolic set point predicts early vulnerability

Statement: Regions enriched in mitochondrial OXPHOS, ribosomal/translation, and proteasome proteins at baseline (MTL: EC/PHC) undergo preferential downregulation of these systems and prominent synaptic protein loss during AD, aligning with early tau spread and clinical onset.

Domain/Scope: Human postmortem cortex across early–late Braak stages; bulk LC-MS/MS proteomics and PET-consistent regional vulnerability patterns.

Special Cases

  1. EC layer II shows early severe neuronal loss despite a more modest ETC nuclear gene underexpression compared with PCC/MTG in one LCM dataset; this may reflect earlier cell death rather than surviving-cell transcriptional suppression.
  2. Cerebellum shows abundant proteomic changes but relative resilience, indicating protective programs.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. MTL baseline proteome enriched in mitochondrial, ribosomal, proteasome proteins; these decline early and strongly with AD in EC/PHC. [e202.2]
  2. Synaptic protein loss is pervasive and progressive, beginning in preclinical stages, across multiple cortical regions. [e202.0] [e201.0] [e196.4]
  3. Posterior cingulate and MTG neurons show strong nuclear-encoded ETC underexpression (70% and 65%, respectively), aligning with FDG-PET hypometabolism in vulnerable association cortex. [e204.0] [e204.1] [e198.8]
  4. Primary visual cortex (V1) and superior frontal gyrus show minimal ETC suppression in AD neurons, consistent with relative regional sparing. [e204.4] [e204.5]
  5. Cerebellum exhibits many AD-related proteomic changes that appear protective, consistent with regional resilience. [e196.3]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Across donors, baseline-region enrichment of mitochondrial/translation/proteasome proteins in controls will predict the magnitude of later synaptic protein loss within the same region.
  2. Within association cortex, subclasses with higher dendritic spine density (e.g., CUX2+ L2/3 IT) will show greater depletion than neighboring subclasses with lower spine density.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Longitudinal in vivo measures of cortical metabolic flexibility (e.g., ketone uptake capacity) will forecast regional resilience independent of amyloid load.
  2. Augmenting protein synthesis quality control (e.g., boosting proteasome activity) region-selectively will slow synaptic protein loss and neuron depletion.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding regions with low baseline OXPHOS/translation/proteasome expression that nonetheless show the earliest and strongest synaptic/neuronal loss would contradict the energetic set-point rule.
  2. Demonstrating that Betz cells are as tangle-laden and depleted as association L2/3 pyramidal neurons would argue against the topology component.
