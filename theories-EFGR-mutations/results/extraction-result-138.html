Paper Title: Lung cancer among women — identifying risk factors

Paper Abstract: The number of lung cancer cases estimates globally 2 million according to WHO, which represents approximately 11.6% of all cancers. The problem of lung diseases among women and women’s lung cancer is relatively not often discussed in the literature. There is evidence that there is a different distribution of histological types between sexes. The prevalence of adenocarcinoma (ADC) among women is observed for many years with an increasing tendency. This review focuses on the lung cancer risk factors such as tobacco smoking, second-hand smoke exposure, genetic and environmental factors, comorbidities and infectious agents. The declining tendency in smoking points to the necessity of focusing on other risk factors. Analysis of them within the context of morbidity and mortality can help to develop more effective screening programs.

proposed_mechanisms The review suggests several possible contributors to ethnic differences in EGFR mutation prevalence: (1) genetic ancestry / germline variation (GWAS loci cited: 5p15.33, 6p21.33, 15q25.1, 9p21.3; candidate gene KIAA0930), (2) differences in smoking history (higher EGFR prevalence in never-smokers), (3) sex/hormonal influences (estrogen receptor presence, local estrogen production via aromatase), (4) environmental exposures and lifestyle (indoor solid fuel use, air pollution, radon, occupational carcinogens), and (5) family history/familial susceptibility.
evidence_for_mechanisms Evidence cited in the review includes: (a) empirical prevalence data showing much higher EGFR mutation frequencies in Asians versus Western populations and in never-smokers versus smokers; (b) GWAS/meta-analysis reports identifying common susceptibility loci (5p15.33, 6p21.33, 15q25.1, 9p21.3) influencing lung cancer risk in Europeans; (c) a 2020 report proposing KIAA0930 as a novel candidate germline gene; (d) systematic review showing elevated lung cancer risk with family history (first-degree relatives RR~1.88); (e) biological data that estrogen receptors are present in lung and cancer tissues and aromatase is expressed in NSCLC supporting hormonal modulation of lung carcinogenesis; (f) multiple meta-analyses linking environmental exposures (solid fuel smoke, PM2.5, radon, diesel exhaust) to lung cancer risk which could interact with genetic background or exposure patterns across populations.
evidence_against_mechanisms The review notes limitations and null/inconsistent findings: (a) it reports that large epidemiological studies did not conclusively confirm greater female susceptibility to tobacco smoke (i.e., sex differences in smoking-related risk are not definitive); (b) family history was not associated with female sex in an EGFR-mutated cohort in one cited study (Gaughan et al.); (c) evidence for gaseous pollutant effects is inconsistent across studies; (d) the review is largely associative and does not provide direct mechanistic proof that the cited genetic loci or candidate genes cause the higher EGFR prevalence in East Asians—thus causal inference remains unproven within the reviewed literature.
clinical_implications Higher EGFR mutation prevalence in East Asian populations (and in never-smokers and women) implies a larger fraction of patients eligible for EGFR tyrosine kinase inhibitors (TKIs) in these groups; the review notes anti-EGFR (and anti-ALK/ROS1) targeted therapies are critical and that TKIs yield greater benefit among women than men. These differences support the need for routine molecular testing and may influence screening and treatment prioritization in different populations.
