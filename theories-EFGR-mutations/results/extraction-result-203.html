Paper Title: Mutation incidence and coincidence in non small-cell lung cancer: meta-analyses by ethnicity and histology (mutMap)

Paper Abstract: Background Meta-analyses were conducted to characterize patterns of mutation incidence in non small-cell lung cancer (NSCLC). Design Nine genes with the most complete published mutation coincidence data were evaluated. One meta-analysis generated a ‘mutMap’ to visually represent mutation coincidence by ethnicity (Western/Asian) and histology (adenocarcinoma [ADC] or squamous cell carcinoma). Another meta-analysis evaluated incidence of individual mutations. Extended analyses explored incidence of EGFR and KRAS mutations by ethnicity, histology, and smoking status. Results Genes evaluated were TP53, EGFR, KRAS, LKB1, EML4-ALK, PTEN, BRAF, PIK3CA, and ErbB2. The mutMap highlighted mutation coincidences occurring in ≥5% of patients, including TP53 with KRAS or EGFR mutations in patients with ADC, and TP53 with LKB1 mutation in Western patients. TP53 was the most frequently mutated gene overall. Frequencies of TP53, EGFR, KRAS, LKB1, PTEN, and BRAF mutations were influenced by histology and/or ethnicity. Although EGFR mutations were most frequent in patients with ADC and never/light smokers from Asia, and KRAS mutations were most frequent in patients with ADC and ever/heavy smokers from Western countries, both were detected outside these subgroups. Conclusions Potential molecular pathology segments of NSCLC were identified. Further studies of mutations in NSCLC are warranted to facilitate more specific diagnoses and guide treatment.

Mean/median age frequently 60–70 years across studies. These demographic differences (notably higher never-smoker fraction in Asian datasets) are highlighted as potentially relevant to EGFR frequency differences.
proposed_explanation The paper proposes multiple, non-mutually-exclusive explanations: (1) Difference in histology distribution and smoking prevalence between regions (EGFR mutations concentrated in ADC and never/light smokers; Asian datasets have relatively more never/light smokers), (2) differing environmental exposures across regions (radon, air pollution, cooking oil vapor, in addition to tobacco) that could influence mutational processes, (3) study/sample biases and methodological heterogeneity across published studies (e.g., variation in sequencing methods, stage of disease sampled, and study sizes), and (4) incomplete geographic sampling (absence of large datasets from some regions) making apparent differences provisional. The authors explicitly state that the involvement of environmental factors in producing EGFR versus KRAS mutations is not currently understood and call for larger, regionally balanced genomic studies.
counterevidence_or_confounds Several confounds are noted: heterogeneity of studies (techniques, reporting), bias toward resected early-stage tumors, limited sample sizes and incomplete geographic coverage (notably sparse African and Latin American data), meta-analysis restricted to genes with sufficient published data, lack of systematic reporting of mutation types/exons, potential overlap of histology and smoking status effects, and inter-study variability even within the same ethnicity. The authors emphasize that EGFR mutations are also detected outside the classical groups (e.g., in smokers and non-ADC histologies), complicating simple ethnic explanations.
