Paper Title: Prevalence and Patterns of EGFR Mutations in Non-small Cell Lung Cancer in the Middle East and North Africa

Paper Abstract: Objectives This study aims to analyze the prevalence and spectrum of epidermal growth factor receptor (EGFR) mutations within the Middle East and North Africa region, compare the findings to other parts of the world, and explore the geographic disparities of EGFR mutations across the region. Methods We conducted a literature search using the terms “[EGFR] AND [mutation] AND [Non-Small Cell Lung Cancer] AND [Middle East OR North Africa]”, using PubMed, Science Direct, Web of science, Embase, Scopus, and Google scholar. Results A total of 15 eligible studies were included and 6122 patients with non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) were analyzed. Male patients were predominant in all of the considered studies, accounting for 70.4%. Of the included patients, 65.6% were smokers and 88.3% had been diagnosed with adenocarcinoma. Overall, EGFR mutations prevalence was 17.2%. In the Middle East, the reported frequency was 16.5%, ranging from 11.3% in Lebanon to 29.7% in the Gulf region. In North Africa, the prevalence of EGFR mutations was 18%, ranging from 17.5% in Egypt to 21.5% in Morocco. The most prevalent mutations were the exon 19 deletions (46.7%) followed by exon 21 substitutions (31.1%). Exon 20 alterations were detected in 10.8% of the analyzed cases, whereas exon 18 mutations were reported in 3.4% of the EGFR-mutated patients. There was 1.1% of patients that had concurrent EGFR mutations. Overall, EGFR mutation prevalence was higher in females [females vs males: 29.7% vs 5.9%, P<.001], non-smokers [non-smokers vs smokers: 31.3% vs 9.6%, P<.001], and patients with adenocarcinoma [adenocarcinoma vs non-adenocarcinoma: 18.8% vs 6.5%, P<.001]. Conclusion EGFR mutation prevalence among the Middle East and North Africa populations is slightly higher than that seen in NSCLC patients of Caucasian ethnicity but is lower than that identified in Asian NSCLC patients. The distribution of these mutations varies considerably throughout the region.

testing_or_selection_bias Reported concerns: heterogeneity in specimen types and genotyping platforms across included studies (FFPE biopsies/resections; direct Sanger sequencing used in 7 studies, qPCR-based assays in 7 studies, INFINITI microarray in 1 study), variable exons tested (one Turkish study did not specify exons), restricted access to EGFR testing in some countries producing potential selection/referral biases, demographic heterogeneity across countries, and differing sensitivity of assays possibly inflating rare-mutation detection (eg qPCR might detect low-frequency alleles). These limitations are discussed as likely contributors to observed variability.
egfr_tki_response_by_ethnicity No ethnicity-stratified EGFR-TKI outcome data are reported from MENA cohorts in this review. The paper reiterates general literature that EGFR-mutated patients show a 70%–80% response rate to EGFR-TKIs, but does not provide comparative efficacy (ORR/PFS/OS) by ethnicity or adjust for subtype.
methods_key_details Systematic search across six databases through Feb 2022; inclusion required ≥100 patients and analysis of EGFR exons 18–21 or selected exons; extracted clinicopathological variables and used χ2 tests for associations. EGFR testing methods: direct sequencing (7 studies), qPCR/ARMS-type assays (7 studies), INFINITI microarray (1 study); specimens predominantly FFPE. Ancestry assigned by study country/region (no genetic ancestry inference).
mechanistic_hypotheses_proposed Authors primarily propose that observed geographic/ethnic differences reflect true population heterogeneity plus methodological and selection biases (differences in testing access, assay sensitivity, specimen types, and demographic composition). The paper does not propose specific germline, environmental, hormonal, or mutational-signature mechanisms to explain higher East Asian frequencies, beyond citing that ethnicity is a known influence on EGFR mutation rates in prior literature.
counter_evidence_or_null_results Within the MENA region there is substantial heterogeneity (e.g., Lebanon 11.3% vs Gulf region 28.7%), showing that not all non-Asian populations have uniformly low rates. The review notes lack of uniform association with histological subtype in some prior work (Benbrahim et al) and emphasizes the potential for assay/methodology differences to account for discrepancies. No data in this review directly refute the higher East Asian frequency once adjusted for smoking/sex, because such adjusted comparisons were not performed.
limitations_and_conflicts Limitations listed by authors include heterogeneity of specimen types and genotyping methods (some studies lacked sequencing confirmation), potential selection bias due to restricted EGFR testing access in some countries (uneven sampling), and demographic heterogeneity across the region creating heterogeneity in pooled estimates. Authors declared no conflicts of interest and no funding for the study.
