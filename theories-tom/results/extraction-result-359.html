Paper Title: Polymorphisms in the epidermal growth factor receptor gene and the risk of primary lung cancer: a case-control study

Paper Abstract: Background Polymorphisms in Epidermal Growth Factor Receptor (EGFR) gene may influence EGFR production and/or activity, thereby modulating susceptibility to lung cancer. To test this hypothesis, we investigated the association between polymorphisms in the EGFR gene and the risk of lung cancer in a Korean population. Methods We first examined the frequencies of 39 candidate polymorphisms in the EGFR gene in 27 healthy Korean individuals. After then, we genotyped five polymorphisms (127378C>T, 142285G>A, 162093G>A, 181946C>T and 187114T>C) that have variant allele frequencies greater than 10%, in 582 lung cancer patients and in 582 healthy controls. Results Of the 5 polymorphisms, the 181946C>T genotype distribution was significantly different between the cases and controls (P = 0.04). Compared with the 181946 CC + CT genotype, the 181946 TT genotype was associated with a significantly decreased risk of lung cancer (adjusted OR = 0.63, 95% CI = 0.45–0.88, P = 0.007). When the analyses were stratified by smoking status, the protective effect of the TT genotype was statistically significant in ever-smokers (adjusted OR = 0.59, 95% CI = 0.41–0.86, P = 0.007), but not in never-smokers (adjusted OR = 0.89, 95% CI = 0.45–1.75, P = 0.73; P = 0.08, test for homogeneity). Consistent with the results of the genotyping analysis, the CGGCT haplotype with the 181946C allele was associated with a significantly increased risk of lung cancer compared to the CGGTT haplotype carrying the 181946T allele (adjusted OR = 1.50, 95% CI = 1.09–2.07, P = 0.012 and Bonferroni corrected P-value = 0.048). Conclusion These results suggest that the EGFR polymorphisms, particularly the 181945C>T polymorphism, could be used as markers for the genetic susceptibility to lung cancer.

gene_environment_interactions The authors report a stratified association of the EGFR 181946 TT genotype with reduced lung cancer risk in ever-smokers: adjusted OR = 0.59 (95% CI 0.41–0.86), P = 0.007; in never-smokers adjusted OR = 0.89 (95% CI 0.45–1.75), P = 0.73. testing_or_selection_bias Potential selection biases acknowledged: controls were volunteers at a general health check-up center (participation rate 54.9% among invited; from 3065 volunteers, 582 frequency-matched controls were randomly selected), cases were newly diagnosed consecutive patients at a single hospital (no age/sex/histology restrictions). All participants were residents of Daegu region; possible referral/selection and regional generalizability limitations. The authors matched cases and controls by age and sex and adjusted analyses for pack-years of smoking. Genotyping was performed blind to case/control status and 10% of samples were re-genotyped with 100% concordance.

methods_key_details Germline genotyping by PCR-RFLP (primers and restriction enzymes specified per SNP), PCR conditions detailed, digestion enzymes: BstUI (127378C>T), BstNI (142285G>A), BanII (162093G>A), MlyI (181946C>T), AciI (187114T>C). mechanistic_hypotheses_proposed The authors propose that the observed association of the synonymous 181946C>T with lung cancer risk likely reflects linkage disequilibrium with an untyped functional EGFR variant that affects EGFR expression or activity; they also suggest a possible gene–environment interaction with tobacco exposure based on stratified results (stronger protective association in ever-smokers).
counter_evidence_or_null_results No association was observed between the nonsynonymous R521K (142285G>A) polymorphism and lung cancer risk in this Korean cohort, despite database-reported inter-ethnic allele frequency differences. The EGFR intron1 (CA)n repeat polymorphism, reported to affect EGFR transcription elsewhere, was previously found by the authors to have no association with lung cancer in Koreans (cited). The -216G>T promoter polymorphism, shown functional in other populations, was rare (1.9%) in the preliminary Korean sample and thus not analyzed here.
limitations_and_conflicts Limitations: single-region Korean population limiting cross-ancestry inference; study designed to examine common variants only (MAF>10%) so rare functional variants not assessed; haplotype estimation may be unreliable because the five polymorphisms were not in strong LD; potential type I error from multiple subgroup analyses and limited power in subgroups (never-smokers); functional consequences of associated synonymous SNP not demonstrated.
