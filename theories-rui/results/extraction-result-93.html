Paper Title: Spatial representation of temporal information through spike-timing-dependent plasticity.

Paper Venue: Physical review. E, Statistical, nonlinear, and soft matter physics

Paper TL;DR: The dependence of learning success on entrainment time, system size, and presence of noise is investigated and a mechanism based on spike-timing-dependent plasticity (STDP) of synapses to store, retrieve and predict temporal sequences is suggested.

Paper Abstract: We suggest a mechanism based on spike-timing-dependent plasticity (STDP) of synapses to store, retrieve and predict temporal sequences. The mechanism is demonstrated in a model system of simplified integrate-and-fire type neurons densely connected by STDP synapses. All synapses are modified according to the so-called normal STDP rule observed in various real biological synapses. After conditioning through repeated input of a limited number of temporal sequences, the system is able to complete the temporal sequence upon receiving the input of a fraction of them. This is an example of effective unsupervised learning in a biologically realistic system. We investigate the dependence of learning success on entrainment time, system size, and presence of noise. Possible applications include learning of motor sequences, recognition and prediction of temporal sensory information in the visual as well as the auditory system, and late processing in the olfactory system of insects.

key_findings The STDP rule transforms temporal order of spikes into directed spatial patterns of synaptic strengths (strengthened synapses along the activation order) enabling pattern completion: a partial cue (2–3 spikes) can cause autonomous completion of the trained sequence. The model is robust to substantial high-frequency membrane noise and to stochastic (Poisson) input to some extent. Capacity in the simulated n=50, k=8 setup was ≈5–6 random sequences before recall errors rose appreciably.

Learning reached steady state after ~1600·Δt (Δt = 10–20 ms used). Typical performance: for n=50, k=8, capacity estimate ≈5–6 sequences (heuristic allowing ~1 recall error). Recall cues of 2 inputs produced completion up to ~7 neurons before global inhibition halted activity. Noise tests: input Poisson rates λ_on varied 60–160 Hz; membrane white noise σ tested 0.2–1.5 mV with little effect on learning.
slow_vs_fast_learning True
consolidation_mechanism No explicit biological consolidation mechanism (e.g., transfer to slower storage) is implemented. The model uses slow exponential decay (τ_g = 200 s) back toward baseline and sigmoid saturation for bounding; repeated stimulation produces a dynamical steady state that mimics retention while input persists. The paper explicitly notes this decay is unrealistically fast biologically and that true long-term consolidation (hours-to-days) is not modeled; alternative mechanisms (synaptic competition, multiplicative rules) are discussed as possibilities for realistic saturation/consolidation.
