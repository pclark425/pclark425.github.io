Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1

Name: Hierarchical Coarse-Graining and Refinement Theory for Deterministic Folding

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of how proteins can be folded algorithmically and deterministically without using black-box machine learning solutions, based on the following results.

Description: Protein folding can be achieved deterministically through iterative cycles between coarse-grained exploration and all-atom refinement, where coarse-grained models capture essential folding driving forces (hydrophobic collapse, backbone hydrogen bonding, secondary structure propensities) through 2-5 interaction sites per residue, while all-atom refinement enforces detailed stereochemical constraints. The theory posits that effective deterministic folding requires: (1) physically meaningful coarse-graining preserving energetic hierarchies (native contact patterns, secondary structure preferences, and hydrophobic/electrostatic balance), (2) efficient global or enhanced sampling at the coarse-grained level to locate low-energy basins, (3) reliable reconstruction protocols using harmonic constraints (40-2000 kcal/mol·Å²) and equilibration (0.01-1 ns), and (4) iterative feedback between scales enabling progressive convergence to native-like structures (<2Å RMSD) within computationally tractable timescales (hours to days on modest computational resources).

Statement 0: Coarse-Grained Speedup and Preservation Law

Statement: Coarse-grained protein representations with 2-5 interaction sites per residue (C-alpha plus side-chain centers and/or peptide groups) enable conformational exploration 10²-10⁴ times faster than all-atom molecular dynamics while preserving essential folding thermodynamics, provided the coarse-graining: (a) maintains backbone connectivity with fixed or harmonically constrained virtual bonds, (b) represents side-chain interactions through united pseudoatoms or analytically integrated discrete states, (c) employs effective potentials derived from statistical analysis of protein structures (knowledge-based) or mean-field averaging of all-atom interactions (physics-based PMFs), and (d) includes implicit or continuum solvent representations that capture hydrophobic effects and electrostatic screening.

Domain/Scope: Applies to globular, single-domain proteins ranging from 10-200 residues in implicit solvent or continuum solvent representations. Performance validated primarily for fast-folding proteins with folding times <1ms and well-defined native states. Effectiveness may degrade for: membrane proteins (requiring specialized implicit membrane models), intrinsically disordered proteins (lacking single native basin), multi-domain proteins with domain-domain interactions (requiring domain decomposition), and proteins with critical metal coordination or disulfide bonds (requiring explicit constraints).

Special Cases

  1. Performance degrades for proteins >200 residues without domain decomposition strategies; UNRES and Upside tests primarily on <100 residue proteins
  2. Requires special explicit treatment for disulfide bonds (cysteine bridges) and metal coordination sites which cannot be adequately represented by standard CG beads
  3. May miss fine conformational details critical for ligand binding site geometry or allosteric mechanisms
  4. Speedup factors decrease for slow-folding proteins or those requiring long timescales to sample critical rare events
  5. Implicit solvent models (generalized Born, SASA-based) used in most CG implementations cannot capture explicit denaturant effects without reparameterization

Statement 1: All-Atom Refinement Necessity and Constraint Law

Statement: Coarse-grained predictions must undergo all-atom refinement through constrained minimization or short MD equilibration to achieve sub-2Å backbone accuracy, with refinement success quantitatively dependent on: (a) harmonic positional constraints on backbone C-alpha atoms (spring constants 1000-2000 kcal/mol·Å² for strong constraints) and side-chain anchor atoms (40-1000 kcal/mol·Å² depending on residue flexibility), (b) equilibration duration of 0.01-1ns sufficient to relieve steric clashes while preserving CG-predicted geometry, (c) quality and structural similarity of template structure used for reconstruction (reconstruction accuracy decreases with template RMSD from target), and (d) appropriate choice of all-atom force field (CHARMM27, AMBER ff99SB) and implicit or explicit solvent model. Without refinement, CG models produce physically unrealistic atomic geometries (steric clashes, incorrect bond angles) despite correct backbone topology.

Domain/Scope: Applies to reconstruction of folded, partially folded, or near-native conformations from CG models into all-atom representations. Most reliable for conformations within ~5-8Å RMSD of native or template structures. Less reliable for highly extended unfolded states, molten globule conformations far from any template, or conformations with extensive non-native contacts. Performance validated for proteins 10-200 residues with standard amino acids.

Special Cases

  1. Reconstruction accuracy systematically lower for flexible charged side chains (Arg, Lys, Glu, Asp) which have multiple low-energy rotamers; SCAAL shows particular improvement for large side chains (R, H, W, K, F, Y)
  2. Template-based methods like SCAAL require selection of appropriate atomistic template structure; performance degrades if no suitable template exists
  3. CG conformations with severe steric violations or physically unrealistic geometries (e.g., >2Å deviation from ideal bond lengths/angles) may not successfully refine even with strong constraints
  4. Reconstruction of unfolded states at high temperature shows average 1.33Å heavy-atom RMSD for SCAAL, ~30% better than rotamer-library PULCHRA method
  5. Short equilibration times (0.01-0.05ns) sufficient for constraint-based reconstructions; longer times (0.5-1ns) used for explicit-solvent refinements in MultiSCAAL

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  2. SCAAL outperforms rotamer-library based PULCHRA by approximately 30% in RMSD on reconstruction of unfolded conformations, particularly for residues with large side chains (R, H, W, K, F, Y, E) [e15.3] [e15.14]

Statement 2: Iterative Multiscale Feedback and Convergence Law

Statement: Optimal deterministic folding performance requires iterative feedback cycles between coarse-grained and all-atom scales, with each cycle comprising: (1) CG simulation (MD, Monte Carlo, or global optimization) generates candidate conformations or target structures, (2) all-atom refinement via constrained minimization/MD produces detailed structures, (3) structural features (contact maps, C-alpha positions, backbone geometry) extracted from refined structures update CG model parameters or targets, (4) cycle repeats until convergence defined by: backbone RMSD changes <0.5Å between consecutive cycles, energy stabilization within thermal fluctuations (~kT), or contact map/order parameter plateaus. This iterative feedback enables progressive improvement and achieves final accuracies (1-3Å RMSD) beyond what either single-scale approach can achieve independently, through: (a) CG exploration locating correct topological basins, (b) AA refinement enforcing realistic geometry, and (c) feedback correcting CG biases based on refined structures.

Domain/Scope: Demonstrated primarily for small single-domain proteins (36-75 residues) with well-defined native states and relatively simple topologies (helical bundles, small beta proteins). Convergence behavior well-characterized for proteins achieving folded states within 100-200 iteration cycles. Scalability to larger proteins (>200 residues), multi-domain systems, or proteins with complex folding pathways (multiple intermediates, significant frustration) requires domain decomposition or hierarchical strategies not fully validated. Most successful for cases where CG model can locate correct topological basin within initial search.

Special Cases

  1. Convergence may be slow or oscillatory for frustrated proteins with multiple competing low-energy states of similar stability; mirror-image folds can trap iterations
  2. Requires known native structure or high-quality structural target for defining contact maps or constructing Q-matrices in some implementations (e.g., structure-based CG models)
  3. Number of required cycles scales with: initial distance from native (more cycles needed for poor initial structures), protein size (larger proteins require more cycles), and complexity of energy landscape (frustrated proteins require more cycles)
  4. Self-consistent approaches like SCPS iterate until pathway density matrices stabilize (Frobenius norm changes below threshold) rather than simple RMSD convergence
  5. CG-MD cycle for Villin converged in ~150 folding steps, with each step involving CG optimization → TMD → MD equilibration
  6. SCPS requires sufficient initial trial trajectories to build representative average path; convergence depends on quality of mean-field approximation

  2. CG-MD cycle uses contact map from MD-refined structures to update CG Laplacian matrix, enabling nonnative contacts to be incorporated as folding progresses [e2.0] [e2.1]

Statement 3: Physical Potential Derivation Requirement

Statement: Effective coarse-grained potentials for deterministic folding must be derived from either: (a) statistical analysis of native protein structures (knowledge-based potentials from PDB frequencies, e.g., Betancourt-Thirumalai), (b) Boltzmann inversion of pair correlation functions from all-atom explicit-solvent MD simulations (PMF derivation), (c) force-matching to reproduce all-atom forces on CG sites, or (d) mean-field averaging of many-body all-atom interactions with thermodynamic consistency. Purely phenomenological or ad-hoc parameterizations without physical or statistical grounding produce CG models that fail to preserve folding thermodynamics, leading to incorrect free-energy landscapes, non-physical transition states, or inability to discriminate native from non-native states.

Domain/Scope: Applies to coarse-grained force field development for protein folding simulations. Most extensively validated for implicit-solvent CG models of globular proteins. Requirement particularly critical for ab initio folding predictions without strong native-biasing terms. Less stringent for structure-based models (Go-type) which explicitly bias toward known native contacts.

Special Cases

  1. Knowledge-based potentials derived from PDB may not accurately represent unfolded state or non-native conformations due to native-structure bias in training data
  2. PMF-derived potentials are system-specific (depend on solvent conditions, temperature, concentration) and may require reparameterization for different chemical environments
  3. Force-matching approaches require extensive all-atom reference simulations and may not capture weak long-range interactions if training data insufficient
  4. Mean-field approximations (like UNRES) may miss important many-body correlations, requiring cooperative correction terms (U_corr in UNRES)
  5. Transferability of potentials across different protein classes (all-alpha, all-beta, alpha+beta) not guaranteed and may require class-specific parameterizations

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. UNRES force field uses Boltzmann inversion of distributions from 195 high-resolution PDB structures to derive statistical potentials for side-chain and peptide interactions, providing physically-grounded effective energies [e3.1]
  2. UNRES includes cooperative multibody correction term (U_corr) to account for correlations beyond mean-field approximation, improving native state discrimination [e3.1]
  3. MultiSCAAL derives CG potentials by: computing pair correlation functions g_ij(r) from explicit-solvent all-atom MD (50ns per amino-acid pair), Boltzmann inversion to get PMFs U_ij(r*) = -kT ln[g_ij(r*)/ρ_0], fitting to Betancourt-Thirumalai reference via offset V_o [e15.0] [e15.1] [e15.11]
  4. PMF-derived epsilon_ij from MultiSCAAL correlate with Betancourt-Thirumalai statistical potential with R=0.79, showing consistency between different derivation approaches [e15.1]
  5. MultiSCAAL PMF derivation enables capturing chemical interference (denaturant effects) by reparameterizing from explicit 8M urea simulations, demonstrating solvent-specific potential adjustment [e15.0] [e15.1]
  6. Betancourt-Thirumalai knowledge-based potential used directly for SCM aqueous simulations, providing validated statistical reference energies [e15.5]
  7. Upside trains spline-based pairwise and environment potentials using contrastive divergence on trajectory data from 456 proteins, learning physically consistent parameters from dynamical data [e11.0] [e11.1]
  8. Force-matching and iterative Boltzmann inversion mentioned as established coarse-graining parameterization strategies requiring extensive atomistic reference data [e2.6]
  9. Structure-based SMOG models derive parameters from native structure (bond lengths, angles, contact distances) using shadow algorithm for contact detection [e5.2] [e5.4]
  10. Teaching computers to fold proteins study uses Boltzmann learning to optimize energy function parameters by maximizing thermodynamic stability of native states in training ensemble [e14.0]
  11. Z-score optimization and related discriminative approaches mentioned as alternative but potentially problematic parameterization strategies that may not properly account for thermodynamic ensembles [e11.11]
