Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-31

Name: Energetic–Geometry Selective Vulnerability (EGSV) of Neurons in Alzheimer’s Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of which brain cell types are most susceptible and most resilient in Alzheimer's disease and why, based on the following results.

Description: Neuronal susceptibility in Alzheimer’s disease follows an energetic–geometry rule: long-range, high-surface-area, high-synapse-load projection neurons embedded in metabolically intensive networks (e.g., medial temporal lobe and posterior cingulate cortex) are earliest and most vulnerable because they enter a coordinated down-regulation of nuclear-encoded mitochondrial electron-transport-chain (ETC) and import (TIMM/TOMM) genes while undergoing early synaptic proteome loss; conversely, neurons in primary sensory/motor cortices and specific projection subclasses (e.g., Betz cells) are relatively resilient. Laminar/subfield patterns (entorhinal layer II/IV and CA1 vulnerable; CA3/dentate more resilient) reflect this principle; network hyperexcitability further amplifies energetic stress and accelerates degeneration in susceptible classes.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Mitochondrial transcriptional downshift threshold predicts regional neuronal vulnerability

Statement: In cortical and hippocampal regions where ≥50% of nuclear-encoded ETC/translocase genes are underexpressed in morphologically non–tangle-bearing pyramidal neurons, those neuronal populations are selectively vulnerable and show early synaptic protein loss and/or cell loss in AD; where ≤20% of these genes are underexpressed, neurons are relatively resilient.

Domain/Scope: Human postmortem late-onset AD; laser-capture–profiled layer III pyramidal neurons from posterior cingulate, middle temporal gyrus, visual cortex, superior frontal gyrus; hippocampal CA1 neurons; bulk cortical proteomes from entorhinal/parahippocampal cortex and neocortex across control→AsymAD→AD. Applies to regional/laminar comparisons (population-level), not to single-synapse physiology.

Special Cases

  1. Entorhinal cortex (EC) layer II stellate neurons show ~23% ETC downshift yet are among the earliest to die, indicating that additional drivers (e.g., local tau vulnerability, circuit topology) can override the metabolic threshold.
  2. Late-stage disease may involve visual cortex despite early sparing; thresholds describe early-to-mid-stage selectivity patterns.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Posterior cingulate cortex (PCC) layer III neurons: 70% of nuclear-encoded ETC/translocase genes underexpressed; protein decreases in ETC complexes I–V confirmed. [e204.0] [e198.8]
  2. Middle temporal gyrus (MTG) layer III neurons: 65% of nuclear-encoded ETC/translocase genes underexpressed. [e204.1]
  3. Hippocampal CA1 pyramidal neurons (non–tangle-bearing): ~61% nuclear-encoded ETC/translocase genes underexpressed. [e204.2] [e198.0] [e190.1] [e191.0]
  4. Primary visual cortex (V1) layer III neurons: 16% underexpressed (relative resilience); superior frontal gyrus: ~5–10% underexpressed. [e204.4] [e204.5]
  5. Bulk proteomics across regions shows early and progressive synaptic proteome loss, especially in MTL; neuronal modules decline in AsymAD. [e202.0] [e201.0] [e203.4]
  6. EC layer II stellate neurons show ~23% ETC/translocase underexpression despite early vulnerability (exception supporting special case). [e204.3] [e194.0] [e190.0] [e191.1] [e198.2]

Statement 1: Geometry–load law for projection neurons

Statement: Neuronal classes with long axons, extensive dendritic arbors, high neurofilament content, and high synaptic input density (e.g., EC layer II/IV, hippocampal CA1, association-layer III–VI pyramids) are more susceptible than local-circuit or primary sensory/motor neurons; CA3 and dentate granule cells are relatively resistant.

Domain/Scope: Human neuropathology across hippocampus/entorhinal cortex and association neocortex; qualitative law of relative susceptibility among principal cells during AD progression (preclinical to symptomatic).

Special Cases

  1. Betz cells (large corticospinal neurons) are large but relatively spared, indicating projection length alone is insufficient without network/topologic and proteostatic context.
  2. Basal forebrain cholinergic neurons are vulnerable via additional mechanisms (loss of neurotrophin receptors and synaptic genes).

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. EC layer II and IV show early, large neuron loss correlated with NFTs and neuritic plaques; laminar specificity. [e194.0] [e194.1] [e191.1]
  2. CA1 vulnerable vs CA3 relatively resistant; dentate granule neurons relatively resistant. [e198.0] [e190.1] [e198.1] [e198.7]
  3. Neocortical pyramidal neurons in layers III–VI are severely affected; association areas undergo atrophy. [e197.4] [e191.6]
  4. Primary sensory/motor cortices are largely spared; corticospinal-projecting Betz cells not involved in AD pathology. [e198.6] [e197.8]
  5. Large association pyramidal neurons with high neurofilament content highlighted as vulnerable. [e198.9]
  6. Basal forebrain cholinergic neuron vulnerability (loss, receptor downregulation) complements geometry–load susceptibility in cortical targets. [e190.3] [e191.2] [e198.4] [e197.6]

Statement 2: Early synaptic proteome depletion marks and mediates vulnerability

Statement: Across vulnerable regions, presynaptic and postsynaptic proteins decline early (AsymAD) and track cognitive decline; this synaptic depletion couples to energetic downshift and predicts subsequent neuronal loss.

Domain/Scope: Human cortical medial temporal and neocortical regions assayed by bulk proteomics across control→AsymAD→AD; cognition associations where available.

Special Cases

  1. APP-derived Aβ peptides increase even when holo-APP protein is stable or slightly reduced, reflecting cleavage dynamics rather than synaptic abundance per se.
  2. Neocortex shows early synaptic dysfunction prior to insoluble tau deposition, indicating synaptic vulnerability can precede overt neurofibrillary pathology in some regions.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Synaptic protein modules (B-M1/B-M4) decline already in AsymAD and correlate with CERAD/Braak; neuronal modules negatively associated with pathology. [e201.0] [e203.4]
  2. Proteomics shows >70% synaptic proteins down in MTL and >60% in neocortex during progression; synapse loss is an early and strong correlate of cognitive decline. [e202.0] [e196.4] [e196.0]

Statement 3: Regional baseline proteomic program predisposes vulnerability

Statement: Regions with higher baseline expression of mitochondrial, ribosomal/translation, and proteasome proteins (e.g., MTL) in healthy brain show earlier and larger down-regulation of these programs and earlier tau pathology in AD, predisposing their principal neurons to early failure.

Domain/Scope: Human regional proteomes from control MTL vs neocortex and progression in AD (EC, PHC, FC, TC).

Special Cases

  1. APOE protein is higher in MTL controls but isoform distribution and cohort size limit isoform-specific inference in that dataset.
  2. Not all baseline-enriched pathways are uniformly down-regulated across all MTL subregions simultaneously.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Healthy MTL shows higher mitochondrial, ribosomal, and proteasome proteins and APOE; these programs are preferentially down-regulated in MTL during AD progression. [e202.2]

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Across subjects with matched Braak stage, the proportion of underexpressed nuclear ETC/translocase genes in LCM neurons will correlate with the magnitude of regional synaptic proteome loss (by LC-MS) and with stereological neuron loss when available (e.g., EC II/IV).
  2. Primary visual cortex and superior frontal gyrus neurons in Braak III–IV will still show ≤20% ETC/translocase downshift compared to PCC/MTG ≥50%.
  3. Individuals with lower neuronal synaptic module eigenproteins in association cortex will exhibit greater measures of cortical hyperexcitability (EEG/MEG) and faster subsequent cognitive decline.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Pharmacologic upregulation of neuronal mitochondrial biogenesis/import (e.g., PGC-1α agonism) in ex vivo vulnerable human cortical slices will attenuate activity-induced spine loss and stabilize synaptic proteins.
  2. Chronic suppression of cortical hyperexcitability (e.g., low-dose levetiracetam) in prodromal AD will slow the progression of neuronal ETC/translocase downshift in association cortex neurons longitudinally.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding that primary visual cortex LCM neurons in Braak III–IV commonly exhibit ≥50% ETC/translocase underexpression comparable to PCC would challenge the threshold rule.
  2. Observing preserved synaptic proteomes in MTL regions despite high neuronal ETC/translocase downshift would argue against the proposed cascade ordering.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. EC layer II stellate neurons show only ~23% ETC/translocase downshift despite early and profound cell loss. [e204.3] [e194.0]
