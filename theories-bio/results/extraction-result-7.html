Paper Title: Self Consistent Path Sampling: Making Accurate All-Atom Protein Folding Simulations Possible on Small Computer Clusters

Paper TL;DR: A powerful iterative algorithm is introduced to compute protein folding pathways, with realistic all-atom force fields, using the path integral formalism, which samples directly the ensemble of reactive pathways, exponentially reducing the cost of simulating thermally activated transitions.

Paper Abstract: We introduce a powerful iterative algorithm to compute protein folding pathways, with realistic all-atom force fields. Using the path integral formalism, we explicitly derive a modified Langevin equation which samples directly the ensemble of reactive pathways, exponentially reducing the cost of simulating thermally activated transitions. The algorithm also yields a rigorous stochastic estimate of the reaction coordinate. After illustrating this approach on a simple toy model, we successfully validate it against the results of ultra-long plain MD protein folding simulations for a fast folding protein (Fip35), which were performed on the Anton supercomputer. Using our algorithm, computing a folding trajectory for this protein requires only 1000 core hours, a computational load which could be even carried out on a desktop workstation.

algorithm_name Self-Consistent Path Sampling (SCPS)
algorithm_description SCPS rewrites the Langevin path integral introducing auxiliary 'tube' variables (s_lambda, w_lambda) defined from the average contact-map trajectory; it then (1) generates initial trial folding trajectories via standard rMD, (2) computes the average contact map from successful trials, (3) constructs collective coordinates s_lambda(X), w_lambda(X) (large-lambda tube variables) that measure progress along and distance from the average path, (4) runs a new ensemble of biased rMD-like simulations using biasing forces F^s_i = -k_s ∇_i s_lambda (s_lambda - s_m) θ(s_lambda - s_m) and F^w_i = -k_w ∇_i w_lambda (w_lambda - w_m) θ(w_lambda - w_m), (5) iterates steps 2–4 until convergence, and (6) selects the least-biased trajectory by minimizing the Bias Functional T[X] = Σ_i (1/(m_i γ_i)) ∫ |F^w_i + F^s_i|^2 dτ. The method uses a mean-field approximation replacing instantaneous contact maps with their ensemble averages to make s_lambda/w_lambda functions of instantaneous configurations. SCPS yields a self-consistent stochastic estimate of the reaction coordinate and is scored via the BF variational criterion.
