Paper Abstract: Sparse, sequential patterns of neural activity have been observed in numerous brain areas during timekeeping and motor sequence tasks. Inspired by such observations, we construct a model of the striatum, an all-inhibitory circuit where sequential activity patterns are prominent, addressing the following key challenges: (i) obtaining control over temporal rescaling of the sequence speed, with the ability to generalize to new speeds; (ii) facilitating flexible expression of distinct sequences via selective activation, concatenation, and recycling of specific subsequences; and (iii) enabling the biologically plausible learning of sequences, consistent with the decoupling of learning and execution suggested by lesion studies showing that cortical circuits are necessary for learning, but that subcortical circuits are sufficient to drive learned behaviors. The same mechanisms that we describe can also be applied to circuits with both excitatory and inhibitory populations, and hence may underlie general features of sequential neural activity pattern generation in the brain. synaptic_rule_description Weight changes follow an STDP-like kernel F(t) convolved with the pre-post cross-correlation: dW_ij = ∫ F(t) C_ij(t) dt. For the model to produce the required pattern of depotentiated and potentiated inhibitory weights, F(t) must be positive at t = 0 and for small positive t and negative elsewhere (the authors note they plot the negative of this function). In practice this produces depotentiation for synapses connecting neurons that are active together or in the correct sequential order, and potentiation for other connections. Learning dynamics are parameterized by potentiation/depotentiation learning rates (α1, α2) and an eligibility-trace time constant τ_w; these set the speed and temporal window over which activity history is converted into lasting weight changes.
temporal_scale Operates over multiple scales: the STDP kernel operates on millisecond-to-tens-of-milliseconds relative spike-timing (the shape of F(t)), while the effective learning (weight change accumulation) is governed by the eligibility-trace time constant τ_w and learning rates α1/α2 (seconds to many seconds per training cycle and across repeated training cycles). The paper gives typical neuronal membrane time constant τ ≈ 10 ms and indicates τ_w must be ≤ T (the time a unit remains active) so τ_w is on the order of the sequence step duration (hundreds of milliseconds to seconds).
brain_region_circuit Striatum — recurrent inhibitory circuit of medium spiny neurons (MSNs); cortical input acts as a 'tutor' (feedforward driving input).
connectivity_pattern Recurrent inhibitory connectivity among MSNs organized into clusters; synapses within a cluster and from cluster j to cluster j+1 must be depotentiated (weakened) to permit co-activation and sequential activation, while all other recurrent inhibitory synapses are potentiated so clusters mutually inhibit one another. Sparse sequential activity and clustered topology are central.
learning_type Sequence learning (learning of sparse, temporally ordered neural sequences / motor sequence learning via cortical tutoring of striatal recurrent circuitry).

multi_timescale_interaction Fast spike timing (ms) through the STDP kernel F(t) determines the sign of weight updates relative to precise pre/post timing; these updates are integrated over an eligibility-trace τ_w (on the order of the duration a unit is active, i.e., hundreds of ms to seconds) and realized across repeated training cycles. Thus millisecond-scale correlations are filtered/integrated by slower eligibility traces and learning rates (α1, α2) to produce long-lasting weight changes across many training repetitions.

synaptic_rule_description A depression variable y (with time constant τ_y and depression magnitude parameter β) modulates synaptic efficacy over time: repetitive activation of a synapse reduces its efficacy on a timescale τ_y, with recovery on the same timescale. In the model β is constrained 0 < β < 1 (experimentally plausible β = 0.2). The depression time constant τ_y is much larger than the membrane time constant τ, producing adaptation that causes a neuron or cluster to stop firing after some time and thereby allowing the next cluster to become active.

The model uses values like T_in/τ_y = 1.25 in training examples; sequence-skipping occurs when T_in ≪ τ_y, with a practical threshold around T_in ≈ τ_y/2.
