Paper Venue: PLoS Comput. Biol.

Paper Abstract: Traveling waves in the developing brain are a prominent source of highly correlated spiking activity that may instruct the refinement of neural circuits. A candidate mechanism for mediating such refinement is spike-timing dependent plasticity (STDP), which translates correlated activity patterns into changes in synaptic strength. To assess the potential of these phenomena to build useful structure in developing neural circuits, we examined the interaction of wave activity with STDP rules in simple, biologically plausible models of spiking neurons. We derive an expression for the synaptic strength dynamics showing that, by mapping the time dependence of STDP into spatial interactions, traveling waves can build periodic synaptic connectivity patterns into feedforward circuits with a broad class of experimentally observed STDP rules. The spatial scale of the connectivity patterns increases with wave speed and STDP time constants. We verify these results with simulations and demonstrate their robustness to likely sources of noise. We show how this pattern formation ability, which is analogous to solutions of reaction-diffusion systems that have been widely applied to biological pattern formation, can be harnessed to instruct the refinement of postsynaptic receptive fields. Our results hold for rich, complex wave patterns in two dimensions and over several orders of magnitude in wave speeds and STDP time constants, and they provide predictions that can be tested under existing experimental paradigms. Our model generalizes across brain areas and STDP rules, allowing broad application to the ubiquitous occurrence of traveling waves and to wave-like activity patterns induced by moving stimuli.

synaptic_rule_description STDP modifies synaptic strengths according to the relative timing Δt = t_pre - t_post of pre- and postsynaptic spikes via a kernel K(Δt). Typical parameterization used in the paper: potentiation lobe with amplitude A_+ and time constant τ_+ (e.g. τ_+ = 20–70 ms used), and a depression lobe with amplitude A_- and time constant τ_- (e.g. τ_- = 40 ms or larger). Both asymmetric (potentiation for Δt < 0, depression for Δt > 0) and symmetric kernels were studied. The integral K0 = ∫ K(Δt) dΔt (bias towards net strengthening or weakening) and the detailed temporal profile of K(Δt) determine stability and the emergent spatial kernel after mapping by waves.

multi_timescale_interaction Explicit separation of fast and slow timescales: fast processes are spike timing (ms) and wave bursts (tens of ms to seconds) that shape the instantaneous cross-correlation C(x,Δt); these fast correlations are filtered by STDP kernel K(Δt) and by burst shape α(t) and EPSP ε(t) to form an effective spatial plasticity kernel κ(x). Slow synaptic change occurs as small incremental ∂_T w(x) = η κ * w(x), accumulating over many waves (T much larger than per-wave ΔT) so that many ms-scale events consolidate into long-lasting synaptic patterning. The mapping means STDP time constants (ms) and wave speeds/durations (ms–s) jointly set spatial scales of learning (1/k*).
key_findings 1) Traveling waves map the temporal STDP kernel into a spatial kernel (K_ν(x) = ν^{-1} K(x/ν)) so that STDP + waves produce convolution dynamics ∂_T w(x) = η κ(x) * w(x). 2) For a broad class of STDP rules (both asymmetric and symmetric), this interaction produces periodic spatial connectivity patterns (dominant spatial frequency k* where Re[κ̂(k)] is maximal); pattern wavelength 1/k* increases with wave speed ν and with STDP time constants τ_+. 3) The pattern-formation mechanism is formally analogous to a Turing instability (initially homogeneous synapses become unstable at k*). 4) Receptive fields are refined by this mechanism: if 1/k* is larger than RF size the RF expands, if similar RF shrinks, if smaller RF splits into subfields; final subfield size ~1/k*. 5) Robustness: periodic patterns are robust over ranges of τ_+ (20–70 ms) and ν (1–6 mm/s) tested; analytical predictions matched simulations (log-log R^2 > 0.85 between predicted and measured spatial frequencies; numerical integration R^2 > 0.92). 6) Noise sensitivity: patterns tolerate wave-speed variability and modest background firing, but degrade when background firing becomes comparable to wave-driven spikes (observed thresholds depend on burst firing rate). 7) Burst duration matters: best robustness for bursts ~0.1–0.5 s with τ_+ = 20 ms; for long bursts (seconds) STDP with longer τ_+ is required (prediction that retinal waves with long bursts require longer STDP timescales). 8) Multiple waves and IWIs: regular periodic wave trains can impose additional spectral peaks (Dirac-comb in frequency), possibly increasing spatial frequency and robustness; there is a critical IWI ≈ 1/(ν k*) (~0.27 s in one parameter set) below which interactions deviate from isolated-wave theory.
