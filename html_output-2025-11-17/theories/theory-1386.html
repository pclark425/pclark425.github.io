Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1386

Name: Iterative Self-Optimization through Reflective Feedback

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of how language models perform self-reflection and improve their answer quality through multiple iterations of generate-then-reflect.

Description: This theory posits that language models improve answer quality through a process of iterative self-optimization, where each reflection phase acts as a feedback loop that identifies and corrects errors, ambiguities, or omissions in the initial output. The model leverages its own generated outputs as new context, enabling a form of self-supervised learning and dynamic adjustment of internal representations, even without external supervision.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Reflective Feedback Loop (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• language model → generates → initial answer, and
• language model → reflects_on → initial answer
THEN
• language model → identifies → errors, ambiguities, or omissions in initial answer, and
• language model → generates → revised answer with improved quality

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Empirical studies (e.g., Self-Refine, Reflexion) show that iterative reflection leads to higher answer accuracy and error correction.
  2. Reflection prompts elicit self-critique and targeted revision in LMs.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While iterative improvement is known, the theory's emphasis on self-generated feedback as a dynamic optimization process is new.

What Already Exists: Iterative refinement and self-critique have been explored in recent LM research.

What is Novel: The explicit framing of reflection as a feedback loop for self-supervised optimization is novel.

References:

  • Madaan et al. (2023) Self-Refine: Iterative Refinement with Self-Feedback [iterative self-improvement]
  • Shinn et al. (2023) Reflexion: Language Agents with Verbal Reinforcement Learning [reflection as feedback]

Statement 1: Self-Supervised Contextualization (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• language model → uses → its own previous outputs as context
THEN
• language model → adapts → internal representations to better align with task requirements

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Models conditioned on their own outputs during reflection show improved task alignment and reduced hallucination.
  2. Reflection enables models to re-contextualize ambiguous or incomplete information.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: Existing work uses context, but the explicit self-supervised, self-contextualization loop is new.

What Already Exists: Contextual adaptation is a known property of LMs.

What is Novel: The use of self-generated outputs as a dynamic context for self-supervised adaptation is novel.

References:

  • Madaan et al. (2023) Self-Refine: Iterative Refinement with Self-Feedback [contextual self-improvement]
  • Shinn et al. (2023) Reflexion: Language Agents with Verbal Reinforcement Learning [reflection as context]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Increasing the number of reflection iterations will improve answer quality up to a point of diminishing returns.
  2. Reflection will be most effective on tasks where initial outputs are error-prone or ambiguous.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. In tasks with highly subjective or open-ended answers, iterative reflection may lead to convergence on a single mode, reducing diversity.
  2. Excessive reflection iterations may cause overfitting to self-generated errors, degrading answer quality.

Negative Experiments

  1. If iterative reflection does not improve answer quality compared to single-pass generation, the theory is challenged.
  2. If using self-generated outputs as context does not lead to measurable adaptation or improvement, the theory is called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases where reflection fails to improve or even degrades answer quality, possibly due to compounding errors.

Existing Theory Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The theory synthesizes and extends recent findings into a unified feedback-based optimization framework.
