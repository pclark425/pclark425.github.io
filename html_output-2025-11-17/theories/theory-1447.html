Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1447

Name: Contextualized Conceptual Map Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This theory proposes that conceptual knowledge is functionally represented as high-dimensional, context-sensitive maps, where each concept is a region in a continuous conceptual space. The position and boundaries of each concept are dynamically modulated by context, task demands, and prior knowledge. Relations between concepts are encoded as transformations or trajectories within this space, allowing for flexible generalization, analogy, and context-dependent meaning shifts.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Conceptual Space Mapping Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• concept → is_stored_in → brain
THEN
• concept → is_mapped_to → region in high-dimensional conceptual space

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Behavioral and neural evidence for graded similarity and prototype effects in concept use.
  2. fMRI studies show that semantic similarity is reflected in the geometry of neural activation patterns.
  3. Representational similarity analysis (RSA) demonstrates that neural patterns for similar concepts are closer in representational space.
  4. Conceptual spaces theory (Gärdenfors, 2000) formalizes concepts as regions in geometric spaces.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While conceptual spaces are known, the explicit claim of dynamic, context-sensitive mapping and relational encoding as trajectories is novel.

What Already Exists: Conceptual spaces theory (Gärdenfors, 2000) and representational similarity analysis in neuroscience.

What is Novel: This law asserts that the mapping is dynamically context-sensitive and that relations are encoded as transformations within the space.

References:

  • Gärdenfors (2000) Conceptual Spaces: The Geometry of Thought [conceptual spaces theory]
  • Kriegeskorte et al. (2008) Representational similarity analysis – connecting the branches of systems neuroscience [neural similarity spaces]

Statement 1: Contextual Modulation Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• conceptual map → is_activated → in context, and
• context → modifies → task demands or prior knowledge
THEN
• region boundaries and positions → are dynamically shifted → according to context

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Context effects in semantic priming and ambiguity resolution show flexible concept boundaries.
  2. Neural evidence for context-dependent shifts in semantic representation (e.g., in prefrontal and temporal cortex).
  3. Behavioral studies show that similarity judgments and category boundaries shift with context.
  4. RSA studies reveal that neural representational geometry changes with task context.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While context effects are known, the explicit claim that context shifts the geometry of conceptual space is a novel formalization.

What Already Exists: Context effects in semantic memory are well-documented; some models allow for context-dependent activation.

What is Novel: This law formalizes context as a dynamic modulator of the representational geometry itself, not just activation.

References:

  • Yee & Thompson-Schill (2016) Putting concepts into context [context effects in semantic memory]
  • Kriegeskorte et al. (2008) Representational similarity analysis [neural similarity spaces]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Neural representational geometry (e.g., as measured by RSA) will shift systematically with changes in task context or prior knowledge.
  2. Conceptual similarity judgments will change when context is manipulated, reflecting dynamic boundaries in conceptual space.
  3. Analogical reasoning will correspond to geometric transformations (e.g., translation, rotation) in conceptual space.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially manipulating context (e.g., via priming or TMS) will induce predictable shifts in neural conceptual maps, which can be decoded to predict behavioral changes.
  2. Training artificial agents with context-sensitive conceptual spaces will yield human-like flexibility in analogy and generalization.
  3. Novel concepts introduced in a new context will occupy new, context-dependent regions in conceptual space, which can be predicted from prior context-concept mappings.

Negative Experiments

  1. If neural or behavioral measures show fixed, context-invariant conceptual boundaries, the theory would be undermined.
  2. If relations between concepts cannot be modeled as transformations or trajectories in conceptual space, the theory's core claim would be challenged.
  3. If context manipulations do not produce systematic changes in similarity judgments or neural geometry, the theory would be falsified.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The mechanism by which context is integrated into the conceptual map is not fully specified.
  2. How abstract, symbolic, or mathematical concepts are represented in this framework is not fully addressed.

Existing Theory Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The theory extends conceptual spaces by making explicit, testable claims about context-driven geometry and relational encoding.
