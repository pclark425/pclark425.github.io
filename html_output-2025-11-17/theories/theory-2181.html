Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2181

Name: Multi-Dimensional Evaluation Theory for LLM-Generated Scientific Theories

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of how to evaluate LLM-generated scientific theories.

Description: This theory posits that the evaluation of LLM-generated scientific theories requires a multi-dimensional framework that integrates logical coherence, empirical adequacy, novelty, and epistemic utility. Each dimension is necessary but not sufficient alone; only their intersection yields robust evaluation. The theory further asserts that the weighting of these dimensions should be context-sensitive, adapting to the scientific domain and the intended use of the theory.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Multi-Dimensional Necessity Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• LLM-generated theory → is_evaluated → True
THEN
• evaluation process → must_include → logical_coherence, and
• evaluation process → must_include → empirical_adequacy, and
• evaluation process → must_include → novelty, and
• evaluation process → must_include → epistemic_utility

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Scientific theory evaluation in philosophy of science (e.g., Kuhn, Lakatos, Popper) emphasizes logical consistency, empirical fit, and novelty as core criteria.
  2. Recent work on AI-generated hypotheses highlights the need for both empirical and epistemic evaluation (e.g., ability to generate new predictions, explanatory power).
  3. LLMs can generate plausible but logically inconsistent or empirically unsupported theories, necessitating multi-dimensional evaluation.
  4. Epistemic utility (e.g., explanatory power, unification, predictive scope) is a recognized but often underemphasized criterion in both human and AI-generated theory assessment.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While the criteria themselves are well-known, their explicit, conjunctive application to LLM-generated theories and the requirement for their intersection is novel.

What Already Exists: Multi-criteria evaluation is standard in philosophy of science, but typically for human-generated theories.

What is Novel: Explicitly formalizes the necessity of all four dimensions for LLM-generated theories and their intersection as a requirement.

References:

  • Kuhn (1962) The Structure of Scientific Revolutions [criteria for theory choice]
  • Popper (1959) The Logic of Scientific Discovery [falsifiability and empirical adequacy]
  • Langley et al. (1987) Scientific Discovery: Computational Explorations of the Creative Processes [AI and scientific theory evaluation]

Statement 1: Contextual Weighting Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• evaluation context → has_domain → domain_X, and
• evaluation context → has_intended_use → use_Y
THEN
• dimension_weights → should_be_adjusted → according_to(domain_X, use_Y)

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Different scientific domains (e.g., physics vs. biology) and uses (e.g., explanation vs. prediction) require different emphases in theory evaluation.
  2. AI-generated content evaluation frameworks increasingly advocate for context-sensitive metrics.
  3. Empirical adequacy may be less relevant in mathematics, while novelty may be more important in exploratory research.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The idea of context-sensitive evaluation exists, but its formalization for LLM-generated scientific theory evaluation is novel.

What Already Exists: Contextual evaluation is discussed in some philosophy of science and AI evaluation literature.

What is Novel: Explicitly formalizes adaptive weighting of evaluation dimensions for LLM-generated scientific theories.

References:

  • Laudan (1977) Progress and Its Problems [contextual theory evaluation]
  • Bender & Friedman (2018) Data Statements for NLP [contextual evaluation in AI]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If an LLM-generated theory is logically inconsistent, it will be rejected regardless of its empirical adequacy.
  2. If a theory is empirically adequate but lacks novelty, it will be rated lower in evaluation frameworks that include novelty as a dimension.
  3. Evaluation rubrics that do not adapt to domain context will yield lower inter-rater agreement across scientific fields.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. In highly interdisciplinary domains, the optimal weighting of evaluation dimensions may be non-linear or require new dimensions.
  2. LLM-generated theories evaluated with adaptive, context-sensitive weighting may outperform human-generated theories in certain niche domains.

Negative Experiments

  1. If a theory that is only empirically adequate (but not logically coherent or novel) is consistently rated highly, the necessity of multi-dimensional evaluation is called into question.
  2. If context-insensitive evaluation yields equal or better results than context-sensitive evaluation, the contextual weighting law is undermined.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The impact of social or institutional biases in the evaluation process is not addressed.
