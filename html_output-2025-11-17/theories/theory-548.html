Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-548

Name: Network Disconnection and Synaptic Failure Theory of Alzheimer's Disease Detection

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the cause of Alzheimer's disease and how it can be effectively detected, based on the following results.

Description: This theory posits that the earliest and most functionally relevant events in Alzheimer's disease (AD) are disruptions of large-scale brain network connectivity and synaptic integrity, which can be detected by advanced neuroimaging and fluid biomarkers before substantial structural atrophy or overt clinical symptoms. The theory asserts that Aβ and tau pathologies, as well as metabolic, inflammatory, and vascular changes, converge on the synapse and network level, leading to impaired functional connectivity (especially in the default mode and memory networks) and synaptic loss, which are the proximate causes of cognitive decline. Therefore, the most sensitive and specific detection of AD, especially in preclinical and prodromal stages, is achieved by combining functional connectivity (fMRI, EEG/MEG), synaptic density (SV2A PET, neurogranin), and network-level biomarkers, rather than relying solely on Aβ/tau or structural atrophy.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Early Network Disconnection Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → is_in → preclinical or prodromal AD stage
THEN
• functional connectivity → is_reduced → in default mode and memory networks (DMN, hippocampal circuits), and
• synaptic density → is_reduced → regionally (e.g., SV2A PET, neurogranin)

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Resting-state fMRI, functional connectivity, and SV2A PET studies show early network and synaptic changes in AD and MCI, often before atrophy. [e3910.1] [e3923.8] [e3936.3] [e4031.5] [e3902.0] [e3940.13] [e3936.8] [e3910.7] [e4025.0]
  2. Functional connectivity impairment (disconnection hypothesis) is observed in early AD and MCI, with high classification accuracy using rs-fMRI network features. [e3902.0] [e3910.1] [e3923.8] [e4025.0]
  3. Synaptic loss is observed early in AD and is the strongest pathological correlate of cognitive decline. [e4031.5] [e3936.3]

Statement 3: Network Disconnection Precedes Structural Atrophy Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → is_in → preclinical or prodromal AD stage
THEN
• network connectivity → is_reduced → before measurable hippocampal or cortical atrophy

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. fMRI studies show DMN and hippocampal connectivity loss precedes gray matter loss; functional changes are detectable before atrophy. [e3940.13] [e3910.1] [e3923.8] [e3936.8] [e4025.0]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Longitudinal studies will show that reductions in DMN connectivity and synaptic density (by fMRI/SV2A PET/neurogranin) precede measurable hippocampal atrophy or clinical symptoms in at-risk individuals.
  2. Combining functional connectivity and synaptic biomarkers with Aβ/tau markers will improve prediction of cognitive decline and conversion from MCI to AD.
  3. Individuals with preserved network connectivity and synaptic density, despite Aβ/tau positivity, will have slower or absent cognitive decline.
  4. Early interventions that restore or preserve network connectivity will delay onset of clinical symptoms even in biomarker-positive individuals.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Interventions that restore or preserve network connectivity and synaptic function (e.g., synaptoprotective drugs, neuromodulation) will prevent or delay cognitive decline even in the presence of Aβ/tau pathology.
  2. Distinct patterns of network disconnection (e.g., DMN vs. attention networks) will predict different clinical phenotypes or rates of progression in AD.
  3. Non-invasive EEG/MEG network biomarkers will be as sensitive as fMRI or PET for early AD detection.
  4. Network-level biomarkers will allow subtyping of AD with different progression rates or responses to therapy.

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals with early Aβ/tau pathology but preserved network connectivity and synaptic density develop cognitive decline at the same rate as those with network disruption, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If functional connectivity and synaptic biomarkers do not improve early detection or prediction of progression compared to structural or Aβ/tau markers alone, the theory's central claim would be undermined.
  3. If network disconnection is not observed in preclinical or prodromal stages, or is not specific to AD, the theory would be called into question.
  4. If synaptic density loss is not observed in early AD or does not correlate with cognitive decline, the theory would be weakened.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with minimal network disconnection but severe atrophy or Aβ/tau pathology are not fully explained. [e3938.10] [e3938.6] [e3938.5] [e3938.9] [e3938.0] [e3938.2]
  2. The role of non-synaptic mechanisms (e.g., vascular, glial, or purely metabolic) in cognitive decline is not directly addressed. [e3916.2] [e3914.2] [e3933.2] [e3933.3] [e3933.10] [e3933.11] [e3913.10] [e3913.11] [e3916.8] [e3916.9] [e3916.6] [e3916.5] [e3916.4] [e3916.3] [e3916.0] [e3915.2]
