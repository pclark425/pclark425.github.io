Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2298

Name: Dynamic Proteostasis-Immunity Imbalance Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory proposes that Alzheimer's disease is initiated and driven by a dynamic imbalance between proteostasis (the maintenance of protein homeostasis) and neuroimmune surveillance. When the capacity for protein clearance (e.g., amyloid, tau) is exceeded by production or impaired by aging/genetic factors, chronic neuroinflammation ensues, leading to synaptic dysfunction and neurodegeneration. Early detection is possible by measuring proteostasis and immune biomarkers in blood and CSF.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Proteostasis-Immunity Threshold Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → protein aggregation rate > clearance capacity, and
• individual → has → impaired neuroimmune response
THEN
• individual → develops → chronic neuroinflammation and neurodegeneration

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Aging and genetic risk (e.g., APOE4) reduce amyloid and tau clearance.
  2. Microglial dysfunction and chronic inflammation are observed in early AD.
  3. Proteostasis impairment leads to protein aggregation and synaptic loss.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While components are known, the dynamic threshold and its predictive value are new.

What Already Exists: Proteostasis and neuroinflammation are recognized in AD pathogenesis.

What is Novel: The explicit threshold model linking clearance, production, and immune response is novel.

References:

  • Heneka (2015) Neuroinflammation in Alzheimer's disease [neuroinflammation]
  • Wyss-Coray (2011) Inflammation in Alzheimer disease—a brief review of the basic science and clinical literature [immune dysfunction]
  • Cohen (2013) The role of proteostasis in aging and neurodegeneration [proteostasis]

Statement 1: Proteostasis and Immune Biomarkers Enable Early Detection (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → elevated proteostasis or immune dysfunction biomarkers in blood/CSF
THEN
• individual → is at increased risk for → developing Alzheimer's disease

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. CSF and plasma biomarkers of amyloid, tau, and neuroinflammation predict AD risk.
  2. Proteostasis and immune markers rise before clinical symptoms.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The integration of these markers for early risk prediction is a new application.

What Already Exists: Biomarkers for amyloid, tau, and inflammation are under study for AD detection.

What is Novel: The combined use of proteostasis and immune markers as a dynamic risk index is novel.

References:

  • Hampel (2018) Blood-based biomarkers for Alzheimer disease: mapping the road to the clinic [biomarkers]
  • Heneka (2015) Neuroinflammation in Alzheimer's disease [neuroinflammation biomarkers]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with high proteostasis and immune dysfunction biomarkers will progress to AD at higher rates.
  2. Interventions that enhance protein clearance or modulate immune response will delay or prevent AD onset.
  3. Longitudinal biomarker monitoring will detect preclinical AD before cognitive symptoms.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Novel therapies targeting proteostasis-immune balance may reverse early AD pathology.
  2. Some individuals may compensate for proteostasis-immune imbalance via unknown resilience mechanisms.
  3. Dynamic biomarker profiles will reveal new AD subtypes with distinct progression rates.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding individuals with high biomarker levels who do not develop AD would challenge the theory.
  2. Demonstrating that proteostasis-immune imbalance does not predict AD risk would call the theory into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with minimal biomarker changes or atypical progression.
