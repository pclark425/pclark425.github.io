Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2326

Name: Dynamic Homeostatic Failure Theory of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory proposes that Alzheimer's disease results from a progressive failure of the brain's dynamic homeostatic mechanisms, including synaptic scaling, glial regulation, and metabolic adaptation. The breakdown of these adaptive systems, triggered by genetic, environmental, and age-related stressors, leads to runaway protein aggregation, synaptic loss, and ultimately, neurodegeneration. Early detection is best achieved by identifying markers of homeostatic stress and compensatory failure, rather than static pathology.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Homeostatic Breakdown Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → impaired synaptic scaling, and
• individual → has → dysregulated glial response, and
• individual → has → metabolic stress
THEN
• individual → has_increased_risk_for → runaway protein aggregation, and
• individual → shows → progressive synaptic loss and neurodegeneration

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Synaptic scaling and glial regulation are essential for maintaining neural stability; their failure is observed in AD models.
  2. Metabolic stress (e.g., impaired glucose metabolism) is an early feature of AD and predicts progression.
  3. Experimental disruption of homeostatic mechanisms in animal models leads to protein aggregation and neurodegeneration.
  4. Human studies show that markers of glial dysfunction and metabolic stress precede overt amyloid or tau pathology.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While homeostatic failure is recognized, this theory elevates it as the central, unifying cause and detection target.

What Already Exists: Homeostatic mechanisms and their failure have been discussed in the context of neurodegeneration.

What is Novel: The explicit linking of dynamic homeostatic failure as the primary cause, and the focus on early detection via homeostatic stress markers, is novel.

References:

  • Frere & Slutsky (2018) Alzheimer's Disease: From Firing Instability to Homeostatic Network Disruption [Discusses homeostatic failure]
  • Henstridge et al. (2019) Glial contributions to synaptic function in health and disease [Glial regulation in neurodegeneration]

Statement 1: Homeostatic Stress Detection Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → biomarkers of homeostatic stress (e.g., altered synaptic proteins, glial activation, metabolic markers)
THEN
• individual → is_detectable_as → preclinical Alzheimer's disease

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. CSF and blood biomarkers of synaptic dysfunction and glial activation are altered before clinical symptoms in AD.
  2. PET imaging of glial activation and metabolic stress identifies at-risk individuals before amyloid or tau positivity.
  3. Longitudinal studies show that homeostatic stress markers predict conversion to AD better than static amyloid levels.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law shifts the detection paradigm to dynamic, stress-based markers, which is a novel emphasis.

What Already Exists: Biomarkers of synaptic and glial dysfunction are being explored for early AD detection.

What is Novel: The law formalizes these as the primary and earliest detection targets, rather than secondary to amyloid/tau.

References:

  • Palmqvist et al. (2019) Discriminative accuracy of plasma phospho-tau217 for Alzheimer disease vs other neurodegenerative disorders [Biomarkers for early detection]
  • Jack et al. (2018) NIA-AA Research Framework: Toward a biological definition of Alzheimer's disease [Framework for biomarker-based detection]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early markers of homeostatic stress will progress to AD at higher rates than those with only amyloid or tau positivity.
  2. Interventions that restore synaptic or glial homeostasis will delay or prevent onset of clinical symptoms.
  3. Dynamic changes in homeostatic biomarkers will correlate with disease progression more closely than static pathology.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Novel therapies targeting homeostatic mechanisms (e.g., synaptic scaling enhancers) will halt or reverse early AD pathology.
  2. Some individuals may maintain homeostatic adaptation indefinitely, remaining asymptomatic despite accumulating pathology.
  3. Homeostatic stress markers may predict resilience or susceptibility to AD in genetically at-risk populations.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding individuals with severe homeostatic stress markers but no progression to AD would challenge the theory.
  2. Demonstrating that static amyloid/tau markers consistently outperform homeostatic stress markers in early detection would contradict the theory.
  3. Interventions restoring homeostasis that fail to impact disease progression would call the theory into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with minimal evidence of homeostatic stress or failure.
  2. Familial AD cases driven by deterministic mutations may not show early homeostatic failure.
