  3. Recent advances in nanofabrication and material science have enabled large-area Casimir experiments.

ConditionDetails
IF
• FTL propulsion system → is constructed → using current or near-future materials, and
• total system energy → is less than → 10^15 J (national lab scale)
THEN
• FTL travel → is feasible → for small, unmanned spacecraft

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Estimates of Casimir energy density and metamaterial scalability suggest macroscopic effects are possible with large but not astronomical energy inputs.
  2. National laboratory budgets (e.g., NIF, CERN) routinely handle energy scales of 10^14–10^15 J.
  3. Miniaturization and automation reduce mass and energy requirements for initial FTL probes.

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Large-scale, layered metamaterial Casimir structures will produce measurable deviations in local vacuum energy density.
  2. Miniaturized, unmanned FTL test vehicles will be the first viable candidates for metric engineering propulsion.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Scaling up Casimir-based metric engineering will not trigger catastrophic quantum instabilities.
  2. Macroscopic negative energy densities can be maintained stably for durations sufficient for interstellar travel.

Negative Experiments

  1. Failure to observe any metric or gravitational anomaly from large-scale Casimir/metamaterial structures would falsify the theory.
  2. Demonstration that energy/material requirements for macroscopic negative energy densities are orders of magnitude above national lab budgets would challenge the theory.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The long-term stability and quantum backreaction of engineered negative energy regions are not fully understood.
