Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2417

Name: Intrinsic Molecular Resilience and Pathway Cohesiveness Theory (General 2: Molecular Signature Threshold Model)

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory proposes that each neuronal population in the visual cortex possesses a unique molecular signature, defined by the expression levels and functional states of key resilience and vulnerability genes (e.g., chaperones, autophagy regulators, antioxidant enzymes, synaptic maintenance factors). Selective vulnerability or resistance to Aβ and tau pathology arises when the collective molecular signature crosses a threshold of resilience, determined by the sufficiency and balance of these factors. Populations below this threshold are vulnerable; those above are resistant.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Resilience Threshold Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal_population → has_molecular_signature_above → resilience_threshold
THEN
• neuronal_population → is_resistant_to → Aβ_and_tau_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Resistant neurons express higher levels of chaperones, autophagy genes, and antioxidant enzymes.
  2. Single-cell RNA-seq reveals distinct molecular signatures in resistant versus vulnerable populations.
  3. Experimental upregulation of resilience genes confers protection against Aβ and tau toxicity.

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law extends existing gene-level findings to a population-level threshold model.

What is Novel: The concept of a quantitative threshold based on the collective molecular signature is novel.

Statement 1: Vulnerability Below the Resilience Threshold (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal_population → has_molecular_signature_below → resilience_threshold
THEN
• neuronal_population → is_vulnerable_to → Aβ_and_tau_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Vulnerable neurons have lower expression of key resilience genes and higher expression of vulnerability factors.
  2. Populations with molecular signatures below the threshold accumulate more pathology in dementia.
  3. Gene editing to reduce resilience gene expression increases vulnerability to Aβ and tau.

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law generalizes gene-level findings into a new, quantitative population model.

What Already Exists: Low expression of resilience genes is associated with neurodegeneration.

What is Novel: The threshold-based, population-level model is novel.

References:

  • De Strooper & Karran (2016) The Cellular Phase of Alzheimer's Disease [Cellular heterogeneity in AD]
  • Mathys et al. (2019) Single-cell transcriptomic analysis of Alzheimer's disease [Molecular signatures in vulnerable neurons]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Artificially elevating the molecular signature of vulnerable populations above the threshold will confer resistance to Aβ and tau pathology.
  2. Populations with molecular signatures near the threshold will show intermediate levels of pathology.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Combinatorial gene therapy targeting multiple resilience factors will be more effective than single-gene approaches in conferring resistance.
  2. Altering the balance of vulnerability and resilience factors in resistant populations may render them susceptible to pathology.

Negative Experiments

  1. If populations above the threshold still develop pathology, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If populations below the threshold are not more vulnerable, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The role of post-translational modifications and protein-protein interactions in resilience is not addressed.
