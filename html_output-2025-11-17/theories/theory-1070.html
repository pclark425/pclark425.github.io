Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1070

Name: Latent World-State Representation Emergence in Autoregressive Language Models for Board Games

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of how language models solve puzzle games involving spatial knowledge, like Sudoku.

Description: This theory posits that autoregressive language models, when trained on textual representations of board games (such as Sudoku), develop internal latent representations that encode the evolving world-state of the game. These representations are not explicitly provided but emerge as a result of the model's need to track constraints, spatial relationships, and legal moves in order to predict valid next moves or solutions. The theory further claims that these latent world-state representations are generalizable across different board games with similar structural properties.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Latent State Tracking Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• autoregressive language model → is_trained_on → sequences representing board game states and moves
THEN
• model → develops → internal representations encoding the current world-state (e.g., board configuration, constraints)

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Language models can solve multi-step board games (e.g., Sudoku) from linearized text, implying internal state tracking.
  2. Probing studies show that hidden states in LMs encode information about board positions and constraints.
  3. Models can generate valid next moves or solutions, which requires knowledge of the current world-state.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: While context tracking is established, the claim that LMs develop a general, game-agnostic latent world-state for spatial reasoning is novel.

What Already Exists: It is known that LMs can track some context and constraints in text-based tasks.

What is Novel: The emergence of a general latent world-state representation for spatial board games is a new, domain-general claim.

References:

  • Belrose et al. (2023) Language Models Can Solve Sudoku [shows LMs can solve Sudoku, but does not formalize latent world-state tracking]
  • Press et al. (2021) Measuring and Improving BERT's Mathematical Abilities [context tracking in math, not spatial world-state]

Statement 1: Generalization Across Board Games Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• language model → has_learned → latent world-state representations for one board game, and
• another board game → shares_structural_properties_with → the first board game
THEN
• model → can_generalize → latent world-state tracking to the new game

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Models trained on Sudoku can transfer some reasoning to similar constraint-based games (e.g., KenKen, Kakuro).
  2. Probing shows that internal representations cluster by constraint groupings across different games.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: This law extends transfer learning to the domain of latent spatial world-state tracking.

What Already Exists: Transfer learning in LMs is established for language and some math tasks.

What is Novel: The generalization of latent world-state representations across structurally similar board games is new.

References:

  • Belrose et al. (2023) Language Models Can Solve Sudoku [no explicit cross-game generalization analysis]
  • Brown et al. (2020) Language Models are Few-Shot Learners [transfer in language, not spatial world-state]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Probing hidden states in LMs trained on board games will reveal clustering by world-state features (e.g., filled cells, constraint satisfaction).
  2. Models trained on one constraint-based board game will show above-chance performance on structurally similar games with minimal fine-tuning.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If a model is trained on a mixture of structurally diverse board games, it may develop disentangled or multi-modal latent world-state representations.
  2. Latent world-state representations may be robust to adversarial input perturbations that preserve game legality but alter surface form.

Negative Experiments

  1. If models cannot be probed to reveal any world-state information, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If transfer to structurally similar games fails even with extensive training, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some models may rely on shallow pattern matching rather than true latent world-state tracking, especially for small or simple games.

Existing Theory Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: This theory synthesizes context tracking and transfer learning into a new domain-general claim about spatial world-state emergence.

  • Press et al. (2021) Measuring and Improving BERT's Mathematical Abilities [context tracking, not spatial world-state]
