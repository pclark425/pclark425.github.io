Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1517

Name: Functional Stable Coactivation Criterion for Concept Individuation (General Form)

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This theory posits that the functional individuation of concepts in the brain is determined by the stable and selective coactivation of feature representations across varying contexts. A concept is individuated if its associated features consistently coactivate in a manner that is both stable (across time and context) and selective (distinct from other concepts), regardless of the underlying neural substrate. This provides a general, functional-level account of how conceptual knowledge is represented and distinguished.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neuroimaging studies show that concepts evoke consistent, context-invariant patterns of activation (e.g., Haxby et al., 2001; Mitchell et al., 2008).
  2. Behavioral priming and interference effects indicate that concepts are functionally individuated by stable feature coactivation (e.g., semantic priming literature).
  3. Computational models (e.g., distributed semantic models) rely on stable, selective coactivation for concept representation.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While related to distributed representation theories, this law formalizes the functional requirements for individuation, not just representation.

What Already Exists: The idea that concepts are represented by distributed, stable patterns is present in distributed semantic models and supported by neuroimaging.

What is Novel: The explicit functional criterion—requiring both stability and selectivity of coactivation for individuation—is formalized here as a necessary and sufficient condition.

References:

  • Haxby et al. (2001) Distributed and Overlapping Representations [distributed, stable patterns in ventral temporal cortex]
  • Mitchell et al. (2008) Predicting Human Brain Activity Associated with the Meanings of Nouns [stable, context-invariant patterns]
  • McClelland & Rogers (2003) The Parallel Distributed Processing Approach to Semantic Cognition [distributed, stable coactivation in models]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Concepts with unstable or non-selective coactivation patterns will be less reliably accessed or confused with other concepts in behavioral tasks.
  2. Training that increases the stability and selectivity of feature coactivation will improve concept discrimination and recall.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Some abstract or context-dependent concepts may not show stable coactivation, challenging the sufficiency of the criterion.
  2. Artificial systems using this criterion may develop novel, non-human-like conceptual boundaries.

Negative Experiments

  1. If concepts can be individuated without stable or selective coactivation, the theory would be undermined.
  2. If increasing coactivation stability/selectivity does not improve concept individuation, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not specify how stability and selectivity are achieved or learned in biological systems.
