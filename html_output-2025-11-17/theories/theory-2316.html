Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2316

Name: Proteostatic Failure and Propagation Theory of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory proposes that Alzheimer's disease is fundamentally a disorder of proteostasis, where age-related and genetic impairments in protein quality control (including autophagy, ubiquitin-proteasome system, and chaperone function) lead to the accumulation and prion-like propagation of misfolded amyloid-beta and tau. The spread of these aggregates through neural circuits drives neurodegeneration. Early detection is possible by identifying proteostatic stress markers and aggregate seeds in peripheral fluids.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Proteostatic Collapse Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → age-related or genetic impairment in proteostasis mechanisms
THEN
• individual → accumulates → misfolded amyloid-beta and tau aggregates

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Genetic mutations in presenilin, APP, and tau increase aggregate formation and impair protein clearance.
  2. Aging is associated with reduced autophagy and proteasome activity in the brain.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law reframes proteostasis failure as the central, causal event in AD pathogenesis.

What Already Exists: Proteostasis impairment is recognized as a contributor to neurodegeneration.

What is Novel: The law formalizes proteostatic collapse as the primary initiating event in AD, not just a consequence.

References:

  • Ciechanover & Kwon (2015) Proteostasis and the ubiquitin-proteasome system in neurodegeneration [proteostasis in AD]
  • Hipp et al. (2019) The proteostasis network and its decline in ageing [age-related proteostasis]

Statement 1: Aggregate Propagation Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → misfolded amyloid-beta or tau aggregates
THEN
• aggregates → propagate_prion_like → connected neural circuits, and
• aggregate propagation → drives → progressive neurodegeneration

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Experimental models show that misfolded tau and amyloid-beta can seed and spread pathology in vivo.
  2. Human imaging and pathology studies reveal stereotyped progression of tau and amyloid pathology along neural networks.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law elevates aggregate propagation to a central, causal role in AD progression.

What Already Exists: Prion-like propagation of tau and amyloid is increasingly recognized in AD.

What is Novel: The law asserts that this propagation is the key driver of neurodegeneration, not just a correlate.

References:

  • Jucker & Walker (2013) Self-propagation of pathogenic protein aggregates in neurodegenerative diseases [prion-like spread]
  • Braak & Braak (1991) Neuropathological stageing of Alzheimer-related changes [stereotyped progression]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early proteostatic stress markers (e.g., increased ubiquitinated proteins, reduced autophagy markers) will develop AD pathology before clinical symptoms.
  2. Detection of misfolded aggregate seeds in blood or CSF will predict future neurodegeneration.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Enhancing proteostasis (e.g., via chaperone upregulation or autophagy inducers) will prevent or halt AD progression.
  2. Aggregate propagation can be blocked by targeting intercellular transfer mechanisms, halting disease spread.

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals with proteostatic impairment do not develop AD pathology, the theory is challenged.
  2. If blocking aggregate propagation does not slow neurodegeneration, the theory is weakened.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with minimal aggregate pathology or with alternative pathologies (e.g., TDP-43, vascular dementia).

Existing Theory Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: This theory synthesizes and elevates proteostasis and propagation to the central mechanism of AD, integrating and extending existing models.

References:

  • Ciechanover & Kwon (2015) Proteostasis and the ubiquitin-proteasome system in neurodegeneration [proteostasis in AD]
  • Jucker & Walker (2013) Self-propagation of pathogenic protein aggregates in neurodegenerative diseases [prion-like spread]
