Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2061

Name: Iterative Prompt-Driven Law Extraction and Validation in LLMs

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of how large language models (LLMs) can be used to distill quantitative laws from large numbers of scholarly input papers.

Description: This theory proposes that LLMs can be systematically guided to extract, refine, and validate quantitative laws from scholarly corpora through iterative, prompt-driven processes. By leveraging chain-of-thought reasoning, self-consistency checks, and external validation (e.g., with held-out data), LLMs can converge on robust, generalizable quantitative laws, even in the presence of noisy or incomplete data.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Prompt Iteration Enhances Law Extraction (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• LLM → is_prompted_with → iterative_extraction_and_refinement_instructions, and
• LLM → has_access_to → scholarly_corpus
THEN
• LLM → improves → accuracy_and_generalizability_of_extracted_quantitative_laws

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Prompt engineering and chain-of-thought prompting have been shown to improve LLM reasoning and extraction accuracy.
  2. Iterative refinement is a common strategy in human scientific discovery and machine learning.
  3. LLMs can be guided to produce more accurate outputs when provided with stepwise, structured prompts.
  4. Empirical studies show that multi-step prompting outperforms single-pass extraction for complex reasoning tasks.

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

What is Novel: The application of iterative, prompt-driven processes specifically for quantitative law extraction and validation from scholarly corpora.

Statement 1: Self-Consistency and External Validation Ensure Law Robustness (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• LLM → applies → self-consistency_checks, and
• LLM → validates_laws_with → held-out_or_external_data
THEN
• extracted_quantitative_laws → are → robust_and_generalizable

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Self-consistency and external validation are standard in scientific and machine learning practice to ensure robustness.
  2. LLMs have been shown to improve output reliability with self-consistency and external feedback.
  3. Scientific law discovery typically requires validation on data not used in the initial hypothesis generation.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The methods are known, but their systematic application to LLM-driven law extraction is a novel synthesis.

What Already Exists: Self-consistency and external validation are established methods for ensuring robustness in scientific and ML contexts.

What is Novel: The explicit integration of these methods into LLM-driven law extraction pipelines.

References:

  • Wang et al. (2022) Self-Consistency Improves Chain of Thought Reasoning in Language Models [Self-consistency in LLMs]
  • Popper (1959) The Logic of Scientific Discovery [External validation in science]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Iterative, prompt-driven LLM pipelines will outperform single-pass extraction in identifying correct quantitative laws from a corpus.
  2. LLMs using self-consistency and external validation will produce more robust and generalizable quantitative laws than those without such checks.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. LLMs may be able to discover subtle, higher-order quantitative laws (e.g., non-linear or multi-variable relationships) through sufficiently deep iterative prompting.
  2. Prompt-driven LLMs may identify previously unknown exceptions or boundary conditions in established quantitative laws.

Negative Experiments

  1. If iterative prompting does not improve law extraction accuracy over single-pass methods, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If self-consistency and external validation do not enhance the robustness of extracted laws, the theory's core claim would be undermined.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The impact of prompt design and LLM architecture on law extraction efficacy is not fully addressed.
