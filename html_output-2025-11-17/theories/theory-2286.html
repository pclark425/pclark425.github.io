Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2286

Name: Feedback-Driven Convergence in LLM Evaluation Protocols

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of how to evaluate LLM-generated scientific theories.

Description: This theory asserts that actionable feedback is not only necessary for improvement, but also for the convergence of evaluation protocols towards consensus and reliability in the assessment of LLM-generated scientific theories. Without actionable feedback, evaluators' criteria and judgments will diverge or remain inconsistent, preventing the establishment of robust, shared standards.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Actionable Feedback Drives Evaluator Consensus (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• evaluation process → provides → actionable feedback to evaluators
THEN
• evaluators → increase → consensus in judgments, and
• evaluation protocol → converges → shared standards

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Studies in peer review and collaborative assessment show that actionable feedback leads to greater inter-rater agreement and protocol standardization.
  2. In machine learning, feedback-driven calibration of annotators increases label consistency.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law extends consensus-building principles to the context of LLM scientific theory evaluation.

What Already Exists: Feedback is known to improve inter-rater reliability in educational and peer review settings.

What is Novel: The explicit link between actionable feedback and convergence of evaluation protocols for LLM-generated scientific theories is novel.

References:

  • Gwet (2014) Handbook of Inter-Rater Reliability [Feedback and consensus in rating]
  • Nicholson & Ioannidis (2012) Peer review: A flawed process at the heart of science and journals [Feedback and reviewer agreement]

Statement 1: Absence of Actionable Feedback Leads to Protocol Drift (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• evaluation process → lacks → actionable feedback
THEN
• evaluation protocol → diverges → in standards and criteria, and
• evaluators → decrease → agreement over time

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Longitudinal studies in peer review and annotation show that, without feedback, evaluators' criteria drift and agreement decreases.
  2. In collaborative scientific assessment, lack of feedback leads to fragmentation of standards.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law generalizes known protocol drift phenomena to a new domain and links it causally to feedback absence.

What Already Exists: Protocol drift in the absence of feedback is observed in annotation and peer review literature.

What is Novel: The application to LLM-generated scientific theory evaluation and explicit causal framing is novel.

References:

  • Artstein & Poesio (2008) Inter-coder agreement for computational linguistics [Annotation drift]
  • Lee et al. (2013) Bias in peer review [Drift in reviewer standards]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. LLM evaluation protocols that incorporate actionable feedback will show increasing inter-rater reliability and protocol convergence over time.
  2. Protocols without actionable feedback will show increasing divergence in evaluator judgments and criteria.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. The rate of convergence may depend on the frequency and quality of actionable feedback.
  2. There may be a threshold effect, where only a certain amount or type of feedback produces convergence.

Negative Experiments

  1. If protocols converge without actionable feedback, the necessity claim is challenged.
  2. If actionable feedback is present but divergence persists, the sufficiency of feedback for convergence is questioned.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases where convergence occurs due to strong external standards or training, independent of feedback.

Existing Theory Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The theory synthesizes known effects in a new context and asserts a necessary role for actionable feedback.
