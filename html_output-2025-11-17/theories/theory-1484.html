Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1484

Name: Contextual Modulation of the Modal–Amodal Continuum

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This theory asserts that the position of a concept's representation on the modal–amodal continuum is not fixed, but is dynamically modulated by contextual factors such as task demands, recent experience, and individual differences. The brain flexibly shifts the representational format of a concept toward more modal or more amodal instantiations as needed, allowing for adaptive conceptual processing across diverse situations.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Context-Dependent Format Shifting (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• task_context → changes → modal_or_amodal_demand
THEN
• conceptual_representation → shifts_on_continuum → toward_modal_or_amodal

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. fMRI studies show that the same concept can activate different brain regions depending on task (e.g., imagery vs. categorization).
  2. Behavioral studies show that priming with perceptual or abstract cues biases subsequent conceptual processing.
  3. Neuropsychological evidence indicates that context can compensate for deficits in one representational format by recruiting another.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law formalizes and extends context effects into a representational framework.

What Already Exists: Task-dependent activation is known, but not formalized as a shift along a modal–amodal continuum.

What is Novel: The explicit mapping of context-driven shifts onto a representational continuum is novel.

References:

  • Yee & Thompson-Schill (2016) Putting concepts into context [context effects, but not continuum]
  • Barsalou et al. (2003) The flexibility of embodied language understanding [contextual flexibility]

Statement 1: Individual Differences in Continuum Modulation (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has_trait → high_visual_imagery
THEN
• conceptual_representation → is_biased_toward → modal_end_of_continuum

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. People with high imagery ability show more sensory cortex activation during conceptual tasks.
  2. Aphantasia is associated with reduced modal activation during conceptual processing.
  3. Individual differences in working memory and attention modulate the degree of context-driven representational shifting.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law extends individual difference findings into a new representational framework.

What Already Exists: Individual differences in imagery and conceptual processing are known.

What is Novel: The mapping of these differences onto a dynamic continuum is novel.

References:

  • Zeman et al. (2015) Lives without imagery – Congenital aphantasia [individual differences]
  • Cui et al. (2007) Vividness of mental imagery: Individual variability can be measured objectively [imagery and brain activation]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Training individuals to use more abstract reasoning will shift their conceptual representations toward the amodal end of the continuum.
  2. Contextual cues (e.g., presenting a word in a sensory vs. abstract context) will modulate the neural signature of its representation.
  3. People with aphantasia will show less context-driven modal activation than controls.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Pharmacological modulation of attention or working memory will alter the flexibility of continuum shifting.
  2. In bilinguals, the language of presentation will modulate the position on the continuum for culturally grounded concepts.
  3. Developmental changes (e.g., in children) will show a shift from modal to more amodal representations with age and education.

Negative Experiments

  1. If representational format is invariant to context or individual differences, the theory would be falsified.
  2. If no neural or behavioral evidence of shifting is found, the continuum model would be undermined.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The mechanisms by which context exerts its influence on representational format are not fully specified.
