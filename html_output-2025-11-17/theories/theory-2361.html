Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2361

Name: Network Disconnection and Propagation Theory of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory proposes that Alzheimer's disease is fundamentally a disorder of large-scale brain network disconnection, initiated by focal pathology (amyloid, tau, or vascular insult) that propagates along vulnerable neural networks. The spread of pathology is governed by network topology, and early detection is possible by identifying subtle network dysfunction via advanced neuroimaging and connectomics.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Network Propagation Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → develops → focal pathological insult (amyloid, tau, or vascular), and
• brain → has → vulnerable network topology (e.g., hub-rich, small-world)
THEN
• pathology → propagates → along connected neural networks, and
• brain → develops → progressive network disconnection

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Tau pathology spreads along functional connectivity networks in both animal models and humans.
  2. Early AD is characterized by disconnection of default mode and memory networks.
  3. Network hub regions are preferentially affected in AD.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: While network models are recognized, the explicit law-like role of topology and propagation is a novel synthesis.

What Already Exists: Network-based models of AD propagation exist.

What is Novel: This law formalizes the primacy of network topology in governing both spread and detection.

References:

  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Network vulnerability]
  • Raj et al. (2012) A network diffusion model of disease progression in dementia [Network propagation]

Statement 1: Network Dysfunction Detection Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• detection_method → measures → large-scale brain network connectivity (e.g., fMRI, MEG, DTI)
THEN
• detection_method → detects → preclinical AD risk before overt atrophy or cognitive decline

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Functional connectivity changes precede structural atrophy and clinical symptoms in AD.
  2. Resting-state fMRI detects early network disruption in at-risk individuals.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The primacy of network-based detection is a novel, testable claim.

What Already Exists: Network imaging is used in research settings.

What is Novel: This law asserts network dysfunction as the earliest, most sensitive detection method.

References:

  • Brier et al. (2014) Functional connectivity and graph theory in preclinical Alzheimer's disease [Network dysfunction precedes atrophy]
  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Network vulnerability]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early network dysfunction (as measured by fMRI or MEG) will develop AD pathology and symptoms earlier than those without such dysfunction.
  2. Interventions that preserve or restore network connectivity will slow AD progression.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Network-based detection will identify at-risk individuals decades before traditional biomarkers become positive.
  2. Altering network topology (e.g., via neurostimulation) will prevent or reverse propagation of pathology.

Negative Experiments

  1. If network dysfunction does not precede atrophy or symptoms, the theory is challenged.
  2. If pathology spreads independently of network connectivity, the propagation law is called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some cases of AD may arise without clear network-based propagation (e.g., diffuse vascular injury).
