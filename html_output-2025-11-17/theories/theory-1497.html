Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1497

Name: Precision-Weighted Selection Mechanism for Conceptual Format Arbitration (General Theory)

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This theory posits that the brain represents conceptual knowledge in multiple, co-existing representational formats (e.g., symbolic, analog, sensory-motor, statistical), and that a domain-general arbitration mechanism dynamically selects or blends these formats for a given cognitive task. The selection is governed by a precision-weighted process, where the reliability (precision) of each format's predictions for the current context determines its influence on behavior and cognition.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Multiple Format Representation Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• conceptual knowledge → is_accessed_for → cognitive task
THEN
• conceptual knowledge → is_represented_in → multiple representational formats

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neuropsychological and neuroimaging studies show that the same concept can be accessed via different neural systems (e.g., visual, verbal, motor), depending on context.
  2. Behavioral evidence indicates flexible use of symbolic, analog, and sensory-motor codes in reasoning and memory.
  3. Patients with selective modality-specific deficits (e.g., visual agnosia, optic aphasia) can access conceptual knowledge through spared modalities, indicating multi-format storage.
  4. Neuroimaging meta-analyses reveal distributed activation patterns for the same concept across sensory, motor, and associative cortices.
  5. Grounded cognition and dual coding theories both provide evidence for multi-format conceptual representation.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While related to dual coding and grounded cognition, this law generalizes to all conceptual knowledge and posits simultaneous multi-format representation.

What Already Exists: The idea that concepts can be represented in multiple formats (e.g., dual coding theory, grounded cognition) is established.

What is Novel: The explicit claim that all conceptual knowledge is always available in multiple formats, not just contextually activated, and that this is a default property of conceptual representation.

References:

  • Paivio (1971) Imagery and Verbal Processes [dual coding theory]
  • Barsalou (2008) Grounded cognition [concepts grounded in sensorimotor systems]
  • Lambon Ralph et al. (2017) The neural and computational bases of semantic cognition [distributed multi-format representation]

Statement 1: Precision-Weighted Arbitration Law (quantitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• representational format → has_precision → P, and
• task context → requires_prediction → outcome
THEN
• format's influence on selection → is_proportional_to → P

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Computational models of arbitration in perception and action (e.g., model-based/model-free RL) use precision-weighted selection.
  2. Behavioral studies show that people rely more on formats that yield more reliable predictions in a given context.
  3. Neuroimaging studies show dynamic shifts in brain activation corresponding to the reliability of different representational systems.
  4. Bayesian models of cognition formalize the use of precision (inverse variance) to weight competing sources of information.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law extends arbitration principles from sensorimotor and RL domains to conceptual format selection, which is not previously formalized.

What Already Exists: Precision-weighted arbitration is established in sensorimotor control and some decision-making models.

What is Novel: Application of precision-weighted arbitration to the selection among conceptual representational formats at a functional level.

References:

  • Daw et al. (2005) Uncertainty-based competition between prefrontal and dorsolateral striatal systems for behavioral control [arbitration in RL]
  • Fleming & Daw (2017) Self-evaluation of decision-making: A general Bayesian framework for metacognitive computation [precision-weighted arbitration in metacognition]
  • Friston (2009) The free-energy principle: a rough guide to the brain? [precision-weighted inference in cognition]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. In tasks where one representational format is made less reliable (e.g., by adding noise to visual input), participants will shift to using alternative formats (e.g., verbal or motor codes) for the same concept.
  2. Neuroimaging will reveal dynamic shifts in activation between brain regions associated with different representational formats, depending on the estimated precision of each format for the current task.
  3. Training participants to increase the reliability of a non-dominant format (e.g., motor imagery for abstract concepts) will increase its influence on conceptual processing.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially increasing the precision of a less-dominant format (e.g., via training or neurostimulation) will cause it to dominate conceptual processing, even in contexts where it is not typically used.
  2. In individuals with atypical format precision profiles (e.g., synesthesia, autism), the arbitration mechanism will produce unique patterns of conceptual access and reasoning.
  3. If a new representational format is introduced (e.g., via sensory substitution), the arbitration mechanism will integrate it based on its precision.

Negative Experiments

  1. If participants do not shift to alternative formats when the dominant format is degraded, the theory's arbitration mechanism is called into question.
  2. If neuroimaging fails to show any context-dependent shifts in representational format use, the theory's predictions are undermined.
  3. If the influence of a representational format on behavior is not proportional to its measured precision, the theory is falsified.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not specify the neural implementation of the arbitration mechanism.
  2. The theory does not address how new representational formats are acquired or integrated.
