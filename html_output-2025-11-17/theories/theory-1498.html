Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1498

Name: Hierarchical Precision-Weighted Arbitration for Conceptual Format Selection (General Theory)

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This theory proposes that conceptual knowledge is organized hierarchically across representational formats, with arbitration occurring at multiple levels (e.g., feature, concept, schema). At each level, the brain computes the precision of available formats and selects or blends them according to a hierarchical, context-sensitive arbitration process. This allows for flexible, task-dependent conceptual access and generalization.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Hierarchical Format Representation Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• conceptual knowledge → is_accessed_at_level → L
THEN
• conceptual knowledge at level L → is_represented_in → multiple formats at level L

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Concepts are composed of features, which can themselves be represented in multiple formats (e.g., visual, verbal, motor).
  2. Schema and scripts (higher-level conceptual structures) can be accessed via different modalities and formats.
  3. Neuroimaging shows hierarchical organization of conceptual representations across cortical regions.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While hierarchical representation is established, the multi-format arbitration at each level is a novel extension.

What Already Exists: Hierarchical organization of conceptual knowledge is established in cognitive science.

What is Novel: The explicit claim that multi-format representation and arbitration occur at each hierarchical level.

References:

  • Rogers & McClelland (2004) Semantic cognition: A parallel distributed processing approach [hierarchical semantic representation]
  • Lambon Ralph et al. (2017) The neural and computational bases of semantic cognition [hierarchical and distributed representation]

Statement 1: Hierarchical Precision-Weighted Arbitration Law (quantitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• representational format at level L → has_precision → P_L, and
• task context → requires_prediction_at_level → L
THEN
• format's influence on selection at level L → is_proportional_to → P_L

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Hierarchical Bayesian models in cognition use precision-weighted inference at multiple levels of abstraction.
  2. Behavioral evidence shows that people flexibly shift between feature-based, exemplar-based, and rule-based reasoning depending on reliability.
  3. Neuroimaging studies show context-dependent recruitment of different brain regions at different levels of conceptual abstraction.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law extends hierarchical Bayesian inference to the arbitration among representational formats at each conceptual level.

What Already Exists: Precision-weighted arbitration and hierarchical inference are established in Bayesian models.

What is Novel: Application of hierarchical precision-weighted arbitration to conceptual format selection at multiple representational levels.

References:

  • Tenenbaum et al. (2011) How to grow a mind: Statistics, structure, and abstraction [hierarchical Bayesian models in cognition]
  • Friston (2009) The free-energy principle: a rough guide to the brain? [hierarchical precision-weighted inference]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. When the reliability of a representational format is manipulated at a specific hierarchical level (e.g., feature vs. schema), participants will shift format use at that level but not necessarily at others.
  2. Neuroimaging will reveal level-specific shifts in activation between regions associated with different formats as a function of context and precision.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Training to increase the precision of a format at a lower level (e.g., feature) will propagate to higher-level conceptual processing.
  2. Disorders affecting arbitration at one hierarchical level (e.g., feature integration) will produce selective deficits in conceptual access.

Negative Experiments

  1. If format selection does not vary by hierarchical level, the theory is falsified.
  2. If manipulating precision at one level does not affect format use at that level, the theory is undermined.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not specify how arbitration signals are communicated across hierarchical levels.
