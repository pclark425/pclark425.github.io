Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2478

Name: Connectivity-Topology-Driven Selective Vulnerability Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory posits that the selective vulnerability of visual cortex neurons to amyloid-β and tau pathology is determined by their position within the mesoscale connectivity topology of the cortical network. Neurons that serve as hubs, bridges, or bottlenecks in the network are more exposed to trans-synaptic spread of pathological proteins and are subject to higher cumulative synaptic input, making them more susceptible to pathology.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Network Hub Status Increases Vulnerability (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuron → is_in_visual_cortex → true, and
• neuron → has_connectivity_degree → high
THEN
• neuron → is_vulnerable_to → amyloid-β_and_tau_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neurons with high connectivity (hubs) are more likely to accumulate tau and amyloid-β in both animal models and human studies.
  2. Pathological proteins spread preferentially along highly connected nodes in the brain network.
  3. Visual cortex contains hub neurons that integrate information across subnetworks.

What is Novel: Application to mesoscale and single-neuron hub status within the visual cortex is new.

Statement 1: Bridge and Bottleneck Neurons Facilitate Pathology Spread (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuron → is_in_visual_cortex → true, and
• neuron → has_high_betweenness_centrality → true
THEN
• neuron → is_likely_to_accumulate → amyloid-β_and_tau, and
• neuron → facilitates_spread_of → pathological_proteins

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Betweenness centrality predicts vulnerability to tau and amyloid-β in network models.
  2. Pathology spreads more rapidly along neurons that serve as bridges between subnetworks.
  3. Visual cortex contains neurons with high betweenness centrality due to their role in integrating feedforward and feedback signals.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law adapts network science concepts to the microcircuit level of the visual cortex.

What Already Exists: Betweenness centrality and network bridges are implicated in large-scale network vulnerability.

What is Novel: Application to single-neuron level in the visual cortex and explicit prediction of facilitation of pathology spread is new.

References:

  • Raj et al. (2012) A network diffusion model of disease progression in dementia [Network diffusion and spread]
  • de Haan et al. (2012) Disrupted modular brain dynamics reflect cognitive dysfunction and neurodegeneration in Alzheimer's disease [Network topology and vulnerability]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Visual cortex neurons with high connectivity degree or betweenness centrality (as mapped by connectomics) will show greater amyloid-β and tau pathology.
  2. Selective ablation or silencing of hub or bridge neurons will slow the spread of pathology in the visual cortex.
  3. Pathology will propagate preferentially along the shortest network paths defined by the mesoscale connectivity topology.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially increasing the connectivity of a neuron (e.g., via optogenetic synaptogenesis) will increase its vulnerability to pathology.
  2. Rewiring the network to reduce bottlenecks will alter the spatial pattern of pathology accumulation.
  3. Hub neurons with enhanced proteostasis mechanisms may resist pathology despite high connectivity.

Negative Experiments

  1. If hub or bridge neurons do not show increased vulnerability or do not facilitate pathology spread, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If pathology spreads independently of network topology, the theory would be falsified.
  3. If altering network topology does not affect vulnerability patterns, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some neurons with high connectivity may remain resistant due to intrinsic molecular defenses.
  2. Pathology may also be influenced by local glial environment or vascular factors not captured by network topology.
