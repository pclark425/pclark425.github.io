Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2484

Name: Connectivity-Driven Propagation Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory proposes that the selective vulnerability or resistance of visual cortical neuronal populations to amyloid-β and tau pathology is determined by their network connectivity patterns. Highly interconnected hub populations, especially those with long-range projections or high synaptic input/output, are more susceptible to trans-synaptic spread of pathological proteins, while sparsely connected or locally restricted populations are more resistant.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Hub Connectivity Increases Pathology Spread (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal_population → has_high → network_connectivity
THEN
• neuronal_population → has_increased → vulnerability_to_Aβ_and_tau

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Highly connected cortical hubs are early sites of amyloid and tau deposition.
  2. Trans-synaptic spread of tau and amyloid has been demonstrated in animal models.
  3. Regions with high betweenness centrality in the connectome show greater pathology.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: Network spread is established, but its use to predict population-level selectivity in the visual cortex is new.

What Already Exists: Network-based vulnerability and trans-synaptic spread are recognized in neurodegeneration.

What is Novel: The explicit application to visual cortex population selectivity and the prediction of resistance in low-connectivity populations is novel.

References:

  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Network vulnerability]
  • de Calignon et al. (2012) Propagation of tau pathology in a model of early Alzheimer's disease [Trans-synaptic spread]
  • Raj et al. (2012) A network diffusion model of disease progression in dementia [Network models of spread]

Statement 1: Sparse Connectivity Confers Resistance (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal_population → has_low → network_connectivity
THEN
• neuronal_population → has_decreased → vulnerability_to_Aβ_and_tau

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neuronal populations with local, sparse connectivity (e.g., certain inhibitory interneurons) are relatively spared in Alzheimer's disease.
  2. Experimental reduction of synaptic connectivity reduces trans-synaptic spread of tau in models.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The general principle is known, but its specific application to visual cortex population selectivity is new.

What Already Exists: Sparse connectivity is associated with reduced vulnerability in some neurodegenerative models.

What is Novel: The formalization of this as a predictive law for visual cortex population selectivity is novel.

References:

  • de Calignon et al. (2012) Propagation of tau pathology in a model of early Alzheimer's disease [Trans-synaptic spread]
  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Network vulnerability]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Visual cortical populations with the highest degree of long-range connectivity will show the earliest and most severe amyloid and tau pathology.
  2. Disrupting synaptic connectivity pharmacologically or genetically will reduce the spread of aggregates in the visual cortex.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially increasing the connectivity of a normally resistant population will render it vulnerable to pathology.
  2. Populations with high local but low long-range connectivity may show intermediate vulnerability.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding a highly connected population that remains resistant to pathology would challenge the theory.
  2. Demonstrating that reducing connectivity does not alter vulnerability would call the theory into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some highly connected populations are resistant, possibly due to intrinsic molecular defenses.
