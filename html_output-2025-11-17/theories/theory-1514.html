Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1514

Name: Functional Stable Coactivation Criterion for Concept Individuation – General Theory (Contextual Modulation Extension)

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This extension of the FSCCCI posits that while stable coactivation patterns define concepts, the stability criterion is modulated by context. Concepts are individuated by a core coactivation pattern that is stable across most contexts, but can be flexibly modulated by contextual inputs, allowing for context-sensitive conceptual representations. This theory accounts for both context-invariant and context-dependent aspects of conceptual knowledge, predicting that concepts have a stable 'core' with contextually modifiable 'peripheral' coactivation patterns.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Core-Peripheral Coactivation Structure (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• concept → is_accessed → in_varied_contexts
THEN
• concept → is_represented_by → core_stable_coactivation_pattern, and
• concept → is_modulated_by → context_dependent_peripheral_coactivation

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neuroimaging studies show that core semantic regions are consistently activated for a concept, with additional context-dependent activations in other regions.
  2. Behavioral priming and semantic flexibility studies indicate that concepts can be flexibly adapted to context while retaining a core meaning.
  3. Computational models with context gates show that core representations are modulated by context-sensitive units.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While context effects are known, this law formalizes a dual-structure (core-peripheral) coactivation model for concept individuation.

What Already Exists: Contextual modulation of conceptual representations is recognized in semantic cognition literature.

What is Novel: The explicit division into core (stable) and peripheral (context-modulated) coactivation patterns as a functional individuation criterion is novel.

References:

  • Yee & Thompson-Schill (2016) Putting concepts into context [contextual modulation of conceptual representations]
  • Rogers & McClelland (2004) Semantic Cognition [distributed and context-sensitive representations]
  • Leshinskaya & Caramazza (2016) For a cognitive neuroscience of concepts: Moving beyond single concepts [contextual flexibility]

Statement 1: Contextual Flexibility Bound by Core Stability (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• contextual_input → modulates → conceptual_coactivation_pattern
THEN
• core_coactivation_pattern → remains_stable → across_contexts, and
• peripheral_coactivation_pattern → varies → with_context

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. fMRI adaptation studies show that core semantic regions are activated for a concept regardless of context, while other regions vary with context.
  2. Semantic priming effects are robust to context, but context can modulate peripheral aspects of meaning.
  3. Patients with semantic control deficits show impaired context-dependent modulation but preserved core conceptual knowledge.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law synthesizes context and stability into a dual-criterion for concept individuation, which is not explicitly present in prior work.

What Already Exists: Semantic control and context effects are recognized in the literature.

What is Novel: The explicit functional law that contextual flexibility is bounded by a stable core coactivation pattern is novel.

References:

  • Jefferies (2013) The neural basis of semantic cognition: Converging evidence from neuropsychology, neuroimaging and TMS [semantic control and context effects]
  • Yee & Thompson-Schill (2016) Putting concepts into context [contextual modulation]
  • Lambon Ralph et al. (2017) The neural and computational bases of semantic cognition [core and control systems]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Core coactivation patterns for a concept will be observed across all contexts, while peripheral patterns will vary with context.
  2. Patients with damage to core semantic regions will lose concepts entirely, while those with damage to control regions will show context-inappropriate concept use.
  3. Artificial neural networks with context gates will show a stable core representation with context-modulated peripheral activations.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Highly context-dependent concepts may have a minimal or absent core, challenging the dual-structure model.
  2. If context is systematically manipulated, the boundary between core and peripheral coactivation may shift, revealing dynamic core-periphery relations.
  3. In cross-linguistic studies, the core-peripheral structure may differ across languages for the same concept.

Negative Experiments

  1. If no stable core coactivation pattern is found for a concept across contexts, the theory would be undermined.
  2. If context can completely override the core pattern, leading to entirely different coactivation patterns for the same concept, the theory would be challenged.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The mechanism by which core and peripheral coactivation patterns are integrated is not specified.
  2. The role of temporal dynamics in context modulation is not addressed.
