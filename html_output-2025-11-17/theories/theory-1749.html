Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1749

Name: Probabilistic Contextual Expectation Theory for LLM-Based List Anomaly Detection

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of how language models can be used for detecting anomalies in lists of data.

Description: This theory proposes that LLMs detect anomalies in lists by forming probabilistic expectations for each item based on the contextual co-occurrence statistics and world knowledge encoded in their parameters. Anomalies are detected when the observed item has a low conditional probability given the context of the other items, as estimated by the LLM's internal language model.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Contextual Probability Assignment Law (quantitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• list → is_input_to → LLM, and
• item → is_element_of → list
THEN
• LLM → assigns_conditional_probability → P(item | context_of_list)

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. LLMs are trained to predict the next token or item given context, and can assign probabilities to candidate items.
  2. Language models' ability to assign likelihoods to sequences is foundational to their architecture.
  3. Empirical work shows LLMs can rank list items by contextual fit using their internal probability estimates.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law is closely related to existing work on language modeling, but its application to list anomaly detection is a new formalization.

What Already Exists: LLMs are known to assign probabilities to tokens and sequences based on context.

What is Novel: The explicit application of this mechanism to anomaly detection in lists, rather than just sequence prediction, is novel.

References:

  • Radford et al. (2019) Language Models are Unsupervised Multitask Learners [LLMs assign probabilities to text]
  • Brown et al. (2020) Language Models are Few-Shot Learners [LLMs use context to predict next item]

Statement 1: Low Probability Anomaly Law (quantitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• LLM → assigns_conditional_probability → P(item | context_of_list), and
• P(item | context_of_list) → is_below → anomaly_threshold
THEN
• item → is_flagged_as_anomaly → by LLM

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. LLMs can flag tokens or items with low conditional probability as out-of-distribution or anomalous.
  2. Hendrycks et al. (2020) show that low-probability tokens are indicative of anomalies in LLMs.
  3. Zhou et al. (2023) demonstrate that LLMs can use probability thresholds to detect anomalies in lists.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law is a direct extension of known OOD detection, but its explicit application to list anomaly detection is new.

What Already Exists: The use of probability thresholds for out-of-distribution detection is established in LLM literature.

What is Novel: The formalization of list anomaly detection as a function of conditional probability within LLMs is novel.

References:

  • Hendrycks et al. (2020) Pretrained Transformers Improve Out-of-Distribution Robustness [LLMs use probability for OOD detection]
  • Zhou et al. (2023) Large Language Models are Zero-Shot Anomaly Detectors [LLMs use probability for anomaly detection]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If an item in a list has a much lower conditional probability than the others, the LLM will flag it as an anomaly.
  2. If all items in a list have similar high conditional probabilities, the LLM will not flag any as anomalous.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If the LLM is presented with a list where all items are rare but equally probable, it may fail to flag any as anomalous, revealing a limitation of probability-based detection.
  2. If the LLM is exposed to lists with adversarially manipulated context, its probability-based anomaly detection may be subverted.

Negative Experiments

  1. If LLMs fail to flag low-probability items as anomalies, this would challenge the theory.
  2. If LLMs flag high-probability items as anomalies, this would contradict the theory's assumptions.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Anomalies based on non-probabilistic features (e.g., visual formatting, pragmatic context) are not explained by this theory.
