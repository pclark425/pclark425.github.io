Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2251

Name: Multi-Dimensional Evaluation Theory for LLM-Generated Scientific Theories

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of how to evaluate LLM-generated scientific theories.

Description: This theory posits that the evaluation of LLM-generated scientific theories requires a multi-dimensional framework that integrates semantic, empirical, methodological, and epistemic criteria. The theory asserts that only by considering these dimensions in concert can the scientific validity and utility of LLM-generated theories be robustly assessed.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Multi-Dimensional Evaluation Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• LLM-generated theory → is_evaluated → for scientific validity
THEN
• evaluation process → must_include → semantic, empirical, methodological, and epistemic criteria

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Scientific theory evaluation in human practice is multi-dimensional, considering logical consistency, empirical adequacy, methodological soundness, and epistemic value.
  2. LLMs can generate text that is semantically plausible but empirically unsupported or methodologically unsound.
  3. Epistemic criteria (e.g., explanatory power, novelty) are central to theory acceptance in science.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While the criteria are known, their formal integration for LLM-generated theory evaluation is novel.

What Already Exists: Multi-criteria evaluation is standard in philosophy of science and scientific practice.

What is Novel: Explicit integration of these criteria into an automated, LLM-specific evaluation pipeline.

References:

  • Kuhn (1962) The Structure of Scientific Revolutions [criteria for theory choice]
  • Langley et al. (1987) Scientific Discovery: Computational Explorations of the Creative Processes [multi-dimensional evaluation in computational science]
  • Popper (1959) The Logic of Scientific Discovery [empirical falsifiability as a criterion]

Statement 1: Dimension Interdependence Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• evaluation criteria → are_applied → to LLM-generated theory
THEN
• criteria → are_interdependent → failure in one dimension can undermine overall validity

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. A theory that is empirically adequate but semantically inconsistent is not accepted in science.
  2. Methodological flaws (e.g., circular reasoning) can invalidate otherwise plausible theories.
  3. Epistemic value (e.g., explanatory power) is diminished if empirical or semantic criteria are not met.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The concept is known, but its operationalization for LLMs is new.

What Already Exists: Interdependence of evaluation criteria is discussed in philosophy of science.

What is Novel: Formalization of this interdependence for automated LLM theory evaluation.

References:

  • Thagard (1978) The Best Explanation: Criteria for Theory Choice [criteria interdependence]
  • Laudan (1977) Progress and Its Problems [criteria trade-offs in theory evaluation]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. LLM-generated theories evaluated with all four dimensions will have higher acceptance rates in scientific review than those evaluated with only one or two.
  2. Automated systems that neglect any dimension (e.g., empirical adequacy) will be more prone to accepting flawed theories.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. The optimal weighting of each dimension for different scientific domains is unknown and may vary.
  2. Some LLM-generated theories may pass all dimensions but still fail in long-term scientific utility.

Negative Experiments

  1. If theories that fail in one dimension (e.g., semantic consistency) are routinely accepted, the theory is challenged.
  2. If single-dimension evaluation proves as effective as multi-dimensional evaluation, the theory is undermined.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not address the role of social or institutional factors in theory acceptance.

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing
