Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2474

Name: Network-Driven Selective Vulnerability Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory proposes that the selective vulnerability or resistance of visual cortical neuronal populations to amyloid-β and tau pathology is determined by their position and function within large-scale neural networks. Neurons that serve as network hubs, exhibit high centrality, or are critical for information integration are more susceptible due to increased exposure to pathological spread (via trans-synaptic mechanisms), higher metabolic stress, and greater reliance on homeostatic mechanisms. Conversely, neurons in peripheral or redundant network positions are more resistant.

Statement 0: Network Hub Vulnerability Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails

• neuron → has_high_network_centrality → true, and
• neuron → is_critical_for_information_integration → true
THEN
• neuron → is_vulnerable_to → amyloid_and_tau_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neurons with high centrality in functional networks show early degeneration in Alzheimer's models.
  2. Pathology spreads preferentially along highly connected network pathways.
  3. Hub neurons exhibit higher metabolic and synaptic stress.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

What Already Exists: Network-based propagation of pathology and hub vulnerability are recognized in neurodegeneration.

What is Novel: The explicit application of network theory to explain selective vulnerability in the visual cortex is novel.

Statement 1: Peripheral Network Resistance Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuron → has_low_network_centrality → true, and
• neuron → is_not_critical_for_information_integration → true
THEN
• neuron → is_resistant_to → amyloid_and_tau_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neurons in peripheral network positions accumulate less pathology and degenerate later.
  2. Redundant or less-connected neurons are spared in early disease stages.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law extends network theory to predict resistance, not just vulnerability, in specific neuronal populations.

What Already Exists: Peripheral network nodes are less affected in early neurodegeneration.

What is Novel: The explicit prediction of resistance based on network position in the visual cortex is novel.

References:

  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Network vulnerability]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Disrupting network hub status (e.g., via targeted synaptic pruning) will reduce vulnerability of those neurons to pathology.
  2. Enhancing network redundancy will confer resistance to previously vulnerable populations.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially increasing the centrality of resistant neurons will render them vulnerable to pathology.
  2. Blocking trans-synaptic spread in hub neurons will prevent early pathology accumulation.

Negative Experiments

  1. If network hub neurons do not show increased vulnerability, the theory is challenged.
  2. If peripheral neurons develop pathology at the same rate as hub neurons, the theory is called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some hub neurons remain resistant, suggesting additional protective mechanisms.
