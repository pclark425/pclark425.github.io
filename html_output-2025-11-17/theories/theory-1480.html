Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1480

Name: Context-Driven Subspace Partitioning for Polysemous Concepts

Type: specific

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This theory posits that polysemous concepts (e.g., 'bank' as riverbank or financial institution) are represented as dynamic subspaces within a high-dimensional conceptual space. Each sense of a polysemous concept corresponds to a distinct, context-activated subspace, and the brain dynamically partitions the overall embedding based on contextual cues. This allows for rapid, context-sensitive disambiguation and flexible switching between senses, without requiring separate, static representations for each sense.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Contextual Subspace Partitioning Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• concept → is_polysemous → True, and
• context → provides_disambiguating_cues → True
THEN
• conceptual embedding → is_partitioned_into → context-activated subspace, and
• subspace → corresponds_to → specific sense of concept

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neuroimaging shows context-dependent shifts in neural activation for polysemous words, indicating that the same word form can activate different neural patterns depending on context.
  2. Behavioral priming and semantic ambiguity resolution are highly context-sensitive, with faster and more accurate responses when context supports a particular sense.
  3. Computational models (e.g., ELMo, BERT) demonstrate that word embeddings for polysemous words shift in high-dimensional space depending on sentence context, supporting the plausibility of subspace partitioning.
  4. ERP studies show rapid context-driven disambiguation of ambiguous words, with N400 modulations reflecting contextually appropriate sense activation.
  5. Lesion studies indicate that damage to regions involved in context integration impairs disambiguation of polysemous words, suggesting a functional role for context-driven partitioning.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While context-dependent disambiguation is known, the subspace partitioning mechanism as a functional representational format is novel and not explicitly articulated in prior work.

What Already Exists: Context-dependent semantic disambiguation is well-established in psycholinguistics and neuroscience, and computational models show context-sensitive embeddings.

What is Novel: The explicit proposal that polysemous concepts are represented as dynamic, context-activated subspaces within a unified embedding, rather than as static or entirely separate representations.

References:

  • Klepousniotou et al. (2012) The neural bases of ambiguous word processing [context-dependent neural coding]
  • Elman (2004) An alternative view of the mental lexicon [contextualized, dynamic representations]
  • Peters et al. (2018) Deep contextualized word representations [contextual subspaces in artificial models]
  • Federmeier & Kutas (1999) A Rose by Any Other Name: Long-term Memory Structure and Sentence Processing [ERP evidence for context-driven sense selection]
  • Rodd et al. (2005) The neural mechanisms of speech comprehension: fMRI studies of semantic ambiguity [neural evidence for context-driven disambiguation]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Neural population codes for polysemous concepts will shift into distinct subspaces depending on context, even when the stimulus is identical.
  2. Rapid context switches will induce corresponding rapid changes in the neural embedding of polysemous concepts.
  3. Behavioral priming for a specific sense of a polysemous word will be stronger when the context matches the subspace partition activated by the prime.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially biasing the neural population toward a particular subspace (e.g., via priming or stimulation) will bias interpretation of ambiguous concepts.
  2. Training artificial neural networks with dynamic subspace partitioning will yield human-like ambiguity resolution.
  3. The degree of subspace separation may predict individual differences in ambiguity resolution ability.

Negative Experiments

  1. If polysemous concepts are represented as fixed, context-invariant codes, the theory would be undermined.
  2. If context does not modulate the neural embedding of ambiguous concepts, the theory would be called into question.
  3. If behavioral ambiguity resolution is not context-sensitive, the theory would be falsified.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not specify how new senses are integrated into the subspace structure over time (e.g., through learning or experience).
  2. The theory does not address whether all types of polysemy (e.g., homonymy vs. metonymy) are handled via the same subspace mechanism.
