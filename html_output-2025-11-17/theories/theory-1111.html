Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1111

Name: Theory of Iterative Self-Verification for Logical Consistency in Language Models

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of how language models can best perform strict logical reasoning.

Description: This theory posits that strict logical reasoning in LMs is best achieved when the model is equipped with an iterative self-verification mechanism, wherein the LM generates candidate logical inferences, checks them for consistency with prior steps and premises, and revises or rejects inconsistent outputs. This process mimics human logical proof-checking and enables robust error correction and logical soundness.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Iterative Self-Verification Enhances Logical Consistency (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• language model → performs → iterative self-verification of logical inferences
THEN
• logical reasoning output → is_consistent_with → premises and prior inference steps, and
• logical errors → are_detected_and_corrected_by → language model

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Human logical reasoning often involves iterative proof-checking and error correction.
  2. LMs with self-consistency or self-verification mechanisms (e.g., chain-of-thought with verification) show improved logical accuracy.
  3. Standard LMs often produce logically inconsistent outputs without explicit verification.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: While related to self-consistency prompting, the theory proposes a more general, iterative, and architectural mechanism.

What Already Exists: Self-consistency and verification are used in some LM prompting strategies (e.g., self-consistency decoding, scratchpad).

What is Novel: The explicit, iterative self-verification mechanism as a core architectural feature for strict logical reasoning.

References:

  • Wang et al. (2022) Self-Consistency Improves Chain of Thought Reasoning in Language Models [self-consistency in LMs]
  • Wei et al. (2022) Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in Large Language Models [stepwise reasoning in LMs]

Statement 1: Absence of Self-Verification Leads to Logical Errors (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• language model → lacks → iterative self-verification mechanism
THEN
• logical reasoning output → is_prone_to → logical inconsistencies and errors

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. LMs without explicit verification often produce outputs that contradict premises or prior steps.
  2. Human error rates in logic increase without proof-checking.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: This is a novel, architectural-level claim, though related to known prompting strategies.

What Already Exists: Known issues with logical errors in LMs and the benefits of verification in human reasoning.

What is Novel: The explicit architectural claim that iterative self-verification is necessary for strict logical reasoning in LMs.

References:

  • Wang et al. (2022) Self-Consistency Improves Chain of Thought Reasoning in Language Models [self-consistency in LMs]
  • Wei et al. (2022) Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in Large Language Models [stepwise reasoning in LMs]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. LMs with iterative self-verification will outperform standard LMs on logic tasks requiring multi-step consistency.
  2. Self-verifying LMs will be able to flag and correct their own logical errors in real time.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Iterative self-verification may enable LMs to discover new logical proof strategies.
  2. Self-verification mechanisms may generalize to other domains, such as mathematical proof or code verification.

Negative Experiments

  1. If LMs without self-verification match or exceed the logical consistency of self-verifying LMs, the theory is challenged.
  2. If self-verifying LMs still produce frequent logical errors, the theory's sufficiency claim is falsified.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some LMs show improved logical consistency with scale or data alone, without explicit self-verification.
