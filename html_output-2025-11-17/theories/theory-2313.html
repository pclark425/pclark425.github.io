Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2313

Name: Integrated Network Disruption Theory of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory posits that Alzheimer's disease (AD) arises from the convergence of multiple network-level disruptions, including proteinopathy (amyloid and tau), vascular dysfunction, and neuroimmune dysregulation. These disruptions interact synergistically, leading to progressive synaptic failure and cognitive decline. Early detection is possible by identifying network-level biomarkers that reflect the combined burden of these pathologies.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Convergent Network Disruption Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• brain → exhibits → amyloid pathology, and
• brain → exhibits → tau pathology, and
• brain → exhibits → vascular dysfunction, and
• brain → exhibits → neuroimmune dysregulation
THEN
• brain → undergoes → accelerated synaptic loss, and
• individual → develops → cognitive decline characteristic of AD

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Amyloid and tau pathologies are both necessary but not sufficient for AD; vascular and immune factors modulate risk and progression.
  2. Neuroimaging and fluid biomarkers show that combined pathologies predict faster decline.
  3. Vascular dysfunction and neuroinflammation are common in AD brains and interact with proteinopathies.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law synthesizes multiple known risk factors into a unified, synergistic network disruption model.

What Already Exists: Amyloid, tau, vascular, and immune contributions to AD are individually recognized.

What is Novel: The explicit network-level convergence and requirement for multiple interacting disruptions is novel.

References:

  • De Strooper & Karran (2016) The cellular phase of Alzheimer's disease [multi-factorial model]

Statement 1: Network Biomarker Detection Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → network-level biomarker abnormalities (e.g., functional connectivity, multimodal fluid markers)
THEN
• individual → is_at_high_risk_for → preclinical or prodromal AD

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Functional MRI and PET studies show network connectivity changes precede clinical symptoms.
  2. Combined biomarker panels (amyloid, tau, neuroinflammation, vascular) improve early detection.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law extends biomarker use to a systems-level, integrative approach.

What Already Exists: Individual biomarkers are used for AD risk assessment.

What is Novel: The use of integrated, network-level biomarker signatures for early detection is novel.

References:

  • Jack et al. (2018) NIA-AA Research Framework: Toward a biological definition of Alzheimer's disease [biomarker framework]
  • Iturria-Medina et al. (2016) Early role of vascular dysregulation in late-onset Alzheimer's disease [network-level biomarker analysis]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with combined abnormalities in amyloid, tau, vascular, and immune biomarkers will show faster cognitive decline than those with only one or two abnormalities.
  2. Network-level imaging (e.g., resting-state fMRI) will detect preclinical AD before traditional cognitive tests.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Interventions targeting multiple network disruptions simultaneously (e.g., anti-amyloid, vascular, and anti-inflammatory agents) will be more effective than single-target therapies.
  2. Novel network biomarkers (e.g., connectome-based signatures) will outperform current single-modality biomarkers in predicting AD onset.

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals with all four pathologies do not develop AD, the theory is challenged.
  2. If network-level biomarkers do not predict AD risk better than single biomarkers, the theory is weakened.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with minimal vascular or immune involvement.
