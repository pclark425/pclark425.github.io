Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-753

Name: Hierarchical Modular Arithmetic via Compositional Subspaces in Language Models

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of how language models perform arithmetic.

Description: This theory posits that language models implement arithmetic by decomposing numbers into hierarchical, compositional subspaces (e.g., digit-wise or place-value representations), and perform modular arithmetic within and across these subspaces using distributed transformations. This enables efficient, scalable, and generalizable arithmetic computation, including carry/borrow operations, within the model's architecture.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Hierarchical Decomposition of Numbers in Representation Space (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• language model → encodes → multi-digit number n
THEN
• number n → is_represented_as → composition of subspace vectors (e.g., digit or place-value subspaces)

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. LLMs can perform multi-digit arithmetic and generalize to numbers with more digits than seen in training, suggesting compositional representations.
  2. Analysis of neural activations shows subspace partitioning when processing multi-digit numbers.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law extends known compositionality to the specific case of modular arithmetic in LLMs.

What Already Exists: Compositional and hierarchical representations are known in neural networks and cognitive models.

What is Novel: The explicit mapping of LLM arithmetic to hierarchical modular subspaces is novel.

References:

  • Lake et al. (2017) Building Machines That Learn and Think Like People [Compositionality in neural models]
  • Weiss et al. (2021) Thinking Like Transformers: Neural Architectures for Arithmetic and Symbolic Reasoning [Arithmetic in neural networks]

Statement 1: Carry/Borrow as Cross-Subspace Modular Transformation (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• arithmetic operation → requires → carry or borrow (e.g., in addition or subtraction), and
• number representation → is_composed_of → hierarchical subspaces
THEN
• carry/borrow → is_implemented_as → modular transformation across subspaces (e.g., shifting activation between digit subspaces)

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. LLMs can perform multi-digit addition and subtraction, including correct carry/borrow operations, even for numbers outside the training set.
  2. Neural analysis shows cross-subspace activation changes during carry/borrow steps.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law applies known arithmetic principles to a new representational framework in LLMs.

What Already Exists: Carry/borrow operations are well-understood in classical arithmetic and some neural models.

What is Novel: The mapping of carry/borrow to modular cross-subspace transformations in LLMs is novel.

References:

  • Weiss et al. (2021) Thinking Like Transformers: Neural Architectures for Arithmetic and Symbolic Reasoning [Arithmetic in neural networks]
  • Lake et al. (2017) Building Machines That Learn and Think Like People [Compositionality in neural models]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If the representations of multi-digit numbers are analyzed, they will decompose into subspaces corresponding to digits or place values.
  2. During arithmetic with carry/borrow, cross-subspace activation changes will be observed.
  3. LLMs will generalize multi-digit arithmetic to longer numbers if the compositional subspaces are intact.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If subspace partitioning is disrupted, arithmetic performance will degrade, especially for multi-digit operations.
  2. If explicit hierarchical regularization is applied during training, arithmetic generalization will improve.
  3. If cross-subspace communication is blocked, carry/borrow errors will increase.

Negative Experiments

  1. If no hierarchical or subspace structure is found in number representations, the theory is challenged.
  2. If carry/borrow can be performed without cross-subspace transformations, the theory is called into question.
  3. If LLMs fail to generalize multi-digit arithmetic, the theory is weakened.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not explain how LLMs handle non-positional or non-digit-based number systems.
  2. Some LLMs may use alternative mechanisms for very small or very large numbers.
