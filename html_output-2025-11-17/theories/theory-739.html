Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-739

Name: Hierarchical Latent Algorithm and Surface Pattern Competition Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of how language models perform arithmetic.

Description: This theory posits that LLMs possess a hierarchy of latent algorithmic routines for arithmetic, which compete with surface pattern-matching mechanisms during inference. The outcome of this competition determines whether the model executes correct arithmetic or falls back on superficial, potentially erroneous, outputs.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Hierarchical Algorithmic Routine Selection (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• LLM → is_prompted_with → arithmetic query, and
• arithmetic query → is_complex → True
THEN
• LLM → selects → higher-level latent algorithmic routine, and
• LLM → executes → multi-step computation

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. LLMs perform better on multi-step arithmetic with chain-of-thought prompting, suggesting hierarchical routine activation.
  2. Interpretability studies show modular, compositional circuits for arithmetic in LLMs.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The modularity is known, but the competitive hierarchy is a new synthesis.

What Already Exists: LLMs can perform multi-step arithmetic with CoT prompting; modular circuits have been observed.

What is Novel: The explicit hierarchy and competition between algorithmic and surface routines is novel.

References:

  • Nanda et al. (2023) Progress Measures for Grokking via Mechanistic Interpretability [Algorithmic circuits in neural networks]
  • Wei et al. (2022) Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in Large Language Models [CoT improves arithmetic reasoning]

Statement 1: Surface Pattern Dominance in Ambiguous Cases (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• LLM → is_prompted_with → arithmetic query, and
• arithmetic query → is_ambiguous_or_novel → True
THEN
• LLM → defaults_to → surface pattern-matching routine, and
• LLM → may produce → incorrect but plausible output

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. LLMs are more error-prone on arithmetic queries with ambiguous or novel formats, often producing plausible but incorrect answers.
  2. Surface-level errors are more frequent when queries deviate from training patterns.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The error patterns are known, but the competitive mechanism is new.

What Already Exists: Surface-level errors in ambiguous or novel queries are documented.

What is Novel: The explicit competition and fallback mechanism is novel.

References:

  • Turpin et al. (2023) Language Models Don't Always Say What They Think [Superficial alignment and error in LLMs]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If arithmetic queries are made more ambiguous, LLMs will increasingly default to surface pattern-matching, increasing error rates.
  2. Providing explicit cues for algorithmic reasoning (e.g., CoT prompts) will increase the likelihood of correct hierarchical routine activation.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If LLMs are trained with adversarial ambiguous arithmetic queries, it is unknown whether they will develop new algorithmic routines or simply shift error patterns.
  2. If LLMs are given meta-cognitive prompts to 'choose the best strategy', the effect on the competition between algorithmic and surface routines is unknown.

Negative Experiments

  1. If LLMs do not show increased error on ambiguous arithmetic queries, the theory's competition mechanism would be challenged.
  2. If LLMs never default to surface pattern-matching, the fallback mechanism would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some LLMs can generalize to novel arithmetic formats without increased error, which is not fully explained by the theory.

Existing Theory Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The phenomena are known, but the competitive hierarchy is a novel synthesis.

References:

  • Wei et al. (2022) Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in Large Language Models [CoT improves arithmetic reasoning]
  • Turpin et al. (2023) Language Models Don't Always Say What They Think [Superficial alignment and error in LLMs]
