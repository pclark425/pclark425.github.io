Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1698

Name: Interactive Feedback Theory for LM-Guided Anomaly Detection

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of how language models can be used for detecting anomalies in lists of data.

Description: This theory proposes that language models can iteratively improve anomaly detection in lists through interactive feedback: by incorporating user or system feedback on initial anomaly predictions, the LM can refine its internal representation of the list's regularities, leading to more accurate and context-sensitive anomaly detection.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Feedback-Driven Refinement Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• language model → outputs → initial anomaly predictions for list, and
• user or system → provides_feedback_on → anomaly predictions
THEN
• language model → updates → internal anomaly detection criteria, and
• language model → improves → subsequent anomaly predictions

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Interactive learning and reinforcement learning with human feedback (RLHF) have been shown to improve LM task performance.
  2. Iterative prompting and feedback loops can refine LM outputs in complex tasks.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: Feedback-driven refinement is known, but its application to anomaly detection in lists is a novel extension.

What Already Exists: RLHF and interactive feedback for LMs are established for alignment and task improvement.

What is Novel: Applying interactive feedback specifically to anomaly detection in lists is a new, targeted use case.

References:

  • Ouyang et al. (2022) Training language models to follow instructions with human feedback [RLHF]
  • Perez et al. (2022) Discovering Language Model Behaviors with Model-Written Evaluations [iterative feedback]

Statement 1: Contextual Adaptation Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• language model → receives → feedback indicating context-specific anomaly definitions
THEN
• language model → adapts → anomaly detection to new context or user preference

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. LMs can adapt outputs based on explicit instructions or corrections, as shown in instruction tuning.
  2. User-in-the-loop systems enable dynamic adjustment of model behavior.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: Contextual adaptation is known, but its systematic use for anomaly detection in lists is novel.

What Already Exists: Instruction tuning and user-in-the-loop adaptation are established for LMs.

What is Novel: Dynamic adaptation of anomaly detection criteria in lists based on feedback is a new, specific application.

References:

  • Wei et al. (2021) Finetuned Language Models Are Zero-Shot Learners [instruction tuning]
  • Ouyang et al. (2022) Training language models to follow instructions with human feedback [RLHF]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If a user corrects the LM's initial anomaly prediction, subsequent predictions will better align with the user's definition of anomaly.
  2. Iterative feedback will reduce false positives and false negatives in LM-based anomaly detection over time.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If feedback is ambiguous or inconsistent, the LM's adaptation may become unstable or degrade performance.
  2. If the LM is exposed to adversarial feedback, it may learn incorrect anomaly criteria.

Negative Experiments

  1. If feedback does not improve the LM's anomaly detection accuracy, the theory is challenged.
  2. If the LM cannot adapt to new anomaly definitions after feedback, the theory's mechanism is undermined.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases where feedback is unavailable or too sparse to guide adaptation.

Existing Theory Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: Feedback mechanisms are established, but their targeted use for anomaly detection in lists is a novel theoretical extension.

References:

  • Ouyang et al. (2022) Training language models to follow instructions with human feedback [RLHF]
  • Wei et al. (2021) Finetuned Language Models Are Zero-Shot Learners [instruction tuning]
