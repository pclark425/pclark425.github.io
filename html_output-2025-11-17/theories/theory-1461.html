Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1461

Name: Contextually Modulated Latent Space Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This theory proposes that conceptual knowledge in brains is represented as points or regions in a high-dimensional latent space, where each dimension corresponds to a latent feature or property. The mapping from sensory input or linguistic tokens to this space is dynamically modulated by context, such that the same concept can occupy different regions depending on task demands, prior knowledge, or current goals. This context-sensitive latent space enables flexible, adaptive conceptual reasoning and accounts for phenomena such as conceptual change, polysemy, and context effects in meaning.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Contextual Modulation Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• conceptual representation → is_accessed_in_context → context_X
THEN
• conceptual representation → is_mapped_to → region_X_in_latent_space

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Behavioral and neural evidence shows that the meaning of concepts shifts with context (e.g., 'bank' as riverbank vs. financial institution).
  2. fMRI studies show context-dependent shifts in neural activation patterns for the same concept.
  3. Priming and task instructions can alter the interpretation and neural representation of ambiguous words.
  4. Polysemous words elicit different neural patterns depending on sentence context.
  5. Contextual cues (visual, linguistic, or task-based) modulate the activation of conceptual features.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: While context effects and latent space models exist, the explicit claim that context modulates the mapping of concepts within a functional latent space is a novel synthesis.

What Already Exists: Context effects in conceptual processing are well-documented, and latent space models are common in computational neuroscience.

What is Novel: This law asserts that context dynamically remaps conceptual representations within a latent space at the functional level, not just at the neural or computational level.

References:

  • Yee & Thompson-Schill (2016) Putting concepts into context [context effects in conceptual processing]
  • Mitchell et al. (2008) Predicting human brain activity associated with the meanings of nouns [latent semantic spaces in brain]
  • Bengio et al. (2013) Representation learning: A review and new perspectives [latent space models]

Statement 1: Latent Space Structure Law (quantitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• conceptual representations → are_in_latent_space → True
THEN
• conceptual similarity → corresponds_to → distance_in_latent_space

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Similarity judgments and neural similarity patterns correlate with distances in learned latent spaces.
  2. Multivariate pattern analysis shows that similar concepts evoke similar neural patterns.
  3. Representational similarity analysis (RSA) links behavioral similarity to neural similarity and latent space distance.
  4. Latent semantic analysis and word embedding models capture human similarity judgments.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law extends existing computational and neural models to a functional-level claim about the representational format.

What Already Exists: Latent semantic analysis and representational similarity analysis are established methods linking conceptual similarity to latent space distance.

What is Novel: This law claims that the functional format of conceptual knowledge is inherently a context-sensitive latent space, not just a computational convenience.

References:

  • Mitchell et al. (2008) Predicting human brain activity associated with the meanings of nouns [latent semantic spaces in brain]
  • Kriegeskorte et al. (2008) Representational similarity analysis [neural similarity and latent spaces]
  • Bengio et al. (2013) Representation learning: A review and new perspectives [latent space models]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Manipulating context (e.g., via priming or task instructions) will systematically shift the neural representation of a concept in a manner predictable by latent space models.
  2. Concepts with multiple meanings (polysemy) will show greater context-dependent neural pattern shifts than monosemous concepts.
  3. Behavioral similarity judgments will change with context in ways that correspond to predicted latent space shifts.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Training individuals to use novel contexts (e.g., in artificial languages) will induce new, stable regions in conceptual latent space, observable in neural data.
  2. Disruption of context-sensitive mapping (e.g., via brain stimulation) will selectively impair context-dependent conceptual tasks but not context-invariant ones.
  3. Latent space structure may predict individual differences in conceptual flexibility or creativity.

Negative Experiments

  1. If conceptual representations remain invariant across all contexts, this would falsify the theory.
  2. If similarity judgments and neural similarity do not correspond to distances in any latent space, the theory would be undermined.
  3. If context manipulations do not produce systematic or predictable changes in conceptual representation, the theory would be challenged.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not specify the origin or learning mechanism for the latent space dimensions.
  2. The theory does not address how innate or highly stable concepts are represented if they are context-invariant.
