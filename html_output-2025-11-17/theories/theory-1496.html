Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1496

Name: Dynamic Contextualization Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This theory proposes that the functional format of conceptual knowledge in brains is inherently dynamic and context-sensitive. Rather than being stored as static entities, concepts are constructed on-the-fly through the interaction of long-term knowledge, current goals, and environmental context. The representational format is thus a process: concepts are temporary, contextually-tuned assemblies of features, relations, and affective/motivational states, allowing for rapid adaptation and flexible inference.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: On-the-Fly Construction Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• conceptual task → is_initiated → True, and
• context → is_present → True
THEN
• conceptual system → constructs → contextually-tuned representation from long-term knowledge

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Behavioral studies show rapid context-dependent shifts in categorization and inference.
  2. Neuroimaging reveals transient, context-specific activation patterns during conceptual tasks.
  3. Priming and goal manipulation studies show that the same concept can be flexibly reconfigured.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law is closely related to existing work but makes a novel claim about the processual nature of conceptual representation.

What Already Exists: Contextual modulation and dynamic construction of meaning are established in cognitive psychology.

What is Novel: The explicit claim that the representational format is a process—contextual assembly—rather than a static structure.

References:

  • Barsalou (1987) The instability of graded structure [Context-dependent construction]
  • Yee & Thompson-Schill (2016) Putting concepts into context [Context effects in conceptual processing]
  • Lebois et al. (2015) Context sensitivity in the multidimensional conceptual space [Dynamic conceptualization]

Statement 1: Goal-Driven Feature Selection Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• current goal → modifies → task demands, and
• concept → is_activated → True
THEN
• conceptual system → selectively emphasizes → features relevant to current goal

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Task instructions and goals modulate which features of a concept are accessed and used in reasoning.
  2. Neuroimaging shows goal-dependent activation of conceptual features.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law is closely related to existing work but elevates goal-driven selection to a defining property of conceptual representation.

What Already Exists: Goal-driven modulation of conceptual processing is well-documented.

What is Novel: The law frames this as a core property of the representational format, not just a modulatory effect.

References:

  • Yee & Thompson-Schill (2016) Putting concepts into context [Goal-driven conceptual processing]
  • Barsalou (2003) Situated simulation in the human conceptual system [Goal and context effects]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Changing task goals will predictably shift which features are activated for a given concept, observable in both behavior and neuroimaging.
  2. Rapid context switches will lead to rapid reconfiguration of conceptual representations.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Training individuals to flexibly shift conceptual features will enhance their ability to adapt to novel tasks.
  2. Individuals with deficits in executive function will show less flexible, more rigid conceptual representations.

Negative Experiments

  1. If conceptual representations remain static across different contexts and goals, the theory is undermined.
  2. If context and goals do not modulate feature selection, the theory is challenged.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not specify how stable, long-term conceptual knowledge is maintained alongside dynamic construction.
