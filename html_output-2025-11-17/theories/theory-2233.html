Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2233

Name: Meta-Epistemic Evaluation Theory for LLM-Generated Scientific Theories

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of how to evaluate LLM-generated scientific theories.

Description: This theory posits that the evaluation of LLM-generated scientific theories should be grounded in meta-epistemic criteria: explanatory power, falsifiability, novelty, and robustness to adversarial prompts. It asserts that these criteria, when operationalized, provide a principled and generalizable framework for assessing the scientific merit of LLM outputs, regardless of domain.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Meta-Epistemic Criteria Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• theory → is_generated_by → LLM
THEN
• theory → should_be_evaluated_by → explanatory_power, and
• theory → should_be_evaluated_by → falsifiability, and
• theory → should_be_evaluated_by → novelty, and
• theory → should_be_evaluated_by → robustness_to_adversarial_prompts

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Philosophy of science emphasizes explanatory power and falsifiability as core criteria for theory evaluation (Popper, Kuhn, Lakatos).
  2. Novelty is a key driver of scientific progress and is often used in peer review and grant evaluation.
  3. LLMs are susceptible to adversarial prompts and hallucinations, so robustness is critical for trustworthiness.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While the individual criteria are established, their integration and application to LLM-generated scientific theories is new.

What Already Exists: Explanatory power, falsifiability, and novelty are established criteria in philosophy of science; robustness to adversarial prompts is a recent concern in AI safety.

What is Novel: The explicit combination and operationalization of these four criteria as a unified framework for LLM-generated scientific theory evaluation is novel.

References:

  • Popper (1959) The Logic of Scientific Discovery [falsifiability, explanatory power]
  • Kuhn (1962) The Structure of Scientific Revolutions [novelty, paradigm shifts]
  • Bender et al. (2021) On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots [robustness, LLM safety]

Statement 1: Generalizability Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• evaluation_framework → is_based_on → meta-epistemic_criteria
THEN
• evaluation_framework → is_generalizable_across → scientific_domains

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Meta-epistemic criteria are domain-agnostic and have been used to evaluate theories in physics, biology, and social sciences.
  2. LLMs generate theories in diverse domains, necessitating a generalizable evaluation framework.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law extends existing philosophical principles to the context of LLMs.

What Already Exists: Generalizability of epistemic criteria is discussed in philosophy of science.

What is Novel: Application to LLM-generated theories and explicit operationalization for automated evaluation is novel.

References:

  • Thagard (1978) The Best Explanation: Criteria for Theory Choice [generalizability of criteria]
  • Langley et al. (1987) Scientific Discovery: Computational Explorations [AI and theory evaluation]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. LLM-generated theories evaluated with this framework will align more closely with expert human judgments than those evaluated with ad hoc or single-criterion methods.
  2. Theories that score highly on all four criteria will be more likely to be published or cited in scientific venues.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Some highly novel but less robust theories may lead to paradigm shifts if further developed.
  2. The relative importance of each criterion may vary by scientific field or LLM architecture.

Negative Experiments

  1. If theories that score poorly on these criteria are consistently accepted by experts, the framework is undermined.
  2. If the framework fails to distinguish between high- and low-quality LLM-generated theories, its utility is called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The framework does not explicitly account for ethical or societal impacts of theories.
  2. It may not capture the value of serendipitous or accidental discoveries.
