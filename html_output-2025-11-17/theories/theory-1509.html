Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1509

Name: Structured Relational Schema Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This theory posits that conceptual knowledge in brains is functionally represented as structured relational schemas, where concepts are encoded as interconnected sets of roles and relations, abstracted from sensory input. These schemas are flexibly instantiated in working memory and can be composed, nested, and generalized, supporting systematicity and productivity in cognition. The format is symbolic at the functional level, but the instantiation of symbols is context-sensitive and dynamically constructed.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Relational Schema Representation Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• concept → is_complex → True
THEN
• concept → is_represented_as → relational schema (roles, relations, fillers)

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Humans can represent and reason about abstract relations (e.g., 'X is taller than Y') independent of specific instances.
  2. Systematicity and productivity in language and thought require compositional, relational representations.
  3. Neuropsychological evidence shows selective deficits in relational reasoning after prefrontal damage.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: While schema and relational models exist, this law generalizes the format to all conceptual knowledge at the functional level.

What Already Exists: Relational and schema-based representations are well-established in cognitive science (e.g., Gentner's structure-mapping, schema theory).

What is Novel: The explicit claim that all complex concepts are functionally encoded as relational schemas, not just some domains.

References:

  • Gentner (1983) Structure-mapping: A theoretical framework for analogy [structure-mapping theory]
  • Rumelhart (1980) Schemata: The building blocks of cognition [schema theory]
  • Halford et al. (1998) Relational complexity: The capacity limitation on processing [relational representations in cognition]

Statement 1: Compositional Generalization Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• conceptual system → encounters → novel combination of known roles and relations
THEN
• system → can_construct → new relational schema by composition

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Humans can understand and generate novel sentences and ideas by recombining known elements.
  2. Children generalize grammatical rules to new words, indicating compositionality.
  3. Analogical reasoning tasks show transfer of relational structure to new domains.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: Compositionality is established, but its universal application to all conceptual knowledge is a novel generalization.

What Already Exists: Compositionality is a core property in symbolic and schema-based models.

What is Novel: The law asserts that compositional generalization is a universal property of conceptual representation, not just language.

References:

  • Fodor & Pylyshyn (1988) Connectionism and cognitive architecture: A critical analysis [systematicity and compositionality]
  • Lake et al. (2017) Building machines that learn and think like people [compositionality in concept learning]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Novel relational concepts (e.g., 'the uncle of my friend') can be understood and manipulated without prior direct experience.
  2. Brain imaging will show activation of prefrontal and parietal regions during relational reasoning across diverse domains.
  3. Deficits in relational schema construction will impair generalization and analogical reasoning.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially training individuals on highly abstract relational schemas will transfer to improved reasoning in unrelated domains.
  2. Schema-based interventions could remediate relational reasoning deficits in neurodevelopmental disorders.
  3. Direct neural stimulation of schema-relevant regions will enhance compositional generalization.

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals cannot generalize relational concepts to novel instances, the theory is undermined.
  2. If brain lesions to schema-relevant areas do not impair relational reasoning, the theory is unsupported.
  3. If non-relational, unstructured representations suffice for complex reasoning, the theory is called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not specify the mechanism for instantiating or updating schemas in real time.
  2. The neural implementation of relational schemas is not addressed.
