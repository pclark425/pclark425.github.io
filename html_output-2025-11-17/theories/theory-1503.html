Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1503

Name: Precision-Weighted Selection Mechanism for Conceptual Format Arbitration

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This theory posits that the brain represents conceptual knowledge using multiple representational formats (e.g., symbolic, analogical, sensorimotor, affective), and dynamically arbitrates among them based on a precision-weighted mechanism. The format with the highest estimated precision for a given context dominates conceptual processing, allowing flexible, context-sensitive cognition.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Precision-Weighted Format Arbitration Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• conceptual knowledge → is_accessed_in → context, and
• representational format → has_highest_precision → for context
THEN
• representational format → dominates → conceptual processing

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Behavioral and neuroimaging studies show flexible recruitment of symbolic, analogical, and sensorimotor representations depending on task demands and context.
  2. Computational models (e.g., Bayesian arbitration) demonstrate that weighting by precision optimizes inference and decision-making.
  3. Neuropsychological evidence shows that damage to specific representational systems leads to compensatory reliance on alternative formats.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While context-dependent format use is known, the precision-weighted arbitration mechanism as a general law is not previously formalized.

What Already Exists: Multiple representational formats for concepts and context-dependent recruitment are established.

What is Novel: The explicit formalization of arbitration as a precision-weighted selection mechanism across all formats is novel.

References:

  • Barsalou (2008) Grounded cognition [context-dependent format use]
  • Pezzulo et al. (2013) The mixed instrumental controller: using value of information to combine habitual and goal-directed strategies [precision-weighted arbitration in action control]
  • Lambon Ralph et al. (2017) The neural and computational bases of semantic cognition [multiple representational systems]

Statement 1: Contextual Modulation of Format Precision Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• context → modulates → precision of representational formats
THEN
• format with highest precision → is_selected_for → conceptual processing

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Task instructions and environmental cues bias the use of linguistic, visual, or motoric representations in concept tasks.
  2. Neuroimaging shows context-dependent shifts in activation between language, visual, and motor areas during concept retrieval.
  3. Behavioral priming studies show that prior context can increase the accessibility and utility of specific representational formats.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: Contextual effects are known, but their formalization as precision modulation for arbitration is new.

What Already Exists: Contextual modulation of representational format is established.

What is Novel: The explicit link to precision estimation and arbitration is novel.

References:

  • Yee & Thompson-Schill (2016) Putting concepts into context [contextual modulation of conceptual processing]
  • Barsalou (2008) Grounded cognition [contextual flexibility]
  • Pezzulo et al. (2013) The mixed instrumental controller [precision-weighted arbitration in other domains]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Manipulating the reliability (precision) of a representational format (e.g., adding noise to visual input) will shift conceptual processing toward alternative formats (e.g., linguistic).
  2. Neuroimaging will show increased activation in the brain regions associated with the format that has the highest precision in a given context.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Training to increase the precision of a non-dominant format (e.g., sensorimotor) will lead to its increased use in conceptual tasks even when other formats are available.
  2. In ambiguous contexts where no format has high precision, the brain will show increased arbitration-related activity (e.g., in frontoparietal control networks).

Negative Experiments

  1. If increasing the precision of a format does not increase its dominance in conceptual processing, the theory is undermined.
  2. If context does not modulate the selection of representational formats, the theory is undermined.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not specify how precision is estimated or learned for each format.
  2. The theory does not address how new representational formats emerge developmentally or evolutionarily.

Existing Theory Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing
