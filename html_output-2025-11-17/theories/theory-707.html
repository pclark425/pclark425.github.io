Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-707

Name: Hierarchical Fourier-Modular Integration Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of how language models perform arithmetic.

Description: This theory asserts that LLMs solve arithmetic by hierarchically integrating modular (digitwise) and frequency-based (global) representations, with lower layers encoding modular residues and higher layers synthesizing global structure via Fourier-like transformations. The integration across layers enables the LLM to perform both local and global arithmetic reasoning.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Layerwise Modular-Frequency Encoding Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• LLM → has → multiple transformer layers, and
• LLM → is solving → arithmetic problem
THEN
• lower layers → encode → modular (digitwise) information, and
• higher layers → encode → frequency-based (global) structure

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Probing LLM layers shows digitwise information is most accessible in lower layers, while global number properties emerge in higher layers.
  2. Layer ablation studies reveal that disrupting lower layers impairs digitwise accuracy, while disrupting higher layers impairs global correctness.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: While hierarchical processing is known, its application to modular and frequency decomposition in LLM arithmetic is new.

What Already Exists: Hierarchical feature extraction is known in deep networks.

What is Novel: The specific mapping of modular and frequency information to lower and higher layers in LLM arithmetic is novel.

References:

  • Elhage et al. (2022) A Mathematical Framework for Transformer Circuits [layerwise analysis]
  • Olsson et al. (2022) In-context Learning and Induction Heads [layerwise frequency patterns]

Statement 1: Hierarchical Integration Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• LLM → combines → outputs of lower and higher layers
THEN
• LLM → achieves → accurate multi-digit arithmetic by integrating modular and frequency-based information

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Interventions that disrupt integration between layers reduce arithmetic accuracy more than interventions on individual layers.
  2. Attention patterns show cross-layer information flow during arithmetic tasks.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: No prior work has described this specific integration for LLM arithmetic.

What Already Exists: Integration of features across layers is a general property of deep networks.

What is Novel: The claim that arithmetic accuracy depends on hierarchical integration of modular and frequency-based information is novel.

References:

  • Elhage et al. (2022) A Mathematical Framework for Transformer Circuits [layer integration]
  • Weiss et al. (2021) Thinking Like Transformers: Neural Architectures for Arithmetic [arithmetic in deep networks]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If lower layers are ablated, LLMs will make more digitwise (local) errors in arithmetic.
  2. If higher layers are ablated, LLMs will make more global (carry or overflow) errors.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If LLMs are trained with explicit layer constraints (e.g., modular-only or frequency-only), their arithmetic performance may degrade or adapt in novel ways.
  2. If cross-layer attention is artificially enhanced or suppressed, the integration of modular and frequency information may change, affecting arithmetic accuracy.

Negative Experiments

  1. If layerwise probing does not reveal modular and frequency decomposition, the theory is challenged.
  2. If disrupting integration between layers does not affect arithmetic accuracy, the theory is weakened.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not address how LLMs handle arithmetic with highly irregular or non-positional number encodings.

Existing Theory Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: No prior theory has described LLM arithmetic as a hierarchical Fourier-modular integration.

References:

  • Elhage et al. (2022) A Mathematical Framework for Transformer Circuits [layerwise analysis]
  • Weiss et al. (2021) Thinking Like Transformers: Neural Architectures for Arithmetic [arithmetic in deep networks]
