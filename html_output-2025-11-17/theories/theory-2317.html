Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2317

Name: Dynamic Peripheral-Central Biomarker Flux Theory (General Formulation)

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory posits that Alzheimer's disease (AD) pathogenesis and detection are governed by dynamic, bidirectional fluxes of molecular biomarkers (such as amyloid-beta, tau, neuroinflammatory mediators, and metabolic byproducts) between the central nervous system (CNS) and peripheral compartments (blood, CSF, lymphatics). The theory asserts that the rates, directions, and regulatory mechanisms of these fluxes are critical determinants of both disease progression and the reliability of peripheral biomarker-based diagnostics.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Bidirectional Biomarker Exchange Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• CNS → produces → biomarker_X, and
• peripheral_compartment → has_transport_mechanism → biomarker_X
THEN
• biomarker_X → fluxes_between → CNS_and_peripheral_compartment

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Amyloid-beta and tau are detected in both CSF and blood, with evidence of transport across the blood-brain barrier (BBB) and via glymphatic/lymphatic routes.
  2. Peripheral inflammation can influence CNS biomarker levels and vice versa.
  3. The glymphatic system facilitates clearance of interstitial solutes from the brain to the periphery.
  4. Blood-brain barrier is semi-permeable to certain proteins and peptides, including amyloid-beta.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While the existence of CNS-peripheral biomarker exchange is established, the theory's emphasis on dynamic fluxes as central to both disease and detection is a new conceptual synthesis.

What Already Exists: It is known that CNS-derived proteins can be detected in peripheral fluids and that the BBB is semi-permeable to certain molecules.

What is Novel: The explicit framing of AD pathogenesis and detection as a function of dynamic, regulated, bidirectional biomarker fluxes, rather than static concentrations, is novel.

References:

  • Zetterberg (2019) Blood biomarkers: revolutionizing Alzheimer's diagnostics [Describes peripheral detection of CNS biomarkers]

  • Sweeney et al. (2018) Blood–brain barrier: from physiology to disease and back [Reviews BBB transport in neurodegeneration]

Statement 1: Flux-Driven Pathogenesis Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• biomarker_X → has_abnormal_flux → between_CNS_and_periphery
THEN
• AD_pathogenesis → is_modulated_by → biomarker_X_flux

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Impaired clearance of amyloid-beta from the brain to the periphery is associated with increased CNS amyloid burden and AD risk.
  2. Peripheral inflammation can alter BBB permeability and CNS biomarker flux, influencing neurodegeneration.
  3. BBB dysfunction is observed in AD and correlates with altered biomarker profiles.
  4. Liver and kidney dysfunction can affect peripheral clearance of CNS-derived biomarkers.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The focus on dynamic fluxes as the primary modulator of AD pathogenesis, rather than static biomarker levels, is a novel extension.

What Already Exists: Impaired amyloid-beta clearance and BBB dysfunction are recognized contributors to AD.

What is Novel: The law generalizes this to all relevant biomarkers and frames the flux (not just static levels) as the key pathogenic driver.

References:

  • Tarasoff-Conway et al. (2015) Clearance systems in the brain—implications for Alzheimer disease [Reviews clearance mechanisms]
  • Montagne et al. (2020) Blood-brain barrier breakdown in Alzheimer disease and other neurodegenerative disorders [Describes BBB dysfunction in AD]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If the rate of amyloid-beta flux from CNS to blood is experimentally increased (e.g., by enhancing glymphatic clearance), CNS amyloid burden will decrease and peripheral amyloid-beta levels will transiently rise.
  2. Individuals with genetic or acquired defects in peripheral clearance mechanisms (e.g., liver or kidney dysfunction) will show altered peripheral biomarker profiles and potentially increased AD risk.
  3. Acute changes in BBB permeability (e.g., during systemic inflammation) will cause transient shifts in peripheral biomarker levels.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Modulating peripheral biomarker flux (e.g., by targeting lymphatic drainage) will slow or reverse AD progression in humans.
  2. Novel, non-traditional biomarkers (e.g., microRNAs or exosomes) with high flux rates will outperform static protein markers for early AD detection.
  3. Artificially manipulating peripheral biomarker concentrations (e.g., via plasmapheresis) will alter CNS pathology.

Negative Experiments

  1. If CNS and peripheral biomarker levels are completely uncorrelated in longitudinal studies, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If interventions that alter biomarker flux (e.g., enhancing glymphatic flow) have no effect on CNS pathology or peripheral biomarker levels, the theory would be called into question.
  3. If individuals with impaired peripheral clearance do not show altered AD risk or biomarker profiles, the theory would be weakened.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases where AD pathology progresses despite normal biomarker flux rates, suggesting alternative mechanisms.
