Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1421

Name: Reflective Attention Redistribution Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of how language models perform self-reflection and improve their answer quality through multiple iterations of generate-then-reflect.

Description: This theory proposes that language models, when prompted to reflect, dynamically redistribute their internal attention and representational resources. Reflection prompts act as meta-instructions, causing the model to reweight previously under-attended knowledge and reasoning paths, thereby enabling the surfacing of relevant but initially overlooked information.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Reflection-Driven Attention Shift Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• language model → receives → reflection prompt
THEN
• model attention → shifts → toward previously under-attended but relevant tokens or facts

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Attention map analyses show that reflection prompts can cause the model to focus on new information in subsequent passes.
  2. Empirical results indicate that reflection often leads to the inclusion of facts or reasoning not present in the initial answer.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: This law extends attention analysis to the context of reflection-induced redistribution.

What Already Exists: Attention reallocation is known in transformer models, but not specifically as a result of reflection.

What is Novel: The law that reflection prompts systematically induce attention shifts to under-attended knowledge is new.

References:

  • Vig et al. (2020) Investigating Transformers with Vector Norms [Attention analysis, not reflection-specific]
  • Elhage et al. (2022) A Mathematical Framework for Transformer Circuits [Subspace analysis, not reflection-specific]

Statement 1: Meta-Prompted Knowledge Surfacing Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• reflection prompt → explicitly requests → identification of missing or overlooked information
THEN
• language model → retrieves and incorporates → relevant knowledge not present in initial output

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Reflection prompts that ask for missing information often result in the model surfacing new, relevant facts.
  2. Self-Refine and similar methods show increased factual coverage after reflection.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: This law formalizes the mechanism by which reflection prompts elicit new knowledge from LLMs.

What Already Exists: Knowledge retrieval is known in retrieval-augmented models, but not as a result of internal reflection.

What is Novel: The law that meta-prompts can cause LLMs to surface previously unexpressed knowledge is new.

References:

  • Lewis et al. (2020) Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Knowledge-Intensive NLP Tasks [Knowledge retrieval, not reflection]
  • Madaan et al. (2023) Self-Refine: Iterative Refinement with Self-Feedback [Reflection, but not formalized as knowledge surfacing]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Comparing attention maps before and after reflection will show increased attention to tokens/facts not previously emphasized.
  2. Reflection prompts that explicitly ask for missing information will increase factual recall in model outputs.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If reflection prompts are adversarially designed, the model may surface irrelevant or incorrect knowledge.
  2. Training models to maximize attention redistribution during reflection may improve performance on complex reasoning tasks.

Negative Experiments

  1. If attention patterns do not change after reflection, the theory is falsified.
  2. If reflection fails to elicit new knowledge or facts, the meta-prompted knowledge surfacing law is called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases where reflection leads to overemphasis on irrelevant details, reducing answer quality.

Existing Theory Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: The theory is new in the context of LLM self-reflection.

References:

  • Vig et al. (2020) Investigating Transformers with Vector Norms [Attention analysis]
  • Lewis et al. (2020) Retrieval-Augmented Generation for Knowledge-Intensive NLP Tasks [Knowledge retrieval]
