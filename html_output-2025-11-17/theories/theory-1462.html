Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1462

Name: Compositional Feature Binding Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This theory posits that conceptual knowledge is represented functionally as structured compositions of feature bindings, where each concept is a dynamic assembly of feature-value pairs. These bindings are flexibly combined according to compositional rules, allowing for the construction of novel concepts and the systematic generalization of knowledge. The theory accounts for the productivity and systematicity of conceptual thought, as well as the ability to represent complex, hierarchical, or relational concepts.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Compositional Binding Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• concept → is_constructed_from → features {f1, f2, ..., fn}
THEN
• conceptual representation → is_composed_of → bindings {f1:v1, f2:v2, ..., fn:vn}

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. People can systematically combine features to create novel concepts (e.g., 'red square', 'flying car').
  2. Neural and behavioral evidence supports feature-based representations (e.g., color, shape, function).
  3. Compositionality is observed in language and thought, enabling productivity and generalization.
  4. Feature binding errors (e.g., in attention or memory) lead to predictable conceptual confusions.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: While feature-based and compositional models exist, the explicit claim that feature binding is the core functional representational format is a novel synthesis.

What Already Exists: Feature-based and compositional models are common in cognitive science and linguistics.

What is Novel: This law asserts that the functional format of conceptual knowledge is a dynamic, compositional binding of features, not just a static list or vector.

References:

  • Fodor & Pylyshyn (1988) Connectionism and cognitive architecture: A critical analysis [systematicity and compositionality]
  • Hampton (1997) Conceptual combination: Conjunction and negation of natural concepts [feature composition in concepts]
  • Treisman (1996) The binding problem [feature binding in perception and memory]

Statement 1: Hierarchical Composition Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• concept → is_complex → True
THEN
• conceptual representation → is_hierarchically_composed_of → sub-concepts and feature bindings

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Complex concepts (e.g., 'pet fish', 'electric car') are understood as hierarchical compositions of simpler concepts.
  2. Hierarchical structure is observed in language, thought, and neural representations.
  3. People can generalize properties from sub-concepts to complex concepts (e.g., 'fish' to 'pet fish').

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law synthesizes compositional and hierarchical models into a unified functional-level claim.

What Already Exists: Hierarchical and compositional structure is well-established in linguistics and cognitive science.

What is Novel: This law claims that hierarchical composition is a core property of the functional representational format of conceptual knowledge.

References:

  • Fodor & Pylyshyn (1988) Connectionism and cognitive architecture: A critical analysis [systematicity and compositionality]
  • Murphy (2002) The Big Book of Concepts [hierarchical structure in concepts]
  • Treisman (1996) The binding problem [feature binding in perception and memory]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Novel combinations of familiar features will be rapidly and systematically represented and understood.
  2. Feature binding errors (e.g., induced by distraction) will lead to predictable conceptual confusions.
  3. Hierarchical concepts will show neural signatures of both their sub-concepts and their unique combinations.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially training new compositional rules (e.g., in artificial grammar learning) will induce new patterns of feature binding in conceptual representation.
  2. Disruption of feature binding mechanisms (e.g., via brain stimulation) will selectively impair compositional but not atomic concept processing.
  3. Hierarchical composition may predict individual differences in abstract reasoning or creativity.

Negative Experiments

  1. If people cannot systematically combine features to create novel concepts, the theory would be undermined.
  2. If complex concepts do not show evidence of hierarchical or compositional structure in behavior or neural data, the theory would be challenged.
  3. If feature binding errors do not produce predictable conceptual confusions, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not specify how feature values are selected or learned.
  2. The theory does not address how context modulates feature binding.
