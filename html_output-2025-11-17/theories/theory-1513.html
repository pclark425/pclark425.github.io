Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1513

Name: Functional Stable Coactivation Criterion for Concept Individuation (FSCCCI) – General Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This theory posits that conceptual knowledge in brains is functionally individuated by the stable coactivation of distributed representational elements. A concept is defined as a set of features or sub-concepts whose coactivation pattern is reliably and selectively re-instantiated across contexts, tasks, and modalities. The stability and selectivity of this coactivation, rather than the specific neural substrate, is the functional criterion for individuating concepts. This theory provides a functional-level account, abstracting away from neural implementation, and predicts that concepts are best identified by their reproducible, context-invariant coactivation signatures.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Stable Coactivation Defines Conceptual Units (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• set_of_representational_elements → exhibits → stable_coactivation_pattern, and
• coactivation_pattern → is_reinstantiated → across_contexts_and_tasks
THEN
• set_of_representational_elements → constitutes → a_single_concept

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Multivariate pattern analysis (MVPA) in fMRI shows that distributed patterns corresponding to concepts are reliably reinstated across different tasks and modalities.
  2. Neuropsychological evidence indicates that damage to distributed feature representations can selectively impair conceptual knowledge.
  3. Cross-modal priming studies show that the same conceptual representation can be accessed via different sensory modalities, indicating a stable underlying coactivation pattern.
  4. Semantic dementia patients lose concepts in a graded fashion, consistent with the disruption of stable coactivation patterns rather than loss of single features.
  5. Computational models (e.g., deep neural networks) show that concepts correspond to stable, distributed activation patterns that are reinstated across input variations.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While related to distributed and connectionist models, this law formalizes a functional, context-invariant coactivation criterion for concept individuation, which is not explicitly present in prior work.

What Already Exists: The idea that concepts are distributed and can be identified by patterns of activation is present in connectionist and distributed semantic theories.

What is Novel: The explicit criterion that stable, context-invariant coactivation is the functional basis for concept individuation, regardless of neural substrate, is novel.

References:

  • Haxby et al. (2001) Distributed and Overlapping Representations of Faces and Objects in Ventral Temporal Cortex [MVPA evidence for distributed representations]
  • Rogers & McClelland (2004) Semantic Cognition: A Parallel Distributed Processing Approach [distributed semantic representations]
  • Patterson, Nestor, & Rogers (2007) Where do you know what you know? The representation of semantic knowledge in the human brain [distributed conceptual knowledge]
  • Lambon Ralph et al. (2010) The neural and computational bases of semantic cognition [distributed and graded conceptual loss]

Statement 1: Functional Individuation is Invariant to Physical Substrate (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• coactivation_pattern → is_stably_reinstantiated → across_contexts, and
• coactivation_pattern → is_supported_by → different_neural_or_computational_substrates
THEN
• concept → remains_functionally_individuated → by_coactivation_pattern

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neural plasticity and recovery after brain injury show that conceptual knowledge can be preserved or remapped despite changes in neural substrate.
  2. Computational models demonstrate that the same functional representations can be implemented in different architectures.
  3. Cross-species studies show that similar conceptual abilities can arise from different neural architectures, suggesting functional rather than anatomical individuation.
  4. Cases of neural reorganization (e.g., after hemispherectomy) show preservation of conceptual knowledge, indicating substrate invariance.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law is inspired by functionalist philosophy but operationalizes it in the context of conceptual representation via stable coactivation, which is a novel synthesis.

What Already Exists: Functionalism in philosophy of mind posits that mental states are individuated by their functional roles, not their physical substrate.

What is Novel: This law applies functional individuation specifically to the stable coactivation criterion for concepts, providing a concrete operationalization.

References:

  • Putnam (1967) Psychological Predicates [functionalism]
  • Fodor (1975) The Language of Thought [functional individuation of mental states]
  • Lambon Ralph et al. (2010) The neural and computational bases of semantic cognition [evidence for remapping and distributed representation]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If a novel concept is learned, a new stable coactivation pattern will emerge that is reliably reinstated across different tasks involving that concept.
  2. If a concept is contextually primed, the same coactivation pattern will be observed regardless of sensory modality (e.g., visual or auditory presentation).
  3. If a concept is forgotten or degraded, the stability of its coactivation pattern will decrease across contexts.
  4. Artificial neural networks trained on conceptual tasks will develop stable, distributed activation patterns corresponding to concepts.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If a brain region supporting part of a coactivation pattern is lesioned, but the pattern is re-established elsewhere, conceptual knowledge will be preserved.
  2. Artificial systems trained to mimic human conceptual knowledge will develop stable coactivation patterns functionally equivalent to those in human brains, even if implemented differently.
  3. Highly abstract concepts may show less stable or more context-dependent coactivation patterns, challenging the criterion.
  4. If a concept is re-encoded using a radically different representational scheme (e.g., in a non-biological substrate), the same functional coactivation pattern will still individuate the concept.

Negative Experiments

  1. If concepts can be reliably individuated without any stable coactivation pattern, the theory would be called into question.
  2. If the same coactivation pattern is not reinstated across different contexts for the same concept, the theory would be undermined.
  3. If neural or computational reorganization leads to loss of conceptual knowledge despite preservation of coactivation patterns, the theory would be challenged.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The role of temporal dynamics (e.g., oscillatory synchrony) in concept individuation is not explicitly addressed.
  2. The influence of emotional or motivational states on the stability of coactivation patterns is not considered.
