Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1477

Name: Hierarchical Compositional Schema Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This theory posits that conceptual knowledge is represented in the brain as a hierarchy of compositional schemas, where each schema encodes a structured set of relations and roles. Concepts are instantiated by binding specific fillers to abstract roles within these schemas, and higher-level concepts are recursively constructed from lower-level schemas. The representational format is symbolic-structural at the schema level, but the instantiation of roles and bindings is realized through dynamic, distributed processes.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Schema-Based Hierarchical Representation Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• concept → is_accessed → in context C
THEN
• concept → is_instantiated_as → schema with roles and relations, and
• schema → is_composed_of → lower-level schemas

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neuropsychological evidence shows that damage to specific brain regions impairs the ability to process relational or role-based information.
  2. Behavioral studies demonstrate compositionality in conceptual combination and analogy.
  3. Neural decoding studies reveal hierarchical structure in conceptual representations.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: While schema and hierarchical models exist, the explicit claim of universal compositional schema instantiation is novel.

What Already Exists: Schema theory and hierarchical models of conceptual knowledge are established.

What is Novel: The law asserts that all conceptual knowledge is instantiated as compositional schemas, with recursive structure and explicit role-filler bindings.

References:

  • Rumelhart (1980) Schemata: The building blocks of cognition [schema theory]
  • Hummel & Holyoak (2003) A symbolic-connectionist theory of relational inference and generalization [role-filler binding, compositionality]
  • Frankland & Greene (2015) An architecture for encoding sentence meaning in left mid-superior temporal cortex [hierarchical, compositional structure]

Statement 1: Dynamic Role-Filler Binding Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• schema → is_instantiated → for concept C
THEN
• roles in schema → are_bound_to → specific fillers via dynamic distributed processes

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neural evidence for rapid, flexible binding of roles and fillers during language and reasoning tasks.
  2. Computational models show that dynamic binding mechanisms can instantiate symbolic structure in distributed representations.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: Dynamic binding is known, but the claim of its universality for all conceptual schema instantiation is novel.

What Already Exists: Role-filler binding and dynamic binding mechanisms are established in cognitive models.

What is Novel: The law claims that all schema instantiation in conceptual knowledge relies on dynamic, distributed role-filler binding.

References:

  • Hummel & Holyoak (2003) A symbolic-connectionist theory of relational inference and generalization [role-filler binding]
  • Frankland & Greene (2015) An architecture for encoding sentence meaning in left mid-superior temporal cortex [dynamic binding in language]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Disruption of dynamic binding mechanisms will impair the ability to instantiate novel conceptual combinations.
  2. Hierarchical neural activation patterns will be observed when processing multi-level conceptual structures.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificial neural networks with explicit schema and dynamic binding mechanisms will outperform standard models on compositional reasoning tasks.
  2. Direct neural evidence for recursive schema instantiation will be found in high-resolution imaging studies.

Negative Experiments

  1. If concepts can be instantiated without any evidence of schema structure or role-filler binding, the theory would be undermined.
  2. If hierarchical compositionality is not observed in neural or behavioral data, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not specify how abstract schemas are initially acquired or grounded in sensory experience.
