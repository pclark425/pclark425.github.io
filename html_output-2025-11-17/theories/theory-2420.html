Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2420

Name: Network Activity and Metabolic Load Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory proposes that the selective vulnerability or resistance of visual cortex neuronal populations to amyloid-β and tau pathology is determined by their patterns of network activity and associated metabolic demands. Neurons with high synaptic activity, elevated calcium influx, and greater oxidative metabolism are more susceptible to Aβ/tau-induced stress, while those with lower activity or more efficient energy management are more resistant.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: High Network Activity Increases Vulnerability (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuron → has_high_synaptic_activity → True, and
• neuron → has_high_calcium_influx → True
THEN
• neuron → is_vulnerable_to → Aβ/tau pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neurons with high firing rates and synaptic activity show increased Aβ/tau accumulation in animal models.
  2. Calcium dysregulation is a known mediator of neurodegeneration in Alzheimer's disease.
  3. Visual cortex neurons with high activity during visual stimulation are more likely to develop pathology.

What is Novel: This law applies the principle to visual cortex populations and links specific activity and calcium influx to vulnerability.

• neuron → has_high_expression_of → mitochondrial efficiency genes, and

  1. Reducing synaptic activity in vulnerable visual cortex neurons will decrease Aβ/tau accumulation.
  2. Enhancing mitochondrial efficiency in visual cortex neurons will confer resistance to pathology.
  3. Visual cortex neurons with high activity during visual tasks will show greater pathology in animal models.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially increasing network activity in resistant neurons will render them vulnerable to Aβ/tau pathology.
  2. Pharmacological reduction of calcium influx in vulnerable neurons will prevent pathology even under high activity.
  3. Boosting metabolic efficiency in already resistant neurons will further increase resistance or have no effect.

Negative Experiments

  1. If high activity neurons do not show increased vulnerability to Aβ/tau pathology, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If enhancing mitochondrial efficiency does not confer resistance, the theory would be undermined.
  3. If reducing calcium influx does not protect high-activity neurons, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some low-activity neurons still accumulate pathology, suggesting other factors are involved.
  2. Certain high-activity neurons remain resistant, possibly due to unknown protective mechanisms.
