Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1370

Name: Iterative Self-Alignment Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of how language models perform self-reflection and improve their answer quality through multiple iterations of generate-then-reflect.

Description: This theory posits that language models improve answer quality through generate-then-reflect cycles by iteratively aligning their outputs with internalized representations of correctness, coherence, and task requirements. Each reflection step acts as a self-supervised feedback loop, allowing the model to re-evaluate and adjust its response based on discrepancies between its output and its learned standards.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Self-Supervised Feedback Loop (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• language model → performs → reflection on its own output
THEN
• language model → compares → output to internalized standards, and
• language model → adjusts → subsequent output to reduce discrepancies

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Empirical studies show that iterative reflection leads to improved factuality and coherence in model outputs.
  2. Models trained with self-refinement or self-critique demonstrate higher answer quality after multiple iterations.
  3. Reflection steps often involve explicit comparison to task instructions or known facts.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While iterative improvement is known, the theory formalizes the mechanism as self-supervised alignment.

What Already Exists: Iterative self-improvement via self-refinement and self-critique is observed in recent LLM research.

What is Novel: The explicit framing of reflection as a self-supervised feedback loop aligning outputs to internalized standards is new.

References:

  • Madaan et al. (2023) Self-Refine [iterative correction, but not formalized as self-supervised alignment]
  • Shinn et al. (2023) Reflexion [reflection as self-improvement, but not formalized as feedback loop]

Statement 1: Convergence Toward Internal Consistency (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• language model → performs → multiple generate-then-reflect cycles
THEN
• model outputs → tend toward → greater internal consistency and alignment with task requirements

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Repeated reflection reduces contradictions and increases answer consistency.
  2. Empirical results show diminishing returns after several reflection cycles, indicating convergence.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The convergence aspect is implicit in prior work but not formalized as a law.

What Already Exists: Improvement and convergence with iterative self-refinement is observed.

What is Novel: The law formalizes convergence as a tendency toward internal consistency, not just accuracy.

References:

  • Madaan et al. (2023) Self-Refine [iterative improvement, but not formalized as convergence to consistency]
  • Shinn et al. (2023) Reflexion [iterative self-improvement, but not formalized as convergence law]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If a model is allowed to reflect multiple times, its answers will become more consistent and less contradictory.
  2. Reflection cycles will lead to diminishing improvements after a certain number of iterations.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If a model's internalized standards are flawed, repeated reflection may converge to consistently incorrect answers.
  2. If models are trained with adversarial standards, reflection may amplify adversarial features.

Negative Experiments

  1. If repeated reflection does not increase answer consistency, the convergence law is falsified.
  2. If reflection cycles do not improve answer quality, the self-supervised feedback loop law is called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases where models introduce new errors during reflection are not fully explained.

  • Shinn et al. (2023) Reflexion [reflection as self-improvement, but not formalized as convergence law]
