Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1460

Name: Contextually Modulated Dynamic Schema Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This theory proposes that conceptual knowledge in brains is represented as dynamic, context-sensitive schemas. Each concept is encoded as a flexible schema—a structured set of slots and fillers—whose instantiation is modulated in real time by contextual cues, task demands, and recent experience. The representational format is not static: the same concept can be instantiated with different features, relations, or levels of abstraction depending on context, supporting rapid adaptation and context-dependent reasoning.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Contextual Modulation Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• conceptual representation → is_accessed_in_context → context_X
THEN
• conceptual schema → is_instantiated_with → features_and_relations_relevant_to_context_X

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Behavioral priming and context effects show that the same concept can activate different features depending on task or context.
  2. Neuroimaging studies reveal context-dependent activation patterns for the same concept in different tasks.
  3. Semantic flexibility in language use (e.g., polysemy) demonstrates context-driven shifts in conceptual representation.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While context effects and schema theories are known, the claim that the representational format is itself dynamic and contextually modulated is a novel, functional-level assertion.

What Already Exists: Context effects in conceptual processing are well-documented, and schema theories exist in cognitive science.

What is Novel: This law asserts that the representational format itself is dynamically instantiated and context-sensitive, not just the activation of static representations.

References:

  • Barsalou (1982) Context-independent and context-dependent information in concepts [context effects]
  • Rumelhart (1980) Schemata: The building blocks of cognition [schema theory]

Statement 1: Schema Flexibility Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• conceptual schema → is_accessed_in_novel_context → True
THEN
• conceptual schema → can_reconfigure_slots_and_fillers → to_match_contextual_demands

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. People can rapidly adapt conceptual knowledge to new situations (e.g., using 'chair' as a stepstool).
  2. Neural evidence shows rapid reconfiguration of conceptual networks in response to changing task demands.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The novelty lies in attributing flexibility to the representational format, not just to processing or retrieval mechanisms.

What Already Exists: Schema flexibility is discussed in cognitive psychology, but often as a process rather than a representational property.

What is Novel: This law claims that the representational format itself is inherently flexible and reconfigurable, not just the process of retrieval or use.

References:

  • Barsalou (1987) The instability of graded structure in concepts [flexibility in conceptual structure]
  • Rumelhart (1980) Schemata: The building blocks of cognition [schema theory]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Neural representations of the same concept will differ systematically depending on context, even when controlling for sensory input.
  2. Behavioral priming effects will be stronger when contextually relevant features are shared between prime and target.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificial systems with dynamically instantiated schema representations will show superior context-dependent generalization compared to static models.
  2. Disruption of context-sensitive schema instantiation (e.g., via TMS to prefrontal cortex) will impair flexible conceptual reasoning but not rote recall.

Negative Experiments

  1. If neural or behavioral data show that conceptual representations are invariant across all contexts, this would challenge the theory.
  2. If people cannot flexibly adapt conceptual knowledge to novel contexts, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not specify the underlying mechanism for how context cues are integrated to modulate schema instantiation.

Existing Theory Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The theory extends schema and context theories by making a strong claim about the dynamic, context-modulated nature of the representational format itself.

  • Rumelhart (1980) Schemata: The building blocks of cognition [schema theory]
