Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-725

Name: Emergent Algorithmic Reasoning Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of how language models perform arithmetic.

Description: Some large language models, especially when prompted with chain-of-thought or step-by-step reasoning, exhibit emergent algorithmic reasoning capabilities for arithmetic. This theory posits that, beyond pattern completion, sufficiently large and well-trained LLMs can learn to approximate multi-step algorithmic procedures for arithmetic, especially when guided by explicit reasoning prompts.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Chain-of-Thought Induction Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• arithmetic_query → is_input_to → language_model, and
• prompt → contains → chain_of_thought_instruction
THEN
• language_model → generates → stepwise_reasoning_sequence, and
• language_model → arithmetic_accuracy → increased

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Chain-of-thought prompting improves LLM accuracy on arithmetic tasks, even for queries not seen during training.
  2. LLMs can output intermediate steps that mirror human-like multi-step arithmetic procedures.
  3. Emergent reasoning abilities are observed in larger models, especially with explicit reasoning prompts.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: Closely related to recent work on chain-of-thought prompting, but this law formalizes its effect on arithmetic and emergent algorithmic reasoning.

What Already Exists: Chain-of-thought prompting is known to improve LLM performance on reasoning tasks.

What is Novel: This law formalizes the link between chain-of-thought prompting and emergent algorithmic reasoning for arithmetic, and predicts increased accuracy and stepwise outputs.

References:

  • Wei et al. (2022) Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in Large Language Models [Chain-of-thought improves arithmetic]
  • Nye et al. (2021) Show Your Work: Scratchpads for Intermediate Computation with Language Models [Stepwise reasoning in LLMs]

Statement 1: Scale-Threshold Law for Algorithmic Reasoning (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• language_model → parameter_count → above_threshold, and
• training_data → contains → diverse_arithmetic_examples
THEN
• language_model → can_approximate → multi-step_arithmetic_algorithms

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Larger LLMs (e.g., GPT-3, PaLM) show improved generalization to novel arithmetic queries, especially with chain-of-thought prompting.
  2. Smaller models or models with limited training data do not exhibit emergent algorithmic reasoning.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: Closely related to existing work on emergent abilities in LLMs, but this law formalizes the threshold for algorithmic arithmetic reasoning.

What Already Exists: Emergent abilities in LLMs as a function of scale are documented.

What is Novel: This law applies the scale-threshold concept specifically to algorithmic reasoning for arithmetic, predicting a qualitative shift in capability.

References:

  • Wei et al. (2022) Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models [Emergent reasoning in LLMs]
  • Srivastava et al. (2022) Beyond the Imitation Game: Quantifying and extrapolating the capabilities of language models [Scaling laws and emergent abilities]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If a sufficiently large LLM is prompted with explicit chain-of-thought instructions, it will solve novel multi-digit arithmetic problems with higher accuracy than without such prompts.
  2. If a model is below the scale threshold, chain-of-thought prompting will not significantly improve arithmetic accuracy.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If a model is trained with chain-of-thought examples for arithmetic, it may generalize to new arithmetic operations not seen in training.
  2. If a model is scaled beyond current sizes, it may develop the ability to perform algorithmic arithmetic without explicit chain-of-thought prompts.

Negative Experiments

  1. If chain-of-thought prompting does not improve arithmetic accuracy in large models, this would challenge the theory.
  2. If small models can perform multi-step arithmetic with high accuracy, this would challenge the scale-threshold law.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some models show improved arithmetic accuracy with chain-of-thought prompting even when their parameter count is below the hypothesized threshold.
