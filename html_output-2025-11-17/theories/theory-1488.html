Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1488

Name: Hierarchical Integration of Modal and Amodal Codes

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This theory proposes that conceptual knowledge is represented in a hierarchical system, where lower levels encode modal (sensory-motor) features and higher levels encode increasingly abstract, amodal features. Integration across levels allows for both grounded and abstract conceptual processing, with dynamic recruitment of levels depending on task demands.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Hierarchical Encoding of Conceptual Features (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• conceptual_feature → is_processed → in_brain
THEN
• conceptual_feature → is_encoded → at_specific_hierarchical_level

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neuroimaging shows a posterior-to-anterior gradient from sensory-motor to association cortices during conceptual tasks.
  2. Lesion studies reveal that damage at different hierarchical levels produces different conceptual deficits.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law extends hierarchical models to the integration of modal and amodal conceptual codes.

What Already Exists: Hierarchical processing is well-established in perception and language.

What is Novel: Explicit mapping of modal–amodal integration onto a functional hierarchy for conceptual knowledge is novel.

References:

  • Patterson, Nestor, & Rogers (2007) Where do you know what you know? [semantic hub theory]
  • Lambon Ralph et al. (2017) The neural and computational bases of semantic cognition [hierarchical models]

Statement 1: Task-Driven Recruitment of Hierarchical Levels (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• task → requires → specific_feature_type
THEN
• brain → recruits → corresponding_hierarchical_level

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Tasks emphasizing perceptual detail activate lower (modal) levels; tasks requiring abstraction activate higher (amodal) levels.
  2. Patients with anterior temporal lobe damage (semantic hub) show deficits in abstract conceptual tasks.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law adapts hierarchical recruitment to the modal–amodal continuum.

What Already Exists: Task-driven recruitment is known in perception and language.

What is Novel: Application to modal–amodal integration in conceptual knowledge is novel.

References:

  • Patterson, Nestor, & Rogers (2007) Where do you know what you know? [semantic hub theory]
  • Lambon Ralph et al. (2017) The neural and computational bases of semantic cognition [hierarchical models]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Tasks requiring integration of perceptual and abstract features will show increased connectivity between hierarchical levels.
  2. Developmental studies will show progressive recruitment of higher (amodal) levels with age.
  3. Lesions at different levels will produce selective deficits in modal or amodal conceptual tasks.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificial stimulation of intermediate levels will bias conceptual processing toward hybrid representations.
  2. Machine learning models with hierarchical integration will better match human conceptual flexibility.
  3. Cross-modal training will enhance integration across hierarchical levels.

Negative Experiments

  1. If no hierarchical organization is observed in conceptual processing, the theory would be falsified.
  2. If task demands do not modulate recruitment of hierarchical levels, the theory would be undermined.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The mechanisms for cross-level integration are not fully specified.
