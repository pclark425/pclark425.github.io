Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1500

Name: Hierarchical Precision-Weighted Format Arbitration

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This theory proposes that conceptual format arbitration operates hierarchically: at lower levels, sensory and motor formats are selected based on their precision for immediate perceptual or action tasks; at higher levels, symbolic and abstract formats are selected for reasoning and planning. Arbitration at each level is governed by a precision-weighted mechanism, and information can be passed up or down the hierarchy as needed.

Statement 0: Hierarchical Arbitration Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails

• cognitive task → requires → multi-level conceptual processing
THEN
• arbitration → occurs_at → multiple hierarchical levels

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Hierarchical models of perception and action show arbitration at multiple levels (e.g., sensorimotor, cognitive).
  2. Neuroimaging shows distinct but interacting brain regions for low-level and high-level conceptual processing.

Statement 1: Cross-Level Precision Propagation Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• lower-level format → has_low_precision → for current task
THEN
• higher-level format → is_selected_with_higher_weight → for conceptual processing

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. When sensory input is ambiguous, people rely more on abstract or symbolic reasoning.
  2. Computational models show that uncertainty at lower levels can trigger higher-level inference.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law adapts uncertainty propagation to conceptual format selection, which is not previously formalized.

What Already Exists: Uncertainty propagation in hierarchical models is established in perception and action.

What is Novel: Explicit application to conceptual format arbitration, with precision signals propagating up the hierarchy.

References:

  • Friston (2010) The free-energy principle: a unified brain theory? [uncertainty propagation in hierarchical models]
  • Clark (2013) Whatever next? Predictive brains, situated agents, and the future of cognitive science [hierarchical predictive processing]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Tasks that degrade lower-level format precision (e.g., visual noise) will increase reliance on higher-level formats (e.g., verbal reasoning) for the same concept.
  2. Neuroimaging will show increased activation in higher-order brain regions when lower-level format precision is experimentally reduced.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Training to increase lower-level format precision (e.g., perceptual learning) will reduce reliance on higher-level formats for conceptual tasks.
  2. Disorders affecting hierarchical processing (e.g., schizophrenia) will show abnormal patterns of format arbitration and cross-level precision propagation.

Negative Experiments

  1. If reducing lower-level format precision does not increase higher-level format use, the theory's hierarchical arbitration is called into question.
  2. If neuroimaging does not show predicted shifts in activation across hierarchical levels, the theory is undermined.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not specify how arbitration signals are computed or communicated between levels.
