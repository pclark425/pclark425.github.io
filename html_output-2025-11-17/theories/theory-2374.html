Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2374

Name: Activity-Dependent Propagation and Selective Vulnerability Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory proposes that selective vulnerability in the visual cortex is governed by the patterns of neuronal activity and connectivity that facilitate the trans-synaptic spread of pathological amyloid-β (Aβ) and tau species. Neurons with high-frequency firing, long-range, or hub-like connectivity are more likely to accumulate pathology due to increased exposure to propagating toxic species, while neurons with local, low-frequency, or inhibitory connectivity are more resistant. The theory integrates evidence from trans-synaptic propagation models, activity-dependent tau/Aβ release, and the observed sparing of inhibitory and local circuit neurons.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Activity-Driven Pathology Propagation (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuron → has_property → high firing rate, and
• neuron → is_connected_to → long-range projection neurons
THEN
• neuron → has_increased_risk → trans-synaptic Aβ/tau propagation

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Experimental models show that increased neuronal activity accelerates tau and Aβ spread.
  2. Hub neurons and long-range projection neurons are early sites of pathology in AD models.
  3. Trans-synaptic spread of tau and Aβ is facilitated by synaptic activity and connectivity.
  4. Visual cortex projection neurons with high activity show earlier and more severe pathology in animal models.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The general principle is established, but its explicit application to visual cortex selectivity and formalization as a conditional law is novel.

What Already Exists: Activity-dependent propagation of tau and Aβ has been demonstrated in animal models, and hub/long-range projection neurons are known to be early sites of pathology.

What is Novel: This law applies the principle specifically to visual cortex populations, and formalizes the link between activity/connectivity and selective vulnerability.

References:

  • Wu et al. (2016) Neuronal activity enhances tau propagation and tau pathology in vivo [activity-dependent spread]
  • de Calignon et al. (2012) Propagation of tau pathology in a model of early Alzheimer's disease [trans-synaptic spread]
  • Yamamoto et al. (2015) Chronic optogenetic activation augments Aβ pathology in vivo [activity-dependent Aβ release]

Statement 1: Inhibitory and Local Connectivity Confers Resistance (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuron → has_property → inhibitory neurotransmitter phenotype, and
• neuron → is_connected_to → local microcircuit
THEN
• neuron → has_decreased_risk → Aβ/tau pathology accumulation

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Inhibitory interneurons are relatively spared in early AD pathology.
  2. Local circuit neurons in primary visual cortex show delayed pathology compared to projection neurons.
  3. GABAergic neurons exhibit lower rates of tau and Aβ accumulation in both human and animal models.
  4. Functional imaging shows preserved inhibitory function in early visual cortex pathology.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The observation is known, but the explicit conditional and mechanistic link to propagation and visual cortex selectivity is novel.

What Already Exists: Relative resistance of inhibitory neurons and local circuits is observed in AD, but the mechanistic link to propagation is less formalized.

What is Novel: This law formalizes the conditional link between inhibitory/local connectivity and resistance in the context of propagation theory, specifically in the visual cortex.

References:

  • Rudy et al. (2011) Three groups of interneurons account for nearly 100% of neocortical GABAergic neurons [inhibitory neuron types]
  • Kuchibhotla et al. (2014) Neurofibrillary tangle-bearing neurons are functionally integrated in cortical circuits in vivo [local circuit resistance]
  • Verret et al. (2012) Inhibitory interneuron deficit links altered network activity and cognitive dysfunction in Alzheimer model [inhibitory neuron sparing]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Artificially increasing activity in resistant visual cortical neurons (e.g., via optogenetics) will increase their vulnerability to Aβ/tau pathology.
  2. Selective silencing of hub or long-range projection neurons will reduce the spread of pathology in visual cortex models.
  3. Enhancing inhibitory tone in vulnerable projection neurons will confer resistance to pathology.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Inducing long-range connectivity in otherwise resistant local circuit neurons will render them vulnerable to pathology.
  2. Chronic suppression of activity in projection neurons will prevent or delay pathology even in the presence of high extracellular Aβ/tau.
  3. Altering the balance of excitation/inhibition in visual cortex will shift the pattern of selective vulnerability.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding that neurons with high activity and long-range connectivity do not accumulate pathology would challenge the theory.
  2. Observing that inhibitory, local circuit neurons develop pathology at the same rate as excitatory, long-range neurons would call the theory into question.
  3. Demonstrating that activity manipulation has no effect on pathology spread in visual cortex would refute the theory.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some projection neurons may be resistant despite high activity, possibly due to unknown protective factors such as gene expression or local microenvironment.
  2. Regional differences in vascularization or glial support may influence vulnerability independently of activity/connectivity.
