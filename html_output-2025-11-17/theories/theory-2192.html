Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2192

Name: Dynamic Feedback Alignment Theory for LLM-Generated Scientific Theory Evaluation

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of how to evaluate LLM-generated scientific theories.

Description: This theory proposes that the evaluation of LLM-generated scientific theories is a dynamic, iterative process in which feedback from multiple evaluators (human and automated) is used to realign the theory along key dimensions. The process is not static: as new evidence, critiques, or perspectives emerge, the alignment of the theory is updated, and the evaluation outcome can change over time.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Iterative Feedback Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• LLM-generated theory → is_evaluated → by multiple evaluators, and
• evaluation → produces → feedback
THEN
• theory alignment → is_updated → based on feedback, and
• evaluation outcome → can_change → over time

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Scientific theories are often revised in response to peer review and new evidence (Kuhn, 1962; Langley et al., 1987).
  2. LLM-generated outputs can be improved through iterative prompting and feedback (Ziegler et al., 2019).
  3. Human-in-the-loop evaluation and revision cycles are standard in both science and LLM development.

Statement 1: Feedback-Driven Realignment Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• feedback → identifies → misalignment on any axis
THEN
• theory → is_revised → to improve alignment

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Peer review and critique lead to theory revision in science.
  2. LLM outputs can be improved by incorporating feedback on specific weaknesses.
  3. Automated and human feedback loops are used to improve LLM outputs and scientific theories.

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If an LLM-generated theory receives critical feedback on empirical adequacy, subsequent revisions will improve empirical alignment.
  2. Theories that undergo more feedback cycles will be rated as more robust by evaluators.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Automated feedback loops may lead to overfitting to evaluator preferences, reducing true scientific novelty.
  2. In adversarial feedback environments, theory alignment may oscillate or fail to converge.

Negative Experiments

  1. If repeated feedback does not lead to improved alignment or evaluation outcomes, the theory is challenged.
  2. If feedback is ignored and theories are still accepted, the necessity of feedback-driven realignment is questioned.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases where feedback is biased or systematically flawed, leading to misalignment.
