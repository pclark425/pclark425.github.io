Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1518

Name: Dynamic Contextual Modulation Theory of Functional Stable Coactivation

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This theory proposes that the functional individuation of concepts is not only determined by stable coactivation of features, but also by the dynamic modulation of these patterns according to context. A concept is individuated if its core coactivation pattern remains stable across a range of relevant contexts, but can be flexibly modulated by contextual inputs without losing its core identity. This allows for both stability and adaptability in conceptual representation.

Statement 0: Contextually Modulated Stability Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails

• feature_set → coactivates_stably → across_core_contexts, and
• feature_set → coactivation_pattern → is_modulated_by, and
• core_pattern → remains_identifiable → across_modulations
THEN
• feature_set → functionally_individuates → a_concept

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neuroimaging shows that concept representations are modulated by context but retain a core pattern (e.g., Yee & Thompson-Schill, 2016).
  2. Behavioral studies show context effects on concept access, but core meaning is preserved (e.g., polysemy literature).
  3. Computational models (e.g., context-sensitive embeddings) show stable core representations with contextual modulation.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While context effects are known, the explicit individuation criterion based on core pattern stability under modulation is novel.

What Already Exists: Contextual modulation of conceptual representations is well-documented in cognitive neuroscience and computational linguistics.

What is Novel: The law formalizes the requirement that core stability must be preserved under contextual modulation for individuation.

References:

  • Yee & Thompson-Schill (2016) Putting concepts into context [contextual modulation of conceptual representations]
  • Peters et al. (2018) Deep contextualized word representations [context-sensitive embeddings]
  • Leshinskaya & Caramazza (2016) For a cognitive neuroscience of concepts: Moving beyond single-word studies [contextual effects in concept representation]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Concepts with core patterns that are not preserved under contextual modulation will be less reliably individuated.
  2. Training that enhances the stability of core patterns under context will improve concept discrimination.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Some concepts may lack a stable core pattern, especially in highly abstract or context-dependent domains.
  2. Artificial systems using this criterion may develop contextually fluid, non-human-like concepts.

Negative Experiments

  1. If concepts can be individuated without a stable core pattern under contextual modulation, the theory would be undermined.
  2. If context modulation destroys core pattern identity but individuation persists, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not specify mechanisms for identifying or extracting the core pattern from modulated patterns.
