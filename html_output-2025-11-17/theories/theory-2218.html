Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2218

Name: Theory of Epistemic Traceability for LLM-Generated Theories

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of how to evaluate LLM-generated scientific theories.

Description: This theory posits that the scientific value and trustworthiness of LLM-generated theories are fundamentally dependent on the epistemic traceability of their claims. That is, every assertion within an LLM-generated theory should be traceable to its source data, underlying reasoning, and, where possible, to the scientific literature or empirical evidence. The theory further asserts that traceability enables robust error analysis, facilitates scientific debate, and is essential for integrating LLM-generated theories into the broader scientific enterprise.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Epistemic Traceability Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• theory → is_generated_by → LLM
THEN
• theory → should_have → traceable_assertions, and
• assertion → should_be_traceable_to → source_data_or_literature, and
• assertion → should_be_traceable_to → reasoning_chain

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Scientific practice requires traceability of claims for verification and error correction.
  2. LLMs are prone to hallucination and may generate unsupported or untraceable claims.
  3. Traceability enables error analysis and scientific debate.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law extends the principle of traceability to the granular level of LLM-generated scientific assertions.

What Already Exists: Traceability is a norm in scientific publishing and data management.

What is Novel: Mandating epistemic traceability for every assertion in LLM-generated theories is novel.

References:

  • Wilkinson et al. (2016) The FAIR Guiding Principles for scientific data management and stewardship [traceability, reproducibility]
  • Bender et al. (2021) On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots [LLM hallucination, need for traceability]

Statement 1: Traceability-Trustworthiness Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• theory → has_high_epistemic_traceability → True
THEN
• theory → is_more_likely_to_be → scientifically_trustworthy

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Scientific claims with clear provenance are more likely to be trusted and integrated into the scientific literature.
  2. Lack of traceability is a major source of error and mistrust in LLM-generated outputs.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law formalizes the relationship between traceability and trustworthiness for LLM-generated scientific theories.

What Already Exists: Trust in science is linked to traceability and reproducibility.

What is Novel: Explicitly linking trustworthiness of LLM-generated theories to epistemic traceability is novel.

References:

  • Wilkinson et al. (2016) The FAIR Guiding Principles [traceability, trust]
  • Bender et al. (2021) On the Dangers of Stochastic Parrots [LLM trust and traceability]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. LLM-generated theories with high epistemic traceability will be more readily accepted and cited in scientific discourse.
  2. Traceable LLM-generated theories will facilitate more effective error correction and scientific debate.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. High traceability may reveal systematic biases in LLM training data that would otherwise go undetected.
  2. Mandating traceability may limit the generative creativity of LLMs, potentially reducing the novelty of their theories.

Negative Experiments

  1. If highly traceable LLM-generated theories are found to be systematically incorrect, the sufficiency of traceability as a trust metric is called into question.
  2. If untraceable but correct LLM-generated theories are routinely discovered, the necessity of traceability is challenged.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not address how to handle assertions based on genuinely novel synthesis or abduction, where no direct source exists.
