Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2362

Name: Integrated Network Disruption Theory of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory posits that Alzheimer's disease (AD) arises from the convergence of multiple, interacting biological networks—genetic, metabolic, vascular, immune, and neural—whose dysregulation leads to a self-reinforcing cascade of neurodegeneration. Rather than a single causative agent, AD is the emergent result of network failures, with early disruptions in one domain (e.g., vascular or metabolic) amplifying dysfunction in others (e.g., immune, synaptic), ultimately manifesting as cognitive decline. Detection is most effective when it targets multi-modal network signatures rather than isolated biomarkers.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Network Cascade Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → dysregulation in at least two biological networks (e.g., vascular, metabolic, immune, neural)
THEN
• individual → is at increased risk for → self-reinforcing neurodegenerative cascade leading to AD

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Epidemiological studies show that combinations of vascular, metabolic, and immune risk factors synergistically increase AD risk.
  2. Multi-omic analyses reveal that AD patients exhibit disruptions across genetic, metabolic, and immune networks.
  3. Longitudinal studies indicate that early vascular or metabolic dysfunction predicts later amyloid and tau pathology.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While multifactorial risk is recognized, the explicit network cascade and feedback model is a novel synthesis.

What Already Exists: Multifactorial models of AD exist, but most focus on additive rather than interactive network effects.

What is Novel: This law formalizes the concept of network interactions and feedback loops as central to AD causation.

References:

  • De Strooper & Karran (2016) The Cellular Phase of Alzheimer's Disease [Multifactorial, network-based view]
  • Iturria-Medina et al. (2016) Early role of vascular dysregulation in late-onset Alzheimer's disease [Network interactions]

Statement 1: Multi-Modal Detection Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• detection_method → integrates → multi-modal data (genetic, metabolic, vascular, immune, neural)
THEN
• detection_method → achieves → higher sensitivity and specificity for early AD detection than single-modality methods

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Machine learning models combining blood, imaging, and genetic data outperform single-biomarker approaches.
  2. Multi-modal risk scores better predict conversion from mild cognitive impairment to AD.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The explicit requirement for network-level, multi-modal integration is a novel theoretical advance.

What Already Exists: Multi-modal biomarker approaches are being explored, but not formalized as a law.

What is Novel: This law asserts the necessity of network-level, multi-modal integration for optimal detection.

References:

  • Hampel et al. (2018) Blood-based biomarkers for Alzheimer disease: mapping the road to the clinic [Multi-modal detection]
  • Iturria-Medina et al. (2017) Multifactorial causal model of brain (dis)organization and therapeutic intervention: Application to Alzheimer's disease [Network-based detection]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with combined vascular and immune dysregulation will progress to AD at higher rates than those with single risk factors.
  2. Multi-modal risk scores will outperform amyloid or tau biomarkers alone in predicting AD onset.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Interventions targeting multiple network nodes (e.g., vascular and immune) simultaneously will be more effective than single-target therapies.
  2. Distinct network disruption patterns will define AD subtypes with different clinical trajectories.

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals with multi-network dysregulation do not show increased AD risk, the theory is challenged.
  2. If multi-modal detection does not outperform single-modality methods, the theory is called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Rare monogenic forms of AD may not require multi-network disruption.
