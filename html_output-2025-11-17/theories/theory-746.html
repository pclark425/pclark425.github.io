Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-746

Name: Dual-Process Theory of LM Arithmetic

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of how language models perform arithmetic.

Description: Language models (LMs) perform arithmetic via two interacting processes: (1) pattern-based retrieval, where the model leverages memorized or frequently seen arithmetic facts and templates, and (2) emergent algorithmic reasoning, where the model composes multi-step symbolic manipulations from its learned representations. The balance between these processes depends on the familiarity, complexity, and context of the arithmetic problem.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Pattern Retrieval Dominance Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• arithmetic_problem → is_similar_to → training_examples, and
• language_model → is_prompted_with → arithmetic_problem
THEN
• language_model → outputs → memorized_or_patterned_result

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. LMs are highly accurate on arithmetic facts and problems seen during training or that follow common patterns.
  2. Performance drops on out-of-distribution or rare arithmetic problems.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: Pattern retrieval is known, but its explicit role as a primary process in LM arithmetic is newly formalized here.

What Already Exists: Pattern retrieval and memorization are well-established in LMs for factual and arithmetic tasks.

What is Novel: This law formalizes the dominance of pattern retrieval in arithmetic when problems are familiar.

References:

  • Brown et al. (2020) Language Models are Few-Shot Learners [Pattern completion and memorization in LMs]
  • Cobbe et al. (2021) Training Verifiers to Solve Math Word Problems [Pattern-based errors in arithmetic]

Statement 1: Emergent Algorithmic Reasoning Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• arithmetic_problem → is_complex_or_unfamiliar → True, and
• language_model → has_sufficient_scale → True
THEN
• language_model → performs → multi-step_symbolic_manipulation

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Large LMs (e.g., GPT-3, PaLM) show emergent abilities to perform multi-step arithmetic not directly seen in training.
  2. Chain-of-thought prompting improves arithmetic accuracy, suggesting algorithmic reasoning.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: Emergent reasoning is a new area; this law formalizes its conditions and role in arithmetic.

What Already Exists: Emergent algorithmic reasoning in LMs is a recent observation, but not fully formalized.

What is Novel: This law explicitly links model scale and unfamiliarity to the emergence of algorithmic reasoning in arithmetic.

References:

  • Wei et al. (2022) Emergent Abilities of Large Language Models [Emergent algorithmic reasoning]
  • Nye et al. (2021) Show Your Work: Scratchpads for Intermediate Computation in Language Models [Multi-step reasoning in LMs]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If a model is prompted with arithmetic problems that are rare or OOD, accuracy will increase with model scale and chain-of-thought prompting.
  2. For arithmetic facts seen in training, even small LMs will perform well, but for novel multi-step problems, only large LMs will succeed.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If a model is trained on synthetic arithmetic data with novel operations, it may develop emergent reasoning for those operations at sufficient scale.
  2. There may exist a threshold model size above which algorithmic reasoning for arithmetic becomes robust, regardless of training data frequency.

Negative Experiments

  1. If small LMs can perform complex, unfamiliar arithmetic as well as large LMs, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If LMs never show improved arithmetic with chain-of-thought prompting, the emergent reasoning law would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some LMs fine-tuned on arithmetic datasets can perform well even on OOD problems, suggesting fine-tuning can override the dual-process balance.
