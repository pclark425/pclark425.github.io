Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-189

Name: Contact-Rich Manipulation Transfer Requirements

Type: specific

Theory Query: Build a theory about sim-to-real transfer for scientific discovery agents that specifies environment fidelity requirements and skill-transfer conditions from virtual labs to real robotics labs, based on the following results.

Description: For contact-rich manipulation tasks (assembly, insertion, wiping, grasping, bolting), successful sim-to-real transfer requires: (1) contact dynamics fidelity sufficient to predict contact forces and deformations accurately enough for the task clearance and compliance requirements, (2) friction modeling with coefficients within task-dependent tolerances, (3) either high-fidelity geometry (mesh-level for tight tolerances) or learned residual corrections, and (4) compliance/impedance parameters that match the task requirements or can be adapted. The required fidelity level depends on task characteristics: tight-tolerance tasks (<2mm clearance) require higher fidelity (force errors <15%, friction ±0.1) than loose-tolerance tasks (>5mm clearance, force errors <30%, friction ±0.2). Transfer can succeed with approximate physics if combined with: online adaptation (force-feedback, admittance learning), learned residuals from real contact data, or robust training (domain randomization with unmodeled-effect proxies). The choice between high-fidelity simulation and approximate simulation with adaptation depends on task complexity, available real-world data, and deployment constraints.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

  1. For tight-tolerance assembly tasks (clearance <2mm), contact force prediction errors must be <15% and friction coefficients within ±0.1 to succeed without online adaptation
  2. For moderate-tolerance manipulation tasks (clearance 2-5mm), contact force prediction errors <30% and friction within ±0.2 are sufficient with appropriate control strategies
  3. Mesh-level geometry accuracy (<1mm error) is required for assembly tasks with <2mm clearance when using rigid-body simulation
  4. Online adaptation of compliance/admittance parameters can compensate for 50-100% errors in simulated contact stiffness if force feedback is available
  5. Learned residual models can correct for systematic contact modeling errors if trained on 100-1000 real contact examples, achieving 2-5x improvement over physics-only models
  6. Explicit penetration detection and prevention in simulation is necessary to avoid policies exploiting simulation artifacts in tight-tolerance tasks
  7. Force-feedback control reduces sensitivity to contact modeling errors by enabling online adaptation and avoiding position-control instabilities
  8. Domain randomization of friction (range 0.5-1.5x nominal) combined with unmodeled-effect proxies can enable transfer without system identification for tasks with >5mm clearance
  9. For deformable object manipulation, FEM-level modeling or learned deformation-to-sensor mappings are required to achieve <5mm position errors
  10. Tactile-based manipulation requires either physics-based sensor simulation (FEM + optical model) or learned latent projections from physics to sensor space
  11. Multi-contact scenarios (e.g., multi-fingered grasping) require modeling of load distribution and contact redundancy, not just individual contact forces
  12. Quasi-static contact tasks are more tolerant of contact modeling errors than dynamic contact tasks, which require accurate impact and restitution modeling
  13. The choice between high-fidelity simulation and approximate simulation with adaptation depends on: task clearance (<2mm requires high fidelity), available real data (>100 samples enables residual learning), and deployment constraints (online adaptation requires force sensing)

Supporting Evidence

  1. Peg-in-hole insertion with OSC required accurate system identification of gravity, link masses, and joint damping; achieved 20/20 success without dynamics randomization [e1654.0] [e1654.2]
  2. Assembly tasks (IndustReal) required mesh-level geometry via learned implicit surfaces, explicit penetration detection (SAPU), and contact modeling to prevent exploiting simulation artifacts; achieved 84.9-87.6% success [e1681.0] [e1681.1] [e1681.2]
  3. Wiping task required contact force modeling (mean 28N in sim) and friction randomization (0.1-1.0); achieved 80% real success with VICES end-effector control [e1788.0]
  4. Wiping and assembly tasks required online admittance adaptation to handle contact dynamics mismatch; manual tuning achieved 100% success but was task-specific [e1650.0] [e1650.2]
  5. Drawer opening required contact dynamics between gripper and handle; SimOpt adaptation of cabinet position (σ~2cm) and friction enabled 100% success (20/20 trials) [e1657.2]
  6. Dexterous manipulation (Shadow Hand) required accurate contact dynamics, friction randomization, and unmodeled-effect proxies (motor backlash, marker occlusion); achieved median 13 consecutive rotations in reality vs 0 without randomization [e1651.0]
  7. Dexterous manipulation (Allegro Hand) required heavy domain randomization including friction (scaling), contact dynamics (restitution), and ADR curriculum; achieved 27.8±19.0 consecutive successes [e1791.0] [e1791.1]
  8. Tactile sensing (BioTac) required FEM-level deformation modeling with optimized material parameters (Young's modulus, friction) to match ANSYS; achieved electrode prediction errors ~20-31 raw units on unseen objects [e1835.0]
  9. Tactile sensing (GelSight) required accurate contact-deformation model and collision margin handling; TACTO baseline with simplified contact model achieved only 42.6% real success vs 90.7% with calibrated pipeline [e1823.0] [e1823.1]
  10. Grasp stability prediction required FEM-based contact simulation or learned residuals; achieved 90.7% sequential prediction accuracy with calibrated contact model [e1644.2]
  11. Soft fish tail required FEM with optimized material/friction parameters to match ANSYS force-deflection (mean error 0.153N) and deformation fields (errors 1.41e-4 to 1.81e-5 m); achieved millimeter-scale accuracy [e1775.0]
  12. Soft robot locomotion required accurate tribology modeling including interfacial shear strength and contact area; Coulomb friction alone was insufficient, causing heading errors [e1811.0]
  13. Planar pushing required hybrid simulator with learned residuals to correct contact model errors; achieved lower electrode prediction errors than physics-only or neural-only approaches [e1799.0]
  14. Bolting task with tight tolerance required C-space collision modeling with learned implicit surface and volumetric state representation to prevent penetration; enabled zero-shot transfer [e1653.1]
  15. Contact-rich manipulation (TRANSIC) required point-cloud perception, action-space distillation to joint-position control, and human-in-the-loop residual learning; achieved 81% average success across four tasks [e1672.0]
  16. Bipedal locomotion required accurate actuator model (DTE gear model) to reproduce backdrivability effects; without DTE, policies failed to transfer (0/3 success) [e1643.1] [e1643.2]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. A screw-insertion task with 0.5mm clearance will require contact force prediction within ±10% and friction within ±0.05 to succeed without adaptation, or will require online force-feedback adaptation
  2. A cable-routing task with >10mm clearance will succeed with approximate contact modeling (30% force error) if combined with force-feedback control and online trajectory adaptation
  3. A snap-fit assembly task will require explicit modeling of contact mode transitions (free, contact, snap) and accurate restitution coefficients (±0.1) to avoid simulation artifacts
  4. A polishing task will require contact force control within ±5N and surface normal estimation within ±10° to achieve uniform pressure, or will require tactile feedback for adaptation
  5. A multi-fingered precision grasp will require modeling of load distribution across contacts with <20% error in individual contact forces to prevent object slip
  6. A deformable object manipulation task (e.g., cloth folding) will require FEM-level modeling or learned residuals trained on >500 real deformation examples to achieve <10mm position accuracy

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Whether learned contact models (neural networks, GPs) can fully replace physics-based contact simulation for transfer, or if physics-based models provide essential inductive biases that cannot be learned from limited data
  2. Whether multi-modal sensing (vision + force + tactile) can compensate for poor contact modeling through closed-loop feedback, and what the minimum required contact model fidelity is when multi-modal feedback is available
  3. Whether contact-rich tasks can transfer to significantly different materials (e.g., trained on rigid objects, deployed on deformable objects with 10x different stiffness) with online adaptation alone, or if retraining is necessary
  4. Whether there exists a minimum contact complexity threshold (e.g., number of simultaneous contacts, contact area, or contact force magnitude) below which approximate modeling suffices and above which high-fidelity modeling is required
  5. Whether hybrid approaches (physics-based simulation + learned residuals) can achieve better sample efficiency than pure learning approaches for contact-rich tasks, and what the optimal balance is
  6. Whether contact modeling requirements change fundamentally for tasks involving impacts and collisions versus quasi-static contact, and what the critical velocity threshold is

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding a tight-tolerance assembly task (clearance <2mm) that transfers with >50% contact force errors would challenge the force-prediction requirement and suggest that other factors (e.g., compliance, feedback control) can compensate
  2. Demonstrating successful transfer of a contact-rich task without any friction modeling (or with friction errors >0.5) would challenge the friction requirement and suggest that friction is less critical than assumed
  3. Showing that position control transfers as well as force control for contact-rich tasks would challenge the force-feedback principle and suggest that accurate forward models can replace feedback
  4. Finding that coarse geometry (>5mm errors) enables transfer for tight-tolerance tasks (<2mm clearance) would challenge the geometry-accuracy requirement and suggest that other factors compensate
  5. Demonstrating that domain randomization alone (without system identification or online adaptation) enables transfer for tasks with <1mm clearance would challenge the high-fidelity requirement for tight-tolerance tasks
  6. Finding that learned residuals trained on <50 real examples achieve similar performance to those trained on >1000 examples would challenge the sample-complexity assumptions
  7. Showing that policies trained without explicit penetration detection transfer successfully to tight-tolerance assembly would challenge the artifact-prevention requirement

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. How contact modeling requirements change with object deformability (rigid vs soft vs fluid) and whether there are discrete transitions or continuous scaling [e1775.0] [e1811.0] [e1799.0]
  2. Whether contact modeling requirements are fundamentally different for quasi-static vs dynamic contact scenarios, and what the critical velocity threshold is [e1654.0] [e1650.0] [e1651.0]
  3. How multi-contact scenarios (multiple simultaneous contacts) affect fidelity requirements and whether load distribution modeling is always necessary [e1651.0] [e1791.0] [e1672.0]
  4. The interaction between contact modeling fidelity and control frequency/bandwidth, and whether higher control rates can compensate for lower contact fidelity [e1641.0] [e1788.0]
  5. How sensor modality (vision, force, tactile, proprioception) affects required contact modeling fidelity, and whether certain sensors enable lower-fidelity simulation [e1823.0] [e1835.0] [e1672.0]
  6. The role of object geometry complexity (simple shapes vs complex meshes) in determining required contact modeling fidelity [e1681.0] [e1653.1]
