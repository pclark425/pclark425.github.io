Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-411

Name: The Semantic Distance-Feasibility Relationship Theory

Type: specific

Theory Query: Build a theory of the fundamental trade-off between novelty and feasibility in automatically generated research hypotheses, including quantification methods and optimization strategies across different research domains and problem types, based on the following results.

Description: The relationship between semantic distance (novelty) and feasibility in hypothesis generation follows a characteristic inverted-U or threshold pattern rather than a simple linear relationship. Hypotheses at very low semantic distance (highly similar to existing work) are highly feasible but minimally novel. Hypotheses at moderate semantic distance represent optimal combinations of novelty and feasibility, recombining validated concepts in new ways. Hypotheses at very high semantic distance are highly novel but often infeasible due to lack of connecting knowledge, methods, or validation pathways. The optimal semantic distance varies by domain, research context, and the specific distance metric used. Multi-hop semantic paths (connecting distant concepts through intermediate concepts) can make high-distance connections more feasible than direct connections. The relationship can be quantified using embedding-based metrics (cosine similarity), graph-theoretic measures (path length, betweenness), taxonomic distance, or human expert judgments, though different metrics may give different predictions.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

  1. The relationship between semantic distance and feasibility follows an inverted-U or threshold pattern rather than a simple linear relationship.
  2. Hypotheses at very low semantic distance are highly feasible but minimally novel, representing incremental variations on existing work.
  3. Hypotheses at moderate semantic distance represent optimal novelty-feasibility combinations, recombining validated concepts in new ways.
  4. Hypotheses at very high semantic distance are highly novel but often infeasible due to lack of connecting knowledge, methods, or validation pathways.
  5. The optimal semantic distance varies by domain: mature domains favor lower distances, emerging domains enable higher distances.
  6. Multi-hop semantic paths (connecting distant concepts through intermediate concepts) are more feasible than direct long-distance connections.
  7. Semantic distance can be quantified using multiple metrics: embedding cosine similarity, graph path length, taxonomic distance, topic-model similarity.
  8. Different distance metrics may give different predictions about novelty and feasibility, with no single metric universally optimal.
  9. The relationship between distance and feasibility is modulated by the availability of connecting knowledge, methods, and validation pathways.
  10. Controlled exploration of the semantic distance space enables systematic navigation of the novelty-feasibility tradeoff.
  11. The 'sweet spot' of semantic distance for maximum impact varies by research goal, context, and domain maturity.
  12. Iterative refinement can increase semantic distance while maintaining feasibility by building on intermediate steps.
  13. Human perception of semantic distance correlates with but is not identical to computational distance metrics.

Supporting Evidence

  1. SciMON iterative novelty boosting used cosine similarity threshold (0.6) to determine 'too similar' and trigger regeneration, with 88.9% of updated ideas substantially different and 55.6% showing increased novelty [e2282.1]
  2. SciPIP observed inverse relationship between similarity-to-existing-ideas and novelty (lower similarity => higher novelty), with feasibility not strongly declining for low-similarity ideas in LLM-based evaluation [e2290.3]
  3. LLMCG semantic distance analysis showed Control-Human group exhibited larger semantic distances vs other groups (F(3,1652)=84.1611, p<0.00001, R²≈86.96%), with pairwise Cohen's d values up to 2.6949 [e2271.4]
  4. Scideator facet-based retrieval at varying distances (near/far/very-far) enabled controlled novelty exploration, with participants preferring input/near facets over far facets in idea-saving behavior [e2322.2]
  5. Cross-domain retrieval prototype participants described 'sweet spot' of distance (not too near, not too far) for useful and inspiring results, with qualitative evidence of optimal distance zones [e2448.0]
  6. SEMNET used cosine similarity thresholds (<0.03, <0.15) to select uncommon/novel concept pairs, with example cosS values ranging from 0.03 to 0.31 [e2382.0]
  7. Random path sampling in SciAgents produced richer concept sets (more nodes and edges) than shortest paths, increasing potential novelty and enabling novel composite-material hypotheses [e2426.1]
  8. PLM-LP link prediction used embedding similarity to identify candidate concept connections, with new-edge detection achieving Precision=0.540, Recall=0.988, F1=0.560 [e2462.1] [e2462.2]
  9. Semantic deduplication using cosine similarity threshold (0.8) revealed severe limits in generation diversity, with only ~5% non-duplicated ideas remaining from 4000 seeds [e2433.4]
  10. AGATHA graph-based approach used path-based semantic distance to identify candidate hypotheses, with multi-hop paths providing interpretability [e2279.1]
  11. Analogical search engine used aspect-conditioned similarity to find analogs at controlled distances, with supervised models trained on aspect-aligned retrieval [e2448.4]
  12. BioSpark taxonomy-guided expansion used taxonomic distance to control organism diversity, with monotonic increases in diversity over iterations [e2399.1] [e2399.5]
  13. Exploration bonuses in RL use state-density and novelty measures based on distance metrics, with count-based and density-based approaches [e2418.5]
  14. Learning progress methods (IMGEP, R-IAC) focus on intermediate-difficulty (moderate distance) tasks, avoiding purely novel but unlearnable activities and trivial ones [e2418.0] [e2418.2]
  15. Personalized outlier suggestion used high-dimensional distance from average (Mahalanobis-like) to find unique pairs with exceptional network-theoretic signatures [e2382.2]
  16. CoQuest depth-first generation produced RQs with higher novelty ratings (M=3.78, SD=1.29) than breadth-first (M=3.28, SD=1.22), with Spearman correlation between depth and novelty rho=0.41, p<.001 [e2269.0]
  17. MOLIERE topic-driven ranking used multiple distance metrics (L2, CSim, TopicCorr) with varying ROC AUCs (0.578-0.783), showing metric-dependent performance [e2285.3]
  18. Novelty-checker in CoI used embedding cosine similarity and LLM assessment to decide if ideas were too similar, triggering iterative regeneration [e2304.2]
  19. LLM Ideation Agent used semantic deduplication with all-MiniLM-L6-v2 embeddings and cosine similarity threshold 0.8 to quantify unique ideas [e2433.0]
  20. Idea Novelty Checker used SPECTER embeddings and facet-aware LLM re-ranking to assess novelty, achieving 89.66% accuracy on 'not novel' predictions [e2322.3]
  21. Human ternary novelty annotation (1=exact seen, 2=similar seen, 3=novel) used in cross-domain retrieval studies to assess perceived novelty [e2448.6]
  22. BERT+t-SNE semantic analysis showed very large group effect on semantic distances (F(3,1652)=84.1611, p<0.00001, R²≈86.96%), with Control-Human showing larger distances [e2271.4]
  23. GN+SR pipeline used message-level representations to enable symbolic regression of compact expressions, with architectural constraints (L^e'=D) improving generalization [e2392.0]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Systems that explicitly optimize for moderate semantic distance (e.g., 0.4-0.6 cosine similarity in embedding space) will produce more impactful hypotheses than systems optimizing for maximum distance.
  2. Providing users with explicit control over semantic distance parameters will improve satisfaction and perceived utility of hypothesis generation systems.
  3. Multi-hop semantic paths (2-3 intermediate concepts) will be rated as more feasible by domain experts than direct connections between highly distant concepts.
  4. Domain-specific calibration of optimal semantic distance thresholds will improve hypothesis quality by 10-20% compared to domain-agnostic thresholds.
  5. Combining multiple distance metrics (embedding, graph, taxonomic) in an ensemble will provide better novelty-feasibility prediction than single metrics alone.
  6. Iterative refinement that gradually increases semantic distance will produce more feasible high-novelty hypotheses than single-step generation.
  7. In mature research domains, the optimal semantic distance will be lower (0.3-0.5) than in emerging domains (0.5-0.7).

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Whether the inverted-U relationship is universal across all domains or whether some domains (e.g., highly theoretical fields) exhibit different patterns such as monotonic relationships.
  2. Whether the optimal semantic distance changes systematically and predictably as a research field evolves and matures over decades.
  3. Whether there exist discrete 'zones' of semantic distance with qualitatively different feasibility characteristics, or whether the relationship is continuous and smooth.
  4. Whether semantic distance in embedding space corresponds linearly to cognitive distance in human expert judgment, or whether there are systematic biases.
  5. Whether the relationship between distance and feasibility is causal (distance causes infeasibility) or merely correlational (both are caused by underlying knowledge structure).
  6. Whether different types of semantic distance (conceptual, methodological, domain) have different relationships with feasibility.
  7. Whether the optimal distance for paradigm-shifting discoveries differs fundamentally from the optimal distance for incremental advances.
  8. Whether temporal dynamics (how quickly a field evolves) affect the optimal semantic distance more than static domain characteristics.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding that semantic distance has no systematic relationship with feasibility across multiple domains would challenge the core theory.
  2. Demonstrating that maximum semantic distance consistently produces the most impactful hypotheses would challenge the inverted-U hypothesis.
  3. Showing that all distance metrics (embedding, graph, taxonomic) produce identical predictions would question the importance of metric choice and multi-metric approaches.
  4. Finding that optimal distance does not vary by domain or research context would challenge the domain-dependence hypothesis.
  5. Demonstrating that humans cannot perceive or utilize semantic distance information in their judgment would question its practical utility.
  6. Finding that multi-hop paths are no more feasible than direct connections would challenge the path-structure hypothesis.
  7. Showing that iterative refinement does not enable higher semantic distances would challenge the refinement hypothesis.
  8. Demonstrating that paradigm shifts occur at the same semantic distances as incremental work would challenge the special-case predictions.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The precise mathematical form of the distance-feasibility relationship (quadratic, exponential, piecewise linear, threshold) is not characterized, with different systems using different functional forms [e2282.1] [e2290.3] [e2418.0]
  2. The role of path structure (direct vs. multi-hop, path length, betweenness) in determining feasibility is not fully modeled, though evidence suggests multi-hop paths are important [e2426.1] [e2279.1] [e2392.0]
  3. The interaction between semantic distance and other factors (data availability, method availability, computational resources, domain expertise) is not explicitly characterized [e2448.0] [e2382.0] [e2444.7]
  4. The temporal dynamics of optimal distance (how it changes as knowledge accumulates, fields mature, or paradigms shift) are not studied longitudinally [e2462.1] [e2433.4] [e2382.2]
  5. The cognitive mechanisms by which humans assess and utilize semantic distance are not modeled, though correlation with computational metrics is observed [e2448.4] [e2322.2] [e2448.6]
  6. The relationship between different types of semantic distance (conceptual, methodological, domain-based) and their differential effects on feasibility [e2285.3] [e2322.3] [e2399.1]
  7. The role of intermediate concepts and bridging knowledge in enabling high-distance connections is qualitatively described but not quantitatively modeled [e2426.1] [e2462.2] [e2392.0]
  8. The mechanisms by which domain maturity affects optimal distance thresholds are not explicitly characterized [e2382.0] [e2462.1] [e2444.7]
