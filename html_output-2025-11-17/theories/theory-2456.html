Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2456

Name: Activity-Dependent Vulnerability Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory proposes that the selective vulnerability of visual cortex neuronal populations to amyloid-β and tau pathology is primarily determined by their patterns of synaptic activity and plasticity. Neurons with high baseline activity, frequent synaptic remodeling, or strong excitatory drive are more prone to pathological protein accumulation and degeneration due to increased metabolic demand, calcium influx, and local production of amyloidogenic and tau-phosphorylating factors.

• neuron_population → is_vulnerable_to → degeneration

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neurons with high activity show increased amyloid-β production and tau phosphorylation.
  2. In vivo imaging links hyperactive neurons to early plaque and tangle formation.

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Reducing activity in highly active visual cortex neurons will decrease their vulnerability to amyloid-β and tau pathology.
  2. Artificially increasing activity in resistant neurons will render them more vulnerable.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Chronic suppression of activity in already resistant neurons will not further reduce pathology, suggesting a floor effect.
  2. Activity modulation in the absence of amyloid-β or tau will not induce pathology, indicating a requirement for both factors.

Negative Experiments

  1. If activity suppression does not reduce pathology in vulnerable neurons, the theory is challenged.
  2. If activity enhancement does not increase vulnerability, the theory is called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Some low-activity neurons still develop pathology, suggesting additional mechanisms.
