Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1479

Name: Dynamic Compositionality Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This theory proposes that the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains is fundamentally compositional and dynamic: concepts are constructed on-the-fly from reusable, lower-level primitives (features, roles, relations) via flexible binding operations. Rather than storing fixed, static concept representations, the brain dynamically assembles conceptual structures in response to task demands, context, and goals. This compositionality enables infinite productivity from finite resources and supports rapid adaptation to novel situations.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Dynamic Assembly Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• conceptual task → is_initiated → True
THEN
• concept representation → is_constructed_from → primitives via binding operations, and
• representation → is_not_stored_as → static holistic entity

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Behavioral evidence shows rapid generalization to novel combinations of features and roles (e.g., 'zebra with wings').
  2. Neuroimaging studies show transient, task-dependent activation patterns for novel concepts.
  3. Computational models with dynamic binding outperform static models on compositional generalization tasks.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While compositionality is well-known, the dynamic, on-the-fly assembly of all concepts is a novel extension.

What Already Exists: Compositionality is a core principle in linguistics and cognitive science.

What is Novel: The explicit claim that all conceptual representations in brains are dynamically assembled rather than statically stored.

References:

  • Fodor & Pylyshyn (1988) Connectionism and cognitive architecture [compositionality in cognition]
  • Lake, Salakhutdinov, & Tenenbaum (2015) Human-level concept learning through probabilistic program induction [compositional generalization]
  • Frankland & Greene (2015) An architecture for encoding sentence meaning [dynamic role binding]

Statement 1: Productivity from Finite Primitives Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• primitives → are_finite → True, and
• binding operations → are_available → True
THEN
• number of possible concepts → is_infinite_in_principle → True

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Humans can understand and generate novel concepts never previously encountered.
  2. Language allows for infinite productivity from finite vocabulary and grammar.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law generalizes a linguistic principle to all conceptual domains in brain function.

What Already Exists: Productivity from compositionality is established in linguistics and cognitive science.

What is Novel: The explicit mapping of this principle to all forms of conceptual knowledge in brains, not just language.

References:

  • Chomsky (1957) Syntactic Structures [productivity in language]
  • Fodor & Pylyshyn (1988) Connectionism and cognitive architecture [compositionality and productivity]
  • Lake et al. (2015) Human-level concept learning [compositionality in concept learning]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Novel combinations of familiar features or roles will elicit rapid, flexible neural assembly patterns rather than retrieval of static templates.
  2. Disruption of binding operations (e.g., via TMS to parietal cortex) will impair compositional concept construction but not recognition of familiar, holistic concepts.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificial neural networks with dynamic binding mechanisms will show human-like productivity in open-ended concept learning.
  2. Direct neural recording during novel concept construction will reveal transient, compositional assembly signatures.

Negative Experiments

  1. If novel concepts are represented as static, holistic patterns rather than dynamically assembled, the theory would be undermined.
  2. If the number of representational primitives is not finite or if productivity is not observed, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not specify the nature or granularity of the representational primitives.
