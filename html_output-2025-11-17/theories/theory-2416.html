Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2416

Name: Intrinsic Molecular Resilience and Pathway Cohesiveness Theory (General 1: Integrated Homeostatic Network Model)

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why certain neuronal populations within the visual cortex are selectively vulnerable or resistant to amyloid-β plaques and tau protein tangles in dementia.

Description: This theory posits that the selective vulnerability or resistance of neuronal populations in the visual cortex to amyloid-β (Aβ) plaques and tau tangles is determined by the intrinsic molecular resilience of each population, which is a function of the cohesiveness and integration of their homeostatic pathways (including proteostasis, mitochondrial quality control, antioxidant defenses, and stress response signaling). Neurons with highly integrated and robust homeostatic networks are more resistant to Aβ and tau pathology, while those with fragmented or weakly coordinated pathways are selectively vulnerable.

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Resistant neurons exhibit coordinated upregulation of proteostasis, mitochondrial, and antioxidant genes in response to stress.
  2. Single-cell transcriptomics reveal that resilient populations have tightly interconnected stress response networks.
  3. Proteomic studies show that resilient neurons maintain protein quality control and energy homeostasis under pathological conditions.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While the roles of individual pathways are established, the theory's focus on their integration and network-level resilience is new.

What Already Exists: Individual homeostatic pathways (e.g., proteostasis, mitochondrial quality control) are known to influence neurodegeneration.

What is Novel: The explicit requirement for cross-pathway integration and network cohesiveness as a determinant of selective vulnerability is novel.

  • Mattson (2007) Calcium and neurodegeneration [Homeostatic signaling in neurodegeneration]

Statement 1: Fragmented Homeostatic Networks Increase Vulnerability (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• neuronal_population → has_low_cohesiveness_of → homeostatic_pathways, and
• neuronal_population → shows_poor_cross-talk_between → proteostasis_mitochondrial_antioxidant_stress_responses
THEN
• neuronal_population → is_vulnerable_to → Aβ_and_tau_pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Vulnerable neurons show isolated or poorly coordinated stress responses, failing to mount integrated defenses.
  2. Transcriptomic and proteomic data indicate that vulnerable populations lack synchronized activation of protective pathways.
  3. Experimental disruption of pathway integration increases vulnerability to Aβ and tau toxicity.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law extends existing knowledge by proposing a network-level mechanism for selective vulnerability.

What Already Exists: Deficits in individual homeostatic pathways are linked to neurodegeneration.

What is Novel: The emphasis on the lack of integration and network fragmentation as a primary vulnerability factor is novel.

References:

  • Saxena & Caroni (2011) Selective neuronal vulnerability in neurodegenerative diseases [Proteostasis and stress response]
  • Mattson (2007) Calcium and neurodegeneration [Homeostatic signaling in neurodegeneration]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Neuronal populations with experimentally enhanced cross-talk between homeostatic pathways will show increased resistance to Aβ and tau pathology.
  2. Populations with naturally high pathway integration will exhibit delayed or reduced pathology in dementia models.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Pharmacological agents that promote network-level integration of homeostatic pathways will reduce selective vulnerability in human neurons.
  2. Artificially fragmenting homeostatic networks in otherwise resilient neurons will render them vulnerable to Aβ and tau toxicity.

Negative Experiments

  1. If enhancing pathway integration does not confer resistance, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If neurons with fragmented networks are not more vulnerable, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The influence of extrinsic factors (e.g., microglial activity, vascular health) on selective vulnerability is not addressed.
