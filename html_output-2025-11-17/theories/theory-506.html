Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-506

Name: Heterogeneous Proxytypes / Dual-Process Representational Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional level, based on the following results.

Description: Conceptual knowledge in the brain is represented by a heterogeneous network of co-referring representational formats—including prototypes, exemplars, theory-like/causal structures, and classical symbolic definitions—stored in long-term memory. Context-sensitive tokenization selects which representation(s) are activated in working memory for a given task. This selection is governed by dual-process mechanisms: fast, automatic, similarity-based (Type 1/System 1) processes operate over prototypes and exemplars (often implemented in conceptual spaces), while slow, deliberative, rule-based (Type 2/System 2) processes operate over symbolic/classical ontologies. The architecture allows for flexible, context-dependent conceptual processing, harmonizing graded typicality, exception handling, and compositional inference.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Heterogeneous Representation Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• concept → is_stored_in → long_term_memory
THEN
• concept → has_representational_bodies → prototype, and
• concept → has_representational_bodies → exemplar, and
• concept → has_representational_bodies → theory_like, and
• concept → has_representational_bodies → classical_symbolic

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Each concept corresponds to a heterogeneous network of co-referring representations (prototypes, exemplars, theory-like, classical) stored in LTM and selectively tokenized in WM. [e3472.5] [e3623.5] [e3459.8]
  2. Behavioral and neural evidence for multiple representational formats coexisting (Squire & Knowlton, Malt 1989); Dual-PECCS implementation shows combining prototypes and exemplars explains a variety of human categorization responses. [e3623.5] [e3472.8] [e3472.9]

Statement 2: Dual-Process Arbitration Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• conceptual_task → is_processed_by → cognitive_system
THEN
• Type_1_processes → operate_on → prototypes_and_exemplars, and
• Type_2_processes → operate_on → symbolic_ontologies

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Dual-process theories: Type 1 (fast, associative) mediates prototype/exemplar-based categorization; Type 2 (slow, rule-based) implements compositional, monotonic classification. [e3623.8] [e3642.7] [e3473.4] [e3472.10]
  2. Implemented in Dual-PECCS where S1 (conceptual spaces) proposes candidates and S2 (ontologies) validates them, reproducing human-like tradeoffs and errors. [e3623.8] [e3472.8] [e3472.9]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Tasks requiring rapid, typicality-based categorization will show behavioral and neural signatures of prototype/exemplar activation (e.g., faster RTs, activation in regions associated with similarity-based processing).
  2. Tasks requiring explicit rule-based or compositional inference will show slower RTs and increased activation in regions associated with symbolic reasoning (e.g., prefrontal cortex).
  3. Manipulating context or task demands will shift which representational format is activated (e.g., providing a highly similar exemplar will bias toward exemplar-based categorization).

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Novel concepts that do not fit existing prototypes, exemplars, or symbolic definitions will trigger the creation of new representational bodies or the recruitment of theory-like representations.
  2. In cases of brain damage selectively affecting one representational format (e.g., damage to ATL hub or modality-specific cortex), the system will compensate by relying more on remaining formats, with predictable patterns of error.
  3. Training individuals to use explicit reasoning strategies (e.g., teaching rules) will increase reliance on symbolic/classical representations, observable in both behavior and neural activation.

Negative Experiments

  1. If individuals consistently use only one representational format across all tasks and contexts, the theory would be undermined.
  2. If neural or behavioral evidence fails to show context-dependent shifts in representational format activation, the theory would be called into question.
  3. If dual-process dissociations (fast/slow, similarity/rule) are not observed in categorization tasks, the theory's process-level claims would be challenged.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not specify the precise neural mechanisms for arbitration and integration between representational formats, nor how thresholds for selection (e.g., Conceptual Coherence Threshold) are empirically calibrated. [e3472.5] [e3472.9] [e3623.5] [e3623.0]
  2. Full computational integration of theory-like representations (e.g., graphical models) remains to be implemented and empirically validated. [e3472.5] [e3472.7] [e3472.8]
