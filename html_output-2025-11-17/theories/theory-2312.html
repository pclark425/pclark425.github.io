Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2312

Name: Network Disintegration and Propagation Theory of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory posits that Alzheimer's disease is initiated by local disruptions in neural network connectivity—due to genetic, metabolic, or environmental insults—which then propagate through the brain via activity-dependent mechanisms. The spread of dysfunction is mediated by both pathological protein transmission (e.g., prion-like spread of tau or amyloid) and loss of network homeostasis, leading to global cognitive decline. Early detection is possible by identifying subtle changes in network connectivity and propagation patterns.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Network Vulnerability Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• brain region → has → reduced connectivity or increased vulnerability (due to genetics, metabolism, or environment)
THEN
• brain region → is_initial_site_of → Alzheimer's pathology

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Regions with high metabolic demand and connectivity (e.g., default mode network) are early sites of AD pathology.
  2. Genetic and metabolic risk factors (e.g., APOE4, diabetes) increase vulnerability of specific brain networks.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law synthesizes network science and AD pathology, extending existing models.

What Already Exists: Network-based vulnerability and selective regional involvement are recognized in AD.

What is Novel: The explicit link between local network vulnerability and the initiation of pathology is emphasized.

References:

  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network vulnerability]
  • Buckner et al. (2005) Molecular, structural, and functional characterization of Alzheimer's disease: Evidence for a relationship between default activity, amyloid, and memory [default mode network involvement]

Statement 1: Propagation Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• pathological protein → is_present_in → vulnerable brain region, and
• region → is_functionally_connected_to → other regions
THEN
• pathological protein → spreads_to → connected regions, and
• network dysfunction → propagates → along connectivity pathways

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Tau and amyloid pathology spread along functional connectivity networks in both animal models and humans.
  2. Network analysis shows that cognitive decline correlates with loss of connectivity and propagation of dysfunction.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law extends propagation models by integrating network science and vulnerability.

What Already Exists: Prion-like spread of tau and amyloid is recognized; network propagation models exist.

What is Novel: The integration of network propagation with homeostatic and metabolic vulnerability is novel.

References:

  • de Calignon et al. (2012) Propagation of tau pathology in a model of early Alzheimer's disease [prion-like spread]
  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network propagation]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Early network connectivity changes (measured by fMRI or EEG) will predict the future spread of AD pathology.
  2. Interventions that stabilize network connectivity will slow the propagation of pathology and cognitive decline.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially enhancing connectivity in vulnerable regions will accelerate or decelerate pathology spread, depending on the nature of the intervention.
  2. Disrupting specific network hubs will alter the pattern of AD progression.

Negative Experiments

  1. If pathology does not spread along connectivity pathways, the theory is weakened.
  2. If network connectivity changes do not precede or predict pathology, the theory is challenged.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with diffuse, non-network-based pathology.
