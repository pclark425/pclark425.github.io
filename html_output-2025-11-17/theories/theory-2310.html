Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2310

Name: Dynamic Homeostatic Failure Theory of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory proposes that Alzheimer's disease results from a progressive failure of the brain's homeostatic mechanisms to maintain synaptic, metabolic, and immune balance in the face of age-related stressors. The breakdown of compensatory processes—such as protein clearance, synaptic plasticity, and neurovascular coupling—leads to the accumulation of pathological proteins, chronic inflammation, and network instability. Early detection is possible by identifying subtle failures in these homeostatic systems before overt pathology emerges.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Homeostatic Breakdown Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → impaired protein clearance, and
• individual → has → impaired synaptic plasticity, and
• individual → has → impaired neurovascular coupling
THEN
• individual → will_accumulate → amyloid and tau pathology, and
• individual → will_experience → chronic neuroinflammation, and
• individual → will_develop → network instability and cognitive decline

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Impaired protein clearance (e.g., via glymphatic system or autophagy) is associated with amyloid and tau accumulation.
  2. Synaptic plasticity deficits precede overt neurodegeneration in AD models.
  3. Neurovascular coupling impairment is observed early in AD and predicts later pathology.
  4. Chronic neuroinflammation is both a cause and consequence of homeostatic failure.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The integration of multiple homeostatic failures as a dynamic system is a novel synthesis.

What Already Exists: Individual homeostatic failures (e.g., protein clearance, synaptic plasticity) have been studied in AD.

What is Novel: The theory unifies these failures as a dynamic, interacting system whose breakdown is the root cause of AD, and predicts early detection via homeostatic biomarkers.

References:

  • Iliff et al. (2012) A paravascular pathway facilitates CSF flow through the brain parenchyma and the clearance of interstitial solutes, including amyloid β [glymphatic clearance]
  • Selkoe (2002) Alzheimer's disease is a synaptic failure [synaptic plasticity]
  • Zlokovic (2011) Neurovascular pathways to neurodegeneration in Alzheimer's disease and other disorders [neurovascular coupling]

Statement 1: Early Homeostatic Biomarker Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• detection_method → measures → biomarkers of protein clearance, synaptic plasticity, and neurovascular coupling
THEN
• detection_method → can_detect → preclinical Alzheimer's disease

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. CSF and imaging biomarkers of protein clearance and synaptic function can detect changes before clinical symptoms.
  2. Functional MRI and PET can reveal neurovascular and synaptic dysfunction in at-risk individuals.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The focus on homeostatic failure as the earliest detectable event is a novel approach.

What Already Exists: Some early biomarkers are being explored, but not as a unified homeostatic failure panel.

What is Novel: The law predicts that a panel of homeostatic biomarkers will outperform traditional pathology-based markers for early detection.

References:

  • Bateman et al. (2012) Clinical and biomarker changes in dominantly inherited Alzheimer's disease [early biomarker changes]
  • Selkoe (2002) Alzheimer's disease is a synaptic failure [synaptic biomarkers]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early deficits in protein clearance, synaptic plasticity, or neurovascular coupling will develop AD pathology and cognitive decline.
  2. Panels measuring these homeostatic biomarkers will identify at-risk individuals before amyloid or tau pathology is detectable.
  3. Interventions that restore homeostatic balance will delay or prevent AD onset.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Novel imaging or fluid biomarkers of glymphatic function will predict AD risk.
  2. Restoring neurovascular coupling in preclinical individuals will prevent downstream pathology.
  3. Dynamic modeling of homeostatic failure will reveal new therapeutic targets.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding individuals with severe homeostatic impairment but no AD pathology or cognitive decline would challenge the theory.
  2. Demonstrating that pathology-based biomarkers outperform homeostatic biomarkers for early detection would call the theory into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Genetic forms of AD with rapid onset may not involve gradual homeostatic failure.
