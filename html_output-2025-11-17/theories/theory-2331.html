Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2331

Name: Integrated Network Disruption Theory of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory posits that Alzheimer's disease arises from the convergence of multiple systemic disruptions—genetic, metabolic, vascular, and immune—leading to a breakdown of neural network homeostasis. The interplay between these factors triggers a self-reinforcing cycle of synaptic dysfunction, proteinopathy (amyloid and tau), and neuroinflammation, which together drive cognitive decline. Detection is most effective when it targets the network-level signatures of dysfunction, rather than single biomarkers.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Convergent Disruption Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → genetic risk factors (e.g., APOE4), and
• individual → has → metabolic dysregulation (e.g., insulin resistance), and
• individual → has → vascular dysfunction (e.g., microvascular disease), and
• individual → has → chronic neuroinflammation
THEN
• individual → is_at_high_risk_for → network-level synaptic dysfunction and Alzheimer's disease

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. APOE4, diabetes, and vascular disease are independent and additive risk factors for AD.
  2. Neuroinflammation and metabolic dysfunction are observed early in AD progression.
  3. Network-level disruptions (e.g., default mode network breakdown) precede overt pathology.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While risk factors are known, the explicit convergence and network-level focus is novel.

What Already Exists: Individual risk factors (genetic, metabolic, vascular, immune) are known for AD.

What is Novel: The law formalizes their convergence as a necessary and sufficient condition for network breakdown and AD onset.

References:

  • Iturria-Medina et al. (2016) Early role of vascular dysregulation on late-onset Alzheimer's disease pathogenesis [Network disruption, multi-factorial risk]
  • Jack et al. (2013) Tracking pathophysiological processes in Alzheimer's disease: an updated hypothetical model of dynamic biomarkers [Biomarker cascade, but not network-level convergence]

Statement 1: Network Signature Detection Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → shows → altered functional connectivity in key brain networks (e.g., default mode network)
THEN
• individual → can_be_detected_as → preclinical or prodromal Alzheimer's disease

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Functional MRI reveals network breakdown before clinical symptoms.
  2. Network-level changes are more sensitive than single biomarker changes in early detection.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: Network changes are known, but their primacy for detection is novel.

What Already Exists: Functional connectivity changes are observed in AD.

What is Novel: The law formalizes network-level detection as a primary, rather than secondary, diagnostic tool.

References:

  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [Network-level focus]
  • Buckner et al. (2005) Molecular, structural, and functional characterization of Alzheimer's disease: Evidence for a relationship between default activity, amyloid, and memory [Network and amyloid]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with combined genetic, metabolic, vascular, and immune risk factors will show early network-level disruptions before clinical symptoms.
  2. Network-level imaging will outperform single biomarker tests in predicting conversion to AD.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Interventions that restore network connectivity (e.g., neuromodulation) will delay or prevent AD onset even in high-risk individuals.
  2. Some individuals with network disruptions may not develop AD, suggesting compensatory mechanisms.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding individuals with all risk factors but no network disruption or AD would challenge the convergence law.
  2. Demonstrating that AD can develop without network-level changes would call the theory into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with minimal or no detectable network disruption.
