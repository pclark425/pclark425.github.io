Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2297

Name: Integrated Network Disruption Theory of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory posits that Alzheimer's disease arises from the breakdown of large-scale brain network integrity, driven by the convergence of molecular pathologies (amyloid, tau, neuroinflammation), vascular dysfunction, and genetic risk factors. The earliest detectable changes are disruptions in functional connectivity and network synchrony, which can be identified by advanced neuroimaging and digital cognitive phenotyping before overt neurodegeneration.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Convergent Pathology Disrupts Network Connectivity (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → amyloid pathology, and
• individual → has → tau pathology, and
• individual → has → vascular dysfunction or neuroinflammation
THEN
• individual → exhibits → disrupted large-scale brain network connectivity

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Amyloid and tau pathologies co-localize in default mode network hubs, which show early connectivity loss.
  2. Vascular risk factors and neuroinflammation exacerbate network breakdown in AD.
  3. Functional MRI studies show early network desynchronization in preclinical AD.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While each factor is individually recognized, their convergence as a network-disruption mechanism is a new synthesis.

What Already Exists: Amyloid, tau, and vascular risk are known contributors to AD; network disruption is observed in imaging.

What is Novel: The explicit integration of these pathologies as convergent drivers of network-level breakdown is novel.

References:

  • Seeley (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network vulnerability]
  • Iturria-Medina (2016) Early role of vascular dysregulation in late-onset Alzheimer's disease [vascular risk]
  • Brier (2012) Loss of intranetwork and internetwork resting state functional connections with Alzheimer's disease progression [network connectivity]

Statement 1: Network Disruption Precedes Structural Atrophy (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → early network connectivity loss (e.g., via fMRI, EEG)
THEN
• individual → will develop → regional brain atrophy and cognitive decline

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Longitudinal imaging shows functional connectivity loss precedes atrophy in AD-vulnerable regions.
  2. EEG and fMRI detect early network changes in at-risk individuals before clinical symptoms.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The predictive, temporal aspect is a new formalization of existing observations.

What Already Exists: Early network changes are observed in preclinical AD.

What is Novel: The law formalizes the temporal sequence and predictive value of network disruption.

References:

  • Brier (2012) Loss of intranetwork and internetwork resting state functional connections with Alzheimer's disease progression [network connectivity]
  • Jones (2016) Cascading network failure across the Alzheimer's disease spectrum [network failure precedes atrophy]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with early network connectivity loss but no atrophy will progress to AD at higher rates than those without such loss.
  2. Interventions that restore network synchrony (e.g., noninvasive brain stimulation) will delay onset of atrophy and cognitive decline.
  3. Digital cognitive phenotyping will detect subtle network-related cognitive fluctuations before standard neuropsychological tests.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Altering network connectivity via targeted neuromodulation may prevent or reverse molecular pathology progression.
  2. Individuals with genetic resilience factors may maintain network integrity despite molecular pathology.
  3. Novel imaging of network synchrony will reveal subtypes of AD with distinct progression patterns.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding individuals with severe network disruption but no subsequent atrophy or cognitive decline would challenge the theory.
  2. Demonstrating that network connectivity loss does not predict AD progression would call the theory into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with minimal network disruption but significant atrophy and cognitive decline.
