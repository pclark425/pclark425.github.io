Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1779

Name: Generative Simulation Theory for LM-based Anomaly Detection

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of how language models can be used for detecting anomalies in lists of data.

Description: Language models can be used to generate plausible continuations or completions of lists. By comparing actual list items to LM-generated samples, anomalies can be detected as items that are unlikely to be generated by the LM, indicating deviation from the learned generative process.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Generative Discrepancy Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• LM → is_prompted_with → partial_data_list, and
• LM → generates → sampled_items, and
• item_i → is_in → data_list, and
• item_i → is_not_in → distribution_of(sampled_items)
THEN
• item_i → is_flagged_as → anomaly

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Generative models can be used to simulate data; items not generated are likely out-of-distribution.
  2. LMs can be prompted to generate plausible list completions, and comparison to actual items reveals anomalies.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law is somewhat-related-to-existing, as it adapts generative anomaly detection to the LM and list context.

What Already Exists: Generative models for anomaly detection are known in ML.

What is Novel: Using LMs to generate list completions and compare to actual list items is novel.

References:

  • Goodfellow et al. (2014) Generative Adversarial Nets [Generative models for anomaly detection]
  • Brown et al. (2020) Language Models are Few-Shot Learners [LMs generate plausible continuations]

Statement 1: Sample Likelihood Law (quantitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• LM → is_prompted_with → partial_data_list, and
• LM → assigns_probability → P(item_i | partial_data_list), and
• P(item_i | partial_data_list) → is_less_than → threshold_p
THEN
• item_i → is_flagged_as → anomaly

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Low-probability samples under a generative model are often anomalies.
  2. LMs can assign probabilities to candidate list items given a prompt.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: This law is closely-related-to-existing, but the explicit use of LM list completions is novel.

What Already Exists: Likelihood-based anomaly detection is established.

What is Novel: Application to LM-generated list completions is novel.

References:

  • Bishop (2006) Pattern Recognition and Machine Learning [Likelihood-based anomaly detection]
  • Brown et al. (2020) Language Models are Few-Shot Learners [LMs assign probabilities to completions]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If an item in a list is never generated by the LM when prompted with the rest of the list, it is likely an anomaly.
  2. If the LM is fine-tuned on a specific list structure, its generative anomaly detection will improve.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If the LM is exposed to lists with subtle, high-level structure, it is unknown whether generative sampling will capture all anomalies.
  2. If the LM is adversarially prompted, it is unclear whether generative anomaly detection will remain robust.

Negative Experiments

  1. If LMs frequently generate known anomalies, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If LMs fail to flag items that are never generated as anomalies, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Lists with high diversity or multiple valid generative modes may not be well-captured by a single LM.
  2. Cases where the LM's generative process is biased or incomplete for the domain.
