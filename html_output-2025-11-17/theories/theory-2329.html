Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2329

Name: Integrated Network Disruption Theory of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory posits that Alzheimer's disease arises from the convergence of multiple network-level disruptions, including synaptic dysfunction, impaired neural connectivity, and aberrant glial-neuronal interactions. These disruptions are initiated and propagated by a combination of genetic susceptibility, environmental exposures, and age-related molecular changes, leading to a self-reinforcing cycle of network breakdown. Detection is most effective when targeting early network-level biomarkers, such as functional connectivity changes on fMRI or EEG, rather than relying solely on amyloid or tau pathology.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Network Vulnerability Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → genetic risk factors (e.g., APOE4), and
• individual → experiences → age-related molecular changes, and
• individual → is_exposed_to → environmental risk factors (e.g., head trauma, pollution)
THEN
• neural networks → exhibit → increased vulnerability to synaptic dysfunction and connectivity loss

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. APOE4 carriers show early network connectivity changes before clinical symptoms.
  2. Environmental exposures and aging are linked to synaptic and network disruptions in animal and human studies.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While components are known, the synthesis into a network-centric, multi-factorial vulnerability law is new.

What Already Exists: Genetic and environmental risk factors are known contributors to AD; network dysfunction is increasingly recognized.

What is Novel: The explicit integration of genetic, environmental, and age-related factors as converging on network-level vulnerability is novel.

References:

  • Seeley et al. (2009) Neurodegenerative diseases target large-scale human brain networks [network focus]
  • Jagust (2018) Imaging the evolution and pathophysiology of Alzheimer disease [network and risk factors]

Statement 1: Network Biomarker Detection Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → shows → early alterations in functional connectivity (fMRI/EEG)
THEN
• individual → is_at_high_risk_for → future cognitive decline and AD diagnosis

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Functional connectivity changes on fMRI/EEG precede clinical symptoms and predict AD progression.
  2. Network-level biomarkers outperform amyloid/tau in predicting early cognitive decline in some studies.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: Detection of network changes is known, but their primacy as early detection tools is a novel emphasis.

What Already Exists: Functional connectivity changes are observed in preclinical AD.

What is Novel: The law formalizes network-level biomarkers as primary, rather than secondary, detection targets.

References:

  • Brier et al. (2014) Functional connectivity and graph theory in preclinical Alzheimer's disease [network biomarkers]
  • Sperling et al. (2011) Toward defining the preclinical stages of Alzheimer's disease [early detection]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with combined genetic and environmental risk factors will show earlier and more severe network connectivity disruptions.
  2. Network-level biomarkers will predict cognitive decline more accurately than amyloid or tau PET in early-stage individuals.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Interventions that restore network connectivity (e.g., neuromodulation) will delay or prevent AD onset.
  2. Individuals with resilient network architectures (e.g., high cognitive reserve) will resist AD progression despite amyloid/tau pathology.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding no association between network connectivity changes and future cognitive decline would challenge the theory.
  2. Demonstrating that amyloid/tau pathology alone predicts AD better than network biomarkers would call the theory into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases of AD with minimal network disruption but significant amyloid/tau pathology.

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: This theory synthesizes known risk factors and network science into a new, unified framework.
