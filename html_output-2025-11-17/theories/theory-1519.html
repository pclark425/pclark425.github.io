Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1519

Name: Functional Stable Coactivation Criterion for Concept Individuation

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This theory posits that conceptual knowledge in the brain is functionally individuated by the emergence of stable coactivation patterns among distributed representational units. A concept is defined not by a single localized representation, but by a reproducible, context-sensitive pattern of coactivation that is stable across relevant cognitive states and tasks. This criterion allows for flexible, compositional, and context-dependent conceptual representations, while maintaining functional individuation and discrimination between concepts.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Stable Coactivation as Individuation Criterion (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• set_of_representational_units → exhibits → stable_coactivation_pattern, and
• coactivation_pattern → is_reproducible_across_contexts → True
THEN
• set_of_representational_units → functionally_individuates → concept

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neuroimaging and multivariate pattern analysis show that concepts are associated with distributed, stable activation patterns across brain regions.
  2. Lesion and stimulation studies indicate that disruption of specific distributed patterns impairs concept access, even when local regions are intact.
  3. Behavioral priming and repetition suppression effects are linked to the reactivation of stable conceptual patterns.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: While distributed representation is well-known, the formalization of stable coactivation as the individuation criterion is new.

What Already Exists: Distributed representation and pattern-based individuation of concepts are established in cognitive neuroscience.

What is Novel: The explicit functional criterion of stable coactivation as the necessary and sufficient condition for concept individuation is novel.

References:

  • Haxby et al. (2001) Distributed and overlapping representations of faces and objects in ventral temporal cortex [distributed patterns]
  • Patterson et al. (2007) Where do you know what you know? The representation of semantic knowledge in the human brain [distributed semantic memory]
  • Rogers & McClelland (2004) Semantic cognition: A parallel distributed processing approach [distributed, pattern-based models]

Statement 1: Contextual Modulation of Stable Coactivation (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• contextual_input → modulates → activation_of_representational_units, and
• coactivation_pattern → remains_stable_within_contextual_variation → True
THEN
• concept → is_flexibly_accessed → across_contexts

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. fMRI studies show that conceptual representations are modulated by context but maintain core stable patterns.
  2. Behavioral studies demonstrate context-dependent priming and generalization, consistent with flexible but stable concept access.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: The law synthesizes context effects with the stable coactivation criterion, which is not present in prior work.

What Already Exists: Contextual modulation of conceptual activation is established, but often not formalized at the pattern level.

What is Novel: The law formalizes the requirement that stable coactivation persists under contextual modulation for functional individuation.

References:

  • Yee & Thompson-Schill (2016) Putting concepts into context [contextual modulation of conceptual representations]
  • Bar et al. (2008) Top-down facilitation of visual object recognition: object-based and context-based contributions [contextual effects on activation patterns]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Novel concepts will be individuated by the emergence of new, stable coactivation patterns that are reproducible across tasks.
  2. Disruption of a stable coactivation pattern (e.g., via TMS) will impair access to the corresponding concept, even if local regions are intact.
  3. Concepts with overlapping features will show partially overlapping but distinct stable coactivation patterns.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificial neural systems trained with this criterion may develop emergent, non-human-like conceptual structures.
  2. In cases of extreme context variability, the stability of coactivation patterns may break down, leading to concept confusion or blending.

Negative Experiments

  1. If concepts can be accessed without any reproducible stable coactivation pattern, the theory would be undermined.
  2. If context changes always produce entirely new patterns with no stable core, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not specify the minimal granularity or scale of representational units required for stable coactivation.
  2. It does not address how new stable coactivation patterns are learned or consolidated.

Existing Theory Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: The theory formalizes a new functional-level criterion for concept individuation, extending distributed representation models.
