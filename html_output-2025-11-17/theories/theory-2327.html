Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-2327

Name: Dynamic Homeostatic Failure Theory of Alzheimer's Disease

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the causes of Alzheimer's disease and effective detection methods.

Description: This theory proposes that Alzheimer's disease (AD) results from a progressive failure of the brain's dynamic homeostatic mechanisms, including proteostasis, synaptic scaling, and glial regulation. The breakdown of these adaptive systems leads to runaway accumulation of pathological proteins, synaptic dysfunction, and ultimately neurodegeneration. Early detection is possible by identifying breakdowns in homeostatic feedback loops, measurable via dynamic biomarkers (e.g., stress response, synaptic plasticity assays).

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Homeostatic Breakdown Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → has → impaired proteostasis, and
• individual → has → impaired synaptic scaling, and
• individual → has → impaired glial regulation
THEN
• individual → develops → progressive protein aggregation and synaptic dysfunction

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Proteostasis impairment leads to amyloid and tau accumulation; synaptic scaling and glial regulation are disrupted in AD brains.
  2. Animal models show that loss of homeostatic feedback accelerates neurodegeneration.
  3. Human postmortem studies reveal widespread failure of protein quality control and glial support in AD.
  4. Proteostasis and glial dysfunction are known contributors to AD.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While components are known, the dynamic systems failure framing is new.

What Already Exists: Proteostasis and glial dysfunction are known contributors to AD.

What is Novel: The focus on dynamic homeostatic feedback failure as the root cause, rather than static pathology, is novel.

References:

  • Selkoe (2002) Alzheimer's disease is a synaptic failure [Synaptic dysfunction focus]
  • Hetz & Saxena (2017) ER stress and the unfolded protein response in neurodegeneration [Proteostasis focus]

Statement 1: Dynamic Biomarker Detection Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• individual → shows → abnormal stress response or synaptic plasticity in dynamic assays
THEN
• individual → is_at_high_risk_for → Alzheimer's disease

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Dynamic stress response and synaptic plasticity measures are altered in preclinical AD and animal models.
  2. EEG and fMRI studies show altered neural circuit responses to stimulation in early AD.
  3. Dynamic biomarkers (e.g., response to cognitive challenge) can precede static biomarker changes.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: Dynamic biomarkers are not yet standard; this law elevates their status.

What Already Exists: Some dynamic biomarkers have been explored in research settings.

What is Novel: The law formalizes dynamic, rather than static, biomarkers as primary early detection tools.

References:

  • Busche & Konnerth (2016) Impairments of neural circuit function in Alzheimer's disease [Dynamic circuit dysfunction]
  • Jack et al. (2018) NIA-AA Research Framework: Toward a biological definition of Alzheimer's disease [Biomarker focus, but not dynamic]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with impaired synaptic plasticity or stress response (measured dynamically) will progress to AD at higher rates.
  2. Restoring homeostatic feedback (e.g., via pharmacological chaperones or glial modulators) will slow or prevent disease progression.
  3. Dynamic biomarkers will detect risk earlier than static amyloid or tau measures in longitudinal studies.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Non-invasive dynamic assays (e.g., EEG response to stimulation) will outperform static biomarkers in predicting AD onset.
  2. Some individuals may compensate for homeostatic failure via alternative adaptive mechanisms, delaying disease.
  3. Interventions targeting homeostatic feedback (rather than amyloid/tau directly) will be more effective in preclinical AD.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding individuals with normal dynamic homeostatic function who develop AD would challenge the theory.
  2. Demonstrating that static biomarkers outperform dynamic ones in early detection would call the detection law into question.
  3. Restoring homeostatic feedback without affecting disease progression would falsify the causal link.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Genetic forms of AD with minimal evidence of homeostatic failure prior to pathology.

Existing Theory Comparison

Likely Classification: new

Explanation: This theory reframes AD as a failure of adaptive systems, not just accumulation of pathology.

References:

  • Hetz & Saxena (2017) ER stress and the unfolded protein response in neurodegeneration [Proteostasis focus]
  • Selkoe (2002) Alzheimer's disease is a synaptic failure [Synaptic dysfunction, not dynamic systems failure]
