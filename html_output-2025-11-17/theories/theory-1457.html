Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1457

Name: UHPH – Predictive Contextualization of Conceptual Knowledge

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This theory proposes that the representational format of conceptual knowledge is dynamically contextualized by predictive signals at multiple hierarchical levels. The brain flexibly reconfigures the weighting and integration of distributed, symbolic, and sensorimotor codes based on current context, task demands, and prior experience, allowing for adaptive conceptual processing.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Contextual Predictive Reweighting Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• conceptual task → is_performed → in a specific context
THEN
• the brain → dynamically reweights → the contribution of distributed, symbolic, and sensorimotor codes, and
• predictive signals → modulate → the integration of these codes at multiple hierarchical levels

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Neuroimaging shows context-dependent activation of different representational formats during conceptual tasks.
  2. Behavioral priming and interference studies reveal flexible use of symbolic and sensorimotor information depending on context.
  3. Predictive coding models account for context-sensitive processing in perception and cognition.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The theory extends predictive coding to the dynamic, context-sensitive integration of hybrid conceptual codes.

What Already Exists: Contextual modulation and predictive coding are established in perception and some aspects of cognition.

What is Novel: The explicit mechanism of predictive, hierarchical reweighting of hybrid conceptual codes.

References:

  • Kiefer & Pulvermüller (2012) Conceptual representations in mind and brain: Theoretical developments, current evidence and future directions [Contextual modulation]
  • Clark (2013) Whatever next? Predictive brains, situated agents, and the future of cognitive science [Predictive coding in cognition]
  • Barsalou (2008) Grounded cognition [Contextual flexibility]

Statement 1: Experience-Dependent Hybridization Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• an individual → acquires → new conceptual knowledge
THEN
• the integration of distributed, symbolic, and sensorimotor codes → is shaped by → prior experience and learning history, and
• predictive hierarchies → are updated → to reflect new contextual contingencies

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Learning studies show that conceptual representations become more abstract and hybridized with experience.
  2. Neural plasticity is observed in regions supporting conceptual knowledge after training or exposure to new domains.
  3. Predictive coding models account for experience-dependent updating of hierarchical representations.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law synthesizes established learning principles with the hybrid, predictive framework.

What Already Exists: Experience-dependent plasticity and learning are established in conceptual and perceptual domains.

What is Novel: The explicit claim that experience shapes the hybridization and predictive hierarchy of conceptual codes.

References:

  • Lambon Ralph et al. (2017) The neural and computational bases of semantic cognition [Learning and plasticity]
  • Friston (2010) The free-energy principle: a unified brain theory? [Predictive updating]
  • Binder & Desai (2011) The neurobiology of semantic memory [Experience-dependent change]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Individuals with more diverse experience in a domain will show greater hybridization and flexibility in conceptual representations.
  2. Contextual cues will modulate the neural weighting of symbolic vs. sensorimotor codes during conceptual tasks.
  3. Training in new conceptual domains will induce measurable changes in the integration and predictive hierarchy of hybrid codes.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Longitudinal studies will reveal that the development of hybrid codes and predictive hierarchies is sensitive to critical periods of experience.
  2. Cross-cultural differences in conceptual knowledge will be reflected in distinct patterns of hybrid code integration and predictive contextualization.
  3. Artificial systems trained with context-sensitive predictive hybrid codes will generalize better to novel conceptual tasks.

Negative Experiments

  1. If conceptual representations do not change with experience or context, the theory would be challenged.
  2. If predictive reweighting is not observed in neural or behavioral data during conceptual tasks, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The influence of innate or genetically determined factors on hybrid code formation is not addressed.
