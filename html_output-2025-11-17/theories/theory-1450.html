Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1450

Name: Contextualized Relational Schema Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This theory proposes that conceptual knowledge is functionally represented as context-sensitive relational schemas—structured networks of roles, relations, and fillers that are dynamically instantiated based on current context and goals. Rather than static representations, schemas are flexibly assembled from a library of relational templates and context cues, allowing rapid adaptation to novel situations and supporting compositional generalization.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Contextual Schema Instantiation Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• context → provides → cues for concept activation
THEN
• relational schema → is instantiated → with context-appropriate roles and fillers

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Behavioral evidence for context-dependent activation and adaptation of conceptual knowledge.
  2. Neuroimaging studies show context-sensitive recruitment of relational processing regions (e.g., angular gyrus, prefrontal cortex).
  3. Schema theory in psychology supports flexible, context-driven instantiation of knowledge structures.

Likely Classification: closely-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law extends schema theory by specifying dynamic, context-driven relational instantiation at the functional level.

What Already Exists: Schema theory and context-dependent activation are established in cognitive science.

What is Novel: This law formalizes the dynamic, compositional instantiation of relational schemas as the core functional format for conceptual knowledge.

References:

  • Bartlett (1932) Remembering: A Study in Experimental and Social Psychology [schema theory]
  • Ghosh & Gilboa (2014) What is a memory schema? [contextual schema activation]
  • Gentner (1983) Structure-mapping: A theoretical framework for analogy [relational structure in concepts]

Statement 1: Compositional Generalization Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• novel situation → matches → elements of existing relational schemas
THEN
• schema components → are recombined → to generate new conceptual structures

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Humans can rapidly generalize relational knowledge to novel situations (e.g., analogical reasoning).
  2. Neural evidence for flexible recombination of relational representations in prefrontal and parietal cortex.
  3. Computational models (e.g., structure-mapping, DORA) require compositionality to account for human generalization.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law synthesizes compositionality and schema theory into a unified functional-level account.

What Already Exists: Compositionality and relational generalization are discussed in analogy and schema theories.

What is Novel: This law asserts that compositional recombination of relational schema components is the primary mechanism for conceptual generalization at the functional level.

References:

  • Gentner (1983) Structure-mapping: A theoretical framework for analogy [compositionality in relational reasoning]
  • Doumas, Hummel, & Sandhofer (2008) A theory of the discovery and predication of relational concepts [DORA, compositional generalization]
  • Ghosh & Gilboa (2014) What is a memory schema? [schema recombination]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Novel combinations of familiar relational roles and fillers will be processed more rapidly and accurately than entirely novel structures.
  2. Contextual cues that prime specific schemas will facilitate relational reasoning and generalization in related tasks.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificially manipulating context cues (e.g., via priming or brain stimulation) will induce novel schema instantiations not observed in natural settings.
  2. Training artificial agents with dynamic schema instantiation mechanisms will yield human-like flexibility in relational reasoning.

Negative Experiments

  1. If humans cannot flexibly recombine schema components to handle novel relational situations, the theory would be undermined.
  2. If context cues do not modulate schema instantiation in neural or behavioral data, the theory's core claim would be challenged.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not specify the neural or algorithmic mechanisms for schema library storage and retrieval.
