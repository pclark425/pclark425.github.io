Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1622

Name: Prompt-Demonstration Bottleneck Theory

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of factors affecting the accuracy of using large language models (LLMs) as text-based simulators for specific scientific subdomains.

Description: This theory posits that the structure and content of prompts and demonstrations provided to LLMs act as a primary bottleneck on the accuracy and fidelity of scientific simulation, regardless of the underlying model's scale or training. The theory asserts that the information encoding, explicitness, and alignment of prompt/demonstration structure with the target scientific subdomain's epistemic norms are limiting factors, and that improvements in prompt engineering can yield accuracy gains even without model retraining.

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Prompt Structure-Accuracy Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• prompt structure → is_misaligned_with → target scientific subdomain conventions
THEN
• LLM scientific simulation accuracy → is_limited_by → prompt structure

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Empirical studies show that LLMs perform better on tasks when prompts are tailored to the conventions and expectations of the target domain (e.g., chemistry, mathematics).
  2. Prompt engineering can significantly improve LLM performance on scientific reasoning tasks without model retraining.
  3. Chain-of-thought and least-to-most prompting methods demonstrate that prompt structure can unlock reasoning capabilities otherwise latent in the model.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While prompt engineering is a known factor, the theory's assertion that prompt structure can be the primary limiting factor in scientific simulation accuracy is a new, more general claim.

What Already Exists: Prompt engineering is known to affect LLM performance, and domain-specific prompting has been explored.

What is Novel: The explicit framing of prompt structure as a bottleneck that can override model scale or training for scientific simulation is novel.

References:

  • Wei et al. (2022) Chain-of-Thought Prompting Elicits Reasoning in Large Language Models [Prompting structure affects reasoning]
  • Zhou et al. (2022) Least-to-Most Prompting Enables Complex Reasoning in Large Language Models [Prompt structure impacts performance]

Statement 1: Demonstration Informativeness Law (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• demonstrations → lack → critical domain-specific information
THEN
• LLM scientific simulation → fails_to_generalize → target subdomain phenomena

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Few-shot demonstrations that omit key domain-specific steps or conventions lead to LLMs failing to reproduce correct scientific reasoning.
  2. Providing more informative, stepwise demonstrations improves LLM accuracy in scientific tasks.
  3. Studies show that LLMs are sensitive to the completeness and explicitness of demonstrations, especially in scientific and mathematical domains.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While related to existing work on few-shot learning, the law's generalization to scientific simulation and its bottleneck framing are novel.

What Already Exists: Few-shot learning and demonstration informativeness are known to affect LLM performance.

What is Novel: The law's focus on the necessity of domain-specific information in demonstrations for scientific simulation is more explicit and general.

References:

  • Brown et al. (2020) Language Models are Few-Shot Learners [Demonstration informativeness affects performance]
  • Min et al. (2022) Rethinking the Role of Demonstrations: What Makes In-Context Learning Work? [Demonstration content matters]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. If prompts are rewritten to match the epistemic conventions of a new scientific subdomain, LLM simulation accuracy will increase without model retraining.
  2. Providing demonstrations that explicitly include all domain-relevant steps will improve LLM performance on complex scientific reasoning tasks.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. For highly novel scientific subdomains with no established conventions, prompt structure may become the dominant limiting factor, potentially leading to unpredictable LLM behavior.
  2. If a prompt is constructed using a hybrid of conventions from two distinct scientific subdomains, LLM simulation accuracy may degrade in non-linear or unexpected ways.

Negative Experiments

  1. If LLMs achieve high scientific simulation accuracy with poorly structured or irrelevant prompts, this would contradict the theory.
  2. If adding domain-specific information to demonstrations does not improve LLM performance, the theory would be called into question.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. Cases where LLMs perform well despite minimal or generic prompting, possibly due to strong pretraining on the target domain.
