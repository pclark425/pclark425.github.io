Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-1516

Name: Functional Stable Coactivation Criterion for Concept Individuation

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of the representational format of conceptual knowledge in brains at a functional (not neural) level.

Description: This theory posits that a concept is functionally individuated by the presence of a stable, recurring pattern of coactivation across multiple cognitive tasks and contexts. The representational format is defined not by the specific neural substrate, but by the reproducibility and functional stability of coactivation patterns that uniquely identify a concept, regardless of sensory modality or task demands. This criterion allows for cross-modal and cross-task generalization, and provides a functional, rather than anatomical, basis for concept individuation.

Theory (Details)

Theory Statements

Statement 0: Stable Coactivation as Individuation Criterion (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• coactivation_pattern → is_stable → across_tasks_and_contexts
THEN
• coactivation_pattern → individuates → concept

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. MVPA and representational similarity analysis show that certain activation patterns are reliably associated with specific concepts across tasks and modalities.
  2. Cross-modal priming and generalization studies demonstrate that concept identity is preserved despite changes in input modality.
  3. Patients with category-specific semantic deficits often lose the ability to activate stable patterns for specific concepts.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: While distributed representations are known, the formalization of functional stability as the individuation criterion is a new contribution.

What Already Exists: Distributed and stable representations are discussed in connectionist and semantic memory literature.

What is Novel: The explicit use of functional stability of coactivation as the individuation criterion, independent of neural substrate, is novel.

References:

  • Rogers & McClelland (2004) Semantic Cognition [distributed representations]
  • Haxby et al. (2001) Distributed and overlapping representations of faces and objects in ventral temporal cortex [stable patterns across tasks]

Statement 1: Cross-Modal and Cross-Task Generalization of Conceptual Patterns (qualitative)

ConditionDetails
IF
• coactivation_pattern → is_stable → across_modalities_and_tasks
THEN
• coactivation_pattern → supports → concept_generalization

Supporting Evidence for this Law

  1. Concept decoding is possible across visual, auditory, and linguistic tasks, indicating a modality-independent functional pattern.
  2. Cross-task generalization in fMRI decoding studies shows that the same pattern can be used to identify a concept in different cognitive tasks.

Existing Law Comparison

Likely Classification: somewhat-related-to-existing

Explanation: The law extends existing ideas of amodal representation by formalizing the functional stability criterion.

What Already Exists: Cross-modal generalization is discussed in the context of amodal semantic hubs.

What is Novel: The explicit link between functional stability and individuation, and the criterion for generalization, is novel.

References:

  • Patterson, Nestor, & Rogers (2007) Where do you know what you know? The representation of semantic knowledge in the human brain [amodal hub theory]
  • Haxby et al. (2001) Distributed and overlapping representations of faces and objects in ventral temporal cortex [cross-modal decoding]

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. Concepts with highly stable coactivation patterns will be more robust to task and modality changes.
  2. Disruption of stable coactivation patterns (e.g., via TMS) will impair concept access across multiple tasks.
  3. Concepts lacking stable coactivation patterns will show poor generalization across modalities.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Artificial neural networks trained to maximize coactivation stability will develop human-like concept individuation.
  2. Concepts with inherently unstable or context-dependent coactivation patterns may resist individuation, leading to fuzzy or overlapping conceptual boundaries.
  3. Developmental changes in coactivation stability may predict the emergence of new concepts in children.

Negative Experiments

  1. If concepts can be individuated without any stable coactivation pattern, the theory would be undermined.
  2. If stable coactivation patterns do not predict concept generalization across tasks or modalities, the theory would be called into question.
  3. If different concepts share identical stable coactivation patterns, the individuation criterion would fail.

Unaccounted for Evidence

  1. The theory does not address how coactivation patterns are initially formed or how stability is achieved.
  2. The theory does not explain how concepts with overlapping features are functionally separated.
