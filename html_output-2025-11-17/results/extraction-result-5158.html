Paper Title: On the representation and processing of social information in grounded cognitive systems: why terminology matters

Paper Abstract: The mechanisms underlying social behavior are indeed complex, yet researchers have made important contributions to our understanding of how people make judgments and behave across various social contexts. In particular, recent years has seen a proliferation of research spotlighting the guiding role of embodied and affective information in social processing. Grounded approaches to cognition offer an exciting opportunity for researchers throughout the cognitive sciences to work within a unified framework to shed light on traditionally nebulous and intractable psychological quagmires (e.g., symbol grounding).

citation_title Perceptual symbol systems.
mention_or_use mention
theory_or_model_name Perceptual Symbol Systems (PSS)
theory_or_model_description Conceptual knowledge is represented functionally as distributed patterns of perceptual and sensorimotor states (perceptual symbols) stored from prior embodied experiences; later conceptual processing recruits (partially re‑instantiates) these perceptual states in context-sensitive, bottom-up simulations.
representation_format_type distributed, perceptual/sensorimotor (simulation-based) representations
key_properties Distributed across perceptual/motor modalities; partially reactivatable; context-sensitive reconstruction; flexible and dynamic; grounded in experience; supports bidirectional influence between concrete and abstract domains.
empirical_support Cited embodied social findings consistent with PSS: physical warmth influencing interpersonal warmth (Williams & Bargh, 2008), gustatory disgust affecting moral judgments (Eskine et al., 2011), cleansing/clean scents affecting moral judgments and prosociality (Schnall et al., 2008a; Liljenquist et al., 2010), elevation increasing altruism (Schnall et al., 2010), sweet tastes predicting helping (Meier et al., 2012a). Barsalou's work (1999, 2008) is cited as the theoretical basis.
empirical_challenges Debates about directionality (unidirectional metaphor effects vs bidirectional simulation effects) are discussed; replication failures such as Fayard et al. (2009) failing to replicate Zhong & Liljenquist (2006) indicate some embodied effects can be fragile or context-dependent, challenging strong claims of invariant simulation effects.
applied_domains_or_tasks Social cognition (moral judgment, prosociality), conceptual processing, metaphorical mappings, person perception; tasks include taste manipulations, scent manipulations, warmth manipulations, economic trust games, volunteer/helping measures.
comparison_to_other_models Contrasted with conceptual metaphor theory: PSS emphasizes reactivation/simulation of perceptual states and predicts bidirectionality, whereas classical conceptual metaphor accounts emphasize structured, often unidirectional mappings from concrete source to abstract target; PSS is also presented as compatible in many respects (both rely on experiential coactivation). Compared to symbolic/semantic network models, PSS is embodied and distributed rather than amodal symbolic.
functional_mechanisms Formation via co-occurrence of perceptual states during experience yielding distributed modality-specific traces; retrieval via partial reactivation (simulation) in response to conceptual cues; combination via coactivation and reconstruction shaped by task demands, context, and top-down knowledge.
limitations_or_open_questions How to formalize the exact format of perceptual symbols and the dynamics of reconstruction; distinguishing representational structure from online processing; specifying boundaries between simulation-based and metaphorical processes; robustness and boundary conditions of embodied effects (replicability, cultural/contextual modulation).

theory_or_model_description Abstract conceptual knowledge is functionally organized by mappings from concrete, embodied source domains (e.g., purity, taste, warmth) onto abstract target domains (e.g., morality, prosociality), so that source-domain experiences can systematically structure thinking about targets.
representation_format_type mapping-based, source–target projection (conceptual mappings grounded in embodied source domains)

empirical_support Many behavioral findings are cited as consistent with metaphorical projections: cleansing affecting moral judgments and guilt (Zhong & Liljenquist, 2006; Schnall et al., 2008a), clean scents increasing reciprocity (Liljenquist et al., 2010), taste–prosocial links (Meier et al., 2012a), disgust influencing moral judgments (Schnall et al., 2008b; Eskine et al., 2011).
empirical_challenges Paper discusses controversy over directionality—empirical demonstrations of abstract-to-concrete effects (e.g., thinking about moral transgressions changing taste perception; Eskine et al., 2012) challenge a strictly unidirectional conceptualization. applied_domains_or_tasks Moral judgment, prosocial behavior, person perception, economic decision-making, perceptual ratings; experiments typically manipulate embodied source cues (taste, scent, cleanliness, warmth) and measure abstract judgments or behavior.
comparison_to_other_models Contrasted with PSS/simulation accounts: conceptual metaphor emphasizes structured mappings (often seen as unidirectional) whereas simulation emphasizes modality-specific reactivation and bidirectionality; the paper argues both rely on experiential coactivation and may be better reconciled than treated as rivals.
functional_mechanisms Mechanism described as projection/mapping of source-domain representations onto abstract target domains formed through repeated co-occurrence and linguistic/cultural encoding; activation of source domain can influence target judgments depending on context and task.
limitations_or_open_questions Whether conceptual metaphors are inherently directional in representational structure or whether directionality is an artifact of linguistic conventions; how metaphorical mappings are neurally or functionally implemented (connectivity/coactivation vs rigid directional links); scope and boundary conditions across cultures and contexts.

theory_or_model_description Conceptual processing functionally depends on on-line partial re-instantiation (simulation) of the perceptual, motor, and affective states that accompanied prior experiences, enabling bidirectional influences between concrete perception and abstract cognition.
representation_format_type modality-specific simulations / distributed embodied representations
key_properties Modality-specific, reactivatable, supports bidirectional effects (abstract→concrete and concrete→abstract), context-sensitive, reconstructive rather than fixed symbolic format.

applied_domains_or_tasks Semantic processing, metaphorical grounding, moral judgment, social perception; tasks include sensory manipulations and measures of abstract judgments, perception, and behavior.
comparison_to_other_models Close to PSS (often treated as the same family); contrasted with classical symbolic/semantic-network accounts (e.g., Quillian, Collins & Loftus) that posit amodal symbolic nodes; compared with conceptual metaphor theory where the latter emphasizes mapping structure and simulation emphasizes reenactment.
functional_mechanisms Partial reactivation of modality-specific traces during conceptual retrieval; coactivation across modalities forms conceptual content; task demands and top-down goals shape which modality traces are recruited.
limitations_or_open_questions Need for precise specification of what constitutes a simulation trace and how simulations combine/compositionalize; formal models of dynamics and limits on bidirectionality; delineating when simulation vs metaphorical mapping better explains data.

limitations_or_open_questions How to implement rich compositional structure and symbolic-like combinatorics; how connectionist representations map to phenomenological modality-specific simulations; need for explicit tests tying connectionist architectures to the specific embodied social findings discussed.

functional_mechanisms Online identification of relevant input spaces, selective projection of structure into a blended space, emergent inference generation from the blend, and back-projection to inputs; heavily context-dependent coactivation drives blending.
limitations_or_open_questions Operationalizing blends for experimental testing and computational modeling; linking blending operations with modality-specific neural/functional processes; delineating when blending vs stable mappings occur.

name_short Connectivity proposal
name_full Connectivity (proposed reframing of directionality)
brief_description The paper's proposed reframing that conceptual representation should be described in terms of connectivity/coactivation between embodied and abstract domains rather than rigid directional mappings.
citation_title here
mention_or_use use
theory_or_model_name Connectivity (coactivation-based reframing)
theory_or_model_description Conceptual representations are best characterized functionally as networks of coactivated embodied and abstract elements whose interactions are determined by experience, task demands, and context rather than fixed unidirectional mappings.
representation_format_type connectivity-based, dynamic coactivation networks (context-sensitive distributed links)
key_properties Context sensitivity, flexibility, bidirectionality as emergent from connectivity, cultural and individual variability, reliance on coactivation histories rather than fixed directional structure.
empirical_support Argument grounded on the suite of embodied social findings cited (warmth, disgust, taste, cleanliness, elevation, scent effects) and theoretical compatibility with PSS, connectionist learning, and blending; the paper cites evidence for bidirectional effects (Eskine et al., 2012) to motivate connectivity framing.
empirical_challenges The reframing responds to empirical observations that sometimes appear unidirectional and to replication/fragility issues (e.g., Fayard et al., 2009), so it identifies the need to explain when connectivity yields directional-appearing effects; empirical validation as a distinct formal model is not provided in the paper.
applied_domains_or_tasks Suggested for use in social cognition research (moral judgment, prosociality), cross-cultural studies of metaphor and embodiment, and for reconciling simulation and metaphorical evidence in conceptual processing experiments.
comparison_to_other_models Proposed as terminological and conceptual middle ground reconciling PSS/simulation and conceptual metaphor accounts: both are seen as relying on coactivation/connectivity, so emphasizing connectivity reduces artificial theoretical divisions based on 'directionality'.
functional_mechanisms Coactivation-driven retrieval: repeated co-occurrence of embodied and abstract elements builds flexible connectivity; online processing recruits subsets of the network according to context, yielding variable directionality of observed effects.
limitations_or_open_questions Requires formal operationalization and empirical tests to distinguish connectivity-as-description from mechanistic models; predictive specificity compared to existing frameworks (PSS, connectionist, metaphor) needs to be established; how to measure and model connectivity dynamics across individuals and cultures remains open.

On the representation and processing of social information in grounded cognitive systems: why terminology matters published: 10 April 2013OPINION ARTICLE *Correspondence: Edited by: Corrado Corradi-Dell' Acqua, University of Geneva, Switzerland Reviewed by:

The mechanisms underlying social behavior are indeed complex, yet researchers have made important contributions to our understanding of how people make judgments and behave across various social contexts. In particular, recent years has seen a proliferation of research spotlighting the guiding role of embodied and affective information in social processing. Grounded approaches to cognition offer an exciting opportunity for researchers throughout the cognitive sciences to work within a unified framework to shed light on traditionally nebulous and intractable psychological quagmires (e.g., symbol grounding).

In what follows I will describe how embodied and affective information influence some hallmark social processes (moral judgments and prosociality) and then clarify some misunderstandings about representation and processing in grounded cognitive systems. I will then argue that the term "directionality" in grounded accounts engenders misleading views about cognition and will conclude with recommendations that should improve our understanding of social behavior from the growing perspective of grounded cognition.

A growing body of literature indicates that embodied and affective states influence moral judgments and prosociality. In the domain of moral judgment, research has shown that inducing physical disgust (via visual, olfactory, and gustatory senses) can harshen moral judgments (Schnall et al., 2008b;Eskine et al., 2011). The conceptual overlap between physical and moral disgust has been further confirmed with physiological evidence (Calder et al., 2001;Moll et al., 2005;Borg et al., 2008;Chapman et al., 2009). In a similar vein, researchers have also demonstrated that feeling physically clean and pure can license people to judge others morally harsher than those feeling dirty . However, physical cleansing can also attenuate people's own moral guilt. Zhong and Liljenquist (2006) found that people felt less guilty about their own transgressions (and were more likely to volunteer) after they had cleansed themselves with an antiseptic wipe, whereas those who did not receive a wipe showed increased volunteerism (but see also Fayard et al., 2009).

This line of research is often described as a moral purity metaphor, in which physical purity is metaphorically projected onto conceptual representations of morality. However, the direction of these effects travels both ways. A disgusting taste in the mouth can harshen moral judgments (Eskine et al., 2011), but thinking about moral transgressions, virtues, or control events can lead people to perceive a neutral tasting beverage as disgusting, delicious, or neutral-tasting, respectively (Eskine et al., 2012; see also Ritter and Preston, 2011). Similarly, cleanliness can attenuate harsh moral judgments (Schnall et al., 2008a), while committing moral transgressions can enhance the desirability of cleansing products (Lee and Schwarz, 2010). The implications of directionality for grounded theories will be discussed.

In the domain of prosociality, Schnall et al. (2010) explored whether emotional elevation affected volunteerism (Study 1) and helping behavior (Study 2). Overall, they found that those who experienced elevation, but not other positive emotions like happiness or amusement, were more likely to volunteer for an unpaid study and help experimenters with a boring task compared to those in control conditions. Similarly, Liljenquist et al. (2010) tested whether clean scents affect financial decisions in an economic trust game with the prediction that clean scents will prime purity and thus enhance altruism. Results confirmed that those in cleanscented rooms gave more money to an alleged team-mate compared with those in baseline rooms. They replicated these findings by showing that clean-scented rooms also encouraged volunteerism and monetary donations for helpful causes.

To determine whether there is any psychological truth in common taste metaphors like "she/he's a sweetie," Meier et al. (2012a) found that preferences for sweet foods significantly predicted prosocial behavior, and in another study they revealed that participants who consumed sweet foods (chocolate) were more likely to help another than those who consumed non-sweet foods (cracker) or nothing. These findings indicated that taste is an important embodied source domain that is projected onto abstract domains like prosociality, which bolsters a conceptual metaphor view that grounds abstract meaning in embodied source domains. But to what extent do views like these overlap in their theoretical assumptions with other views of grounded cognition (e.g., simulation theories)?

Broadly, principles of grounded cognition assert that sensorimotor and perceptual experiences are instrumental in the representation and processing of concepts. Simulation (Barsalou, 1999(Barsalou, , 2008 and conceptual metaphor Johnson, 1980, 1999) approaches represent the two dominant theories of conceptual grounding. Simulation models posit that conceptual processing recruits (roughly) the same perceptual states that were originally instantiated during one's initial embodied experiences, and metaphorical models contend that concrete, embodied experiences are projected onto abstract target domains. Although both views are "embodied," it remains unclear whether the origin and organization of conceptual knowledge ultimately reside in simulation-based models rooted in perceptual simulation or conceptual metaphors as explained by cognitive linguistics. I view this point as important but somewhat tangential with respect to the current debates on the structure of conceptual knowledge. Metaphorical theories are traditionally argued to have a unidirectional structure (concrete-to-abstract effects), whereas simulation theories imply bidirectionality (concrete-to-abstract and abstract-to-concrete effects). These views are compatible to the extent that they both ground meaning in embodied/affective states, yet the issue of directionality has caused concern among many researchers (Landau et al., 2010;IJzerman and Koole, 2011;Lee and Schwarz, 2012;Slepian et al., 2012). Lee and Schwarz (2012) maintain that the manner in which concepts are generally represented (representational structure) does not necessitate how concepts will be processed in real-time cognition. They argue that conceptual representations can have unidirectional structure but can still reveal bidirectional effects when processed online. While their insights are accurate, this seems to be a point that should not require defending if one considers the interaction between conceptual representation and processing. Representational structure and online processing are intricately interwoven, so much so, that teasing the two apart, particularly in terms of causality (directionality), can seem at times like more of an exercise for the armchair than the laboratory. While classic findings from cognitive science have helped refine our understanding of representation and processing (e.g., the rejection of traditionally accepted semantic network models à la Quillian, 1969, andLoftus, 1975, in lieu of more complex connectionist models à la McClelland, 2000), this representation-processing distinction is a core component of Barsalou's (1999) perceptual symbol systems (PSS).

Sensorimotor activity that naturally accompanies various perceptual states becomes incorporated into the representational and processing structure of concrete (e.g., cats) and abstract (e.g., generosity) category domains. These embodied perceptual states are stored in memory and (partially) reactivated in bottom-up format during later conceptual processing. For example, many early experiences of interpersonal warmth naturally co-occur with physical warmth, such as cradling infants. Hence, perceptual experiences involved with physical and interpersonal warmth become part of the same representational and processing structure, which is one way to explain the nowpopular finding that experiencing physical warmth can promote interpersonal warmth toward a stranger (Williams and Bargh, 2008). Here, the representations themselves are the patterns of neural activity that span different regions of the brain, specifically perceptual and motor areas. While they may have some rough-andready structure, the task demands, social context, embodiment, top-down knowledge, affective states, etc. will re-construct representational-processing paths on a case-by-case basis. Thus, it should be no surprise that embodied states can affect abstract judgments and vice versa (implying bidirectional structure); they all participate in the same conceptual domain. Therefore, while Lee and Schwarz (2012) are correct in arguing for bidirectionality in conceptual metaphors, a significant aspect of cognition is glossed over. I would assert that metaphorical knowledge is organized along lines of connectivity, not directionality. Both Lakoff and Johnson's (1999) and Lee and Schwarz's (2012) explanation of metaphor indicate that embodied and abstract domains are linked with each other and acquired through experiential coactivation, which is theoretically consistent with PSS. This truth in itself obviates the need for discussion of directionality because the manner in which these representations are activated (concrete-to-abstract or abstract-to-concrete) is simply a matter of the task-demands and context for a given embodied agent.

If this is indeed the case, then (1) why such emphasis on directionality and (2) what does this mean for social behavior? First, conceptual metaphor theory was born (in part) out of research in linguistics (Lakoff and Johnson, 1980), and directionality matters in linguistic metaphors. For example, calling a "butcher a surgeon" is very different than calling a "surgeon a butcher." The direction of the metaphor completely changes its meaning. Thus, directionality seems to be crucial to understanding/inferring meaning in linguistic metaphors, but it can be argued to be irrelevant to conceptual metaphors because brains do not process information in terms of rigid directionality; their processing is often determined by context-sensitive experiences that provide coactivations between various processing regions. Second, in addition to propagating misleading views about conceptual processing, another danger of overemphasizing directionality in grounded theories is that it can lead researchers down garden path research programs. Bidirectional and unidirectional effects can still be accommodated by simulation models, and both distract researchers from testing more specific models of groundedness. Third, though it is well-documented that metaphorical approaches can transfer embodied source domains to various dissimilar abstract/target domains (Landau et al., 2010), which implies directionality, context-sensitivity can still account for these differences, and it cannot be ruled out that more complex activation patterns in association areas of the brain undergird these effects. Therefore, since both PSS and conceptual metaphors appear to equally rely on such coactivations to create substrates for conceptual development, the use of the term "directionality" engenders misleading views about conceptual representation-processing and needlessly creates theoretical divisions among similar-minded researchers.

This clarification is particularly important for social behavior because conceptual knowledge has been demonstrated to be an important but flexible foundation that shapes people's social processing (see Lee and Schwarz, 2011, for evidence for context specificity). Therefore, I propose that we shift the language from "directionality" to "connectivity" to highlight the relative flexibility of conceptual systems, and how variability (culturally, contextually, and individually) helps determine how people think, judge, and act toward others. This view is also consistent with alternative metaphorical models that rely on "blends" to engender the kinds of temporary, embodied, contextually sensitive, and dynamical conditions that seems most consistent with what is currently known about cognitive processing (see Fauconnier and Turner, 1998, for a blending theory of metaphor that is more compatible with Johnson, 1999, than Lakoff andJohnson, 1980). Thus, while directionality is an appropriate tool for linguistic metaphors, it proves problematic for conceptual processing. Since these are separate domains, this proposed distinction will similarly be unable to accommodate linguistic metaphors because it is tooled for investigating conceptual processing.

In short, embodied/affective states not only help ground meaning and guide social processing but are also intricately linked to online processing and experience, upon which representationalprocessing states are founded. Along these lines, researchers have rightly argued that more attention should be given to cultural differences in metaphorical and embodied cognition (Meier et al., 2012b), which is better accommodated by "connective" rather than "directional" terminology, as the latter implies a certain amount of rigidity that fails to empirically occur in the brain or in social conceptual processing. In this way, there is considerable overlap in simulation and metaphorical views of grounded cognition.

Embodied effects that were once striking, intriguing, and perhaps confounding, are now commonplace, and it is indeed time to breathe life into these effects with systematic theory-building that is predicated on a deeper analysis of the mechanisms underlying grounded theories of cognition. By nature, embodied and affective information are flexible sources of information, which is evidenced by their context dependence (Lee and Schwarz, 2011), and researchers have just begun tapping into their malleable (and adaptive) properties. To better investigate the nuances of grounded theories of social behavior, I have proposed that we reconsider our terminology in simulation-and metaphorical-based approaches. Rather than focusing on aspects of directionality that are couched in linguistic theories, highlighting the connective properties that develop through coactivation seems like a more promising path that better reflects how the brain actually processes information.
