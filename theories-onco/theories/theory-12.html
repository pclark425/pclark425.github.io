Theory (General Information)

ID: theory-12

Name: Coal–PAH Environmental Signature Theory of Regionally Distinct EGFR Mutational Spectra

Type: specific

Theory Query: Build a theory of why EGFR activating mutations in exons 18-21 in lung cancer are more common in East Asian populations, based on the following results.

Description: Chronic exposure to high levels of polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) and related mutagenic compounds from indoor coal combustion and coal mining activities induces a characteristic shift in the EGFR mutational spectrum in lung cancers among exposed populations. Specifically, these populations have a higher frequency of EGFR exon 18 (G719X) and exon 20 (S768I, G719X+S768I) mutations, along with a higher prevalence of G>T and T>G transversions in both the EGFR and TP53 genes, compared to matched populations with similar ancestry but lower environmental exposure. This pattern contrasts with the exon 19 deletion/exon 21 L858R-dominant profile observed in most global East Asian and non-Asian populations and serves as a molecular signature of environment-driven carcinogenesis.

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Environmental Mutagen-Induced Spectrum Shift Law

Statement: In populations experiencing chronic, high-level exposure to coal-derived PAHs and particulate matter, there is a statistically significant increase in the frequency and proportion of EGFR exon 18 (G719X) and exon 20 (S768I, G719X+S768I) mutations among EGFR-positive lung cancers, as well as an increased absolute frequency of G>T and T>G transversions in both EGFR and TP53 coding regions, compared to populations of similar ancestry but with lower exposure.

Domain/Scope: Applies to human populations residing in coal-producing or coal-burning regions in East Yunnan (Qujing), Xuanwei, Southwestern China, and comparable high-exposure coal/PAH environments, particularly where chronic indoor and outdoor coal use (poor ventilation, household/fuel usage) or occupational mining is prevalent. The law is specific to the observed EGFR/TP53 mutational spectra in lung cancer and does not necessarily generalize to all somatic mutation processes in other tissue types.

Special Cases

  1. In coal-exposed populations with simultaneous high prevalence of other mutagenic exposures (radon, heavy metals), spectrum may combine or blur into composite patterns.
  2. If coal-exposed individuals move to low-exposure environments and sufficiently reduce their exposure duration, their somatic mutation risk profile may partially revert toward the non-exposed population spectrum (time/dose dependency).
  3. Within-person/household exposure may be gender-dependent (e.g., indoor cooking), creating intra-population substructure.

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. In prospective or retrospective cohort studies, other human populations with chronic high PAH/coal exposure (e.g., rural China outside Yunnan, heavily coal-exposed regions in Eastern Europe or India) will demonstrate similar enrichment of EGFR G719X/S768I and higher G>T transversions in EGFR/TP53 among lung cancer cases compared to matched low-exposure controls.
  2. Regional or household-level intervention programs (e.g., stove upgrades, removal of coal as primary fuel source) implemented in coal-exposed communities will, after a latency, reduce the fraction of new lung cancers with uncommon (exon 18/20) EGFR mutations relative to the baseline period.
  3. Individuals with biochemically measurable PAH-DNA adducts in airway tissues (or surrogate biosamples) will have lung tumors with higher prevalence of G719X/S768I EGFR mutations and G>T/T>G transversions than individuals without such adducts, even after controlling for ancestry and tobacco use.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. If a high-PAH-exposure population of non-East Asian ancestry (e.g., Eastern European, African) is studied, it is unknown whether the environmental spectrum shift would override the underlying genetic background, or if the gene-environment interaction is modulated by ancestry.
  2. The application of whole-exome or genome sequencing to lung cancers from industrial pollution hotspots (non-coal, e.g., petrochemical or diesel exhaust) may or may not reveal analogous infrequent driver enrichment; result will clarify mutagen specificity.
  3. The frequency of G719X/S768I in a given time and region could be used as a proxy to track historical shifts in coal use and environmental remediation impact—in settings with known chronological exposure data.

Negative Experiments

  1. Finding that EGFR mutational spectra (exon 19/21 dominance) are identical in regions/individuals with and without chronic coal/PAH exposure, controlling for ancestry and tobacco, would strongly contradict the stated law.
  2. Repeated absence of increased G>T/T>G transversions in EGFR or TP53 in cancers from high-exposure populations would undermine the mutagenic mechanism specified.
  3. Demonstrating no association between household or personal PAH exposure levels and uncommon EGFR mutation frequency (after rigorous exposure ascertainment) would refute the exposure–mutation spectrum linkage.
