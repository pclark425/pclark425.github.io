Name: Integrated Environmental–Germline Interaction Model for EGFR Mutation Prevalence

Type: general

Theory Query: Build a theory of why EGFR activating mutations in exons 18-21 in lung cancer are more common in East Asian populations, based on the following results.

Description: This theory proposes that the observed geographic distribution of EGFR activating mutations in lung cancer is determined by the interaction between inherited genetic susceptibility (frequency of certain risk alleles and admixture proportions) and exposure to regional or global environmental and lifestyle factors (including smoking, domestic air pollution, PAHs from coal, cooking oil fumes, nitrosamines from diet, and air quality). Both axes—the germline and the environment—are necessary but not individually sufficient to produce the extremely high rates seen in East Asia and in some other hotspots; the theory predicts that specific environmental mutagens shape not only overall prevalence but also the subtype spectrum of EGFR mutations (e.g., G719X/S768I in coal regions).

Theory (Statement/Laws)

Theory Statements/Laws

Statement 0: Multiplicative Environmental–Genetic Prevalence Law

Statement: The frequency of EGFR-activating mutations in a given population is a function of (a) germline risk allele frequency (determined by ancestry/admixture), (b) the prevalence and type of environmental/lifestyle exposures (smoking, indoor/outdoor particulates, PAHs, etc.), and (c) their interaction; this interaction is synergistic (multiplicative), not merely additive—populations with both high-risk genotypes and intense environmental exposures (or protective environments) manifest the most extreme frequencies.

Domain/Scope: All global and regional human populations; particularly valid for comparisons across populations with substantial ancestry or environmental heterogeneity (e.g., East/Southeast Asia, MENA, South America, coal regions of China, urban vs rural comparisons). Applies primarily to adult lung adenocarcinoma.

Special Cases

  1. In unique extreme environments (e.g., coal-burning regions), environmental effect may override germline background and drive unique mutational spectra.
  2. Major changes in environmental risk factors (e.g., rapid urbanization, reduction of domestic coal use) can shift population EGFR-prevalence over decades.

Theory (Additional Details)

New Predictions (Likely outcome)

  1. In newly urbanized populations where air pollution and high ancestry risk alleles coexist (e.g., in developing Asian megacities), EGFR mutation prevalence in never-smoker adenocarcinoma will increase over time.
  2. If major environmental interventions (e.g., air pollution/pollutant reduction, transition away from coal) are implemented in high-risk populations, EGFR-prevalence and spectrum will shift over subsequent generations.
  3. In admixed populations (e.g., urban MENA, Hispanic/Latin American, certain Pacific Islanders), intermediate EGFR frequencies will map to admixture and localized exposures.

New Predictions (Unknown outcome/high-entropy)

  1. Intervention studies replacing high-PAH fuels in coal-burning areas will result in both lower rates of overall EGFR-mutant tumors and revert the spectrum (e.g., reduce G719X/S768I proportion).
  2. In countries with high East Asian ancestry but new, non-traditional environmental exposures (e.g., high ambient nitrosamine from diet), novel EGFR mutation types may emerge or shift in frequency.
  3. Targeted environmental remediation (e.g., air cleaning, cooking-exposure abatement) will yield measurable pre–post reductions in EGFR-driven LUAD, particularly among genetically susceptible individuals.

Negative Experiments

  1. Populations with similar environmental exposures but widely differing ancestry-based risk alleles showing identical EGFR frequencies would argue against an interaction model.
  2. Demonstration that coal-exposed populations have altered mutation spectra, but not increased overall prevalence, would challenge the model's assumptions about effect size.
  3. If environmental exposure alone is sufficient to drive high EGFR frequencies in absence of measurable germline risk (i.e., in purely European or African populations), the model would require revision.
